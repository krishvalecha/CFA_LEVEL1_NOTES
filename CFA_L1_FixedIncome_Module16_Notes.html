Qualitative Factors — Business Model, Industry & Competition

Corporate creditworthiness depends primarily on a company's ability to generate profits and cash flow sufficient to meet interest and principal payments. Qualitative analysis assesses the stability, predictability, and sustainability of those cash flows — before any numbers are run. The key insight for credit analysts: unlike equity analysts who value all future cash flows, fixed-income analysts focus on whether cash flows are sufficient and timely enough to cover fixed debt obligations over the specific maturity of the debt claim.

Business Model
  • Demand/Revenue/Margin stability: Stable, predictable cash flows → lower POD
  • Asset quality: Tangible vs. intangible assets available as collateral
  • Capital-light vs. asset-heavy models have different debt capacities
  • Recurring vs. non-recurring revenue — credit analysts focus on recurring
  • Firms with stable cash flows can carry more debt safely
Industry & Competition
  • Industry structure: Concentration, competitive forces, barriers to entry
  • Long-term growth and demand: Secular growth vs. declining industry
  • Life cycle: growth stage vs. mature vs. declining (affects debt sustainability)
  • Cyclicality: commodity, auto, construction — highly cyclical = higher credit risk at same leverage
  • Intra-industry rivalry: pricing power → margin stability
Business Risk
  • Deviations from expected demand, revenue, and margin — the key risk
  • Issuer-specific: company-level execution risk
  • Industry-specific: cyclicality, intra-industry rivalry, life cycle stage
  • External: macroeconomic, technology disruption, demographic shifts, geopolitical, ESG
  • Higher business risk → less debt capacity → higher POD at a given leverage level
💡 Credit Analyst vs. Equity Analyst — Different Lens on the Same Company

An equity analyst values all future cash flows accruing to shareholders — they care about growth potential and upside.

A credit analyst asks: can this company make its next interest payment? Its payment in five years? Will it still be generating sufficient cash flow when this bond matures? The focus is on the downside and the finite time horizon of the debt claim. Credit analysts are less excited about upside potential and more concerned about the stability of the floor — the minimum cash flows that ensure debt service.

Collateral — Tangible vs. Intangible, Secured vs. Unsecured

Tangible Collateral (Hard Collateral) — Preferred by Secured Lenders
  • Identifiable physical assets that can be seized and sold in default
  • Property, plant & equipment (factories, buildings, power plants)
  • Inventory (can be liquidated)
  • Cash and marketable securities
  • Vessels, aircraft (Royal Caribbean example)
  • Higher recovery rates — easier to value and liquidate
  • Strongly preferred for secured debt pledges
Intangible Collateral (Soft Collateral) — Less Preferred
  • Non-physical assets — harder to seize and sell
  • Patents and intellectual property rights
  • Trademarks, brands, trade names
  • Goodwill (cannot be sold separately from the business)
  • Lower recovery in default — value may evaporate with the business
  • May be pledged (RCL pledged IP along with vessels) but less reliable than tangible

When does collateral matter most? Collateral is most relevant for companies with lower credit quality — when POD rises to a level where the secondary source of repayment (collateral) is genuinely needed. For high-quality investment-grade issuers, the primary repayment source (operating cash flow) is so strong that collateral is largely irrelevant. For high-yield or stressed issuers, the type and quality of collateral can be the difference between full recovery and near-total loss.

Secured debt advantages: Either (1) reduces the borrowing cost vs. unsecured alternatives, or (2) provides access to debt markets that would otherwise be unavailable at all. Royal Caribbean's pandemic borrowing illustrates the second use case — the company's POD had risen dramatically (COVID suspension of operations) but investors were willing to lend at a reasonable rate because the ships and IP provided strong collateral (lower LGD).

Example 2 — Royal Caribbean COVID-19 Secured Borrowing (May 2020)
SITUATION: Operations suspended March 2020 (COVID-19) → POD rises sharply

SOLUTION: $3.3 billion senior secured notes (due 2023/2025)
COLLATERAL: 28 of RCL's fleet of 60+ vessels + material intellectual property
PURPOSE: Repay $2.35B 364-day senior secured term loan + general corporate purposes

KEY INSIGHT: Despite greatly increased POD (operations stopped entirely),
investors were willing to lend BECAUSE of REDUCED LGD from secured collateral
→ High-quality tangible assets (cruise ships) provided strong recovery backstop

LESSON: Secured collateral can unlock debt market access even when POD is elevated
EL = POD × LGD — if LGD falls enough, EL stays manageable even as POD rises
03

Corporate Governance & Accounting Red Flags — The Character Factor

Corporate governance quality is a critical qualitative factor for debt investors. Unlike equity investors who can vote on management decisions, debtholders primarily protect themselves through covenants and deal structure at the time of issuance. Assessment of management's character and track record is therefore essential before lending.

Covenants — IG vs. HY

Investment-Grade Bonds — Lighter Covenant Package
  • Typically affirmative covenants only (must do)
  • Compliance with applicable laws and regulations
  • Maintaining current lines of business
  • Insuring and maintaining company assets
  • Paying taxes on time
  • General corporate purpose use of proceeds with few restrictions
  • IG issuers have reputation and credit quality to protect → fewer restrictions needed
High-Yield Bonds — Tighter Covenant Package
  • Affirmative AND negative covenants (must do + cannot do)
  • Restriction on dividend payments to shareholders
  • Restriction on additional debt incurrence
  • Financial maintenance covenants: minimum coverage ratios, maximum leverage ratios
  • Restrictions on asset sales, M&A, related-party transactions
  • Springing liens, change of control provisions
  • Greater protection needed due to higher POD and agency risk

Management Character Red Flags — What to Watch For

⚠️ Key Warning Signs in Corporate Credit Analysis

These signals suggest management may not act in debtholders' best interests or may be concealing problems:

Actions That Harm Debtholders
  • Overleveraged debt-financed acquisitions (increases leverage dramatically)
  • Large debt-financed special dividends to shareholders (cash leaves the firm)
  • Major debt-financed stock buyback programs (same effect)
  • Any financing strategy that prioritizes shareholders at cost of rising debt burden
  • These actions shift value from debtholders to equity holders
Aggressive Accounting Policies
  • Significant off-balance-sheet financing (hides true leverage)
  • Preferring capitalization over immediate expensing (inflates assets, delays expense recognition)
  • Early or premature revenue recognition (inflates current-period revenues)
  • Complex, opaque business structures with off-shore entities (Wirecard)
  • Revenue concentrated in few undisclosed partners (Wirecard: 3 partners = most profits)
Personnel & Auditor Red Flags
  • Frequent change of auditors — the #1 red flag
  • Frequent change of CFOs
  • Rapid international expansion through acquisitions (harder to audit)
  • Auditors unable to confirm existence of cash balances
  • Fraud or malfeasance allegations — any evidence requires heightened scrutiny
  • Whistleblower reports or short-seller attacks
Example 3 — Wirecard AG Accounting Fraud (Germany, 2020) — Classic Red Flags Case
COMPANY: German payments firm; joined DAX-30; EUR 27B peak market cap (2018)
OUTCOME: Filed for insolvency June 2020; unsecured bonds fell to 20 cents/EUR; CEO arrested
→ Largest accounting fraud and bankruptcy in Germany's post-WWII corporate history

RED FLAGS (all visible before collapse):
① Revenue concentration: 3 third-party partners = large share of reported revenue and most profits
② Opaque business model: rapid international expansion through acquisitions masked rising numbers
③ Off-shore entities: significant assets held through external offshore trust accounts
④ Self-dealing: bank subsidiary lent to fintech "partners" who generated revenue for Wirecard
⑤ Complex structure: revenue deposited in third-party trustee escrow (not Wirecard's accounts)

TRIGGER: Auditors could not confirm existence of EUR 1.9 billion in cash
(the bulk of Wirecard's reported profits — it didn't exist)

LESSON: Qualitative analysis of management strategy and accounting policy could have
identified all five red flags well in advance of the June 2020 collapse.

Quantitative Framework — Four Dimensions of Credit Analysis

① Profitability

Key question: Does the company generate enough operating income?

  • Focus on operating profits and recurring revenues — not one-time gains
  • EBIT and EBITDA margins are the primary metrics
  • Strong, stable earnings generate reliable cash flows for debt service
  • Macro factors (economic downturn) and market share loss threaten profitability
  • Cyclical companies: profitability varies — average through-the-cycle margins matter most
  • Higher EBIT margin → more profit available to pay interest → lower POD
② Coverage

Key question: Do income/cash flows cover debt service payments?

  • Compare periodic income or cash flows to interest and principal payments
  • Interest coverage ratio (EBIT / Interest): most commonly used
  • Also: EBITDA, EBITDAR (adds rental expense) in numerator
  • Lease payments often included in denominator (operating leases are debt-like)
  • Higher coverage → more buffer → lower POD
  • Coverage ratios can deteriorate quickly in downturns — watch trends
③ Leverage

Key question: How much debt relative to resources/earnings?

  • Compare total debt to assets, capital, or earnings/cash flow
  • Debt investors prefer LOWER leverage (more resources per unit of debt)
  • Equity investors often benefit from HIGHER leverage (financial leverage amplifies ROE)
  • Key ratio: Debt/EBITDA — used by rating agencies as rating trigger and in loan covenants
  • Also: Retained Cash Flow (RCF) / Net Debt — cash flow-based leverage
  • Higher Debt/EBITDA = higher leverage = higher credit risk
  • CAUTION: Debt/EBITDA volatile for cyclical companies with high operating leverage
④ Liquidity

Key question: Can the company meet near-term obligations even if CF is lumpy?

  • A solvent company (assets > liabilities) can still default if illiquid
  • Short-term resources: cash, marketable securities, current assets
  • Committed bank revolving credit facilities — if contractually drawable, count as liquidity
  • Most critical for companies with lumpy or seasonal cash flows
  • Liquidity analysis asks: "can they pay next quarter's interest?"
  • Current ratio, quick ratio — balance sheet liquidity measures
  • IG investors less focused on liquidity; HY investors: critical near-term concern
💡 IG vs. HY — Different Emphasis in Quantitative Analysis

Investment-grade investors are almost solely focused on POD — they assume recovery (LGD) will be adequate if default occurs. They monitor profitability, coverage, and leverage to assess whether the probability of default is creeping up.

High-yield investors must consider both POD and LGD because the probability of default is non-trivial. They analyze subordination structures and collateral quality to estimate likely recovery rates. For very distressed companies, pricing focuses on expected default timing and expected recovery — not YTM.

Approach (Exhibit 2): Top-down (macro → industry → company), bottom-up (balance sheet → income statement → cash flow), or hybrid. The hybrid combines macroeconomic cyclicality expectations with bottom-up issuer financial forecasting.

Key Financial Ratios in Credit Analysis — Exhibit 3 (Full)

EBIT Margin — Profitability Ratio
EBIT Margin = EBIT (Operating Income) / Revenue

EBIT = Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (= operating income)
Why EBIT? Interest is paid before taxes → EBIT shows profit before capital structure costs

EBITDA Margin = EBITDA / Revenue
EBITDA = EBIT + Depreciation + Amortization
EBITDA adds back non-cash charges → closer to cash operating profit

Higher EBIT/EBITDA margin → More profit available to service debt → Lower POD
Focus on RECURRING operating profit, not one-time gains
Why not net income margin? Net income is after interest expense and taxes — but interest is the very payment we're assessing credit risk for. EBIT removes the capital structure effect, allowing apples-to-apples comparison across companies with different debt levels.

EBITDA vs. EBIT: EBITDA is a proxy for cash operating earnings. Used when D&A is large relative to earnings (capital-intensive industries). But EBITDA ignores capex (capital expenditures) — a company with high capex needs may have good EBITDA but poor free cash flow.
EBIT to Interest Expense — Coverage Ratio (KEY EXAM RATIO)
Interest Coverage Ratio = EBIT / Interest Expense

Also expressed as: EBITDA / Interest Expense (more generous, adds back D&A)
Or: EBITDAR / (Interest + Lease Payments) (adds rental expense for lease-heavy industries)

Higher ratio = More operating profit per unit of interest = LESS credit risk
Typical IG threshold: 4× or higher
Typical HY range: 1.5× to 4×
Below 1×: Operating profit less than interest expense → cash flow insufficient → severe distress

KEY: Watch for TRENDS — declining coverage over time signals credit deterioration
even if coverage is still adequate today.
Denominator variations: Plain interest expense is most common. Sometimes reduced by interest EARNED on cash/securities (net interest). Sometimes increased to include operating lease payments (relevant for retailers with large lease obligations). EBITDAR/Fixed Charges = best coverage ratio for lease-heavy industries (retail, airlines).

Direction to remember: Coverage ↑ = credit quality ↑. Coverage ↓ = credit quality ↓. This is opposite from leverage ratios.
Debt to EBITDA — Leverage Ratio (KEY EXAM RATIO)
Debt/EBITDA = Total Debt / EBITDA

Total Debt may be adjusted for: operating lease obligations, off-balance-sheet commitments
EBITDA = EBIT + D&A

Higher Debt/EBITDA → More debt relative to earnings → HIGHER credit risk
Investment-grade typical: <3× to 4×
High-yield typical: 4× to 8×+
CCC/near-distress: 7×+

CRITICAL: Debt/EBITDA is highly VOLATILE for:
(1) Cyclical companies — EBITDA swings dramatically through cycle
(2) High operating leverage companies (fixed costs) — small revenue drops → large EBITDA drops
Both cause ratio to spike in downturns even without additional debt
Rating agency use: Debt/EBITDA is commonly referenced in Moody's and S&P methodologies as a trigger for rating actions (e.g., "a sustained Debt/EBITDA above X× could lead to a downgrade"). Banks use it in loan covenants (maintenance covenants: "Debt/EBITDA must not exceed X×").

The volatility warning is critical: A cyclical company might show Debt/EBITDA of 3× at the peak and 8× in a downturn, without any new debt. Credit analysts must adjust for the cycle — use normalized or through-the-cycle EBITDA for cyclical industries.
Retained Cash Flow (RCF) to Net Debt — Cash Flow Leverage Ratio
RCF to Net Debt = Retained Cash Flow / (Debt − Cash and Marketable Securities)

RCF = Net cash flow from operating activities − Dividends paid
(= the cash the company retains after paying dividends, available to repay debt)

Net Debt = Total Debt − Cash and Marketable Securities
(= the amount of debt that is not covered by available liquid assets)

Higher RCF/Net Debt → Lower leverage (more cash flow relative to net debt) → Better credit

Advantages over Debt/EBITDA:
(1) Uses cash flow (not accounting earnings) → harder to manipulate
(2) Nets out available cash → reflects true debt burden
(3) More conservative — dividends are subtracted before computing numerator

Ratio Direction Quick Reference — Always Know Which Way is Better

RatioFormulaHigher = ?Analogy
EBIT Margin EBIT / Revenue Better (more profit per revenue dollar) Profitability margin
EBITDA Margin EBITDA / Revenue Better Cash profitability margin
EBIT / Interest EBIT / Interest Expense Better (more coverage) Interest coverage ratio
Debt / EBITDA Total Debt / EBITDA Worse (more leverage) Debt-to-earnings leverage
RCF / Net Debt Retained Cash Flow / (Debt − Cash) Better (more cash flow per net debt) Cash flow leverage (inverse)

Cash Flow Measures Used in Credit Analysis — FCF, FFO, RCF

Credit analysis focuses on cash flows from operating activities, not asset disposals or financing activities (which are one-off and unsustainable). Three cash flow measures are commonly used, all more conservative than EBITDA because they make adjustments for cash used in the business or distributed to shareholders.

Three Credit Cash Flow Measures — Definitions
FREE CASH FLOW (FCF):
FCF = Net Income − Working Capital Increases − Capital Expenditures (Fixed Assets) − Net Interest Paid
Most conservative — captures all cash used in running and growing the business
Debt repayment capacity after investing in the business

FUNDS FROM OPERATIONS (FFO):
FFO = Net Income from Continuing Operations + Depreciation & Amortization
+ Deferred Income Tax + Other Non-Cash Items
Adjusts net income for non-cash charges to get closer to operating cash generation
Used heavily by Moody's and S&P in sovereign and corporate credit analysis

RETAINED CASH FLOW (RCF):
RCF = Net Cash Flow from Operating Activities − Dividends Paid
The operating cash flow that the company keeps after shareholder distributions
Used in the denominator: RCF / Net Debt leverage ratio

All three are NON-IFRS — no official GAAP/IFRS definition; vary by analyst/agency
Why use these instead of EBITDA? EBITDA ignores changes in working capital, capital expenditure requirements, interest payments, and shareholder dividends. A company with strong EBITDA but heavy capex needs (like a semiconductor company) may have very little actual free cash flow available for debt repayment. These three measures capture more of the real cash available to service debt.

Also: Debt and interest expense may be adjusted for operating leases (these are contractual payment obligations that function like debt but may not appear on the balance sheet under some accounting standards). Credit analysts add back operating lease obligations to debt and add operating lease payments to interest expense to capture the full fixed payment burden.

Seniority Rankings & Priority of Claims — Exhibit 4

Seniority Ranking
The priority of payment in the event of default or bankruptcy. The most senior (highest-ranking) debt has the first claim on the cash flows and assets of the issuer. Seniority directly determines the recovery rate (RR) and thus the LGD for a specific debt issue. Higher seniority = higher recovery = lower LGD = lower Expected Loss (for a given POD).
1st Lien / Mortgage
HIGHEST. Pledge of specific property (first mortgage) or specific assets including PP&E, licenses, patents, brands, inventory (first lien). First to be paid from collateral proceeds. Most secured lenders.
Lowest LGD (~27% historical)
Senior Secured
Secured claim on pledged assets. Ranks below 1st lien but above junior secured. RCL's 2020 bonds fall here (vessels + IP as collateral). Most second-lien loans are senior secured obligations.
Low LGD (~39% historical)
Junior Secured
Secured interest in pledged assets that ranks BELOW first lien in both collateral protection and priority of payment. Still higher than unsecured claims on collateral, but lower than senior secured.
Moderate LGD
Senior Unsecured
Most common corporate bond type. Only a general claim on assets and cash flows — no specific collateral pledge. BRWA's bonds are senior unsecured. Ranks below all secured debt but above subordinated. Standard "issuer rating" level.
~53% LGD historical
Senior Subordinated
Ranks below senior unsecured. Has priority over subordinated and junior subordinated, but gets paid after all senior claims are satisfied. Step-down in recovery vs. senior unsecured.
Higher LGD
Subordinated
General unsecured claim but contractually subordinated. Often little or no recovery in default. Issued by issuers needing cheap non-dilutive capital — costs less than equity (tax-deductible interest) but is non-dilutive. VIVU's 10% notes are here.
Very High LGD (~72% historical)
Junior Subordinated
LOWEST priority among debt claims. Almost always zero recovery in default. Sometimes called "mezzanine" debt in leveraged finance. Essentially equity-like risk with debt-like coupon.
Near 100% LGD
Equity (Common/Preferred)
LOWEST of all. Paid only after ALL debt claims satisfied in full. In most corporate bankruptcies, equity recovers nothing. Common shareholders typically wiped out; preferred shareholders may recover a small fraction.
100% LGD (zero recovery)

Priority of Claims — Key Rules

  • Same seniority = pari passu = same pro-rata claim. A senior unsecured bondholder with 30-year maturity has the exact same pro-rata claim as one with 6-month maturity — maturity doesn't affect seniority. All senior unsecured creditors share equally from the remaining pool after secured creditors are paid.
  • If secured collateral is insufficient: The deficiency (value of claim minus collateral proceeds) becomes a senior unsecured claim — NOT a secured claim. For example, if secured debt is $100M but collateral only generates $60M, the remaining $40M becomes an unsecured claim ranked pari passu with other senior unsecured creditors.
  • Practical reality: Lower-seniority creditors and even shareholders may receive some consideration without more senior creditors being paid in full. Why? Bankruptcy takes time, legal fees are substantial, company value declines (employees leave, customers churn), and faster resolution is valuable. Higher seniority holders may accept compromise to accelerate exit from bankruptcy.
  • Country variation: US bankruptcy law (Chapter 11) biases toward reorganization and company survival. Many other countries lean toward liquidation and maximizing recovery for senior bank lenders. Priority of claims varies by jurisdiction.

Issuer vs. Issue Ratings — Notching Explained

Notching
The rating agency practice of adjusting an individual debt issue's rating up or down from the issuer-level rating to reflect differences in LGD due to seniority, subordination, and collateral. The POD is typically the same for all issues of the same issuer (cross-default provisions mean all debt defaults simultaneously), but LGD differences by seniority create issue-specific ratings.
Issuer Rating (Corporate Family Rating / CCR)
  • Addresses the overall creditworthiness of the issuer
  • Typically mapped to the senior unsecured debt level for consistency
  • Also called: Corporate Family Rating (CFR, Moody's), Corporate Credit Rating (CCR, S&P), Issuer Default Rating (IDR, Fitch)
  • Reflects the issuer's overall POD (and EL for Moody's)
  • Starting point for notching individual issues
Issue Rating (Individual Debt Issue)
  • Reflects specific obligations — takes seniority into account
  • POD is typically the same as issuer (cross-default provisions)
  • Differs from issuer rating due to different LGD by seniority
  • Secured debt: rated ABOVE issuer rating (lower LGD → better issue rating)
  • Subordinated debt: rated BELOW issuer rating (higher LGD → worse issue rating)
  • Amount of notching depends on credit quality and degree of subordination
Notching Direction and Magnitude
Secured debt issue: Issuer rating + notches UP (lower LGD → better recovery)
Senior unsecured issue: Same as issuer rating (baseline)
Subordinated issue: Issuer rating − notches DOWN (higher LGD → worse recovery)

NOTCHING SIZE (general principle):
Higher-rated issuers (IG): Smaller notching adjustments (lower base POD → LGD differences matter less)
Lower-rated issuers (HY): Larger notching adjustments (higher base POD → LGD differences matter more)

ROYAL CARIBBEAN EXAMPLE (from Example 6):
Issuer CFR (Moody's): B1
Senior unsecured notes (2021): B2 (one notch BELOW issuer — less collateral protection)
Senior secured notes (2020): Ba2 (THREE notches ABOVE issuer — strong vessel/IP collateral)

Ba2 to B2 = FOUR notch spread between secured and unsecured → large notching for HY issuer
Cross-default provisions: Most bond indentures contain cross-default clauses — if one issue defaults, all issues are deemed to be in default simultaneously. This means all issues of the same issuer have the same POD. The only reason for different ratings is different LGD (from seniority).

Structural subordination: When a holding company has debt outstanding and its operating subsidiaries also have debt, the subsidiary debt has priority over the holding company debt — operating cash flows service the subsidiary debt first, and only what's left over gets "upstreamed" to the parent. Parent holding company debt is therefore structurally subordinated to subsidiary debt, even if it's technically senior unsecured at the parent level.

How Rating Agencies Approach EL vs. POD

Moody's — EL Approach

Defines credit risk as the risk an entity may not meet contractual obligations AND the estimated financial loss in the event of default. Considers BOTH POD AND LGD (recovery) in its ratings. Rating scale reflects Expected Loss (EL = POD × LGD). Particularly relevant for speculative-grade debt where recovery differences are meaningful. Notching incorporates both default probability and recovery expectations.

S&P — POD Approach

Ratings primarily reflect POD. Publishes SEPARATE recovery ratings (RR1–RR6) for individual issues in addition to issue credit ratings. Recovery ratings explicitly capture loss severity (seniority and collateral). S&P may also incorporate recovery into notching adjustments for issue ratings. POD is the primary rating driver; recovery is a supplemental analytical tool.

Fitch — POD Approach (like S&P)

Assigns Issuer Default Ratings (IDRs) reflecting a POD view — similar to S&P's approach. Notching adjustments then apply to issue ratings by considering recovery expectations for specific issues. Fitch recovery ratings scale their notching of individual issue ratings up or down from the IDR based on expected recovery outcome (superior, average, or below-average recovery).

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Corporate credit analysis framework: Assesses POD (probability of default) and LGD (loss given default). Both qualitative (business model, industry, governance) and quantitative (financial ratios) factors used.
  • ✅ Credit analyst vs. equity analyst: Credit analyst focuses on downside — whether cash flows are sufficient and timely to meet fixed obligations. Equity analyst values all future cash flows for upside. Credit focus: finite maturity of debt claims.
  • ✅ Qualitative factors (Exhibit 1): (1) Business model — demand/revenue/margin stability, asset quality; (2) Industry and competition — structure, competitive forces, long-term growth; (3) Business risk — deviations from expected performance; (4) Corporate governance — appropriate use of proceeds, covenant compliance, management character.
  • ✅ Tangible vs. intangible collateral: Tangible (PP&E, inventory, cash, vessels) preferred by secured lenders — easier to seize and sell. Intangible (patents, IP, goodwill) is less reliable as collateral. Both may be pledged but recovery from tangibles is typically higher.
  • ✅ IG covenants: Primarily affirmative covenants (comply with laws, maintain business, insure assets, pay taxes). Few restrictions — issuer quality serves as protection.
  • ✅ HY covenants: Both affirmative AND negative covenants. Dividend restrictions, additional debt restrictions, financial maintenance covenants (max leverage, min coverage). Stronger protection because higher POD creates greater agency risk.
  • ✅ Management red flags: Debt-financed acquisitions that spike leverage; large debt-financed special dividends or buybacks; off-balance-sheet financing; capitalizing vs. expensing; premature revenue recognition; frequent change of auditors or CFOs (most important red flag).
  • ✅ Four quantitative dimensions: Profitability (stable earnings drive cash flows), Coverage (income/CF vs. debt service), Leverage (debt vs. resources/earnings), Liquidity (short-term resources to meet near-term obligations).
  • ✅ EBIT Margin = EBIT / Revenue. Higher = better (more profit per revenue dollar to service debt). Focus on recurring operating profit, not one-time items.
  • ✅ EBIT / Interest Expense (coverage ratio). Higher = better (more coverage). Below 1× = operating profit insufficient to cover interest — severe distress signal.
  • ✅ Debt / EBITDA (leverage ratio). Higher = worse (more leverage = higher credit risk). Highly volatile for cyclical companies — normalize EBITDA through the cycle. Used by rating agencies as rating trigger; in loan covenants as maintenance test.
  • ✅ RCF / Net Debt. RCF = operating cash flow − dividends. Net Debt = Debt − Cash. Higher ratio = lower leverage = better credit. Uses cash flows (harder to manipulate than earnings).
  • ✅ Ratio directions: Coverage ↑ = better. Leverage ↑ (Debt/EBITDA) = worse. Profitability ↑ = better. RCF/Net Debt ↑ = better.
  • ✅ Cash flow measures: FCF = net income − WC increases − capex − net interest. FFO = net income + D&A + deferred taxes + non-cash items. RCF = operating CF − dividends. All three more conservative than EBITDA. Non-IFRS definitions vary.
  • ✅ Seniority ranking (Exhibit 4), highest to lowest: First Lien/Mortgage → Senior Secured → Junior Secured → Senior Unsecured → Senior Subordinated → Subordinated → Junior Subordinated → Equity.
  • ✅ Senior unsecured = most common corporate bond type. General claim on issuer's assets; no specific collateral pledge. Issuer rating is typically mapped to this level.
  • ✅ Priority of claims — pari passu within seniority class. A 30-year senior unsecured bond has the same pro-rata claim in bankruptcy as a 6-month senior unsecured bond. Maturity doesn't affect priority.
  • ✅ If collateral insufficient: Deficiency balance becomes a senior unsecured claim. Secured creditors don't automatically get more recovery just from having a pledge — if the collateral falls short, they join the unsecured pool for the balance.
  • ✅ Historical recovery rates (Exhibit 5): Term loans ~72.6% (long-run avg). Senior secured bonds ~61.4%. Senior unsecured bonds ~46.9%. Subordinated bonds ~27.9%. Note: 2020 COVID crisis showed dramatically lower recoveries (senior unsecured: 8.6%).
  • ✅ Recovery rates vary by: (1) Industry — secular decline industries recover less. (2) Cycle — strong economy = higher recovery; crisis = lower recovery. (3) Capital structure — more secured debt means less left for unsecured creditors.
  • ✅ Notching: Issuer rating typically mapped to senior unsecured level. Secured issues: rated ABOVE issuer (lower LGD → lower notches). Subordinated issues: rated BELOW issuer (higher LGD → negative notches). Larger notching for lower-rated (HY) issuers because higher POD means LGD differences matter more.
  • ✅ Moody's = EL approach (POD × LGD both matter). S&P = POD approach (separate recovery ratings for LGD). Fitch = POD approach (IDR + notching for recovery).
  • ✅ Structural subordination: Parent holding company debt is subordinated to operating subsidiary debt because operating CF services subsidiary debt first before being upstreamed to the parent.
  • ✅ RCL case: Pandemic → POD rises → secured notes (Ba2) unlocked debt market access. Post-pandemic unsecured notes: B2 (one notch below B1 CFR). Secured vs. unsecured spread: 4 rating notches (Ba2 vs. B2) — large because HY issuer where LGD differences matter more.

═══ FOUR QUANTITATIVE DIMENSIONS ═══
Profitability: Strong, stable recurring earnings → reliable CF for debt service
Coverage: Periodic income/CF vs. interest/principal → higher = better
Leverage: Total debt vs. assets/earnings/CF → lower = better
Liquidity: Short-term resources to meet near-term obligations

═══ KEY CREDIT RATIOS (EXHIBIT 3) ═══
EBIT Margin = EBIT / Revenue [higher = better: profitability]
EBIT/Interest = EBIT / Interest Expense [higher = better: coverage]
Debt/EBITDA = Total Debt / EBITDA [higher = worse: leverage]
RCF/Net Debt = Retained CF / (Debt − Cash) [higher = better: CF leverage]

═══ SENIORITY LADDER (HIGH → LOW PRIORITY) ═══
1st Lien/Mortgage → Senior Secured → Junior Secured
Senior Unsecured (most common bond) → Sr. Subordinated
Subordinated → Junior Subordinated → Equity

═══ RECOVERY RATES (LONG-RUN AVERAGE) ═══
Term Loans: 72.6% | Senior Secured: 61.4%
Senior Unsecured: 46.9% | Subordinated: 27.9%
Cyclical: 2020 senior unsecured fell to 8.6% (COVID crisis)

═══ NOTCHING ═══
Issuer rating → mapped to senior unsecured level
Secured issues: notched UP (lower LGD)
Subordinated: notched DOWN (higher LGD)
IG issuers: smaller notching | HY issuers: larger notching

═══ RATING AGENCY APPROACHES ═══
Moody's: EL = POD × LGD (both matter in ratings)
S&P: POD primary + separate recovery ratings for LGD
Fitch: IDR (POD) + notching for recovery

═══ GOVERNANCE RED FLAGS ═══
Frequent auditor/CFO changes (most important!)
Off-balance-sheet financing | Premature revenue recognition
Debt-financed acquisitions/buybacks that spike leverage
Complex offshore structures (Wirecard pattern)
Debt/EBITDA warning: This ratio is commonly used by rating agencies and banks but is highly volatile for cyclical companies with high operating leverage. When analyzing cyclical sectors (auto, commodities, construction), always normalize EBITDA through the business cycle — don't evaluate leverage at the peak when EBITDA is inflated.

Notching logic: Cross-default provisions mean ALL issues of the same issuer have the same POD. The only reason for different issue ratings is different LGD from seniority. For IG issuers (low POD), even if LGD differs significantly between secured and unsecured, the EL difference is small. For HY issuers (high POD), the same LGD difference creates a large EL difference — hence larger notching. This is why the RCL example shows a 4-notch spread between secured and unsecured at the B1 CFR level.
