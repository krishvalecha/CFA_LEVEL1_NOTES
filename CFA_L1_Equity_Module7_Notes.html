What to Forecast — The Four Forecast Objects

Forecast Object
The specific item or quantity an analyst chooses to project. An analyst's choice of forecast object depends on: available information, efficiency, accuracy, explanatory value, and verifiability. Always prefer forecast objects that are regularly disclosed or directly calculable from regular disclosures — so forecasts can be confirmed in a timely manner.
Object 1 — Drivers of Financial Statement Lines

Rather than forecasting a line item directly, forecast its underlying drivers separately and multiply them.

  • Example: Net sales = (average number of stores open) × (average net sales per store)
  • Example: COGS = gross margin % × net sales forecast
  • Benefit: Improved explanatory value and potentially better accuracy — a line item can have multiple drivers moving in different directions that are difficult to forecast on an aggregated basis
Object 2 — Individual Financial Statement Lines

Directly forecast individual income statement, balance sheet, or cash flow items.

  • Used for lines without clear drivers, less-material items, or items the analyst has no strong opinion on
  • Analyst may use a management estimate or simply assume the quantity remains the same
  • Examples: amortization expense (often guided by management), "other non-current assets" when small, certain cash flow statement lines with minimal disclosures
Object 3 — Summary Measures

High-level aggregated metrics: free cash flow, EPS, total assets, EBITDA margins.

  • Benefit: Efficiency — fewer individual forecasts required
  • Disadvantage: Less transparency; harder to audit the forecast
  • Most appropriate when the summary measure is stable and predictable, or when issuer disclosures are severely limited
Object 4 — Ad Hoc Objects

Items not yet reported in historical financial statements but needed for an investment decision.

  • Used when an analyst needs to forecast a loss/gain before the issuer recognizes an accrual
  • Examples: material legal proceedings, government regulatory actions, tax disputes, natural disasters
  • Forecasted based on judgment and external information (legal opinions, industry precedents, etc.)
⚠️ Avoid Overly Complex Models

More forecasts ≠ better accuracy. Complex models take longer to build and update with no improvement in forecast quality. Use judgment to focus on the most important drivers; aggregate and simplify where incremental value is low. Example: Novartis has 35+ drugs in 2 regions — forecasting each drug by illness and geography would take months and the forecasts can't be verified from company disclosures anyway.

💡 The Verifiability Principle

Only use forecast objects that are regularly disclosed (quarterly or annually). If a quantity is disclosed only annually, you can verify the forecast only once a year. If disclosed only at consolidated level, you can't verify segment-level forecasts. Information from third-party consultancy reports is fine to inform a forecast but problematic as a direct forecast object — can't be confirmed in a timely manner.

The Four Forecast Approaches

💡 Mnemonic — "HHMAD"

The four approaches: Historical Results · Historical Base Rates & Convergence · Management Guidance · Analyst's Discretionary Forecast. In practice, they are often combined. Use multiple approaches to cross-check assumptions.

Approach 1 — Historical Results (Assume Past is Precedent)

Use past observed or calculated values (e.g., historical median) as the forecast. Easiest approach — the default.

When appropriate:

  • Companies in industries where industry structure (Porter, PESTLE) is not expected to change
  • Companies with low sensitivity to the business cycle
  • Forecast objects that are not material or that the analyst has no opinion on

When NOT appropriate:

  • Cyclical industries — future period likely at a different point in the cycle than past; an "over-cycle" average disguises year-to-year volatility
  • Companies changing their competitive strategy or restructuring (large acquisition/divestiture makes historical results non-comparable)
Approach 2 — Historical Base Rates and Convergence

Use an industry or peer group average/median (computed over a long period) as the "base rate" that the company's metric will converge to over some time frame. The base rate can also be a macroeconomic variable (e.g., GDP growth). Requires analyst discretion in selecting the object, the sample for the base rate, and the convergence time frame.

When appropriate:

  • Well-established industries with many publicly traded peers: banks, airlines, restaurants, automakers, retailers
  • When analyst's industry analysis does NOT call for material structural changes
  • Smaller companies "maturing into" a financial profile similar to larger peers (e.g., biotech company growing into industry R&D ratios)

When NOT appropriate:

  • New or changing industries where calculating a base rate is difficult or irrelevant
  • Highly cyclical industries where smooth convergence would obscure year-to-year volatility
  • Industry leaders that dominate the peer group — the base rate is mostly their own historical results (e.g., using "digital advertising industry" averages to forecast Alphabet/Meta would just be forecasting Alphabet/Meta using their own history, missing any economies-of-scale benefit)
Approach 3 — Management Guidance

Public companies may provide targets for revenues, earnings, capex, and other measures for the next quarter, year, or longer term. Guidance is often provided as a range and embeds management's assumptions on macro growth, cost inflation, market share, pricing, and exchange rates.

  • Guidance accounts for a majority of quarterly financial reporting information used by investors (Beyer et al. 2010) — due to its forward-looking nature and management's informational advantage at the firm level
  • Analysts should scrutinize the plausibility of the assumptions embedded in guidance
  • Ciconte et al. (2013): the upper bound of a guidance range often better represents management's true expectations — the lower bound is "padded" to create an easily cleared hurdle for which management is rewarded
  • Management may suspend guidance in periods of high uncertainty (e.g., COVID-19, recessions)

When NOT appropriate:

  • Companies highly sensitive to the business cycle — management has no informational advantage over investors at forecasting macro variables like GDP or commodity prices (Hutton et al. 2012: investors have the macro advantage; management has the firm-level advantage)
  • Management with a poor track record of accurate estimates (always analyze past guidance vs. actuals)
Approach 4 — Analyst's Discretionary Forecast

All other approaches: surveys, quantitative models, probability distributions, analogies to historical precedents, and other unobservable inputs. Requires significant judgment. Most demanding but most flexible.

When appropriate:

  • Companies in cyclical industries
  • Companies with no or few comparables
  • Companies that do not provide guidance
  • Companies undergoing a fundamental change (shift in competitive or regulatory environment)
  • Novel situations with no historical observations (e.g., energy transition to renewables and EVs — unprecedented; no direct comparables)

Choosing an Approach — Decision Factors

Factor Implication for Approach
Industry structure stable? Yes → Historical Results or Base Rates; No → Discretionary
Cyclical industry? Avoid plain historical results; use "over-cycle" average or discretionary
Company changing strategy? Avoid historical results (non-comparable); use discretionary
Mature industry with many peers? Historical base rates & convergence appropriate
Management provides reliable guidance? Use management guidance; always check track record first
Company is industry leader dominating the peer group? Avoid base rates (base rate = own history); use discretionary
Object not material / no strong opinion? Historical results or just hold constant

Selecting the Forecast Horizon

The forecast time horizon is determined by four factors:

  1. Investment strategy: The strategy's described investment time frame and average holding period. Long-term fund managers → focus on 3–5 year forecasts. Short-term managers → focus on next 1–2 quarters.
  2. Cyclicality of the industry: Forecast period should be long enough to allow the business to reach an expected mid-cycle level of sales and profitability.
  3. Company-specific factors: Recent acquisition or restructuring activity → extend horizon to allow expected benefits to be reflected in financial statements.
  4. Analyst's employer's preferences: Some employers specify fixed parameters — in that case, there is no individual analyst choice.

Forecasting Revenues — Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up

Top-Down Revenue Forecast Objects

1. Growth Relative to GDP Growth

Analyst forecasts nominal GDP growth rate, then determines how the company's growth compares — expressed as a bps premium or discount, or as a relative percentage.

Formula: If GDP forecast = 4% and company is expected to grow 50% faster → revenue growth = 4% × (1 + 0.50) = 6.0% (or +200 bps above GDP)

Alternatively: use real GDP forecast for volumes + inflation forecast for prices.

2. Market Growth and Market Share

Analyst forecasts growth of the company's product market, then forecasts the company's market share within it.

Formula: Revenue = Market size × Market share %

Example: Company has 8% share of CNY144B market → revenues = CNY11.5B. If market grows to CNY154B and share stays at 8% → revenues = CNY12.3B.

If market revenue has a predictable relationship with GDP → use regression analysis to estimate it.

Bottom-Up Revenue Forecast Objects

Bottom-up drivers are forecast individually then aggregated to total revenue. Using both top-down and bottom-up approaches simultaneously is valuable — cross-checking uncovers implicit assumptions or errors in either approach alone.

  • Volumes and average selling prices: Forecast volumes and prices separately → multiply. Used when companies disclose these (media/internet: users × revenue per user; airlines; asset managers: AUM × management fee rate; commodity producers).
  • Product-line or segment revenues: Forecast individual products, business lines, geographic areas, or reporting segments → aggregate. Used when company makes such disclosures and when objects have different economic exposures.
  • Capacity-based measures: Retail: number of stores × sales per store; same-store sales growth (stores open ≥12 months) + new-store contribution.
  • Return- or yield-based measures: Forecast based on account balances and yield on them. Example: bank net interest income = (loans × avg. lending rate) − (deposits × avg. funding rate).

Non-Recurring Items — Must Be Separated

⚠️ Exam Trap — Non-Recurring Revenue Items

Non-recurring items must be excluded from the recurring revenue forecast object and considered separately. They don't have the same drivers as recurring revenue and will distort it. Two types:

Type 1 — Disclosed by Management
  • Effects of FX rate changes
  • Extra selling days/weeks in the period
  • Acquisitions and divestitures
  • Other "one-time" revenues or gains

Separate these to identify "underlying" organic revenue. Straightforward unless the issuer's financial reporting credibility is in doubt — in which case "non-recurring" items may actually be recurring.

Type 2 — Require Analyst Judgment
  • Not quantified by management
  • Example: COVID-19 e-commerce boost — believed to be "new normal" but reversed by H2 2021
  • Example: GPU sales from crypto mining — companies couldn't/wouldn't quantify exact amount; analyst must judge as potentially non-recurring

Analyst must identify and estimate the non-recurring component using judgment.

Four Risk Factors to Incorporate in Revenue Forecasts

Competition

Market share pressure from existing competitors and new entrants

Business Cycle

Cyclical companies: revenue highly sensitive to GDP swings

Inflation / Deflation

Affects pricing power and real volume demand

Technological Change

Disruptive technologies can cannibalize existing demand

Forecasting Operating Expenses

Issuer disclosures about operating costs are typically less detailed than revenue disclosures. Analysts are often forced to use more aggregated forecast objects: consolidated financial statement lines (cost of sales, SG&A) or summary measures (EBITDA margins) on a consolidated or segment basis.

Key coherence principle: Cost forecasts must be coherent with revenue forecasts. If a relatively low-margin product/segment/geography is expected to grow faster than the rest, some margin deterioration should be forecasted — even without knowing precise segment margins.

Cost of Sales (COGS) / Gross Margin

Best approach: Forecast as a percentage of sales (gross margin %) — because COGS has a direct link with sales.

  • Losing market share to lower-priced substitutes → gross margins likely decline
  • Gaining share from differentiated products + cost advantages → gross margins likely rise
  • COGS is typically the single largest cost — even 1 basis point change in gross margin can have a material impact on operating profit and free cash flow
  • For companies with fluctuating input costs: separate analysis into volume and price components (especially for low-margin businesses — e.g., airline exposure to unhedged jet fuel costs)
Important: The Gross Margin Dilution Effect When Costs Are Passed On
Scenario: COGS/Sales = 25% initially. Input costs double. Company passes 100% of increase to customers via 25% price increase.

Period 1: Sales = 100, COGS = 25, Gross Profit = 75 → Gross Margin = 75%
Period 2: Sales = 125, COGS = 50, Gross Profit = 75 → Gross Margin = 60%
KEY INSIGHT: Even if the absolute gross profit is UNCHANGED ($75 both periods), the gross margin FALLS (75% → 60%) because an equal absolute dollar amount was added to both numerator and denominator of the COGS/Sales ratio.
This is a classic exam trap: don't assume "passing on cost increases = stable margins."
💡 Hedging Strategy Matters for Gross Margin Forecasts

Incorporate a company's hedging strategy into the cost forecast. Example: brewers often hedge barley costs one year in advance. A company expecting higher input costs may implement gradual price increases to avoid a sudden price spike and volume loss. Hedging details are disclosed in the notes to financial statements (though specific positions usually are not).

⚠️ Gross Margin Comparability Between Companies

Competitors' gross margins can be a useful cross-check, but margins may NOT be comparable due to business model differences. Example: a retailer that owns and operates its own stores vs. a wholesaler selling to franchisee retailers — the wholesaler will have a much lower gross margin but also much lower operating costs. Comparing their gross margins directly would be misleading.

SG&A Expenses

SG&A has a less direct relationship with revenues than COGS. Two components have different behaviors:

  • Selling and distribution expenses: Large variable component → model as a percentage of sales
  • General corporate costs: More fixed → model using a fixed growth rate based on expected wage inflation

Segment disclosures often include operating/EBITDA margins by segment but generally do NOT include COGS or SG&A by segment. For segment-based models, use summary measures (EBITDA margins) at the segment level.

Forecasting Working Capital

Working Capital Forecasting Method
Working capital forecasts use efficiency ratios as the forecast object, combined with revenue and cost forecasts to project accounts receivable, inventories, accounts payable, and other current assets/liabilities. Historical results approach is most common, but all four approaches can be used.
Working Capital Efficiency Ratios — Key Forecast Objects
DSO (Days Sales Outstanding) = Accounts Receivable / (Revenue / 365)
→ Accounts Receivable = DSO × (Revenue / 365)

DOH (Days of Inventory on Hand) = Inventory / (COGS / 365)
→ Inventory = DOH × (COGS / 365)

DPO (Days Payable Outstanding) = Accounts Payable / (COGS / 365)
→ Accounts Payable = DPO × (COGS / 365)

Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) = DSO + DOH − DPO
Forecast the ratio (DSO, DOH, DPO) using the selected approach, then multiply by the revenue/COGS forecast to get the balance sheet item.
Lower DSO = collecting faster (good). Lower DOH = leaner inventory (good). Higher DPO = paying suppliers slower (good for cash).
💡 Working Capital Approach Selection

A company with structurally better working capital ratios than peers (due to its business model) should NOT use the peer base rate approach — those better ratios are a feature of its model, not something to converge away from. Example: warehouse clubs typically have very low DSO (near zero — customers pay cash), low DOH, and competitive DPO. Industry average would be irrelevant unless the company changes its business model (e.g., launches e-commerce with credit terms).

Forecasting Capital Investments & Capital Structure

Capital Expenditures (CapEx)

Maintenance CapEx
  • Sustains the current business (replaces worn-out assets)
  • Forecast often based on historical depreciation & amortization expenses + small upward adjustment for capital goods inflation
  • For businesses with low fixed asset turnover → maintenance CapEx requirements can be very high
  • D&A approximation: gross fixed assets ÷ D&A expense = assumed useful life
Growth CapEx
  • Expands the business (new stores, new capacity, etc.)
  • More discretionary — tied to management's expansion plans and revenue growth
  • Linked to new-store openings, new geographies, new product launches, etc.
  • Often guided by management's capex outlook

Balance sheet linkage: Net PP&E and intangible assets on the balance sheet:

  • Increase due to capital expenditures
  • Decrease due to depreciation and amortization (D&A) expenses
  • D&A forecast = based on net PP&E and intangibles + assumed useful lives (from management's accounting policies)

Capital Structure

Leverage ratios are the primary forecast objects for projecting future debt and equity levels. Common ratios:

  • Debt to capital
  • Debt to equity
  • Debt to EBITDA (most common — also often a debt covenant ratio)
  • Net debt to EBITDA (debt − cash) / EBITDA

Three inputs to consider when projecting capital structure:

  1. Historical company practice — what has the company's leverage looked like over time?
  2. Management's financial strategy — target capital structure, target credit rating, debt covenants
  3. Capital expenditure requirements — implied financing needs from the capex forecast

Future gross debt = Forecast Debt/EBITDA ratio × Forecast EBITDA

💡 CapEx and Leverage — Coherence Check

Capital structure forecasts should be coherent with capex forecasts. A company expanding rapidly (high growth CapEx) may need to increase leverage or issue equity to fund the investment. A mature company slowing store openings (declining growth CapEx) might instead return capital to shareholders via buybacks and dividends — and increase leverage (following peer norms), as mature retailers often do.

Scenario Analysis

Scenario Analysis
Rather than a single point estimate, analysts develop multiple forecast scenarios based on different outcomes for key risk factors, and judge the likelihood of each scenario. Used when there is a wide range of potential outcomes (e.g., a binary event like clinical trial results, regulatory approval, or technological adoption rate).

Generic risk factors that affect all companies (to varying degrees):

  • Changes in the business cycle
  • Competition
  • Inflation and deflation
  • Technological developments

Technological developments can affect:

  • Demand for a product (new substitute → demand curve shifts left → lower quantity or price)
  • Supply of a product (lower manufacturing costs → supply curve shifts right → lower prices at same or higher quantity)

Three Scenarios: Bull, Base, Bear

🐂 Bull Case

Optimistic assumptions on key risk factors. E.g., clinical trial succeeds; technology adoption is slow; competitive intensity decreases. Results in highest revenue, margin, and EPS estimates.

📊 Base Case

Most likely set of assumptions — analyst's best estimate of the most probable future. Center of the scenario distribution. Used as the primary forecast for valuation.

🐻 Bear Case

Pessimistic assumptions. E.g., technology cannibalizes demand faster than expected; competition intensifies; regulatory environment worsens. Results in lowest revenue, margin, and EPS estimates.

💡 How Investors Use Scenarios

Investors compare their own scenarios with sell-side analyst forecasts and with the forecasts implied by current market valuations (i.e., what must be true for the current stock price to be justified). If the market's implied scenario is more optimistic than the analyst's base case, the stock may be overvalued. The analyst adjusts assumptions (sensitivity analysis) to understand how much the outcome changes per unit change in key assumptions.

⚠️ Exam Trap — Scenario vs. Sensitivity Analysis

Scenario analysis: Changes multiple assumptions simultaneously to represent a coherent "world" (e.g., bull/base/bear). Each scenario tells a complete story. Sensitivity analysis: Changes one assumption at a time while holding all others constant, to measure the impact of that single variable. Both are tools for understanding forecast uncertainty; they are complementary.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ 4 Forecast Objects: (1) Drivers of financial statement lines (best explanatory value), (2) Individual financial statement lines (for items without clear drivers), (3) Summary measures — free cash flow, EPS, total assets (efficient but less transparent), (4) Ad hoc objects — not yet in historical statements (legal proceedings, regulatory actions, tax disputes, disasters).
  • ✅ Verifiability principle: Use forecast objects that are regularly disclosed — so forecasts can be confirmed in a timely manner. Third-party consultancy data is fine to inform a forecast but not as a direct forecast object. Avoid overly complex models — more detail ≠ better accuracy.
  • ✅ 4 Forecast Approaches: (1) Historical Results — assume past is precedent; default; NOT for cyclicals or restructuring companies. (2) Historical Base Rates & Convergence — use industry/peer average as base rate; NOT for new/changing industries, cyclicals, or dominant industry leaders. (3) Management Guidance — most important for firm-level objects; NOT for cyclical companies (management has no macro advantage); always check guidance track record; upper bound of range often better represents management's true expectation. (4) Analyst's Discretionary — for cyclicals, no comparables, no guidance, fundamental change.
  • ✅ Management guidance: Accounts for majority of quarterly financial reporting information used by investors (Beyer et al. 2010). Management advantage = firm-level (expenses, capex). Investor advantage = macroeconomic level (Hutton et al. 2012). Guidance may be suspended in high-uncertainty periods (recessions, COVID-19).
  • ✅ Forecast horizon determinants: Investment strategy time frame, industry cyclicality (must reach mid-cycle), company-specific factors (recent acquisitions/restructurings), employer's preferences.
  • ✅ Top-down revenue objects: (1) Growth relative to GDP — nominal GDP forecast × premium/discount based on life cycle position. (2) Market growth and market share — market size × company's share. If GDP-market relationship is predictable → use regression.
  • ✅ GDP revenue formula: If GDP = 4% and company grows 50% faster → revenue growth = 4% × (1+0.50) = 6.0% = +200 bps. OR if +200 bps premium: revenue = 4% + 2% = 6%.
  • ✅ Market share formula: Revenue = Market size × Market share %. Example: 8% share × CNY144B = CNY11.5B.
  • ✅ Bottom-up revenue objects: (1) Volumes × ASP (media: users × ARPU; airlines; asset managers: AUM × fee rate), (2) Product-line/segment revenues, (3) Capacity-based — stores × sales/store, same-store sales growth (≥12 months open), (4) Return/yield-based — bank NII = loans × lending rate − deposits × funding rate.
  • ✅ Non-recurring revenue items: Must be separated from recurring forecast. Type 1 (disclosed): FX effects, extra selling days, acquisitions/divestitures, one-time gains. Type 2 (analyst judgment): COVID e-commerce boost, crypto GPU demand. Never include in the base recurring forecast.
  • ✅ 4 revenue risk factors: Competition, business cycle changes, inflation/deflation, technological developments. All 4 apply to every company — to varying degrees.
  • ✅ COGS forecasting: Best as % of sales (gross margin %). Low-margin companies — break into volume and price components. Gross margin falls even when cost increases are fully passed on to customers (because adding equal absolute amounts to numerator AND denominator reduces the ratio — e.g., COGS/Sales goes from 25% to 40% when input costs double and prices rise 25%).
  • ✅ SG&A forecasting: Less direct link to revenue than COGS. Selling & distribution = variable → % of sales. General corporate costs = more fixed → fixed growth rate (tied to wage inflation). Segments: use EBITDA margins (individual cost lines not usually disclosed by segment).
  • ✅ Working capital forecasting: Use efficiency ratios as forecast objects → multiply by revenue or COGS to get balance sheet items. DSO → accounts receivable. DOH → inventory. DPO → accounts payable. CCC = DSO + DOH − DPO (lower is better for cash generation).
  • ✅ CapEx — two types: Maintenance CapEx = sustains current business; typically forecast as ≈ D&A + inflation adjustment. Growth CapEx = expansion; discretionary; tied to management's expansion plan and revenue growth. Net PP&E increases with CapEx, decreases with D&A.
  • ✅ Capital structure forecasting: Use leverage ratios as forecast objects: Debt/Capital, Debt/Equity, Debt/EBITDA. Future gross debt = Forecast Debt/EBITDA × Forecast EBITDA. Consider: historical practice, management's target structure, debt covenant ratios, CapEx requirements.
  • ✅ Scenario analysis: Multiple coherent forecasts based on different outcomes for key risk factors, each with a judged probability. Used when wide range of outcomes is possible (binary events, cyclical exposure, technological disruption). Bull (optimistic) / Base (most likely) / Bear (pessimistic). Investors compare analyst scenarios with sell-side consensus and market-implied expectations.
  • ✅ Scenario vs. sensitivity: Scenario = multiple assumptions change simultaneously (coherent "world"). Sensitivity = one assumption changes at a time, all others held constant. Complementary tools.
  • ✅ Coherence principle: Revenue forecasts, cost forecasts, working capital forecasts, capex forecasts, and capital structure forecasts must all be internally consistent. If a low-margin product segment grows faster → forecast some margin compression even without knowing the precise segment margin.
