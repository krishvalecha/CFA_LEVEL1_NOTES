Credit Risk — Definition, Components & Key Distinctions

Credit Risk
The risk of economic loss resulting from a borrower's failure to make full and timely payments of interest and/or principal. It is a form of performance risk in a contractual relationship — the borrower has promised specific cash flows; credit risk is the possibility that those promises are not kept.

Default: A borrower that fails to make promised interest and/or principal payments on a bond or loan contract. Default is the credit event that triggers credit losses.

Credit risk is dynamic — it changes over time with economic conditions, borrower financial performance, market sentiment, and events specific to the issuer. It is not static and must be continuously re-evaluated.

Impact on the lender/investor: Loss of some or all principal and interest due (capital loss or income shortfall), disruption to the investor's own cash flows, increased legal and collection costs, reduced income margins.

Illiquid vs. Insolvent — A Critical Distinction

Illiquid Borrower

Cannot raise the necessary funds to make a timely payment — but their assets may still be worth more than their liabilities. They cannot tap credit lines, sell assets quickly, or otherwise secure funding in time. Liquidity problem: a timing issue, not necessarily a solvency issue. An illiquid but solvent borrower may survive if given time or emergency financing.

Insolvent Borrower

Assets are worth LESS than liabilities. Even if all assets were liquidated, there wouldn't be enough to repay all debts. This is a structural solvency problem — not just a timing issue. An insolvent borrower almost always defaults; restructuring or bankruptcy typically follows.

💡 Key Exam Distinction: Illiquid ≠ Insolvent

A company can be liquid but insolvent (can make payments today but liabilities exceed assets long-term), or illiquid but solvent (assets > liabilities but can't pay right now). Credit analysis must assess both dimensions. Liquidity risk is about timing of cash flows; solvency risk is about the fundamental balance sheet. Most defaults begin as liquidity crises but may be underpinned by solvency concerns.

Expected Loss Formula — The Two Components of Credit Risk (Equation 1)

Equation 1 — Expected Loss (EL)
EL = POD × LGD

WHERE:
POD = Probability of Default
(likelihood that the issuer fails to make full and timely payments)
(typically annualized — the chance of default in a given year)

LGD = Loss Given Default
= EE × (1 − RR)

EE = Expected Exposure (= Exposure at Default, EAD)
Amount the investor stands to lose at default
≈ bond face value + accrued interest − current market value of collateral

RR = Recovery Rate
Percentage of the outstanding debt claim recovered if default occurs
(bond investors usually recover something — rarely 0%)

(1 − RR) = Loss Severity
Unrecovered portion of the claim
LGD can be expressed two ways:
(a) Currency terms: e.g., EUR 450,000 of loss on EUR 1,000,000 bond
(b) Percentage of principal: e.g., 45% LGD
The percentage form is preferred for analysis because it allows comparison across borrowers of different sizes.

Example: A bond with POD = 2% annually and LGD = 40% has EL = 0.02 × 0.40 = 0.008 = 0.8% per year. If the credit spread on this bond is 80bps (0.80%), the investor is exactly fairly compensated for expected loss.
Credit Spread as Compensation for Expected Loss
Credit Spread ≈ POD × LGD = Expected Loss

WHERE: Credit Spread (G-spread) = YTM of corporate bond − YTM of comparable government bond

INTERPRETATION:
Credit spread > EL → Investor overcompensated (bond is cheap relative to risk)
Credit spread = EL → Fairly compensated (breakeven)
Credit spread < EL → Investor undercompensated (bond is expensive relative to risk)

This is the fundamental link between credit risk measurement and bond pricing.
Important: This is an approximation — the G-spread also includes liquidity risk premium and other components beyond pure credit risk. In practice, Credit Spread = Credit Risk Premium + Liquidity Risk Premium (+ sometimes a tax component). The expected loss captures only the credit risk portion.

Component Relationships — Exhibit 4 Walkthrough

The credit risk components cascade as follows:

  • Expected Exposure (EE) → total at risk if default occurs
  • × Recovery Rate (RR) → what's recovered → EE × RR = recovered amount
  • = Loss Given Default (LGD) = EE × (1−RR) → what's LOST if default occurs
  • × Probability of Default (POD) → probability-weight the loss
  • = Expected Loss (EL) = POD × LGD → the probability-weighted loss

The credit spread should, at minimum, compensate investors for this expected loss. Any excess spread above EL compensates for liquidity risk, uncertainty risk, and the investor's required risk premium.

The 8 Cs of Credit Analysis — Bottom-Up & Top-Down Factors

The traditional "Cs of credit analysis" framework organizes the factors that determine creditworthiness into two groups: 5 bottom-up factors specific to the individual borrower, and 3 top-down factors that apply to all borrowers in a given environment.

Top-Down Factors (3 Cs) — Apply to All Borrowers
  • Conditions: General economic, competitive, and business environment — recessions, industry disruption, commodity prices. Affects all borrowers' ability to service and refinance debt.
  • Country: Geopolitical environment, legal and political system, rule of law, risk of expropriation, sovereign risk. Especially important for cross-border lending and emerging market bonds.
  • Currency: Exchange rate risk — affects issuers whose cash flows are in one currency but debt is in another. Particularly critical for sovereign issuers with foreign currency debt (e.g., Romania's EUR-denominated bonds).
Bottom-Up Factors (5 Cs) — Specific to Individual Borrower
  • Capacity: Ability to make debt payments on time — the most critical factor. Assessed using financial ratios (coverage, leverage, profitability). Quantitative.
  • Capital: Other financial resources (equity, reserves) that reduce reliance on debt. Higher capital → lower default risk. Quantitative.
  • Collateral: Quality and value of assets pledged to support debt. If borrower defaults, collateral can be seized → lowers LGD. Qualitative + quantitative.
  • Covenants: Legal terms of debt agreements (restrictions on dividends, leverage limits, maintenance tests). Protect bondholders; violations may trigger default. Qualitative.
  • Character: Quality of management and willingness to repay debt. Track record, reputation, transparency, governance quality. Qualitative.
💡 Quantitative vs. Qualitative Cs

Quantitative (financial statement based): Capacity (coverage ratios, leverage ratios, profitability) and Capital (equity/debt levels). These can be measured precisely and compared across borrowers.

Qualitative (judgment-based): Collateral (what are the assets worth? how liquid?), Covenants (how protective are the terms?), Character (is management trustworthy?), Conditions (how tough is the economy?), Country (how stable is the legal system?), Currency (how exposed to FX?).

Analysts assess collateral, covenants, and character based on historical company performance, credit relationships, and the reputation and track record of current management.

Unsecured vs. Secured Debt — Impact on LGD

Unsecured Debt
  • Primary repayment source: issuer's operating cash flows
  • No collateral pledged — unsupported claim
  • Higher LGD (recovery depends entirely on firm's remaining assets after secured claims are paid)
  • Typical of investment-grade corporate bonds and sovereign debt
  • Risk management focus: minimize POD (more important than LGD)
Secured Debt
  • Primary repayment: cash flows — same as unsecured
  • Secondary: specific collateral pledged (real estate, equipment, receivables)
  • Lower LGD (if default, collateral can be seized and liquidated)
  • Typical of high-yield bonds, bank loans, mortgages
  • Risk management focus: minimize both POD and LGD via covenant restrictions and collateral quality

Drivers of POD — Profitability, Coverage & Leverage

The probability of default (POD) is driven primarily by a borrower's ability to service debt — measured through quantitative financial ratios across three dimensions:

Profitability

Key metric: EBIT margin (operating profit as % of revenue)

Stable, predictable cash flows and profits → lower POD. Highly profitable companies can absorb downturns more easily. Volatile or thin margins → higher POD.

Higher profitability → Lower POD → Better credit quality

Coverage

Key metric: EBIT / Interest Expense (interest coverage ratio)

Measures whether cash flows/profits are sufficient to cover interest payments. A coverage ratio of 3× means earnings are 3 times the interest bill. Low coverage → high default risk.

Higher coverage → Lower POD → Better credit quality

Leverage

Key metrics: Debt/EBITDA, Cash Flow to Net Debt

Measures relative reliance on debt financing. High leverage = large debt burden relative to earnings capacity → higher chance of distress if conditions deteriorate.

Lower leverage → Lower POD → Better credit quality

💡 IG vs. HY: Different Focus in Credit Analysis

Investment-grade investors: Less concerned about default (POD is low); more focused on spread risk — how changes in the credit environment affect bond prices. A 1% decline in credit quality spreads can cause significant mark-to-market losses even without a default.

High-yield investors: Accept higher POD; focus on minimizing LGD through covenants (operational restrictions, financial maintenance tests) and collateral (secured claims, specific asset pledges). They also analyze recovery rates very carefully — for a CCC-rated bond that is likely to default, the expected recovery rate is as important as the POD.

The three major global credit rating agencies are Moody's Investors Service, Standard & Poor's (S&P), and Fitch Ratings. They independently assess creditworthiness on a forward-looking basis using quantitative and qualitative analysis. Ratings are symbol-based measures of potential default risk.

Business model: "Issuer pays" — the bond issuer pays the rating agency to rate its debt. This creates a potential conflict of interest (agencies may have incentive to give favorable ratings to retain clients). Post-2008 financial crisis reforms have reduced but not eliminated this conflict.

💡 Three Key Relationships in the Rating Scale (Exhibit 8)

1. Higher rating → lower yield for a given maturity. Investors demand less compensation for lower default risk.

2. Longer maturity → higher yield (generally). Default risk accumulates over time; more uncertainty about the future.

3. Yield spread between IG grades is narrower than IG–HY gap. The jump from BBB– to BB+ (the IG/HY boundary) is often the largest single step in the spread structure — crossing this boundary dramatically changes the investor base, liquidity, and yield required.

Key Features of the Credit Rating Process

  • Ratings are issued on behalf of the issuer (not the investor) — the issuer pays for and requests the rating.
  • Rating agencies often receive access to material non-public information (e.g., financial projections) not available to public investors — giving them informational advantages.
  • Ratings are monitored on an ongoing basis — upgraded if credit quality improves, downgraded if deterioration is likely.
  • Rating agencies may issue a positive or negative "outlook" when creditworthiness is shifting but a rating change is not yet warranted. Outlooks are more aligned with current market conditions than ratings themselves.
  • Credit migration risk (downgrade risk) = the risk that a bond issuer's rating deteriorates, increasing perceived default probability and widening spreads.
  • Ratings also trigger contractual consequences: step-up coupons on step-up bonds (coupon rises if rating falls), covenant violations, collateral posting requirements, investment mandates (institutional investors often cannot hold below-IG bonds).

Limitations of Credit Ratings — Three Core Pitfalls

⚠️ Never Rely Solely on Credit Ratings — Three Fundamental Limitations

Market participants must conduct their own credit analysis. Sole reliance on ratings has led to substantial losses, including during the 2008 Global Financial Crisis. Three specific pitfalls are identified by the curriculum.

① Ratings Lag Market Pricing
  • Credit spreads change daily; ratings change much less frequently
  • Bond prices move faster than agencies can update ratings
  • Investors who wait for rating changes before acting may underperform those who anticipate them
  • Even long-term: ratings may lag changes in bond prices for extended periods
  • For distressed debt: pricing focused on default timing and recovery rates — ratings capture expected loss (POD × LGD) which may be a different signal
  • "Sticky" ratings — agencies prefer stability over frequent changes
② Ratings Miss Hard-to-Quantify Risks
  • Litigation risk (environmental lawsuits, product liability)
  • Natural disaster risk (not predictable)
  • Environmental risk (long-term asset impairment)
  • Leveraged transactions (debt-financed acquisitions, large buybacks) — difficult to anticipate
  • Complex or novel business models create divergent ratings across agencies
  • WeWork example: Fitch: BB–, S&P: B+, Moody's: Caa1 — same company, three very different assessments
③ Miscalculations & Unforeseen Events
  • Rating models may be built on assumptions that break down in extreme scenarios
  • 2008 GFC: highly rated AAA subprime MBS defaulted massively — rating agencies failed to model the scenario of nationwide housing price decline
  • Accounting fraud: Enron, WorldCom (US), Wirecard AG (Germany) — all maintained high ratings until very close to disclosure
  • Wirecard: fraud discovered 19 June 2020 — investors holding solely on IG rating suffered severe losses
  • No model can fully capture management fraud or unprecedented market events

Factors Impacting Yield Spreads — Three Categories

Yield spread = yield of corporate bond − yield of comparable government bond (the "default-risk-free" benchmark). Spreads widen when credit risk rises (higher expected loss, lower liquidity) and narrow when credit risk falls. Credit spread risk is the risk of greater expected loss due to changing macro, market, or issuer-specific conditions.

Total corporate bond spread decomposition:
G-spread ≈ Credit risk premium + Liquidity risk premium + (Tax component)

① Macroeconomic Factors
  • Credit cycle: Business cycle conditions drive spreads — expansion (narrowing), contraction (widening)
  • Spreads narrowest at top of cycle (low perceived risk); widest at bottom (peak fear)
  • Heavy new issue supply without sufficient demand → spreads widen
  • Flight to quality: investors sell risky assets → buy government bonds → corporate spreads widen dramatically
  • Tighter financial regulations → higher cost of market-making → structurally wider spreads
  • Funding stress among broker-dealers → wider spreads
  • HY more sensitive than IG to macroeconomic cycles
② Market / Liquidity Factors
  • Corporate bond spread = credit risk + liquidity risk premium
  • Bid–ask spread: difference between purchase and sale prices → wider = more liquidity risk
  • Issuer size: More publicly traded debt outstanding → more liquid → lower spread
  • Credit quality: Lower quality → less liquid → higher spread
  • Government bonds: fraction of a bp bid–ask; illiquid corporate bonds: much wider
  • During crises: liquidity evaporates → spreads widen sharply (not just from credit deterioration)
  • Spillover: HY stress → spreads widen even for lower-rated IG bonds
③ Issuer-Specific Factors
  • Coverage ratios: EBIT/interest — falling coverage → spreads widen
  • Leverage: Debt/EBITDA rising → spreads widen
  • Profitability changes: Margin compression → credit deterioration → wider spreads
  • Rating changes: Downgrade → spread widening; upgrade → spread narrowing (credit migration risk)
  • Sector comparison: investors compare spreads within same sector/rating to isolate issuer-specific factors
  • Events: M&A (leveraging), management changes, legal issues, technological disruption
Corporate Bond YTM Decomposition
Corporate Bond YTM = Government Benchmark Yield + Credit Spread

Where: Government Benchmark Yield = Real Rate + Expected Inflation Premium
Credit Spread = Credit Risk Premium + Liquidity Risk Premium (+ Tax component)

A change in YTM can come from:
(a) Change in government benchmark yield (rate risk)
(b) Change in credit spread (spread risk)
(c) Both simultaneously (e.g., flight to quality: benchmark falls + spreads widen)

KEY: For option-free bonds, duration and convexity work the same way for
spread changes as for benchmark yield changes — same formulas apply.

💡 Why Invest in High-Yield Bonds?

Despite higher credit risk, HY bonds offer specific benefits: (1) Higher income — significantly higher coupons than IG or government bonds. (2) Portfolio diversification — lower correlation with IG bonds and interest rates; HY behaves more like equity. (3) Capital appreciation potential — economic recovery or issuer improvement has larger positive impact on HY than IG prices; ratings upgrades (the "fallen angel" becoming "rising star") can dramatically narrow spreads. (4) Equity-like returns with lower volatility — HY bond prices are more stable than equities due to larger income component and seniority in capital structure (bonds ranked above equity in insolvency). Some studies suggest better risk-adjusted returns than equities over the long term.

Price Impact of Spread Changes — Equations 3 & 4

Since the YTM of a corporate bond = government benchmark yield + credit spread, a change in YTM can come from either or both components. For option-free fixed-rate bonds, the same duration and convexity formulas apply whether the yield change comes from the benchmark or the spread. The formulas are identical — just substitute ΔSpread for ΔYield.

Equation 3 — Small Spread Changes (Duration Only)
%ΔPV^Full ≈ −AnnModDur × ΔSpread

WHERE:
AnnModDur = annualized modified duration
ΔSpread = change in credit spread (as decimal, e.g., +0.0100 for +100bps)

Spread NARROWS (ΔSpread < 0): Price RISES → positive holding period return
Spread WIDENS (ΔSpread > 0): Price FALLS → negative holding period return

Valid for small, instantaneous spread changes. For larger moves → add convexity.
Equation 4 — Larger Spread Changes (Duration + Convexity)
%ΔPV^Full ≈ (−AnnModDur × ΔSpread) + [½ × AnnConvexity × (ΔSpread)²]

CONVEXITY SCALING RULE — CRITICAL EXAM DETAIL:
Convexity must be re-scaled to be consistent with how the spread change is expressed.

Rule: AnnConvexity should have the same order of magnitude as (AnnModDur)²

Example:
ModDur = 5.0 → ModDur² ≈ 25
If reported AnnConvexity = 0.235 → this is in the wrong scale (too small)
Re-scale: 0.235 × 100 = 23.5 (now consistent with ModDur² ≈ 25)

Then for +1% (100bps) spread increase:
%ΔPV ≈ (−5.0 × 0.01) + [½ × 23.5 × (0.01)²]
= −0.05 + [½ × 23.5 × 0.0001]
= −0.05 + 0.001175
= −0.048825 = −4.8825%
The convexity scaling rule is a key exam detail — if convexity is reported as a small decimal (e.g., 0.235), you must multiply by 100 before applying the formula when the spread change is expressed as a decimal percentage. This ensures both terms in the approximation are of comparable magnitude.

Why does duration dominate for small spread changes? For small ΔSpread, the (ΔSpread)² term is tiny (squaring a small number makes it tinier). For a 1bp = 0.0001 spread change: (0.0001)² = 0.00000001 → convexity term is negligible. For 100bps: (0.01)² = 0.0001 → convexity term is meaningful.

Key Observations on Spread Sensitivity

Longer Duration = Higher Spread Sensitivity
  • A 30-year bond has much higher spread sensitivity than a 5-year bond
  • Same spread change → much larger price impact for long duration bonds
  • IG investors in long-dated bonds must monitor spread changes carefully
  • Spread sensitivity = AnnModDur × spread change in bps
Uncertainty Increases with Maturity
  • Short-term creditworthiness is easier to assess
  • Long-term: harder to forecast management strategy, technology, competition
  • Investors demand more compensation for greater uncertainty in long-dated bonds
  • Results in upward-sloping credit spread curve
Holding Period Return Decomposition — Credit Bond
Total Holding Period Return ≈ Coupon income + Price change from benchmark ΔYield
+ Price change from ΔSpread

Price change from ΔSpread ≈ −AnnModDur × ΔSpread (+ convexity for large moves)

SPREAD NARROWING → Price ↑ → Positive contribution to return (capital gain)
SPREAD WIDENING → Price ↓ → Negative contribution to return (capital loss)

For IG bonds: spread changes often dominate total return (small coupon, large duration effect)
For HY bonds: coupon income often more important than price appreciation from spread moves
(but HY prices are also very sensitive to spread changes — higher yield, potentially lower duration)

EXAMPLE: ModDur = 7.0; Spread narrows by 50bps
Price gain ≈ 7.0 × 0.0050 = 3.5% (meaningful capital appreciation)

  • ✅ Credit risk definition: Risk of economic loss from borrower's failure to make full and timely payments of interest and/or principal. It is a form of performance risk. Dynamic — changes continuously with economic conditions and borrower performance.

  • ✅ Illiquid vs. insolvent: Illiquid = cannot raise funds to make timely payment (timing problem; assets may still exceed liabilities). Insolvent = assets worth less than liabilities (structural balance sheet problem). Both can lead to default but for different reasons.
  • ✅ Two components of credit risk: POD (probability of default — annualized likelihood of missing payments) and LGD (loss given default = EE × (1−RR)). Both required to compute EL.
  • ✅ Expected Loss formula (Eq. 1): EL = POD × LGD. LGD = EE × (1 − RR). EE = expected exposure at default (≈ face value + accrued interest − collateral MV). RR = recovery rate; (1−RR) = loss severity.
  • ✅ Credit spread ≈ POD × LGD. If credit spread = EL → fairly compensated. Spread > EL → overcompensated (cheap bond). Spread < EL → undercompensated (expensive). In practice, spread also includes liquidity premium.
  • ✅ LGD expressed as percentage of principal preferred over currency terms — allows comparison across borrowers of different sizes.
  • ✅ The 8 Cs of Credit Analysis: 5 bottom-up (Capacity, Capital, Collateral, Covenants, Character) + 3 top-down (Conditions, Country, Currency). Capacity and Capital are quantitative; the rest largely qualitative.
  • ✅ POD driven by: Profitability (EBIT margin ↑ → POD ↓), Coverage (EBIT/Interest ↑ → POD ↓), Leverage (Debt/EBITDA ↑ → POD ↑). Higher quality = more profitable, better covered, less leveraged.
  • ✅ IG vs. HY credit analysis focus: IG investors focus on SPREAD RISK (spreads change even without default). HY investors focus on MINIMIZING LGD via covenants and collateral (expect higher POD, want to recover more).
  • ✅ Three major rating agencies: Moody's, S&P, Fitch. Symbol-based ratings; "issuer pays" model; forward-looking analysis; ratings monitored on ongoing basis; positive/negative outlooks issued.
  • ✅ IG/HY Boundary: Baa3/BBB– (Moody's/S&P/Fitch) and above = investment grade. Ba1/BB+ and below = non-investment grade (high yield / junk).
  • ✅ Credit migration risk (downgrade risk): Risk that a bond's rating deteriorates, leading investors to perceive higher default probability and widening spreads.

═══ PRICE IMPACT OF SPREAD CHANGES ═══
Small: %ΔPV ≈ −ModDur × ΔSpread [Eq. 3]
Large: %ΔPV ≈ (−ModDur × ΔSpread) + [½ × Convexity × (ΔSpread)²] [Eq. 4]
SCALE CONVEXITY: must be ~same order of magnitude as ModDur²
(if reported as 0.235 with ModDur=5 → re-scale to 23.5)
Spread narrows → price rises (+return). Spread widens → price falls (−return).
Spread narrowing increases holding period returns — two channels:
(1) Higher coupon income (already priced into the bond's yield at purchase). (2) Capital gain from price appreciation as spread narrows. Spread narrowing is the capital gains channel.

IG vs. HY on spread dynamics: IG spreads narrower and more stable. HY spreads wider and more volatile — move more through the credit cycle. Both have upward-sloping spread curves (longer maturity → higher spread). The IG-to-HY jump at the BBB–/BB+ boundary is typically the largest step on the spread scale.

Flight to quality effect: Government yields fall (demand for safety) AND corporate spreads widen (sell-off of risk) simultaneously. The net effect on corporate bond prices depends on which moves more — falling benchmark vs. widening spread. For HY bonds in crises, spread widening typically overwhelms the benchmark yield decline.
