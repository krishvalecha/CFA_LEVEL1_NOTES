CFA LEVEL 1 · STANDARD II · MODULE 3

Standard II(B): Market Manipulation

The market integrity standard. Covers information-based manipulation (false rumours, pump-and-dump, exaggerated recommendations) and transaction-based manipulation (artificial price and volume distortion, wash trading, cornering markets). Also covers manipulating model inputs, retaliatory trading, and the critical intent threshold distinguishing manipulation from legitimate strategy. LES + Handbook synthesised — includes 9 worked examples, several unique to each source.

01

The Standard — Verbatim Text

Standard II(B) — Market Manipulation

Members and Candidates must not engage in practices that distort prices or artificially inflate trading volume with the intent to mislead market participants.

II(B) prohibits two types of conduct, both requiring intent to mislead market participants:

  1. Distorting prices — any practice that artificially moves or maintains a price away from what it would be in a fair, efficient market
  2. Artificially inflating trading volume — creating the appearance of market activity that does not genuinely exist

Market manipulation damages all investors by disrupting the smooth functioning of financial markets and reducing investor confidence. It may lead to higher risk premiums, reduced investor participation, and reduced market efficiency — negatively affecting economic growth. Cross-border investing increasingly exposes all global investors to manipulation even in markets where it is less prevalent.

02

Two Types of Market Manipulation

Type 1 — Information-Based Manipulation

Disseminating false or misleading information to induce trading by others or to distort price perceptions.

Examples:

  • Spreading false rumours to induce trading
  • "Pump and dump": issuing misleading positive information to inflate price, then selling at the artificially high price
  • Exaggerating earnings projections to force a corporate comment or fuel a quick gain
  • Posting false merger rumours in investor chatrooms
  • Publishing purportedly independent analyses that are actually paid promotional content
  • Manipulating model inputs to achieve desired ratings or prices
  • Creating fake user profiles to spread false information
Type 2 — Transaction-Based Manipulation

Transactions that deceive or would mislead market participants by distorting the price-setting mechanism of financial instruments.

Examples:

  • Transactions that artificially affect prices or volume to give the impression of market activity (wash trading)
  • Trading a security between two funds owned by the same manager to create artificial price/volume signals
  • Securing a controlling/dominant position in a financial instrument to exploit and manipulate the price of a related derivative and/or the underlying asset (cornering a market)
  • Committing to minimum trading volume on a new contract to create false impression of liquidity (ACME Futures example — unless fully disclosed)
  • Placing and cancelling orders to manipulate bid/offer prices
💡 Good Cause Does Not Neutralise Market Manipulation

The Sekar example is the canonical test of this principle. Sekar is trading DD stock between two of his own funds — not to benefit himself personally, but to reduce an overweight position in a low-liquidity stock. He believes the artificial price/volume signals will attract other buyers, allowing him to exit without inducing price declines, which benefits the fund participants. Violation of II(B) regardless. The fact that the manipulation is intended to benefit clients rather than the manager personally does not change the analysis. The act of creating artificial price and volume signals is the violation — the beneficiary of the manipulation is irrelevant.

03

The Intent Threshold — Manipulation vs. Legitimate Strategy

Standard II(B) is not intended to preclude transactions undertaken on legitimate trading strategies based on perceived market inefficiencies. Legitimate trading activity may appear abusive, especially where the market is illiquid or volatile. The critical distinction is intent:

  • Legitimate strategy → trading on a genuine market view, even if it moves prices (acceptable)
  • Manipulation → trading specifically to create a false impression of price or volume in order to mislead other market participants (violation)

The test from the LES for transaction-based manipulation: the member or candidate "knew or should have known" that their actions could affect the pricing of a security. Note the objective standard — not just "intended to manipulate" but also "should have known" the price effect.

✓ Legitimate — Not Manipulation
  • Trading a large position and accepting the natural price impact as the cost of liquidity
  • Short selling based on genuine negative view of a company's fundamentals
  • Writing a research report with legitimate negative views on a company (even if it benefits short sellers)
  • Gordon's acceptable alternative: writing a report framing earnings projection as a range of possible outcomes with clearly stated assumptions, distributed equitably to all clients
  • Market-making activities (passive stance on contra side of unsolicited orders)
  • ACME liquidity promotion — if fully disclosed to investors
⚠️ Manipulation — Prohibited
  • Trading specifically to create false price or volume signals to attract other buyers/sellers
  • Issuing research reports with exaggerated projections specifically to move price (not based on reasonable belief)
  • Timing the release of negative reports during a company's quiet period to maximise price impact for short-selling clients
  • Running a rumour campaign alongside artificial trading to reduce a position without price impact
  • Using compensation structures that incentivise manipulating ratings inputs for short-term gains
Examiner Focus — Murphy vs. Gordon: Research Report as Manipulation

These two examples contrast legitimate and manipulative research. Murphy (Example 3): issued a research report specifically timed to sensationalise negative aspects and create downward pressure during Wirewolf's quiet period — to benefit his firm's short-selling clients. The report's conclusions are based on speculation, not fact. This is manipulation — the report's purpose is to distort price, not to provide genuine analysis. Gordon (Example 5/6): had genuine research findings from a Latin America trip. Her violation was exaggerating her earnings projection to force a comment from the company (telling select momentum clients that consensus is "much too low" with a figure she knew was unrealistically high). The acceptable alternative: write a factual report with a range of outcomes and stated assumptions, distributed equitably to all clients. The distinction: genuine analysis with honest uncertainty ranges = acceptable. Deliberate exaggeration designed to move price = manipulation.

04

Indicators That May Signal Market Manipulation

The Handbook provides a non-exhaustive list of indicators that surveillance systems should monitor:

Order-Level Indicators
  • Orders entered for an unusually short time period
  • Orders above market price
  • Orders resulting in no change in beneficial ownership (wash trading)
  • Orders that intend to change bid or offer prices and are cancelled before execution (layering/spoofing)
  • Orders on both sides of the trade using different platforms
Market-Level Indicators
  • Coordinated action by two or more traders in a market with a small number of participants
  • Unusual price movements without corresponding news or fundamental developments
  • Volume spikes concentrated in low-liquidity stocks before price moves
  • Patterns suggesting dominant position building to corner a market
05

Recommended Procedures for Compliance

Firms should implement the following procedures to prevent and detect manipulative trading:

  • Order procedures: Procedures designed to prevent placing and cancelling orders with the intent to deceive others into buying or selling at artificial prices
  • Real-time monitoring/surveillance: Across multiple platforms, designed to identify suspicious order entries, potential improper coordination among customers, wash trading, cross-product securities manipulation, and other manipulative trading activity
  • Automated order management: Pre-trade controls to identify and prevent manipulative orders before they are executed
  • Algorithm controls: Controls that address the use of trading algorithms for manipulative purposes
  • Trade exception reporting: Systematic reporting of trades that deviate from normal patterns

The LES also emphasises that compensation structures should not incentivise manipulation — compensation should not be linked to ratings levels or product sales volumes without any link to long-term performance (Mandeville example). Perverse compensation creates the conditions for manipulation even without explicit intent.

Worked Examples — LES + Handbook (All 9)

Example 1
Both Sources
FIS — Paid Promotion Disguised as Independent Analysis
VIOLATION
FIS's principal owner enters into compensation agreements with two microcap companies to promote their stocks. FIS disseminates emails, maintains websites, and distributes an online newsletter — all presenting the information as independent analysis. The inaccurate and promotional content drives stock prices dramatically higher.
Violation of II(B). Using inaccurate reporting and misleading information under the guise of independent analysis = information-based manipulation.
Also Violated (LES adds)
V(A) Diligence and Reasonable Basis — no reasonable and adequate basis for the recommendations. VI(A) Avoid or Disclose Conflicts — compensation arrangements from the covered companies not disclosed.
🔑 Core Rule
The violation combines information-based manipulation with the failure to disclose paid promotional arrangements. "Independent analysis" framing applied to paid promotions = misrepresentation AND manipulation. Note the multiple standards violated — II(B) for the market impact, V(A) for the lack of basis, VI(A) for the undisclosed conflicts.
Example 2
Both Sources
Gray — Rumour Campaign + Coordinated Multi-Account Trading
VIOLATION
Gray holds a large position in Fame Pharmaceuticals (low-volume small-cap). Wanting to reduce his holdings without crashing the price, he: (1) spreads rumours on blogs and social media promoting the company, (2) creates accounts in the names of family members, friends, and a religious institution, and (3) conducts back-and-forth trading through multiple brokers to create the appearance of trading activity and price momentum. He systematically sells slightly more than he buys over time to exit his position.
Violation of II(B) through both mechanisms: (1) information-based manipulation — false rumour campaign to create the appearance of interest, (2) transaction-based manipulation — coordinated trading through multiple accounts to create the appearance of liquidity and price momentum that doesn't genuinely exist.
🔑 Core Rule
Gray's manipulation is sophisticated — combining misinformation with artificial trading in a coordinated plan. Two distinct II(B) violations in one scheme. Note: he conducted the trades through family, friends, and institutional names — the use of other people's accounts to disguise the coordinated nature of his trading is part of the manipulation, not a shield against it.
Example 3
Both Sources
Murphy — Timed Research Report to Create Artificial Price Volatility
VIOLATION
Murphy (analyst) knows his firm's hedge fund clients are short Wirewolf Semiconductor. He also knows Wirewolf is in its quiet period before reporting earnings (making a rebuttal difficult). He times the release of a speculative negative research report specifically to "sensationalise the negative aspects" and create "significant downward pressure" on Wirewolf stock — benefiting the short-selling clients who can then cover at the depressed price.
Violation of II(B). Murphy timed a report based on speculation (not fact) specifically to create artificial price volatility to benefit his firm's clients' short positions. The deliberate timing during the quiet period to prevent rebuttal is an aggravating factor showing clear manipulative intent.
Also Violated (LES adds)
V(A) Diligence and Reasonable Basis — the report's conclusions are based on speculation, not on fact.
🔑 Core Rule
Key distinction from legitimate short-selling research: legitimate research is based on factual analysis and published without strategic timing. Murphy's report is (1) based on speculation not fact, (2) deliberately timed to exploit the company's quiet period, and (3) explicitly intended to create downward price pressure. All three elements confirm manipulative intent. The analysis of who benefits from the manipulation (Murphy's firm's hedge fund clients) is relevant but not determinative — the intent and the artificial volatility are the primary violations.
Example 4
Both Sources
Sekar — Inter-Fund Wash Trading to Create False Price/Volume Signals
VIOLATION — EVEN WITH GOOD INTENT
Sekar manages two funds (equity and balanced) both supposed to follow the same model, which says their DD stock holdings are excessive. DD has low liquidity. He decides to trade DD stock back and forth between the two funds to slowly increase price and volume — attracting outside buyers so he can reduce the overweight position without price impact. He believes this benefits fund participants (who get a better exit price) even though extra commissions are incurred.
Violation of II(B) — regardless of the beneficial intent for fund participants. The plan is based on artificial distortion of both trading volume AND the price of DD stock.
🔑 Core Rule
Good intent for clients does not neutralise market manipulation. Sekar's scheme creates false signals that would mislead other market participants into buying DD based on apparent market interest and price momentum that is entirely artificial. The harm is to the broader market, not just to any specific client. Note: this also raises III(A) (Loyalty, Prudence, Care) issues — trading between his own funds at the detriment of overall market integrity, and incurring unnecessary commissions, is not acting in the best interests of clients.
Example 5 (LES-Only)
LES Only
ACME Futures Exchange — Liquidity Pumping: Disclosed vs. Undisclosed
CONDITIONAL
ACME is launching a new bond futures contract. To demonstrate liquidity and attract initial users, it enters into agreements with members committing them to substantial minimum trading volumes over a specific period in exchange for reduced commissions. The result: artificially inflated trading volume during the launch period, which then falls once the commitment period ends.
Violation if not disclosed. Investors have been intentionally misled to believe they chose the most liquid instrument — but actual liquidity will be significantly reduced after the commitment period. Not a violation if fully disclosed: ACME's intent is not to harm investors but to give them a better service. If ACME fully discloses the agreement with members to boost transactions over an initial launch period, it does NOT violate II(B).
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only Example)
This is a nuanced case: artificially inflating volume is generally II(B) — but if the purpose is to establish a new market, not to mislead investors for personal gain, and the arrangement is fully disclosed, it is acceptable. The disclosure cures what would otherwise be manipulation. This demonstrates that intent + disclosure together determine whether a market liquidity strategy crosses into manipulation. Key takeaway: any arrangement that creates an impression of market activity different from genuine market interest must be fully disclosed if it is to avoid being manipulation.
Example 6 (LES) / Example 5 (Handbook)
Both Sources
Gordon — Exaggerated Earnings Projection to Force Corporate Comment
VIOLATION
Gordon has legitimate research from a Latin America trip suggesting Hygene's earnings will be 5% above her current estimate. The CFO refuses to confirm. Gordon then tells select momentum-style clients that consensus earnings are "much too low" and she is considering raising her estimate by an "ambitious" $0.15 to $2.15 per share — a figure she believes is unrealistically high. Her goal: force Hygene to update its guidance. She hopes clients who act will profit from a quick gain.
Violation of II(B). Exaggerating earnings projections to fuel a quick gain in Hygene's stock price = information-based manipulation. The LES version adds a second violation: by previewing intentions to raise estimates only to select momentum clients, she also violated III(B) Fair Dealing (selective disclosure of investment recommendations). Acceptable alternative: write a report framing the projection as a range of possible outcomes, outlining clearly her assumptions, distributed equitably to all Picador clients.
🔑 Core Rule
Two separate violations: (1) Exaggerating a projection beyond what she genuinely believes = false information = II(B). (2) Sharing the exaggerated projection only with select momentum clients before distributing to all = III(B). Even if Gordon's underlying research was correct (earnings would beat consensus), the method — using an inflated figure to force a corporate response — is manipulation. Genuine analysis with honest uncertainty ranges ≠ manipulation. Deliberate exaggeration to move price = manipulation.
Example 7 (LES) / Example 6 (Handbook)
Both Sources
Weinberg — Classic Pump and Dump via Internet Chatrooms
VIOLATION
Weinberg logs on to investor chatrooms to spread false rumours that Moosehead & Belfast Railroad is about to expand its rail network in anticipation of a large lumber shipping contract, in order to inflate the price of his holdings.
Violation of II(B). Disseminating false information about Moosehead & Belfast with the intent to mislead market participants = classic pump-and-dump information-based manipulation.
🔑 Core Rule
The simplest and most straightforward example of II(B): false rumours in internet chatrooms intended to inflate a stock price for personal gain. Social media platforms (chatrooms, forums) are explicitly covered by the standard — the same rules apply regardless of the channel used for dissemination.
Example 8 (LES-Only)
LES Only
Mandeville — Manipulating Structured Product Model Inputs for Better Ratings
VIOLATION
Mandeville supervises a structured financing team at Superior Investment Bank. To achieve the best possible rating, he uses mostly positive scenarios as model inputs — those that reflect minimal downside risk. The resulting statistics in the rating request and underwriting prospectus suggest the structured products have minimal potential downside. His compensation is partially based on the level of rating achieved and the successful sale of new products, with no link to long-term performance. He leads Superior as the top originator of structured products for two years. In year three, the economy deteriorates, asset values collapse, defaults ensue, and Superior goes bankrupt.
Violation of II(B). Mandeville manipulated the inputs of a model to minimise associated risk and achieve higher ratings — a form of information-based manipulation. He knowingly decided which inputs to include to achieve the desired rating and price, creating misleading output statistics that investors relied on.
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only)
Model input manipulation = II(B) violation. This is the 2008 financial crisis analogue — structured products with manipulated ratings inputs. Three key points: (1) Selective use of positive scenario inputs to minimise apparent risk = deliberately presenting misleading statistical output to investors. (2) Compensation structures that link pay to ratings levels without a long-term performance component create perverse incentives — Mandeville had every financial reason to manipulate. (3) The harm is systemic: the loss of confidence in these products causes not just individual losses but "a loss of investor trust in the system" affecting the entire capital market's ability to operate efficiently. The LES notes that "promoting a rating and price with inaccurate information" caused enormous downstream damage — market manipulation has systemic, not just individual, consequences.
Example 9 (LES-Only)
LES Only
King — Posting False Rumours Under Another Person's Name to Trigger Regulatory Investigation
VIOLATION — NO PERSONAL PROFIT REQUIRED
King (performance analyst at Torrey Investment Funds) is angry at the portfolio manager because the manager doesn't give him baseball tickets. To incite a regulatory review of the manager, King creates user profiles on online forums under the portfolio manager's name and starts false merger rumours for several small companies in the portfolio. As the stock prices rise, the portfolio manager sells the positions — triggering an investigation, as King desired.
Violation of II(B). King did not personally profit from the market's reaction to the rumour. His intent was to create trouble for the portfolio manager, not to profit personally. But he still violated II(B) — by posting false information, he misled others into believing the companies were likely to be acquired, manipulating the factual information available to the market.
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only)
Personal profit is NOT required for a II(B) violation. The violation is the manipulation of market information — regardless of who benefits. King's motive was revenge, not profit. But the effect on the market (other investors induced to buy on false merger rumours) is exactly what II(B) prohibits. This example also illustrates that manipulation through others' identities (fake profiles under another person's name) is still the manipulator's violation — identity concealment is a method, not a defence.

Exam Checklist — Standard II(B): Market Manipulation

  • ✅ Two prohibited acts: (1) Distorting prices. (2) Artificially inflating trading volume. Both require intent to mislead market participants.
  • ✅ Two types of manipulation: Information-based (false rumours, pump-and-dump, exaggerated projections, misleading model inputs) and transaction-based (wash trading, artificial volume, cornering markets).
  • ✅ Intent is the critical threshold. Legitimate trading strategies based on genuine market views — even if they move prices in illiquid markets — are NOT manipulation. The test: is the purpose to create a false impression, or to execute a genuine investment strategy?
  • ✅ "Knew or should have known" standard for transaction-based manipulation. Not limited to deliberate intent — objective awareness that actions could distort prices is sufficient.
  • ✅ Good intent for clients doesn't cure manipulation. Sekar: trading between own funds to benefit fund participants at exit = still a violation. The harm is to the broader market.
  • ✅ Personal profit is NOT required. King: manipulated market to harm a colleague (not for profit) = still a violation. The manipulation of market information is the violation, regardless of motive.
  • ✅ Legitimate trading activity may appear manipulative in illiquid/volatile markets — intent determines the outcome.
  • ✅ Research reports can be manipulation if their purpose is to move prices rather than provide genuine analysis. Murphy: timed speculative report during company's quiet period specifically to create downward pressure = II(B).
  • ✅ Genuine research with exaggerated projections to force corporate comment or fuel price movements = II(B). Gordon: even with legitimate underlying research, the exaggerated projection used to manipulate = violation.
  • ✅ Acceptable research alternative: Range of outcomes + clearly stated assumptions + equitable distribution to all clients = not manipulation.
  • ✅ Model input manipulation = II(B). Mandeville: using positive-only scenario inputs to minimise apparent risk in structured product ratings = information-based manipulation of the market.
  • ✅ Compensation structures that incentivise manipulation without long-term performance links create the conditions for violations. Members should flag and resist such structures.
  • ✅ Artificial liquidity arrangements (like ACME Futures) may avoid violation IF fully disclosed. Undisclosed: violation. Disclosed: legitimate market development strategy.
  • ✅ False rumours through any channel (internet chatrooms, social media, blogs, email newsletters disguised as independent analysis) = II(B). Channel is irrelevant.
  • ✅ Using fake identities or other people's account names to spread rumours is manipulation by the person who creates and posts the false information — not a defence.
  • ✅ Multiple standards often violated simultaneously: II(B) + V(A) Diligence (no reasonable basis for speculative report); II(B) + III(B) Fair Dealing (selective pre-distribution of exaggerated projection); II(B) + VI(A) Disclose Conflicts (paid promotion not disclosed).

Top II(B) exam traps:
(1) "He did it to benefit his fund participants, not himself" — good intent for clients doesn't cure manipulation (Sekar)
(2) "She had real research to support a general positive view" — using an exaggerated number to force corporate action is still manipulation (Gordon)
(3) "He didn't profit, so no violation" — personal profit not required; market distortion is the test (King)
(4) "The report was distributed to all clients" — market impact from a speculative report timed to exploit the company's quiet period = manipulation regardless of distribution (Murphy)
(5) "The volume arrangement was to help launch a new product" — intent matters but disclosure is also required; undisclosed = violation (ACME)
(6) "Legitimate short selling based on negative view of fundamentals" — selling short based on genuine analysis is NOT manipulation; the violation is manufacturing the downward pressure through false/exaggerated information (Murphy)
