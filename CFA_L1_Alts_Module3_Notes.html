Private Equity — Overview & Key Strategies

Private Equity
Capital raised from sources other than public markets and traditional institutions (not from exchanges, bonds, or government/bank sources) in the form of an equity investment. Three main strategies: Leveraged Buyout (LBO), Venture Capital (VC), and Growth Capital.

Private equity vs. public equity — similarities:

  • Both represent direct ownership and control
  • Shareholders have voting rights (AGM: board election, strategy, impactful decisions)
  • All owners have a direct, proportional claim on residual cash flows (dividends)
  • Capital gains are typically the largest return driver (price appreciation or free cash flow from holdings)
  • Unlimited upside potential; downside limited to amount invested

Private equity — key differences from public equity:

  • Significant shareholdings allow more direct control over day-to-day decisions
  • Requires specialized knowledge specific to the industry and sector
  • Distinct entry and exit points; vintage year important for comparative performance
  • Illiquid; no active secondary market

Investment forms:

  • Direct: Investment in a single specific asset
  • Co-investment: Invest alongside a lead sponsor who sources, structures, and executes the transaction
  • Fund (single PE fund): Investor places capital in a fund that holds multiple portfolio companies
  • Fund-of-funds: Indirect vehicle with stakes in multiple other private funds — additional diversification but additional fee layer
02

Leveraged Buyouts — LBO, MBO, MBI

Leveraged Buyout (LBO)
Private equity firm acquires a public or established private company using a significant percentage of purchase price financed through debt. Target company's assets serve as collateral; target's cash flows service the debt. The acquired company becomes or remains privately owned ("going private" transaction). Post-acquisition debt becomes part of the target's capital structure.
Management Buyout (MBO)

The current management team participates in the acquisition. Management becomes part-owner of the newly private company. Aligns management incentives with ownership.

Management Buy-In (MBI)

The current management team is replaced with the acquiring team, who then manage the company. New management team brings external expertise to restructure.

How LBO managers add value: Improve operations, boost revenue, increase profits and cash flows. Cash flow growth contributions, in order:

  1. Organic revenue growth
  2. Cost reductions and restructuring
  3. Acquisitions
  4. All other sources

Returns depend heavily on leverage. If debt financing is unavailable or costly → LBOs become less attractive and less likely. Private equity managers may shift focus as business conditions and financing availability change.

Portfolio companies: Companies owned by a PE fund are called "portfolio companies" — they form the fund's portfolio.

💡 LBO Target Characteristics

Ideal LBO targets have: (1) stable, predictable cash flows to service debt, (2) undervalued or underutilized assets that can serve as collateral, (3) potential for operational improvement or cost reduction, (4) strong management team (or a replaceable one). The target's assets = collateral; target's cash flows = debt service.

Venture Capital — Investment Stages

Venture Capital (VC)
Investing in or providing financing to private companies with high growth potential — typically startups or young companies. Can be injected at various stages from concept creation to just before an IPO. VC investors are active investors directly involved with portfolio companies. Returns required increase with earlier stage (higher risk).
Stage 1 — Pre-Seed (Angel Investing)

Capital at the idea stage. Used to develop a business plan and assess market potential.

Investors: Founders, friends & family, angel investors. Amount: USD5k–USD500k. Source: Mainly individuals (not VC funds).

Stage 2 — Seed-Stage Financing

Supports product development and marketing efforts, including market research.

Investors: Seed funds, angel investors. Amount: USD25k–USD5M. Note: This is the first stage at which VC funds usually invest.

Stage 3 — Early-Stage (Start-Up)

For companies moving toward operation but prior to commercial production or sales. Financing initiates operations.

Investors: Venture capital funds, corporate venture funds. Amount: USD5M+.

Stage 4 — Later-Stage (Expansion VC)

After commercial production and sales have begun but before an IPO. Funds support initial growth, major expansion (plant upgrade), product improvements, or major marketing.

Later-stage financing generally involves management selling control of the company to the VC investor. Financing via equity AND debt (including convertible bonds or convertible preferred shares). Debt used for asset control in bankruptcy, NOT to generate income.

Investors: PE investors, institutional investors, family offices, strategic investors.

Stage 5 — Mezzanine-Stage Financing

Bridge financing to prepare a company for an IPO or sale as it expands capacity. Infused between private and public company status (hence "mezzanine" = middle). Distinguished by timing of financing rather than method.

Financing type: typically equity-like (to capture IPO gains) or short-term debt. May also use mezzanine financing instruments (convertible debt/preferred).

Important distinction: Mezzanine-stage financing ≠ Mezzanine financing. "Mezzanine-stage" = timing (between private and public). "Mezzanine financing" = hybrid debt/equity instrument (convertible debt, convertible preferred).

Convertible Preferred Shares in VC

Commonly used in startups to raise VC funding:

  • Option to convert preferred shares into a fixed number of common shares after a predetermined date (and sometimes at a predetermined price)
  • Aligns incentives between entrepreneurs and VC investors (conversion value tied to startup valuation)
  • Provides investor protection: in liquidation, preferred convertible shareholders have seniority over common shareholders and recover full investment before common shareholders receive anything
  • Also protects investor if startup fails — has claim priority and options on equity if it succeeds

Growth Capital (Growth Equity) & PIPE

Growth Capital (Growth Equity / Minority Equity Investing)
A PE firm takes a less-than-controlling (minority) interest in more mature companies looking for capital to expand or restructure operations, enter new markets, or finance major acquisitions. Often initiated by the investee company's management — seeking to realize earnings from selling a portion of shares before IPO while retaining control and participation in future upside.
PIPE (Private Investment in Public Equity)
A private offering to select investors with fewer disclosures and lower transaction costs. Allows an already-publicly-traded company to raise capital more quickly and cost-effectively than through regulated, expensive, and lengthy public processes.

PIPE transaction mechanics:

  • Newly issued common stock or shares sold by existing stockholders made available to select investors
  • Typical investors: investment firms, mutual funds, other institutional investors
  • Definitive purchase agreement at a fixed price (typically at a discount to market)
  • Common in work-out or rescue situations where there is a material difference between market price and fundamental valuation
  • Special case: PIPE via convertible debt or convertible preferred stock

Key PIPE considerations:

  • Dilutive to existing shareholders — new shares issued at a discount reduces existing shareholders' proportional ownership and intrinsic per-share value
  • New investors typically require a discount to market price as compensation for illiquidity and risk
  • Creates potential incentive conflicts between existing shareholders and new (discounted) shareholders

Private Equity Exit Strategies

PE firms buy and hold companies for an average of 5 years (range: <6 months to >10 years). Before deciding on exit, managers assess: industry dynamics, economic cycle, interest rates, and company performance. Goal: sell to the highest bidder to maximize returns.

PE fund structure: ~5-year investment period (deploy capital) + subsequent harvesting period (exit investments, return capital to LPs).

Two Main Exit Strategies: Trade Sale and Public Listing

Exit 1 — Trade Sale

Portion or division of private company sold via direct sale or auction to a strategic buyer interested in increasing scale and scope.

Advantages

  • Immediate cash exit
  • Strategic buyer pays premium (synergies priced in)
  • Fast and simple execution; lower costs vs. IPO
  • Higher confidentiality (few parties involved)
  • Strategic buyer can evaluate fit without external scrutiny

Disadvantages

  • Potential management/employee resistance (job security fears, competitor ownership)
  • Limited set of potential buyers → may reduce price and raise regulatory scrutiny
  • Employees may prefer IPO to monetize share options
Exit 2A — IPO (Initial Public Offering)

Portfolio company sells shares to public investors using financial intermediaries to underwrite. PE firm may sell some or all of its shares.

Advantages

  • Potentially highest share price
  • Management approval; job security maintained
  • PE sponsor gains notoriety and visibility
  • PE retains share → still benefits from future price appreciation

Disadvantages

  • High transaction fees (banks, lawyers)
  • Long lead time; onerous disclosure requirements
  • Stock market volatility → price uncertainty
  • Post-IPO lockup period limits quick exit
  • Not suitable for smaller companies, out-of-favor industries, or limited operating history
Exit 2B — Direct Listing

Equity floated on public markets directly, without underwriters. Reduces complexity and cost. Less common than IPO.

Exit 2C — SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company)

A "blank check" company created solely to acquire an unspecified private company within a predetermined period; otherwise returns capital to investors.

Advantages

  • Extended disclosure time; can provide forward guidance to build investor interest
  • Fixed valuation → lower price volatility and uncertainty
  • Flexible transaction structure
  • High-profile, seasoned SPAC sponsors + their investor networks

Disadvantages

  • Higher capital costs (warrants, fees → dilution)
  • Valuation spread between SPAC equity value and equity purchased
  • Deal risk (successful execution of merger agreement)
  • Post-merger stockholder overhang → downward share price pressure
  • Increasing regulatory scrutiny (SEC reconsidering SPAC classification)

Other Exit Strategies

💡 Recapitalization ≠ Exit

A recapitalization allows the PE firm to extract cash (via dividends from new debt) and improve IRR metrics — but it is NOT a true exit because the PE firm retains ownership and control of the portfolio company. It's a liquidity event, not a divestiture.

06

PE Risk-Return & Data Quality Issues

Sources of PE's higher return potential:

  • Ability to invest in private companies not available in public markets
  • Greater direct control and influence over portfolio company management and operations
  • Use of leverage amplifies returns on invested equity

PE is riskier than public equity: Higher illiquidity risk + leverage risk → investors should demand a higher return premium.

⚠️ Private Equity Index Data Problems

Published PE indexes may be unreliable measures of performance due to multiple issues:

  • Self-reporting bias: PE returns rely on voluntary self-reporting → same survivorship and backfill biases as hedge funds → overstatement of returns
  • Failure to mark-to-market: Prior to the 2008 GFC, PE firms didn't always mark investments to market in the absence of a liquidity event → understatement of volatility and correlations
  • Smooth returns artifact: Illiquidity lag in marking investments causes artificially smooth reported returns → understated measured risk
  • Data adjustments required to more reliably measure PE benefits. Many investors now require quarterly or annual marking by an independent party
  • Vintage year comparability: Performance depends heavily on vintage year — same life cycle phase comparison required

Private Debt — Four Main Categories

Private Debt
Various forms of debt provided by investors directly to private entities (not through public bond markets). Investors receive interest payments and return of principal after a designated term. Debt typically secured with covenants. Expansion driven by private lending funds filling the gap left by traditional lenders after the 2008 financial crisis.
1 — Venture Debt

Private debt funding for start-up or early-stage companies that may have little or negative cash flow. Often a line of credit or term loan. Purpose: obtain additional financing without further diluting shareholder ownership.

  • Complements existing equity financing; allows current shareholders to maintain ownership/control longer
  • May carry features compensating lender for increased default risk (e.g., rights to purchase equity under certain circumstances)
  • Similar to mezzanine debt in structure; may lack substantial assets for collateral
2 — Direct Lending

Investor provides capital directly to borrowers and receives interest + original principal + possible other payments. Typically senior and secured with covenants. Provided by a small number of investors to private (and sometimes public) entities.

  • Similar to bank loans: fixed payment schedule
  • Differs from publicly traded bonds: provided to fewer participants and not publicly tradeable
  • Private debt funds (or PE firms with private debt arms) raise investor capital then seek mid-market lending opportunities or provide loans to other PE funds for acquisitions
  • Target borrowers: entities needing capital but lacking good alternatives to traditional bank lenders
  • Leveraged loans: Private debt firm borrows money itself, then lends it to a borrower — enhances return on loan portfolio but increases risk
3 — Mezzanine Debt

Private credit subordinated to senior secured debt but senior to equity. Pool of additional capital beyond senior secured debt.

  • Used for: LBOs, recapitalizations, corporate acquisitions, and similar transactions
  • Riskier than senior secured debt due to junior ranking and typically unsecured status
  • Compensation for higher risk: higher interest rates AND equity participation features
  • Common features: warrants (right to purchase equity) or conversion rights (option to convert debt to equity)
  • Capital structure position: senior secured debt → mezzanine debt → equity
4 — Distressed Debt

Buying the debt of mature companies in financial difficulty — in bankruptcy, defaulted, or likely to default. Two approaches:

  • Value investors: Identify companies with temporary cash flow problems but good business plan → buy debt expecting company and debt to increase in value
  • Turnaround investors: Buy debt with intent to be active in management and direction → restructure and revive the company
  • Requires specialized knowledge: likelihood of default, recovery rates (distressed debt is priced to expected recovery rate), how to restructure companies and debt
  • Bankruptcy procedures: lengthy, complex, capital intensive
  • DIP financing (Debtor-in-Possession): Provides operating funds to firms already in bankruptcy — common strategy for distressed debt funds
5 — Unitranche Debt

A hybrid/blended loan combining different tranches of secured and unsecured debt into a single loan at a single blended interest rate.

  • Interest rate falls between rates on pure secured and pure unsecured debt
  • Capital structure position: typically between senior and subordinated debt in priority ranking
  • Simplifies deal structure — single loan agreement vs. multiple tranches
6 — Specialty Loans

Debt extended to niche borrowers in specific situations.

  • Example: Litigation finance — specialist funding company provides debt to plaintiffs for legal fees and expenses in exchange for a share of judgments

08

Private Debt — Risk & Return

Sources of higher return potential vs. public bonds:

  • Illiquidity premium: Excess return investors require for lack of liquidity
  • Market inefficiencies: Private debt funds take opportunistic positions in less-efficient markets
  • Diversification benefit: Different risk/return profile from public fixed income
  • Gap-filling after 2008: Traditional bank lending contracted → private debt funds stepped in at favorable pricing

Interest rate structure: Often expressed as a floating rate relative to a reference rate — e.g., SOFR + 375 bps. Coupon varies with changes to the reference rate (market reference rate, MRR). Contrast with fixed-rate public bonds.

Risk-return spectrum within private debt (low to high risk/return):

  1. Infrastructure debt (safest, lowest return)
  2. Senior real estate debt
  3. Senior direct lending
  4. Unitranche debt
  5. Mezzanine debt
  6. Private equity co-investments (highest risk/return)

Senior private debt: Steadier yield, moderate risk. Mezzanine private debt: Higher growth potential, equity upside, higher risk.

⚠️ Private Debt Risk Factors

Riskier than traditional bonds: (1) Illiquidity — no active secondary market for most private debt. (2) Heightened default risk — loans extended to riskier borrowers or riskier situations. (3) Specialized knowledge needed — life cycle stage of borrower, debt structure, underlying assets (especially for secured lending). (4) Modeling difficulties — lack of good-quality data + artificially smooth returns.

Vintage Year & Diversification Benefits

Vintage Year
Typically defined as the year in which the fund makes its first investment. Critical for comparing private equity performance because the economic and valuation environment at the fund's origin can substantially affect realized returns over the fund's lifespan. The most appropriate performance comparisons are between funds of the same vintage year.

Standard PE fund timeline:

  • Total fund life: 10–12 years
  • Investment period (first ~5 years): Capital sourced from LPs and invested in portfolio companies
  • Harvesting period (remaining years): Fund exits investments and returns capital to LPs

Vintage Year and Business Cycle Effects

Favorable Vintage Years

Funds starting in a low-valuation, low-risk-appetite, economic recovery phase can ride the wave of economic recovery → stronger returns.

Expanding business cycle + early-stage companies → excess returns.

Unfavorable Vintage Years

Funds investing bulk of capital in a high-valuation environment preceding a market crash or prolonged economic contraction → reduced returns.

Contracting business cycle + distressed companies → excess returns if focused on distressed strategies.

💡 Vintage Diversification Strategy

To offset the risk of an ill-timed fund launch, investors pursue vintage year diversification — investing across funds started in multiple different years. This ensures some funds are started in favorable cycles and others can still generate returns through distressed strategies in downcycles. No single vintage year is universally "best."

Portfolio Diversification Benefits

  • Private capital can add a moderate diversification benefit to a portfolio of publicly traded stocks and bonds
  • Correlations with public market indexes: 0.63 to 0.83 — meaningful but not perfectly correlated (some diversification benefit)
  • Skillful manager selection → potential excess returns from additional leverage, market, and liquidity risk premia
  • Difficult to hedge performance risk of private investments vs. public investments (less hedging flexibility)

Risk-Return Hierarchy Across the Capital Structure

Private capital risk and return mirrors the traditional risk-return trade-off across the corporate capital structure. From lowest to highest risk and expected return:

Rank (Risk ↑)Asset ClassReturn LevelRisk Level
1 (Lowest)Infrastructure DebtLowestLowest — most secured, essential services, stable CFs
2Senior Real Estate DebtLow-moderateLow-moderate — secured by real property
3Senior Direct LendingModerateModerate — senior secured, covenants
4Unitranche DebtModerate-highModerate-high — blended tranche risk
5Mezzanine DebtHighHigh — subordinated, unsecured, equity-like upside
6 (Highest)Private Equity / Co-InvestmentsHighestHighest — residual claim, illiquid, leveraged
💡 Capital Structure Trade-Off — The Core Principle

The more senior the claim in the capital structure → the lower the risk → the lower the expected return. The more junior the claim (closer to equity) → the higher the risk → the higher the expected return. This applies equally to private and public market investments. The difference in private markets: higher illiquidity and concentration risk AND greater uncertainty about underlying businesses AND fewer hedging options → demand a further premium over public equivalents.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Private capital: Funding NOT from public markets or traditional institutions. Private equity = equity form. Private debt = loan/debt form. Covers entire capital structure.
  • ✅ 3 PE strategies: (1) LBO — mature/declining firms, high leverage, assets as collateral, cash flows service debt. (2) VC — early-stage/startup, high growth potential. (3) Growth capital — minority stake in mature companies seeking expansion capital.
  • ✅ LBO: Significant % of purchase price = debt. Target assets = collateral. Target cash flows = debt service. Post-buyout = private company ("going private"). MBO = current management participates. MBI = current management replaced by acquiring team.
  • ✅ LBO value creation (in order): Organic revenue growth → cost reductions/restructuring → acquisitions → other. Returns depend greatly on leverage; if financing unavailable → LBOs less attractive.
  • ✅ VC stages (memorize order): Pre-seed (idea; individuals; $5k–500k) → Seed (product dev; first VC stage; $25k–5M) → Early-stage (operations startup; before commercial production) → Later-stage (after sales begun; expansion; management may cede control) → Mezzanine-stage (bridge to IPO; equity-like or short-term debt).
  • ✅ Mezzanine-stage financing ≠ mezzanine financing: Mezzanine-STAGE = timing (between private and public = bridge to IPO). Mezzanine FINANCING = instrument type (subordinated hybrid debt with equity features). The stage can USE mezzanine financing, but they are distinct concepts.
  • ✅ Convertible preferred shares in VC: Convert to common shares after predetermined date/price. Aligns incentives. In liquidation: preferred convertible shareholders are SENIOR to common shareholders. Protects investor if company fails; provides equity upside if it succeeds.
  • ✅ Growth capital: Less-than-controlling (minority) stake. Mature company seeks expansion capital. Management-initiated; seller retains control. Common in PIPEs for public companies too.
  • ✅ PIPE: Private offering to select investors. Fewer disclosures, lower costs. Fixed price (at discount to market). Common in workout/rescue situations. Dilutive to existing shareholders. May use convertible debt or convertible preferred.
  • ✅ PE exit — avg holding period ~5 years (range: <6 months to >10 years). Fund = ~5-year investment period + harvesting period.
  • ✅ Trade sale: Sell to strategic buyer. Quick, confidential, synergy premium. BUT: management resistance, limited buyers, regulatory scrutiny.
  • ✅ IPO: Potentially highest price; management approval; PE retains upside. BUT: high costs, long lead time, disclosure, stock market volatility, post-IPO lockup, only for larger/established companies.
  • ✅ Direct listing: Public float without underwriters. Simpler and cheaper than IPO.
  • ✅ SPAC: Blank check company acquires a private company within set period. Fixed valuation (lower uncertainty); extended disclosure period; high-profile sponsors. BUT: dilution from warrants, valuation spread, deal risk, stockholder overhang, regulatory scrutiny.
  • ✅ Recapitalization: NOT a true exit. PE adds leverage → takes dividend. Retains ownership. Improves IRR by returning capital early.
  • ✅ Secondary sale: Sell to another PE firm. Increasingly common due to large PE fund pools globally.
  • ✅ Write-off/liquidation: Failed investment; value revised downward or company liquidated.
  • ✅ PE data problems: Self-reporting → survivorship + backfill bias (overstated returns). No mark-to-market (pre-2008) → understated volatility. Smooth returns = artificially low measured risk. Data adjustments required; independent quarterly/annual marking preferred.
  • ✅ 4 main private debt categories: Direct lending (senior secured, bank alternative), venture debt (startup, non-dilutive), mezzanine (subordinated, equity features via warrants/conversion), distressed (near-bankrupt; priced to recovery rate; DIP financing).
  • ✅ Mezzanine debt position: Senior secured debt → mezzanine debt → equity. Unsecured, subordinated. Higher rates + warrants/conversion rights to compensate for risk.
  • ✅ Unitranche: Blended secured + unsecured → single loan, single blended rate. Between senior and subordinated in priority.
  • ✅ Distressed debt: Priced to expected recovery rate. DIP financing for companies already in bankruptcy. Bankruptcy = lengthy, complex, capital intensive.
  • ✅ Private debt interest rate: Often floating = reference rate (SOFR) + spread (e.g., SOFR + 375 bps). Varies with interest rate environment.
  • ✅ Vintage year: Year fund makes its FIRST investment. Determines economic/valuation environment at origin. Comparable performance = same vintage year funds. Fund life = 10–12 years (investment period ~5 years + harvesting period).
  • ✅ Vintage diversification: Invest across multiple vintage years to offset risk of ill-timed fund launch. Expanding cycle + early-stage → excess returns. Contracting cycle + distressed companies → excess returns.
  • ✅ Correlation with public markets: 0.63–0.83 → moderate diversification benefit. Not zero correlation → not a complete diversifier.
  • ✅ Risk-return hierarchy (lowest to highest): Infrastructure debt → Senior RE debt → Senior direct lending → Unitranche → Mezzanine → Private equity co-investments. Higher claim seniority = lower risk = lower return.
