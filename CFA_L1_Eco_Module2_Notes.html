01

Business Cycle — Definition & Types

Core Definition

Business cycles are recurrent expansions and contractions in economic activity affecting broad segments of the economy. Key features:

  • Characteristic of market-based economies (not agrarian or centrally planned)
  • Phases occur broadly simultaneously across the economy
  • Recurrent but not periodic — they repeat but vary in intensity and duration (1 to 10–12 years)
  • Two primary turning points: Peak (top) and Trough (bottom)

Three Cycle Concepts

Cycle TypeWhat It MeasuresKey FeatureUsed By
Classical Cycle Absolute level of GDP (volume) Contraction = actual decline in GDP. Peaks/troughs are absolute levels. Rarely used
Growth Cycle GDP relative to long-run trend/potential Measures output gap (actual minus trend). Peaks earlier, troughs later vs. classical. Most common
Growth Rate Cycle Rate of change of GDP (growth rate) Peaks/troughs recognized earliest. No need to estimate trend path. Also used
💡 Key Insight — Growth Cycle is the Standard

For CFA purposes, the growth cycle is the primary framework. Business cycles = fluctuations around potential output (the trend). Output gaps (positive or negative) drive the phase descriptions. Remember: the cycle measures deviation from trend, not just absolute output levels.

Output Gap (Growth Cycle Concept)
Output Gap = Actual GDP – Potential GDP
Positive gap: Actual > Potential → Economy overheating (Expansion/Slowdown peak)
Negative gap: Actual < Potential → Economy underperforming (Contraction/Recovery trough)

Recession Definition

Simple rule: Two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth = recession.

Limitation: Misleading if pattern is negative → slightly positive → negative. NBER (National Bureau of Economic Research) uses a broader approach — analyzing employment, industrial production, and sales data — to officially date US business cycle peaks and troughs. NBER determinations often occur well after turning points due to data revision lags.

⚠ Exam Trap

The "two consecutive quarters of negative GDP" rule is a simple rule of thumb, not the official definition. The NBER committee analyzes multiple economic variables, not just GDP. Know both, and know the limitation of the simple rule.

03

Phase-by-Phase Deep Dive

Recovery
Economy at/near trough. Negative output gap narrowing.
  • Output below potential but improving
  • Layoffs slow; firms use overtime
  • Unemployment still above average
  • Inflation moderate
  • Interest rates low / being cut
  • Risky assets repriced upward
  • Equity market troughs 3–6 months early
  • Capex: efficiency focus, software/hardware reinstated first
Expansion
Growth above average. Positive output gap opens → Boom.
  • Actual output exceeds potential
  • Firms move from overtime → new hires
  • Unemployment falls and stabilizes
  • Inflation picks up modestly
  • Interest rates start rising
  • Possible supply shortages, overinvestment
  • Capex: heavy equipment, warehouses, factories
  • Central bank may tighten to prevent overheating
Slowdown
Output at peak relative to potential. Gap begins to narrow.
  • Largest positive output gap
  • Growth decelerates (still above-average)
  • Consumers still optimistic
  • Firms use overtime, not new hires
  • Unemployment slowly declining
  • Inflation still accelerating
  • Interest rates high; encouraging slowdown
  • Capex: new orders signal late-stage overinvestment
Contraction
Output falls below potential. Recession if severe enough.
  • Negative output gap reopens
  • Confidence declines; spending falls
  • Cut hours → freeze hiring → layoffs
  • Unemployment rises
  • Inflation decelerates (with a lag)
  • Banks reluctant to lend
  • Inventories: unwanted build-up
  • Investors prefer safe assets: govt bonds, utilities, staples
  • Capex: new orders halted; light equipment cut first
⚠ Critical Exam Points on Lags

Employment lags the cycle — firms are slow to hire in recovery (want confirmation) and slow to fire early in contraction (want to retain trained workers). Unemployment rate is a lagging indicator.

Inflation lags the cycle — inflation decelerates in contraction but with a lag. It may still be rising even as output is already falling.

Equity markets lead by 3–6 months — financial markets reprice expectations ahead of economic data.

Investor Strategy by Phase

PhasePreferred AssetsAvoid
Recovery Cyclical equities, corporate bonds; risky assets repriced upward Government bonds (priced high from recession)
Expansion Equities (especially cyclicals), high-yield bonds Safe haven assets
Slowdown Begin rotating to defensives; start reducing cyclical exposure Late-cycle, overvalued cyclicals
Contraction Government bonds, utilities, consumer staples ("steady cash flows") Cyclical equities, high-yield bonds
04

Credit Cycles

Definition & Mechanism

Credit cycles describe the changing availability and pricing of credit (private sector credit: loans for business investment and real estate purchases).

When economy is strong: Lenders extend credit freely on favorable terms → asset prices rise (especially real estate) → further economic expansion.

When economy weakens: Lenders tighten credit → asset values fall → further weakness + higher defaults → deeper recession.

Key Properties of Credit Cycles

Characteristics
  • Longer, deeper, and sharper than business cycles
  • Not always synchronized with the business cycle
  • Closely linked to housing and real estate markets
  • Strong peaks → associated with subsequent systemic banking crises
Why Analysts Monitor Credit Cycles
  • Understand housing/construction market dynamics
  • Assess depth of business cycle expansion or contraction
  • Anticipate policy responses (macroprudential policy)
  • Recessions with financial disruption → longer and deeper
  • Recoveries with rapid credit growth → stronger
Critical Relationship
Strong credit cycle peak → Systemic banking crisis risk ↑↑
Historical examples: Latin America 1980s, Asian crisis 1997–98, Global Financial Crisis 2008–09. Expansive credit → asset bubbles → bust when outflows occur.

Credit Cycle vs. Business Cycle

FeatureBusiness CycleCredit Cycle
Primary measureReal GDPPrivate sector credit & property prices
Duration1–12 yearsGenerally longer
AmplitudeModerateDeeper and sharper peaks/troughs
Synchronization—Not always in sync with business cycle
AmplificationBaseAmplifies business cycle (deeper recessions or stronger expansions)
💡 Policy Implication

Traditional monetary/fiscal policy targets business cycle volatility. Macroprudential policy specifically targets credit cycle booms (e.g., capital requirements, LTV limits) to prevent systemic banking crises. This is a newer and increasingly important tool.

05

Resource Use Over the Business Cycle

Workforce & Employment — Pattern Logic

Employment lags the cycle because of the cost of hiring and training. Firms prefer to adjust hours (overtime → cut hours) before adjusting headcount. This explains why unemployment is a lagging indicator.

Order of adjustment:

  1. Adjust overtime hours first (quickest, no fixed cost)
  2. Adjust temporary/contract workers
  3. Adjust permanent full-time headcount last

Capital Spending (Capex) Over the Cycle

PhaseCapex BehaviorExamples
Recovery Low but increasing. Focus on efficiency rather than capacity. Software, hardware, high-obsolescence equipment reinstated first. IT systems, software upgrades
Expansion Increasing strongly. Focus shifts to capacity expansion. Orders precede shipments (new orders = leading indicator). Warehouses, factories, heavy equipment
Slowdown Still placing new orders at or near capacity — possible late-stage overinvestment. Fiber-optic overinvestment in tech bubble is a classic example. Fiber optic, tech infra overbuilding
Contraction New orders halted; some existing orders canceled. Light equipment with short lead times cut first; heavy equipment cuts follow. Construction and heavy equipment cuts
💡 Analyst Note on New Orders

Use "core" capital goods orders (excluding defense and aircraft) for a cleaner economic signal. Defense orders are driven by government policy; aircraft orders are lumpy multi-year contracts. Both distort the underlying trend.

Inventory Dynamics

Key metric: Inventory-to-Sales Ratio — measures inventories available for sale relative to actual sales. A rising ratio can signal economic slowdown; a falling ratio signals recovery.

PhaseSales vs. ProductionInventoriesInventory-Sales Ratio
Recovery Sales recover; production lags Falling (excess cleared) Falling toward low levels
Expansion Production rises fast to restock Rising (restocking phase) Stabilizes at normal level
Slowdown Sales slow faster than production Rising (unintended build-up) Rising — signals weakening economy
Contraction Production cut below sales Unwanted, being liquidated Falls back toward normal
🧠 Inventory Trick: High I/S ratio = Bad signal; Falling I/S ratio = Recovery brewing

Think of inventory as unsold goods piling up on shelves. When the ratio rises, demand is slowing. When it falls, demand is recovering. Analysts watch this ratio as an early signal of turning points.

Economic Indicators

Three Types

Leading Indicators

Turning points precede the economy. Used to predict future state.

  • Stock market index (S&P 500)
  • Avg. weekly hours (manufacturing)
  • Avg. weekly initial claims for unemployment insurance
  • Manufacturers' new orders (consumer goods)
  • ISM new order index
  • Manufacturers' new orders (capital goods, ex-defense & aircraft)
  • Building permits (new housing)
  • Interest rate spread (10-yr vs. overnight)
  • Leading Credit Index
  • Consumer expectations
Coincident Indicators

Turning points match the economy. Used to identify current state.

  • Industrial Production Index
  • Real personal incomes
  • Retail sales
  • Manufacturing and trade sales
  • Non-farm payrolls (employment)
Lagging Indicators

Turning points follow the economy. Used to confirm past trends.

  • Average duration of unemployment
  • Inventory-to-sales ratio
  • Change in unit labor costs
  • Average prime lending rate
  • Ratio of consumer instalment debt to income
  • Commercial & industrial loans outstanding
  • Inflation
🧠 Mnemonic — "SOP" for Leading Indicators

Stock market, Orders (new), Permits (building) — three of the most commonly tested leading indicators. Add Spread (yield curve) and Claims (unemployment insurance, inverse) to complete the core list.

⚠ Exam Trap — Unemployment Insurance Claims

Average weekly initial claims for unemployment insurance is a leading indicator and moves inversely with the economy (rising claims → weakening economy). Do not confuse with the unemployment rate itself, which is a lagging indicator.

Key Leading Indicator Logic — Why They Lead

IndicatorWhy It Leads
Avg. weekly hours (manufacturing) Firms cut overtime before laying off — hours fall before employment changes
Unemployment insurance claims Layoffs show up in claims before broader unemployment stats
New orders (consumer goods) Companies order before producing — signals future production
Building permits Permits required before construction begins — signals future housing activity
S&P 500 stock index Markets price in future expected earnings; equity troughs 3–6 months before GDP
Interest rate spread (10yr – overnight) Inverted yield curve (negative spread) anticipates short rate cuts → economic slowdown ahead
Consumer expectations Optimism/pessimism directly precedes changes in consumer spending

Conference Board Leading Economic Index (LEI) — US

Published by The Conference Board. Combines the 10 leading indicators listed above into a single composite. Uses the classical business cycle concept. New orders for capital goods exclude defense and aircraft for cleaner signal.

OECD Composite Leading Indicator (CLI)

Published for ~30 countries and aggregates (EU, G7). Uses the growth cycle concept. Methodology is consistent across countries → easy cross-country comparison. Includes PMI surveys for services (Euro area) and business failures (Japan) — country-specific components.

Other Key Composite Indicators

Survey-Based Indicators
  • PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index) — IHS Markit; global business surveys; widely used
  • Tankan Report — Bank of Japan quarterly business survey
  • ISM new order index — US manufacturing orders; diffusion-based
  • Consumer confidence — Conference Board (global)
Big Data / Nowcasting
  • CFNAI (Chicago Fed) — 85 monthly series; principal components analysis
  • EuroCOIN (Bank of Italy/CEPR) — 100+ series for Euro area
  • GDPNow (Atlanta Fed) — real-time running estimate of current quarter GDP; converges to BEA advance estimate
  • Nowcasting = forecasting the present using high-frequency data
💡 PMI Threshold to Know

A PMI reading above 50 signals expansion; below 50 signals contraction. This is a commonly tested fact for PMI-type survey indicators. It works the same for ISM indexes.

07

Diffusion Index

Definition & Purpose

A diffusion index measures the breadth of change in a composite indicator — what proportion of components are moving in the same direction as the overall index. It supplements the level of the index by showing whether a move is broad-based or driven by outliers.

Conference Board Diffusion Index — Calculation Method

Component Scoring Rules (Conference Board)

For each of the 10 LEI components in a given month:

  • 1.0  if component rises by more than 0.05%
  • 0.5  if component changes by less than 0.05% (flat)
  • 0.0  if component falls by more than 0.05%
Diffusion Index Formula
Diffusion Index = (Sum of assigned values ÷ Number of components) × 100
Range: 0 (all falling) to 100 (all rising). A reading of 50 means half rising, half falling. Higher = broader economic expansion.

Worked Example

Month 1: 4 components: stock prices +2% (→1.0), money growth +1% (→1.0), orders flat (→0.5), confidence −0.6% (→0.0)

Sum = 2.5 ÷ 4 × 100 = 62.5

Month 2: stock prices −0.8% (→0.0), money growth +0.5% (→1.0), orders +0.5% (→1.0), confidence +3.5% (→1.0)

Sum = 3.0 ÷ 4 × 100 = 75.0

→ Index rose from 62.5 to 75.0: more components rising → broader economic momentum confirmed.

💡 Why Diffusion Index vs. Simple Index?

A straight composite can be pulled up by one massive outlier component. The diffusion index ignores magnitude — only counts direction. A high diffusion index means broad participation in the trend, making the signal more reliable. An analyst uses both: composite level + diffusion breadth = complete picture.

⚠ Exam Trap — What Diffusion Index Doesn't Show

The diffusion index does NOT reflect the magnitude of changes in individual components — only their direction. A component rising by 0.06% gets the same score (1.0) as one rising by 50%. Use it for breadth signals, not magnitude signals.
