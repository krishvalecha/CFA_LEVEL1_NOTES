01

The Operating Cycle — Basics

For a firm that makes and sells physical goods, the operating cycle consists of sequential steps:

  1. Acquire materials from suppliers → recorded as accounts payable (future cash outflow)
  2. Produce inventory → recorded as inventory on the balance sheet
  3. Sell products to customers → recorded as accounts receivable (future cash inflow)
  4. Collect cash from customers → cash inflow realized

Cash outflows and inflows do not occur at the same time as the underlying activity. Materials may be received but not paid for weeks later; goods sold but cash not received for weeks. This timing mismatch is the fundamental working capital challenge.

Balance Sheet Treatment of Operating Cycle
Future cash INFLOWS within operating cycle → SHORT-TERM ASSETS (e.g., accounts receivable, inventory)
Future cash OUTFLOWS within operating cycle → SHORT-TERM LIABILITIES (e.g., accounts payable)
Balance sheets list assets and liabilities in descending order of liquidity. Assets/liabilities not settling within 12 months are classified as long-term.

The Three Key Time Measures (Activity Ratios)

DPO — Days Payable Outstanding

How long, on average, the firm takes to pay its suppliers. Higher DPO = better (keeps cash longer).

DOH — Days of Inventory on Hand

How long inventory sits before being sold. Lower DOH = better (converts inventory to sales faster).

DSO — Days Sales Outstanding

How long it takes to collect cash from customers after a sale. Lower DSO = better (receives cash faster).

The Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

★ Core Formula — Cash Conversion Cycle
CCC = DOH + DSO − DPO
DOH = Days of Inventory on Hand
DSO = Days Sales Outstanding
DPO = Days Payable Outstanding

CCC = number of days between PAYING suppliers and RECEIVING cash from customers
= the number of days the firm needs external financing to bridge the gap

CCC = DOH + DSO − DPO

Interpretation:

  • Short CCC → converts inventory investment into cash quickly → less financing needed → better
  • Long CCC → needs more financing to pay bills (payroll, suppliers) while waiting for customer payments
  • Negative CCC → receives cash from customers BEFORE paying suppliers → cash can be invested elsewhere → best scenario (e.g., Amazon, Costco)

Industry Context — 2020 US Averages

Average US-listed company CCC: 78 days (significant variance by industry)

  • Pharmaceuticals: Long CCC — large inventory of drugs must be held on hand (sometimes regulatory requirement)
  • Airlines: Short/Negative CCC — service business with minimal goods inventory; mostly prepaid sales (cash or credit card)
  • Spirits/Whiskey: Very long DOH — product must age for years before sale
⚠ Exam Signal — Lengthening CCC Is a Red Flag

A CCC that is longer than competitors or has been lengthening over time may signal: worsening customer demand, deteriorating customer financial health/credit quality, or loss of bargaining power with suppliers. It is NOT always a problem if it reflects legitimate industry characteristics (e.g., pharma, spirits).

03

Working Capital — Definitions

Total Working Capital vs. Net Working Capital
Total Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities

Net Working Capital = (Current Assets − Cash − Marketable Securities)
                         − (Current Liabilities − Short-term debt − Current portion of LT debt)
Net working capital EXCLUDES: cash, marketable securities (not operational), and short-term debt (financing items).
Net WC focuses purely on the OPERATING cycle components: receivables, inventory, and payables.
Both are often expressed as a % of annual sales for cross-firm comparability.

Relationship between CCC and Working Capital/Sales ratio:

  • Long CCC → High Net WC/Sales ratio (more capital tied up in operations)
  • Short CCC → Low Net WC/Sales ratio (less capital tied up)

High WC/sales can reflect legitimate industry necessity (spirits aging, pharma compliance) but generally issuers are better off holding less working capital and investing excess capital in higher-return projects or returning it to shareholders.

💡 Worked Example — Total vs. Net Working Capital

Licht Vernieuwend N.V. (31 Dec 20X2): Current assets = EUR 335M; Current liabilities = EUR 220M.
Total WC = 335 − 220 = EUR 115M
After excluding cash (EUR 40M), marketable securities (EUR 70M), and short-term bank loans (EUR 60M):
Net WC = (335 − 40 − 70) − (220 − 60) = 225 − 160 = EUR 65M

04

Shortening the Cash Conversion Cycle

Reduce DOH (Inventory)
  • Discontinue low/niche-demand products
  • Negotiate "just-in-time" (JIT) deliveries with suppliers
  • Use data analytics to improve demand forecasts and rationalize stocking levels
Reduce DSO (Receivables)
  • Offer prompt-payment discounts to customers
  • Impose late fees and tighten credit standards
  • Require upfront deposits or accelerate installments
  • Work with third-party collection agencies
Increase DPO (Payables)
  • Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers
  • Establish preferred supplier relationships (volume in exchange for better terms)
  • Constrained by supplier power dynamics — can't always extend terms

Prompt-Payment Discount Trap — A Key Formula

Implicit Cost of Forgoing Prompt-Payment Discount
Example: Supplier offers "2/10 net 30" — 2% discount if paid in 10 days, else full payment in 30 days.

If firm FORGOES discount and pays on Day 30:
→ Implicitly borrowing from supplier for (30 − 10) = 20 days at the cost of the 2% discount

Annualized cost ≈ (Discount% / (1 − Discount%)) × (365 / Days extended)
= (2% / 98%) × (365 / 20) ≈ 37.2% annualized cost!
Supplier financing is VERY expensive. Better strategy: borrow from a bank at a low rate → pay supplier early → get the 2% discount → repay the bank. This preserves cash while avoiding the exorbitant implied cost of supplier trade credit.
05

Liquidity — Core Concepts

Liquidity (Asset/Liability Level)
The nearness to cash or settlement of an individual asset or liability. Cash is the most liquid asset. Inventory is less liquid (must first sell, then collect). Accounts payable due in 5 days is more liquid than a lease due next month.
Liquidity (Issuer Level)
An issuer's ability to meet its short-term liabilities. Determined by the amounts and liquidity of its short-term assets and liabilities — which are in turn determined by its business model and cash conversion cycle.

Asset Liquidity Spectrum

Liquidity Order — Most to Least Liquid
Cash → Short-term Marketable Securities → Accounts Receivable → Inventory → Long-term Assets
Balance sheet lists assets in descending order of liquidity (most liquid at top). This ordering is crucial for understanding which assets count in each liquidity ratio.
06

Primary Sources of Liquidity

Primary sources are the most readily accessible cash. Using them does NOT signal financial distress and is unlikely to affect ongoing operations.

1. Cash & Marketable Securities

Cash in bank accounts, currency held, or securities that can be sold quickly without significant loss of value.

Most immediate; always available.

2. Borrowings

From banks, bondholders, or suppliers (trade credit). Yields cash quickly but creates a future repayment obligation.

Important short-run source; must be repaid.

3. Cash Flow from Operations

Revenue generated by the business after paying employees, suppliers, taxes, and interest. Takes time to generate but is the primary LONG-TERM source of liquidity for most profitable firms.

Early-stage or loss-making firms may not generate sufficient operating cash flows.

Cash Flow from Operations (CFO)
Cash received from customers
+ Interest and dividends received on financial investments
− Cash paid to employees and suppliers
− Taxes paid to governments
− Interest paid to lenders
= Cash Flow from Operations (CFO)
CFO does NOT account for capital expenditures (long-term asset investments). Analysts therefore also calculate Free Cash Flow.
Free Cash Flow (FCF)
FCF = Cash Flow from Operations − Investments in Long-term Assets (Capex)

Alternative: FCFF (Free Cash Flow to the Firm) adds back interest paid to lenders
→ represents cash available for BOTH debt AND equity investors
FCF is the cash truly available after maintaining/expanding the asset base. A core metric for equity valuation (covered in later modules).
07

Secondary Sources of Liquidity

Secondary sources are used when primary sources are insufficient. Their use signals deteriorating financial health — typically obtained at high cost or significant disadvantage to existing stakeholders.

Secondary Source Description Key Cost / Trade-off
Suspend/Reduce Dividends Stop or reduce cash distributions to shareholders. Signals distress to market; may trigger stock price decline.
Delay/Reduce Capex Postpone or cut capital expenditure spending. Preserves near-term cash but may miss opportunities and impair long-term value.
Issue Equity Issue new shares publicly or via private placement. Dilution of existing shareholders; signal of weakness.
Renegotiate Contract Terms Refinance short-term to long-term debt; seek interest/rent/lease concessions; restructure covenants; renegotiate customer/supplier terms. May involve higher interest costs; damages supplier/customer relationships.
Sell Assets Liquidate short-term or long-term assets for cash. Depends on asset liquidity; often involves significant discount (liquidation cost).
File for Bankruptcy Protection Reorganize under court protection; restructure debt contracts; possibly sell assets. Most severe signal; legal costs; disrupts operations and relationships.
💡 Real-World Example — COVID-19 Airlines (2020)

Airlines used secondary liquidity sources as revenue collapsed in early 2020: Lufthansa raised EUR 600M via convertible bond offering. Singapore Airlines raised SGD 8.8B via rights issue (equity issuance). Delta Airlines deferred USD 500M in capital expenditures. All are classic secondary liquidity strategies triggered by a crisis.

08

Drags & Pulls on Liquidity

🔻 Drags on Liquidity

Cash INFLOWS lag — available funds decline

  • Uncollected receivables: Longer outstanding = greater chance of non-collection. Measured by DSO and % payment delinquencies.
  • Obsolete inventory: Goods held too long may no longer be in demand or can only be sold at a discount → reduces cash realization.
  • Borrowing constraints: If credit conditions tighten (economic downturn), short-term debt becomes more expensive or unavailable.

Note: Stricter credit/collection reduces drags but may also lower sales if customers can't/won't pay upfront.

🔼 Pulls on Liquidity

Cash OUTFLOWS accelerate or credit is interrupted

  • Making payments early: Paying vendors/employees before due dates with no financial benefit → loses use of funds unnecessarily.
  • Reduced credit limits: Suppliers with history of late payments may cut credit outstanding at any time.
  • Limits on short-term credit lines: Bank limits lending → liquidity constraints. Can be government-mandated, market-related, or company-specific.
  • Chronic low liquidity: Due to industry conditions or weak financial position; worsened by aggressive WC management.
💡 Memory Trick — Drags vs. Pulls

DRAGS pull DOWN your cash inflows (think: dragging feet on collecting). PULLS pull your cash OUT faster than expected (think: someone pulling your wallet). Drags = inflow problem. Pulls = outflow problem.

Liquidity Ratios — All Three Formulas

★ Current Ratio — Broadest Measure
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
Includes ALL short-term assets (even inventory, which is less liquid).
Current Ratio > 1 → positive total working capital → can cover all short-term liabilities from current assets.
Higher = more liquid (under this broad measure).
Limitation: inventory may not be easily converted to cash quickly.
★ Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio) — Excludes Inventory
Quick Ratio = (Cash + Short-term Marketable Securities + Accounts Receivable) / Current Liabilities
Also: Quick Ratio = (Current Assets − Inventory) / Current Liabilities
Quick Ratio > 1 → can meet short-term obligations WITHOUT liquidating inventory.
But still requires collecting ALL receivables without delays or delinquencies.
More conservative than current ratio.
★ Cash Ratio — Most Conservative
Cash Ratio = (Cash + Short-term Marketable Securities) / Current Liabilities
Cash Ratio ≥ 1 → can satisfy ALL short-term obligations without selling inventory or waiting for receivables.
Most stringent test of immediate liquidity.
Very few companies maintain a cash ratio of 1 — would mean holding excessive idle cash.
Ratio Numerator Excludes vs. Current Conservatism > 1 Means...
Current Ratio All current assets Nothing excluded Least conservative Can cover all ST liabilities from current assets
Quick Ratio Cash + ST securities + Receivables Inventory excluded Moderate Can cover ST liabilities without selling inventory
Cash Ratio Cash + ST marketable securities only Inventory + Receivables excluded Most conservative Can cover all ST liabilities from cash/securities alone
⚠ Exam Trap — Higher Is Not Always Better for Current Ratio

A very high current ratio could mean the company is holding excessive inventory or receivables — i.e., inefficient working capital management rather than exceptional liquidity. Always interpret ratios in context: compare with industry peers and track trends over time. A ratio that is unusually high vs. peers and rising deserves investigation.

10

Working Capital Management Approaches

Primary goal of WC management: Maximize firm value while maintaining ready access to funds needed for day-to-day operations and creditor obligations. Often means: shorten CCC, estimate required liquidity, and minimize excess (deploy capital to higher-return projects or return to shareholders).

Permanent vs. Variable Current Assets

Permanent Current Assets

Base levels of inventory, staffing, and receivables that are relatively constant over time. Funded with long-term sources in conservative/moderate approaches.

Variable Current Assets

Additional inventory and labor needed during seasonal peak production/sales or growth phases. Funded with short-term sources in moderate/aggressive approaches.

The Three Approaches — Comparison

Dimension Conservative Moderate ("Matched") Aggressive
WC Level Higher (more cash, receivables, inventory relative to sales) Moderate Lower (minimize excess cash, receivables, inventory)
Permanent Assets Funded By Long-term debt + equity Long-term debt + equity Short-term liabilities
Variable Assets Funded By Long-term debt + equity Short-term debt Short-term liabilities
Financing Cost Highest Moderate Lowest
Rollover / Refinancing Risk Lowest Moderate Highest
Financial Flexibility Most flexible Moderate Least flexible
Who Uses It Early-growth firms (limited ST debt access); high-margin firms who can pass on cost Firms that can forecast base needs accurately; balanced risk/cost preference Low-margin firms needing cost advantage; firms with high sales forecast accuracy
Conservative — Pros & Cons
  • ✅ Stable funding; avoids rollover risk
  • ✅ High flexibility during market stress
  • ✅ Financing costs known upfront
  • ❌ Long-term debt = higher interest cost
  • ❌ High cost of equity
  • ❌ Long-term debt may restrict business operations
  • ❌ Permanent financing → can't borrow only as needed
Aggressive — Pros & Cons
  • ✅ Lower financing cost (ST rates typically lower)
  • ✅ Borrow only as needed → less idle capital
  • ✅ Fewer business operation restrictions
  • ✅ Flexibility to refinance if rates fall
  • ❌ Interest expense fluctuates with rate changes
  • ❌ Higher short-term cash needs to repay maturities
  • ❌ Rollover risk → bankruptcy risk during market stress (2008, COVID-19)
  • ❌ May need costly trade credit or asset sales if can't refinance
When to Choose Each Approach
CONSERVATIVE → Expectation of rising rates · Preference for cash flow stability · Limited short-term debt access · Early-growth stage

AGGRESSIVE → Expectation of stable/falling rates · High sales forecast precision · Ability to shorten CCC · Low-margin industry

MODERATE → Accurate base needs forecast + uncertain variable needs · Want lower cost than conservative but lower risk than aggressive

Short-Term Funding Strategy

Even profitable companies can encounter financial difficulties by failing to maintain sufficient liquidity. A prudent short-term financing strategy should achieve:

  1. Diversified credit sources — don't rely on a single lender or type of funding
  2. Adequate capacity for changing cash needs — accommodate seasonal peaks and planned growth
  3. Competitive financing rates — understand how rates change under different economic conditions
  4. Account for all funding costs — both explicit (interest rates) and implicit (e.g., cost of forgoing supplier prompt-payment discounts)

Factors Affecting a Firm's Short-Term Funding Options

Factor Impact on Short-Term Funding
Size Small firms → limited to bank credit advances. Large firms → access ST fixed-income markets (commercial paper, etc.). Many options involve minimum sizes or fixed costs → unavailable to small firms.
Creditworthiness Determines loan approval, interest rate, and restrictive conditions. Less creditworthy firms face more restrictions (e.g., limits on selling/using assets).
Legal Considerations Firms in emerging/frontier markets with less-defined legal systems → fewer options from intermediaries → more reliance on supplier trade credit.
Regulatory Constraints Regulated industries (utilities, banks) may be restricted in borrowing amount/type. But financial institutions have unique access: can borrow/lend central bank reserves; can access central bank funding directly.
Underlying Assets Companies with assets suitable as collateral (e.g., inventory) can access secured short-term funding at better rates.
💡 Best Practice — Matching Debt Maturities to Cash Receipts

Forecasting cash positions over the CCC and beyond reduces financial distress risk. Matching the timing of debt maturities to expected cash receipts and spacing maturities out over time reduces short-term funding concentration risk — a key principle of prudent liquidity management.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Operating cycle: Acquire materials → Produce inventory → Sell → Collect cash. Cash flows and activities don't happen simultaneously — this timing gap requires financing.
  • ✅ CCC Formula: CCC = DOH + DSO − DPO. Measures days between paying suppliers and receiving customer cash. SHORT = good. NEGATIVE = best (customer cash received before supplier paid).
  • ✅ Short CCC → Low WC/Sales; Long CCC → High WC/Sales. Both measures are interrelated.
  • ✅ Total WC = Current Assets − Current Liabilities. Net WC excludes cash, marketable securities, and short-term debt from both sides (focuses on operational items only).
  • ✅ Reduce DOH: JIT, discontinue niche products, demand analytics. Reduce DSO: prompt-pay discounts, late fees, tighter credit. Increase DPO: negotiate longer supplier terms.
  • ✅ Prompt-payment discount: "2/10 net 30" means 2% discount if paid within 10 days, else full payment by day 30. Forgoing = implicitly borrowing from supplier at very high annualized rate (~37%). Better to borrow from bank and pay early.
  • ✅ Long CCC relative to peers or lengthening over time is a red flag: signals weaker demand, deteriorating customer credit, or lost supplier bargaining power.
  • ✅ Liquidity (asset): Nearness to cash. Order: Cash → ST securities → Receivables → Inventory → LT assets. Liquidity (issuer): ability to meet short-term liabilities.
  • ✅ 3 Primary liquidity sources: (1) Cash & marketable securities (2) Borrowings (3) Cash flow from operations. CFO = primary LONG-TERM source.
  • ✅ CFO formula: Cash from customers + Interest/dividends received − Cash to employees/suppliers − Taxes − Interest to lenders.
  • ✅ FCF = CFO − Capex. FCFF adds back interest paid (available to debt + equity investors).
  • ✅ Secondary liquidity sources signal distress: Suspend dividends, delay capex, issue equity (dilution!), renegotiate contracts, sell assets, file for bankruptcy.
  • ✅ Drags on liquidity = cash INFLOWS lag (uncollected receivables, obsolete inventory, borrowing constraints). Pulls on liquidity = cash OUTFLOWS accelerate (early payments, reduced credit limits, credit line limits, chronic low liquidity).
  • ✅ Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities. Broadest; includes all ST assets including inventory.
  • ✅ Quick Ratio = (Cash + ST Securities + Receivables) / Current Liabilities. Excludes inventory. More conservative.
  • ✅ Cash Ratio = (Cash + ST Securities) / Current Liabilities. Most conservative; excludes inventory AND receivables.
  • ✅ Conservative WC approach: More WC, long-term funding → highest cost, lowest rollover risk, most flexible. Best for: early-stage firms, rising rate environment.
  • ✅ Aggressive WC approach: Less WC, short-term funding → lowest cost, highest rollover risk, least flexible. Best for: low-margin industries, stable/falling rates, high sales forecast precision.
  • ✅ Moderate ("matched") WC approach: Permanent needs funded long-term, variable needs funded short-term. Balances cost and risk.
  • ✅ ST funding strategy goals: Diversify sources, adequate capacity, competitive rates, include both implicit (supplier discount forgone) and explicit costs.
  • ✅ Size matters for ST funding: Small firms → single bank. Large firms → commercial paper and capital markets. Financial institutions have unique access to central bank reserves.
  • ✅ Even profitable companies can fail from insufficient liquidity — profit ≠ cash flow. Liquidity management is a core finance function.
