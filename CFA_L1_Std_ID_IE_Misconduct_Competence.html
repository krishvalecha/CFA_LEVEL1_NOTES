CFA LEVEL 1 · STANDARD I · MODULE 3

Standards I(D): Misconduct & I(E): Competence

I(D) covers all professional conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit, and acts that reflect adversely on professional reputation, integrity, or competence — including the important distinction between professional and personal conduct. I(E) is a 2023 addition requiring members to act with and maintain competence necessary to fulfil their professional responsibilities. LES + Handbook synthesised.

I(D)

Standard I(D): Misconduct — The Standard

Standard I(D) — Misconduct

Members and Candidates must not engage in any professional conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, or deceit or commit any act that reflects adversely on their professional reputation, integrity, or competence.

I(D) differs from I(A) (which covers compliance with laws and regulations specifically) by addressing all professional conduct that reflects poorly on reputation, integrity, or competence — whether or not the conduct is illegal. Two categories:

  1. Professional conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, or deceit — lying, cheating, stealing, or other dishonest conduct IF the offense reflects adversely on professional activities
  2. Any act that reflects adversely on professional reputation, integrity, or competence — even if not technically dishonest (e.g., intoxication at work)
D2

What Constitutes "Professional Conduct" — The Handbook's Key Addition

The Handbook provides a significantly expanded definition of "professional activities" and "professional conduct" — which is the critical threshold for whether I(D) applies. Professional activity = any activity relating to financial analysis, investment management, security analysis, stewardship, or similar professional endeavours that:

  1. Involves activity or conduct in the workplace or academia, or participation in the investment profession or security markets, OR
  2. Explicitly or implicitly encompasses the use of the CFA charter or CIPM designation, membership in CFA Institute or local societies, or candidacy for a CFA Institute-sponsored designation
Professional Activities Include
  • Interactions with colleagues, employees, or clients in the workplace
  • Activities where the member represents as a CFA charterholder
  • Conduct as unpaid officer/volunteer with CFA Institute or local society
  • Conduct at CFA Institute, local society, or industry events
  • Writing about investments or markets in articles, books, message boards, or blogs outside the workplace
  • Communicating with government/regulators about matters within the scope of professional responsibilities
NOT Professional Activities (Personal Conduct)
  • Activities with no connection to employment or the investment profession
  • Activities that don't involve the CFA charter or CFA Institute affiliation
  • Personal civic or political activities not involving investment profession role
  • Acts tangential to employment that don't implicate CFA candidacy/membership (Tan parking permit example)
Key Examples from Handbook
  • Brink (charity board): serves on board because of CFA charter status → professional activity → I(D) applies to the deceptive pricing scheme
  • Mewis (ESG blog): investment-related analysis on a blog in her personal time, with employer permission → professional activity → I(D) applies to false statements
  • Nehjer (casino letter): retired CFA charterholder uses "CFA" designation when publishing falsified statistics on economic impact → professional activity via charter use
⚠️ Conduct Outside the Workplace Can Still Be Professional Conduct

The two "Mondelo" and "Tan" examples from the Handbook are designed to calibrate the line. Mondelo (violent protests at election, racist slurs, fire-setting) — deplorable conduct involving dishonesty (lying to investigators) — but NOT professional conduct because unrelated to his employment, investment profession participation, or CFA charter use. However, if convicted of a crime punishable by more than one year in prison, he could be summarily suspended under CFA Institute Rules. Tan (fraudulent disabled parking permit) — dishonest and involving false documentation — but acts are "at most tangential to his employment and do not implicate his participation in the CFA Program." Not I(D) because not professional conduct.

Examiner Focus — The Professional/Personal Conduct Line

The critical test is whether the conduct relates to financial analysis, investment management, or similar professional endeavours AND either (1) occurs in a workplace/academia/market context or (2) uses the CFA charter/affiliation. Hanse (civil disobedience at Extinction Rebellion) = NOT professional conduct — protests are personal beliefs, no dishonesty, fraud, or deceit involved, and location in the financial district doesn't create the professional link. Brink (charity board fraud) = PROFESSIONAL CONDUCT — she serves because of her CFA charter status and investment industry prominence. Key question: does the conduct involve dishonesty AND does it connect to professional activities?

Conduct That Reflects on Competence (Not Just Integrity)

I(D) covers acts that reflect adversely on competence, not just integrity. Example: Sasserman — excessive drinking at lunch and subsequent intoxication at work = I(D) violation because the conduct raises questions about his ability to fulfill professional responsibilities (competence), not just his honesty. Note: personal bankruptcy by itself does NOT reflect on integrity — but bankruptcy arising from fraudulent or deceitful business conduct DOES.

Absence of Appropriate Conduct = Violation

The Handbook notes that in some cases, the absence of appropriate conduct or lack of sufficient effort can be an I(D) violation. Investment professionals are expected to conduct necessary due diligence before making recommendations. Failing to do so — relying on others to perform steps they should perform themselves — may violate the trust clients have placed in them, damaging reputation and the operation of financial markets.

D3

The Civil Disobedience Exception — A Critical Bright Line

Both the LES and Handbook are explicit: "Standard I(D) is not meant to cover legal transgressions resulting from acts of civil disobedience in support of personal beliefs, because such conduct does not reflect poorly on the member's or candidate's professional reputation, integrity, or competence."

Garcia (Example 4 / LES) and Hanse (Module 5 LES) are both canonical examples: arrested multiple times for nonviolent protests/trespassing in support of environmental causes. These acts do not involve dishonesty, fraud, or deceit — and do not reflect adversely on professional competence or integrity.

Examiner Focus — Why Civil Disobedience Is NOT I(D)

Three elements must be present for I(D) to apply to personal conduct: (1) the conduct involves dishonesty, fraud, or deceit OR reflects adversely on professional reputation/integrity/competence, AND (2) the conduct is related to the member's professional activities or carries professional implications. Civil disobedience arrests typically satisfy neither: (1) no dishonesty, fraud, or deceit involved — the acts are openly done in public based on personal beliefs, and (2) no professional connection — the arrests reflect personal political convictions, not professional conduct. The key trap: the examiners may place protests in "the financial district" or near the member's workplace. Location does NOT create the professional connection.

D4

I(D) Worked Examples

Example 1 (LES)
Sasserman — Intoxicated at Work
VIOLATION
Sasserman, a trust investment officer, drinks heavily at lunch daily. Back at work after lunch, he is clearly intoxicated while making investment decisions. Colleagues distrust his afternoon judgment.
Violation of I(D). Excessive drinking at lunch and subsequent intoxication at work = conduct that has raised questions about his professionalism and competence. His behavior reflects poorly on him, his employer, and the investment industry.
🔑 Core Rule
I(D) covers conduct that damages competence — not just dishonesty. Intoxication impairs professional functioning. Note: drinking off-hours and off-duty in private would likely not be I(D). The violation is the intoxication DURING professional activities.
Examples 2 + 3 (LES)
Hoffman + Brink — Fraud in Workplace / Charity Board
VIOLATION
Hoffman: submits altered healthcare bills to his firm's insurance over two years. Brink: agrees to a price 20% above normal for charity vans, splits the surcharge with a friend (to repay a debt and compensate herself).
Both: violation of I(D). Hoffman: intentional fraud in the workplace adversely reflecting on integrity. Brink: her board service is a professional activity (serves because of CFA status/investment expertise) → dishonesty, fraud, and misrepresentation in that role = I(D) violation.
🔑 Core Rule
Brink is the key Handbook addition — distinguishing from the LES version where she was simply "an analyst." The Handbook stresses her volunteer board position is a professional activity because she serves on the board DUE TO her professional status as an experienced investment analyst and CFA charterholder. When conduct occurs in a professional capacity (even unpaid), I(D) applies.
Example 5 (Handbook-Only)
O'Hara — Sexual Harassment as CFA Society Volunteer
VIOLATION
O'Hara volunteers as an instructor at his local CFA society's exam review course. He engages in inappropriate behaviour with female candidates — suggestive remarks, unwanted touching, repeatedly asking candidates out on dates after being rebuffed.
Violation of I(D). O'Hara is engaging in sexual harassment that reflects poorly on his professional reputation and integrity. Although the actions don't take place in the workplace, they occur as part of his work with the local society and encompass the use of his charter and CFA Institute membership.
🔑 Core Rule
Volunteer work for CFA Institute or local societies = professional activity. Misconduct in that capacity = I(D) violation even though no employment relationship exists and no financial transaction is involved. The professional affiliation is the link.
Example 6 (Handbook-Only)
Nehjer — Retired CFA Charterholder Uses Charter on False Letter
VIOLATION
Retired Nehjer objects to a casino being built near her estate. She publishes an open letter with fabricated/altered statistics about economic impacts of gambling venues, signing with "PhD and CFA charterholder" to add credibility.
Violation of I(D). By engaging in fraud, dishonesty, and deceit using fabricated statistics AND by identifying herself as a CFA charterholder, she explicitly used her charter to give her analysis more credibility — making this professional conduct under I(D) even though she is retired.
🔑 Core Rule
Using the CFA designation in connection with an activity converts that activity into professional conduct. The fact that she is retired is irrelevant — she chose to invoke her professional credentials to lend weight to a communication that contained fabrications. The charter use is the professional link.
Example 5 (LES)
Rasmussen — Discovering Possible Fraud in Hedge Fund Reporting
MUST ESCALATE
Rasmussen, a buy-side trader, notices that the hedge fund she trades for is reporting performance that doesn't reflect a major market decline she witnessed. Her supervisor and the compliance officer both dismiss her concerns.
Rasmussen should pursue the issue by gathering proof and, if all internal communications fail to satisfy her concerns, consider reporting the potential unethical activity to the appropriate regulator. This also raises IV(A) (supervisor's whistleblowing obligations) and IV(C) (responsibilities of supervisors).
🔑 Core Rule
When internal escalation fails, the option to report to regulators remains — and in cases involving clear client harm, may be necessary. The supervisor and compliance officer's dismissal is itself problematic under IV(C). Rasmussen must gather evidence of her concerns before escalating externally.
I(E)

Standard I(E): Competence — The Standard

Standard I(E) — Competence (New in 2023)

Members and Candidates must act with and maintain the competence necessary to fulfill their professional responsibilities.

I(E) is a 2023 addition to the Standards, making explicit what was already implicit in the Code of Ethics requirement to "act with integrity, competence, and diligence" and "maintain and improve their professional competence." The standard has two elements:

  1. Act with competence — having sufficient knowledge, skills, and abilities to currently perform professional responsibilities at a professional level
  2. Maintain competence — ongoing obligation to continuously maintain or improve competence as roles and knowledge requirements evolve
💡 I(E) Does NOT Require

Standard I(E) does not mandate participation in any specific continuing education programme or professional development plan. There are many ways to achieve and maintain competence — formal CE, self-study, conferences, expert groups, peer consultation, professional certifications, internal training. The standard is flexible on method; it is not flexible on the outcome: you must actually be competent in your role.

E2

Knowledge, Skills, and Abilities — The Three Components

Knowledge

The body of information applied directly to the performance of a function and how effectively it is applied. Covers factual content, frameworks, regulatory understanding, and domain expertise required for the role.

Skills

Capabilities to perform role-specific acts or functions to complete specific tasks and achieve professional goals. Covers analytical capabilities, technical proficiencies, communication abilities, and role-specific execution competencies.

Abilities

Capabilities and attitudes that support behaviours resulting in observable outcomes. Covers judgment, decision-making ability, interpersonal skills, and the capacity to apply knowledge and skills effectively in context.

Key Principles of I(E)

  • Competence is role-specific: What constitutes competence varies by role and by the facts and circumstances of each member or candidate. There is no single definition.
  • Failure ≠ incompetence: Lack of competence cannot necessarily be determined by an unsuccessful or negative outcome. A competent investment manager may not always make profitable decisions. A competent analyst may not accurately predict future prospects despite diligent analysis.
  • Education level ≠ competence: A highly educated investment consultant may not have the experience to undertake consulting activities in an unfamiliar area. An accounting expert in financial statement audits may not be competent in performance presentation standards (GIPS). Education is necessary but not sufficient.
  • Roles change: When professional responsibilities change or expand, members must obtain new or different knowledge, skills, and abilities required by the new role — not just the old ones. (Evans example: promoted to director of research without developing management/supervisory competencies)
Examiner Focus — Mahajan (Module 5 LES) — Partial Competence

Mahajan takes a new role with two new dimensions: (1) new sectors (transportation, construction) and (2) first-time supervisory responsibility. He develops sector competence through courses and reading. He takes no steps to develop supervisory skills. Violation of I(E) on the supervisory dimension. The key principle: when a role requires multiple new competencies, the member must develop ALL of them — not just the ones that feel natural or familiar. Supervisory competence includes management training, compliance credentials, and internal training on managing subordinates — distinct from investment analysis competence.

Recommended Activities for Achieving and Maintaining Competence

Formal Pathways
  • Regularly engaging in professional development or CE programmes
  • Studying for or earning professional certifications or designations
  • Attending conferences, seminars, or webinars
  • Regularly participating in employer training
  • Earning compliance credentials (e.g., for supervisory roles)
Informal Pathways
  • Diligently engaging in self-study: outside reading of subject matter articles, treatises, and publications
  • Participating in expert groups or organisations
  • Consulting with experts in fields outside one's direct expertise
  • Becoming proficient with any new skill or knowledge as professional responsibilities change
E3

I(E) Worked Examples

Example 1
Lee — Trade Deal Expert: Staying Current
COMPLIANT
Lee runs a geopolitical consulting firm. A major multinational trade deal is ratified. Lee immediately downloads and reads the agreement, consults with experts to understand its implications, and produces an informed research report for her investment management firm clients.
Satisfied I(E). Lee maintained competence by staying current with developments in her field, independently studying relevant primary sources, and consulting experts to confirm her understanding. Her process ensures her knowledge is up to date before advising clients.
🔑 Core Rule
Staying current with relevant developments in your field = maintaining competence. This doesn't require formal CE — diligent independent research and expert consultation are sufficient. The key: the effort must be taken before advising clients, not after.
Example 2
Choe — Expanding into ESG Before Offering ESG Research
COMPLIANT
Choe is director of research. Clients ask for ESG ratings incorporated into research reports. She hires an experienced ESG analyst, requires all analysts (including herself) to attend ESG seminars and earn an ESG investment specialist certificate. Then they begin incorporating ESG into reports.
Satisfied I(E). Choe extended competence before offering the new service — she hired for the skill gap, obtained external education, and only then incorporated ESG. The key: competence was built first, then deployed.
🔑 Core Rule
When expanding into new service areas, build competence before offering the service — not after. Choe didn't attempt to incorporate ESG analysis with her existing knowledge and learn as she went — she filled the knowledge gap systematically first.
Example 3
Glusker — Passing Licensing Exam Without Learning the Material
VIOLATION
Glusker is a regulatory attorney transitioning to a sales role. There is a licensing exam covering client fairness rules and regulations he needs to pass. He was never asked to cover these rules as an attorney and doesn't know them. He doesn't study or learn the material — relies on his general industry knowledge to pass.
Violated I(E). Passing the exam alone is not adequate to demonstrate competency. Glusker's failure to actually learn the rules and regulations relating to fairness when dealing with clients means he doesn't possess the required knowledge for his new role, despite having passed the licensing examination.
🔑 Core Rule
Compliance with licensing requirements is necessary but not sufficient for competence. The standard is actual knowledge, skills, and abilities — not mere credentialling. Passing an exam by guessing/relying on general knowledge without truly understanding the material = I(E) violation.
Example 5
Mifune — Thorough Research, Unforeseen Fraud
NOT A VIOLATION
Mifune recommends Vouchsafe Insurance based on high credit rating, strong financials, and a decade of history. Within 18 months, Vouchsafe goes bankrupt due to previously unknown CFO fraud. Clients claim he was incompetent.
Not a violation. Assuming Mifune's research of Vouchsafe was thorough and appropriate, his failure to uncover fraudulent practices that had misled the entire investment industry does not, by itself, indicate incompetence. A negative outcome from a professionally conducted process ≠ incompetence.
🔑 Core Rule
Bad outcomes do not prove incompetence. Competent professionals can make recommendations that turn out badly due to factors beyond reasonable discovery. The test is the quality of the process, not the outcome. This is the I(E) analogue of the "best interests" standard under III(A) — process-based, not outcome-based.
Example 6
Halsey — Investing in Cryptocurrency Without Understanding It
VIOLATION
Halsey is a portfolio manager whose clients urge him to invest in cryptocurrency. He has read some newspaper articles about it but is unfamiliar with the nature of the asset or the differences between cryptocurrency products. Not wanting to seem out of touch or delay, he hastily buys a cryptocurrency product heavily promoted through social media for several clients.
Violation of I(E). Halsey is not competent to invest in cryptocurrency for his clients. By hastily investing without understanding the nature of the investment or its suitability, Halsey violated I(E).
🔑 Core Rule
Client pressure to invest in an asset class the member doesn't understand = the member must first build competence before acting. Fear of seeming "out of touch" or losing clients is not a justification for proceeding without the required knowledge. Competence must precede action.

Combined Exam Checklist — Standards I(D) and I(E)

Standard I(D): Misconduct

  • ✅ Two triggers: (1) Professional conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, or deceit. (2) Any act that reflects adversely on professional reputation, integrity, OR competence.
  • ✅ "Professional activities" defined broadly: Workplace conduct + academia + investment profession participation + use of CFA charter/CFA Institute affiliation — even when unpaid or retired.
  • ✅ Using the CFA charter in any context makes that context a professional activity — including in published letters, blogs, or public statements while retired.
  • ✅ Volunteer work for CFA Institute/local societies = professional activity. Misconduct in that context = I(D) violation.
  • ✅ Civil disobedience exception: Legal transgressions resulting from acts of civil disobedience in support of personal beliefs generally do NOT violate I(D) — no dishonesty, fraud, or deceit involved; does not reflect adversely on professional competence or integrity.
  • ✅ Location doesn't create professional connection. Protests in "the financial district" ≠ professional conduct unless the member is acting in a professional capacity.
  • ✅ Competence-based violations: Intoxication at work, failing to perform necessary due diligence, or other conduct that damages professional functioning can violate I(D) even without dishonesty.
  • ✅ Personal bankruptcy does NOT violate I(D) unless it involves fraudulent or deceitful business conduct.
  • ✅ Absence of appropriate conduct can violate I(D) — failing to do necessary due diligence when clients rely on you to do so.
  • ✅ I(D) ≠ I(A): I(A) addresses compliance with laws/regulations governing professional activities. I(D) addresses ALL professional conduct reflecting on reputation/integrity/competence, whether or not it violates a law.

Standard I(E): Competence

  • ✅ New 2023 standard. Makes explicit the competence obligation from the Code of Ethics. Two-part: act with competence AND maintain competence.
  • ✅ Competence is role-specific: Different criteria for different roles based on facts and circumstances. No single universal definition.
  • ✅ Failure ≠ incompetence. Competent professionals can have bad outcomes. The test is the quality of the process, not the outcome. (Mifune/Vouchsafe example)
  • ✅ Education level ≠ competence. A highly educated person may lack experience in a specific area. A licensed professional may lack knowledge of specific regulations.
  • ✅ Passing a licensing exam ≠ competence if the underlying material wasn't actually learned. (Glusker example)
  • ✅ Role changes require new competencies — must develop knowledge, skills, and abilities for ALL dimensions of the new role, not just familiar ones. (Mahajan, Evans examples)
  • ✅ Build competence before acting in a new area — not after. Client pressure does not justify proceeding without required knowledge. (Halsey/cryptocurrency)
  • ✅ No specific CE programme required. Many pathways: formal CE, professional certifications, conferences, self-study, expert consultation, employer training, expert groups.
  • ✅ Ongoing obligation: Must continuously maintain or improve competence — it is not a one-time achievement. Competence must keep pace with evolving professional responsibilities.
  • ✅ I(E) connects to I(A): The Handbook notes that maintaining knowledge of applicable laws is also required under I(E) competence, not just I(A). Failure to stay current on regulations = both I(A) and I(E) issue.

Top I(D) exam traps:
(1) "She was protesting in the financial district so it relates to her financial work" — location alone doesn't create professional connection
(2) "He's retired so the CFA charter doesn't apply" — retired charterholders who invoke the charter = professional activity
(3) "The firm approved his volunteer role so no liability" — firm approval doesn't override individual I(D) obligation
(4) "The bankruptcy was unfortunate but not dishonest" — correct, unless the bankruptcy itself involved fraudulent conduct

Top I(E) exam traps:
(1) "He passed the licensing exam, so he has demonstrated competence" — passing ≠ competence if material wasn't learned
(2) "The investment lost money so she was incompetent" — negative outcomes don't prove incompetence
(3) "He must enrol in a formal CE programme to comply" — no specific programme required; many pathways
(4) "She studied the new sector so her role change is covered" — must study ALL new dimensions (including supervisory responsibilities, not just technical content)
