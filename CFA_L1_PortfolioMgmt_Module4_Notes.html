The Investment Policy Statement — Purpose & Components

Investment Policy Statement (IPS)
A written planning document governing the portfolio management process. Describes the client's investment objectives and the constraints that apply to the client's portfolio. The starting point of the portfolio management process. Without a full understanding of the client's situation and requirements, successful results are unlikely.

Why Have a Written IPS? — Reasons

  1. Standard procedure: every investment manager should have a well-constructed IPS for all clients
  2. Legal/regulatory requirement: in some countries, the IPS (or equivalent) is mandatory. Examples:
    • UK Pensions Act 1995 (S.35): pension schemes must have a "statement of investment principles"
    • UK FSA: "know your customer" requirements
    • EU MiFID: assign clients to categories (eligible counterparties, institutional, retail) — category determines protections
  3. Blueprint for decision making: enables the manager to assess whether any investment is suitable for the client
  4. Governance framework: for institutions (e.g., pension plans, endowments) it can set governance arrangements — investment committee powers, manager appointment process, discretion levels
  5. Dispute resolution: good recordkeeping may be crucial if the client relationship comes into dispute

When to Review and Update the IPS

  • Regularly: at least every 3 years (UK Pensions Regulator guideline)
  • When material changes occur: change in client objectives, time horizon, liquidity needs, or circumstances
  • On client's initiative: whenever client's own goals or situation changes significantly

💡 The Most Important Sections

The sections most closely linked to client's distinctive needs are Objectives (risk + return) and Constraints (LTTLU). An IPS focused on just these two is called the "objectives and constraints" format — this is the standard exam focus.

Key principle: The IPS must achieve internal consistency:

  • Risk objectives ↔ Return objectives must be consistent (high return requires high risk)
  • Return objectives ↔ Constraints must be consistent
  • Risk objectives ↔ Constraints must be consistent
  • All three must align with current market environment (15% return impossible when inflation is 3%)

Risk Objectives — Absolute vs. Relative

Risk Objectives
Specifications for portfolio risk that reflect the client's risk tolerance. Can be stated on an absolute basis, a relative basis, or a combination of both. The IPS must state the risk tolerance of the client clearly.
Absolute Risk Objectives

Stated without reference to any external benchmark. Self-standing standards.

  • "Do not suffer any loss of capital"
  • "Do not lose more than X% in any 12-month period"
  • More operational version: probability statement — "With 95% probability, the portfolio shall not lose more than 4% in any 12-month period"

Measures: variance/standard deviation, Value at Risk (VaR)

Relative Risk Objectives

Stated relative to one or more benchmarks.

  • Large-cap UK equities → FTSE 100
  • Large-cap US equities → S&P 500
  • Cash-like → T-bill rate
  • Policy portfolio → band of ±X% around the benchmark return

Measure: tracking risk / tracking error

Liability-Driven Investment (LDI)
An approach that takes both assets AND liabilities into account when setting risk objectives. Used when the size, timing, and/or relative certainty of future financial obligations is known. Examples: life insurance companies, DB pension plans, individual post-retirement budgets. LDI risk objective: minimize the probability of failing to meet future liability payments. LDI return objective: outperform the discount rate used to find the present value of liabilities.
💡 Making Risk Objectives "More Operational"

Stating a risk objective as a probability statement makes it measurable and actionable. "Never lose money" is not operational — you can't know if you've met it without a probability. "With 95% confidence, annual loss not exceeding 4%" IS operational — it can be backtested and monitored using tools like VaR.

Risk Tolerance — Willingness vs. Ability

A client's overall risk tolerance is determined by two distinct components:

Ability to Bear Risk (Objective)
  • Based on objective financial factors
  • Time horizon (longer → greater ability)
  • Expected income and job stability
  • Level of wealth relative to liabilities
  • Assets comfortably exceeding liabilities → HIGH ability
  • Example: 20-year horizon has greater ability than 2-year horizon (time to recover from losses)
  • Example: wealthy individual who remains comfortable after large loss → high ability
Willingness to Take Risk (Subjective)
  • Based on psychological and subjective factors
  • Personality type, self-esteem, inclination to independent thinking
  • Personal comfort or distress with financial uncertainty
  • Measured via: discussion with client, psychometric questionnaires (e.g., Grable's 5- or 13-item scale)
  • Grable 5-item questionnaire: scores 5-20; higher = more risk tolerant; mean ≈ 12.86 (university sample)

Resolving Conflicts Between Ability and Willingness

  • Ability low, willingness high (top-right): Risk tolerance is below average. Investor wants to take more risk than they can afford. Use the LOWER (ability) factor. Prevent overconfident risk-taking.
  • Ability high, willingness low (bottom-left): Risk tolerance is still constrained. Adviser may counsel client — explain WHY they have high ability, outline the cost of not taking risk (reduced expected return). But: adviser must NOT attempt to change the client's willingness to take risk if the low willingness is a genuine personality trait (not based on misperception). Use the LOWER factor.
  • Golden rule: when ability and willingness differ, the prudent approach is to use the LOWER of the two and document the decision.
⚠️ Critical Exam Point — The "Lower of Two" Rule

When ability and willingness conflict, ALWAYS use the lower of the two to set risk tolerance. This protects the client. Modifying a client's genuine personality-based willingness to take risk is outside the investment adviser's role. Only if the low willingness is based on a misperception or miscalculation should the adviser try to educate the client.

💡 Institutional Conflict Example

DB pension plan: Trustees (fiduciary duty to beneficiaries) may want LOW risk to protect funding status. Plan sponsor may want HIGH risk to reduce future funding costs. When interests conflict, the trustee must act in the best interests of the BENEFICIARIES — not the sponsor. The fiduciary duty to beneficiaries overrides the sponsor's preferences.

04

Return Objectives

Absolute Return Objectives
  • Achieve a specific percentage rate of return
  • Stated as nominal OR real (inflation-adjusted)
  • Required return: minimum needed to meet a specific future goal (e.g., retirement income)
Relative Return Objectives
  • Outperform a specific benchmark
  • E.g., "outperform the S&P 500 by 1% per year"
  • Peer group benchmarks (e.g., top quartile among PE universe)
  • Problem with peer benchmarks: limited information, not investable, impossible for all to be "above average"

Key Requirements for Return Objectives

  • Realistic: consistent with current market environment (15% return unrealistic in a 3% inflation world)
  • Consistent with risk objective: high return requires accepting high risk
  • Clearly stated basis: nominal vs. real; gross vs. net of fees; pre-tax vs. after-tax
  • Taxable investors: baseline is to state returns on an after-tax basis
  • A good benchmark should be investable (can be replicated) — peer benchmarks typically are NOT investable
⚠️ Exam Trap — Nominal vs. Real Return

Nominal return = inflation included. Real return = inflation-adjusted (what the portfolio actually buys in purchasing power). Both can appear in IPS goals. Be careful: the adviser must be sure the client and manager agree on which basis is being used. Real returns relate better to the actual investment objective but nominal is easier to measure. Always check the exam question's basis before calculating.

The Five Investment Constraints — LTTLU

💡 Mnemonic — "LTTLU" for the 5 Constraints

Liquidity · Time horizon · Tax concerns · Legal and regulatory factors · Unique circumstances. These are the five standard IPS constraint categories — every exam on constraints refers to these five.

① Liquidity Requirements

Definition: The likelihood and amount of withdrawals needed from the portfolio in the near term.

Individual examples: healthcare payments, tuition fees, major purchases

Institutional examples: endowment spending rules, P&C insurance claims, pension benefit payments

Implication: Allocate part of the portfolio to liquid, low-risk assets to cover near-term needs with known value at the time of need (e.g., a bond maturing when tuition is due). Higher liquidity needs → larger cash/short-term fixed income allocation → less available for higher-return, illiquid investments.

② Time Horizon

Definition: The period over which the portfolio is investing before assets need to be withdrawn OR before circumstances change significantly.

Example: 55-year-old planning to retire at 65 → 10-year horizon (portfolio structure may need to change at retirement, even if not fully liquidated)

Implications for asset choice:

  • Short horizon: illiquid or highly risky investments UNSUITABLE (not enough time to recover losses)
  • Long horizon: illiquid/risky investments suitable (expected higher returns justify the illiquidity)

Time horizon and liquidity requirements are closely linked — but they are distinct constraints.

③ Tax Concerns

Key principle: Portfolio should reflect the tax status of the client. Taxable investors and tax-exempt investors should be managed differently.

  • Tax-exempt investors (e.g., many pension funds, endowments): indifferent to form of returns (income vs. capital gains)
  • Taxable investors: typically prefer capital gains over income (income usually taxed at higher rates; capital gains may be taxed at lower rates or deferred)
  • US-specific: taxable investors often include municipal bonds (interest exempt from federal and often state taxes)
  • Capital gains deferral: gains taxed when REALIZED (not accrued) → time-value-of-money benefit vs. income taxed as earned
  • Tax-loss harvesting: selling securities at a loss to offset capital gains; implemented by robo-advisers
④ Legal and Regulatory Factors

The IPS must state any legal/regulatory restrictions constraining the portfolio. Two main areas:

  • Institutional restrictions: some countries restrict pension fund asset allocation (e.g., limit on equity %, overseas investment %). US has NO such limits but many other countries do. Also, "self-investment limits" — restrictions on investing in securities issued by the plan sponsor (to prevent conflict of interest)
  • Individual restrictions (insider trading): If an individual has access to material nonpublic information about a security, this is a constraint. Example: company directors may be restricted from trading the company's stock during "blackout periods" before earnings releases. The IPS must note this so the portfolio manager doesn't inadvertently trade those securities.
⑤ Unique Circumstances (Including ESG)

Any other aspect of the client's circumstances, beliefs, or values that materially impacts portfolio composition.

  • Religious beliefs: Shari'a-compliant investing (avoid interest-bearing instruments, gambling — e.g., no bonds, no casinos)
  • Socially responsible investing (SRI): exclude certain products (weapons, tobacco, gambling) or practices (environmental damage, poor labor standards)
  • ESG considerations: environmental, social, governance factors (discussed in depth in Section 10)
  • Human capital overlap: an employee concentrated in employer stock should avoid MORE exposure to that stock in their portfolio (double exposure to same entity)
  • Income-industry overlap: a stockbroker's income falls when equities decline → should hold relatively LOW equity weight (income is already equity-correlated)
  • Entrepreneur: reluctant to invest in competitors or businesses with similar risk exposures to their venture

Gathering Client Information

KYC requirement: EU MiFID II requires substantial fact-finding from financial intermediaries — including in advisory AND execution-only relationships (not just full-service wealth management).

Data to gather from individuals:

  • Family situation and employment (including spouse/dependents)
  • Health of client and dependents
  • Financial information (assets, liabilities, income, expenses, goals)

Data to gather from institutions:

  • Key stakeholders and their perspectives/requirements
  • Governance structure
  • Asset-liability management considerations
  • Legal, tax, and regulatory situation
💡 Why Good Recordkeeping Matters

Accurate recordkeeping of client information gathered during the IPS process is crucial because it may be the only evidence available if the client relationship comes into dispute. "Know your customer" is not just best practice — it protects both parties legally.

Strategic Asset Allocation (SAA) & Capital Market Expectations

Strategic Asset Allocation (SAA) / Policy Portfolio
The set of long-term percentage allocations to IPS-permissible asset classes that is expected to achieve the client's long-term objectives given their risk profile and constraints. Also called the "policy portfolio." It defines the baseline exposures to systematic risk factors. May include hedge ratios for currency, interest rate, and inflation risk.
Capital Market Expectations
The investor's expectations about the risk and return prospects of asset classes. Quantified as: expected returns, standard deviations, and correlations among asset class pairs. These are combined with the IPS (objectives + constraints) to produce the SAA.

How the SAA is Formed

Inputs (two streams):

  1. Investment Objectives & Constraints (IPS): risk tolerance, return requirements, liquidity, time horizon, tax, legal, unique circumstances
  2. Capital Market Expectations: expected returns, standard deviations, and correlations for each asset class (historically estimated, economic analysis, or valuation models)

Process: Optimization (mean-variance analysis) or simulation → produces efficient frontier → overlay investor's indifference curves → point of tangency = optimal SAA.

Portfolio Return and Risk (Used for SAA Optimization)
E(Rp) = Σ wi E(Ri) [weighted average of asset class expected returns]

σp = √[Σ Σ wp,i wp,j Cov(Ri, Rj)]

Cov(Ri, Rj) = ρi,j × σi × σj
Same portfolio math as covered in Module 1 — now applied to ASSET CLASSES rather than individual securities.
Key difference: we're now allocating among asset classes (e.g., equities, bonds, alternatives) rather than individual stocks.
Adding low-correlation asset classes improves the efficient frontier — lowers portfolio risk for each return level.
Utility Function for SAA Optimization
Up = E(Rp) − λσ²p
Up = investor's expected utility from the portfolio
E(Rp) = expected return of portfolio
σp = standard deviation of portfolio returns
λ = measure of investor's risk aversion (higher λ → more risk averse)

Note: uses σ² (variance) NOT σ, similar to U = E(r) − ½Aσ² from Module 1 (with λ = A/2).
Optimal SAA = asset allocation that maximizes this utility function.
💡 What Changes the SAA?

Two things can move the optimal SAA:

1. Capital market expectations change → moves the efficient frontier → tangency point shifts → SAA adjusts.

2. Client objectives/constraints change → indifference curves change shape/location → tangency point shifts → SAA adjusts.

This is why the IPS must be reviewed regularly — changes in either the market or the client require SAA reconsideration.

Asset Class Criteria — Defining Good Asset Classes

How asset classes should be defined for SAA purposes. Four key criteria:

  1. Relatively homogeneous: assets within the class should have similar risk-return characteristics
  2. High within-class correlations: assets within the same class should move similarly
  3. Low cross-class correlations: assets in different classes should have lower correlations with each other → enables diversification across classes
  4. Mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive: classes should not overlap, but together should cover the relevant investable universe
💡 Why Subdivide Asset Classes?

Finer subdivision gives MORE control over risk-return characteristics. Example: instead of one "bonds" class, using government bonds (domestic), government bonds (foreign), investment-grade corporates, and high-yield corporates gives four classes with distinct risk-return profiles. Correlations among these sub-classes can be explicitly estimated and managed.

Practical reason: asset class definitions often align with how investment managers are actually hired and organized (e.g., separate EM equity manager vs. domestic equity manager).

Common Benchmarks for Asset Classes

  • Asset class benchmarks typically provided by FTSE, MSCI, Bloomberg
  • ESG screens (negative screening, best-in-class) can restrict the investable universe → standard benchmarks may not apply → need ESG-specific benchmarks from the same providers
  • Benchmark should be investable: capable of being replicated by the investor
⚠️ Peer Benchmarks Are Problematic

Setting return objectives relative to a peer group (e.g., "top 50% of endowments") has three problems: (1) limited information about peers' strategies and methodologies, (2) it is impossible for all institutions to be above average, (3) peer benchmarks are NOT investable. A good benchmark must be investable — the manager can actually replicate it.

Portfolio Construction Principles

Three Sources of Portfolio Return

Strategic Asset Allocation (SAA)

Long-run policy weights across asset classes based on IPS. Provides exposure to systematic risk factors. This is the primary driver of long-run portfolio performance.

Tactical Asset Allocation (TAA)

Deliberate short-term deviation from SAA policy weights to exploit near-term return forecasts. E.g., temporarily overweighting equities if you expect them to outperform soon.

Security Selection

Selecting securities with higher expected return than the asset class benchmark. E.g., overweighting IBM vs. benchmark if you expect IBM to outperform.

Risk Budgeting
The process of deciding the overall amount of risk to assume in the portfolio (the risk budget) and then subdividing that risk across the three sources: SAA, TAA, and security selection. Sets risk limits and desired payoffs for each activity. Total risk = SAA risk + TAA risk + security selection risk.

Active vs. Passive Management Decision

Feature Active Management Passive Management
Goal Outperform benchmark through TAA and security selection Match/replicate benchmark return
Return source SAA + TAA + security selection SAA only (systematic risk)
Costs Higher (research, trading, management) Lower
Long-run average result Average active manager UNDERPERFORMS market net of costs Closely tracks market minus small tracking costs
Security selection Zero-sum game before costs; negative-sum game net of costs Not applicable (hold market weights)
When active adds value Skilled managers in INEFFICIENT markets Efficient markets (especially large-cap US equities)
⚠️ Security Selection Is a Zero-Sum Game

In aggregate, all active investors in an asset class earn the market return (gross). One investor's gain above market = another's loss below market. After costs, the average active investor UNDERPERFORMS the passive investor. This does NOT mean all active managers underperform — some skilled managers consistently beat benchmarks. But on average, active underperforms passive NET of fees.

Drift and Rebalancing

  • Drift: over time, as asset class prices change, portfolio weights drift away from the SAA policy weights
  • Rebalancing policy: a set of rules to restore the portfolio to policy weights. Two trigger types:
    • Calendar rebalancing: rebalance at fixed intervals (monthly, quarterly, annually)
    • Threshold/bandwidth rebalancing: rebalance when a weight deviates beyond a specified corridor from the target weight
  • Rebalancing is part of the Feedback step in the portfolio management process
  • Even absent a formal risk budget, a rebalancing policy is an essential element of risk management

New Developments in Portfolio Management

ETFs + Robo-Advice

ETFs provide inexpensive, liquid exposure to asset classes. Robo-advisers use algorithms to provide automated asset allocation, rebalancing, and tax-loss harvesting. Together, they reduce costs for retail investors to build well-diversified portfolios.

Risk Parity Investing

Weights asset classes by their risk contribution rather than by market value or return forecasts. Argues that traditional portfolios are dominated by equity risk (60%+ equity by weight = even more by risk contribution due to equity volatility). After GFC, risk parity results partly explained by bond market tailwind (declining rates).

ESG Considerations in Portfolio Planning & Construction

ESG investing: nearly US$31 trillion of AUM dedicated to responsible investment mandates at start of 2018. ESG integration is now mainstream — not just limited to specialized mandates.

Drivers of ESG integration: scarcity of natural resources, climate change, demographic trends, diversity/inclusion, rise of social media scrutiny.

★ The Six ESG Investment Approaches ★

① Negative Screening
Excluding companies or sectors from the investable universe based on business activities or ESG concerns. Example: exclude tobacco companies, weapons manufacturers, gambling, countries with human rights violations. Most common ESG approach historically. Also called "exclusionary screening."
② Positive Screening (Best-in-Class)
Including sectors or companies based on specific ESG criteria, typically ESG performance relative to industry peers. Select the "best ESG performers" within each industry — doesn't exclude entire sectors, just rewards leaders. Example: include only the top 25% ESG-rated companies in each industry.
③ ESG Integration
Systematic consideration of material ESG factors in traditional asset allocation, security selection, and portfolio construction. Treats ESG as a process enhancement — factoring in ESG alongside financial analysis. NOT a separate strategy; ESG data is incorporated into valuation models and risk assessment. Most widely adopted approach in mainstream funds.
④ Thematic Investing
Investing in themes or assets specifically related to ESG factors. Example: clean energy funds, water infrastructure funds, affordable housing investments. Requires specialist managers who can identify thematic opportunities. Important to manage impact on total portfolio risk-return profile.
⑤ Engagement / Active Ownership
Using shareholder power to influence corporate behavior toward ESG objectives alongside financial returns. Includes: exercising voting rights, filing shareholder proposals, engaging with management through direct conversations. Collaborative engagement (multiple investors acting together) has gained popularity — easier to gain management's attention collectively. Example: Climate Action 100+ (100+ carbon emitters, two-thirds of global industrial emissions).
⑥ Impact Investing
Investments made with the intention to generate positive, measurable social and environmental impact alongside a financial return. Goes beyond avoiding harm — actively seeks to create measurable positive outcomes. Most focused/intentional ESG approach.
💡 Mnemonic — "NPETEI" for the Six ESG Approaches

Negative screening · Positive screening · ESG integration · Thematic investing · Engagement/active ownership · Impact investing. From most exclusionary (negative) to most intentional (impact).

ESG Impact on Portfolio Construction

  • Negative screening restricts the universe → potentially impacts expected return and risk estimates. Standard benchmarks may not be appropriate → need ESG-adjusted benchmarks
  • ESG integration is viewed as process enhancement; managers expected to beat REGULAR benchmarks (not just ESG-adjusted ones)
  • Engagement requires strong client-manager cooperation on voting rights, shareholder proposals, and dialogue with companies. Either delegated to manager or handled directly by client (or via proxy agent)
  • Impact investing requires intentional selection of opportunities with positive measurable outcomes
  • Academic research mixed on ESG impact on returns — but ESG continues rapid adoption (now $31T in AUM)

Key ESG data/metrics used in integration:

  • Environmental: carbon footprint, energy usage, water consumption, waste management
  • Social: employee turnover, lost-time injuries and fatalities, diversity, absenteeism
  • Governance: executive salaries and bonuses, board independence, shareholder rights

Key frameworks/standards: Principles of Responsible Investment (PRI), UN Global Compact, OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises

  • ✅ Negative screening: EXCLUDE based on business activity or ESG concerns (tobacco, weapons, gambling).
  • ✅ Positive screening: INCLUDE best ESG performers within each industry (best-in-class).
  • ✅ ESG integration: systematic incorporation of ESG into TRADITIONAL financial analysis. Most widely adopted mainstream approach. Managers expected to beat REGULAR benchmarks.
  • ✅ Impact investing: invest to generate positive, MEASURABLE social/environmental impact alongside financial return — the most intentional ESG approach.
  • ✅ Engagement/active ownership: use shareholder power (voting, proposals, management dialogue) to influence company ESG behavior. Collaborative engagement (multiple investors) is growing. Climate Action 100+ = 100+ carbon emitters.
  • ✅ ESG restricts universe: negative screening limits investable universe → standard benchmarks may not apply → need ESG-adjusted benchmarks from FTSE/MSCI/Bloomberg.
  • ✅ ESG academic evidence mixed but adoption is massive (~$31T in AUM). Not yet settled whether ESG hurts or helps returns.

σp = √[Σ Σ wi wj Cov(Ri, Rj)] [portfolio std dev]
Cov(Ri, Rj) = ρi,j × σi × σj
