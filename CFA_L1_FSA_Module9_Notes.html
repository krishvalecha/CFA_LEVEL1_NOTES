01

Core Concepts & Definitions

Income tax is complex because companies follow two separate sets of rules simultaneously: accounting standards (IFRS / US GAAP) for their financial statements, and tax laws for their tax returns. These rules frequently diverge, producing differences that must be carefully tracked on the balance sheet as deferred tax assets and liabilities.

The Four Key Tax Concepts

TermDefinitionWhere It Appears
Accounting Profit Pre-tax income computed under IFRS/US GAAP. Also called "income before taxes" or "pretax income." Does NOT include tax expense. Income Statement
Taxable Income Income computed under the relevant jurisdiction's tax laws. This is the base for calculating taxes owed. May differ significantly from accounting profit. Tax return (not FS)
Taxes Payable Taxes owed to the government based on taxable income × statutory rate. A current liability on the balance sheet. The cash obligation for the current period. Balance Sheet (current liability)
Income Tax Expense The tax charge on the income statement computed using accounting profit. Equals taxes payable ± changes in deferred tax assets/liabilities. Follows the matching principle. Income Statement
The Bridge — From Taxes Payable to Tax Expense
Income Tax Expense (IS) = Taxes Payable + ΔDTL − ΔDTA

Equivalently:
Tax Expense = Current Tax + Deferred Tax

Where:
Current Tax = Taxable Income × Statutory Rate (= Taxes Payable)
Deferred Tax = Change in Net DTA/DTL balance during the period
If temporary differences cause taxable income < accounting profit: taxes payable < tax expense. The shortfall is recorded as a deferred tax liability. If taxable income > accounting profit: taxes payable > tax expense. The excess already paid is recorded as a deferred tax asset.

Tax Base vs. Carrying Amount

The tax base of an asset or liability is its value for tax purposes. The carrying amount is its value in the financial statements (book value). When these two differ for the same item, a deferred tax asset or liability arises.

The difference (carrying amount − tax base) is the temporary difference. Its direction determines whether a DTA or DTL is created.

Examiner Focus — Core Concepts

(1) Accounting profit ≠ taxable income — learn to calculate both. (2) Tax expense ≠ taxes payable — the difference is deferred tax. (3) Tax expense follows accounting profit (matching principle). Taxes payable follow the tax return. (4) The exam will test whether you can identify which line on the IS is tax expense and which is on the B/S as taxes payable.

02

Temporary vs. Permanent Differences

Temporary Differences
  • Differences between tax base and carrying amount that will reverse in future periods
  • Create deferred tax assets or deferred tax liabilities on the B/S
  • The total tax paid over the asset/liability's life is ultimately the same — just the timing differs
  • Examples: Accelerated depreciation for tax vs. straight-line for accounting; warranties expensed for accounting but only deductible when paid for tax; advance payments taxable when received but recognized as revenue later for accounting
  • Both taxable and deductible temporary differences are subcategories
Permanent Differences
  • Differences that will never reverse — arise because an item is treated one way for accounting and permanently excluded/included for tax
  • Do NOT create deferred tax assets or liabilities
  • Cause a lasting wedge between effective tax rate and statutory rate
  • Examples: Tax-exempt interest income (e.g., municipal bond interest); non-deductible fines and penalties; tax credits (e.g., R&D tax credits, solar investment credits); dividends from subsidiaries that are non-taxable
  • Identified in the effective tax rate reconciliation note disclosure

Two Types of Temporary Differences → Two Types of Deferred Tax

TypeWhen It Arises (Asset)When It Arises (Liability)Creates
Taxable Temporary Difference Carrying amount > Tax base Tax base > Carrying amount Deferred Tax Liability (DTL)
Deductible Temporary Difference Tax base > Carrying amount Carrying amount > Tax base Deferred Tax Asset (DTA)
💡 Memory Aid — DTL vs. DTA Direction

DTL (Deferred Tax Liability): Asset carrying amount > tax base → you've claimed less expense for tax so far → you'll pay MORE tax later → future obligation = liability.
DTA (Deferred Tax Asset): Asset tax base > carrying amount → you've already paid more tax than the accounting expense → you get a future tax benefit = asset. Think of DTA as "prepaid tax."

Common Examples — Sorted by Type

Creates DTL (pay less tax now, more later)
  • Accelerated depreciation for tax — tax expense > accounting expense early on → taxable income < accounting profit → owe less now, more later
  • Development costs capitalized for accounting but expensed for tax → carrying amount > tax base
  • Accounts receivable recognized as income for accounting but taxed only on cash receipt → carrying amount > tax base
  • Instalment sales where profit recognized upfront for accounting but spread for tax
Creates DTA (pay more tax now, less later)
  • Warranty liabilities — expense recognized for accounting when product sold; deductible for tax only when actually paid
  • Revenue received in advance — taxable when received; recognized as revenue later for accounting → carrying amount > tax base of liability
  • Tax loss carryforwards — prior year losses that can reduce future taxable income
  • Research costs expensed for accounting but with a tax base that allows future deduction
  • Restructuring charges accrued for accounting but tax-deductible only when paid
⚠️ Permanent Differences — The Effective Rate Trap

Tax-exempt income (e.g., municipal bond interest received) lowers the effective tax rate permanently below the statutory rate. Non-deductible fines and penalties raise it above the statutory rate. Since these never reverse, they create a persistent divergence — not a timing issue. Analysts must distinguish permanent from temporary effects in the rate reconciliation.

03

Deferred Tax Liabilities — Deep Dive

A Deferred Tax Liability (DTL) arises when a company has paid less tax than its accounting tax expense would imply. It represents taxes that will be owed in the future when the temporary difference reverses. DTLs are the most common deferred tax item and arise most frequently from accelerated depreciation for tax purposes.

DTL Calculation
DTL = (Carrying Amount − Tax Base) × Tax Rate [for an asset where CA > TB]
DTL = (Tax Base − Carrying Amount) × Tax Rate [for a liability where TB > CA]

Annual Change in DTL = Current Year DTL − Prior Year DTL
This change in DTL is added to taxes payable to get income tax expense on IS.
If the statutory tax rate changes, the recorded value of DTLs must be remeasured at the new rate. A rate reduction → DTL decreases → deferred tax benefit (income) on the IS in that period.

Analyst Treatment of DTLs

When analyzing DTLs, the key question is: Will this DTL ever actually reverse?

  • If reversal is likely (e.g., asset will eventually be sold or fully depreciated): treat DTL as a real liability. Include in debt for leverage ratios.
  • If reversal is unlikely (e.g., the company continuously replaces assets — so accelerated dep. DTL never reverses because new assets keep creating new DTLs): the DTL is effectively permanent. Many analysts treat it as equity in this case, or at minimum discount it heavily.
  • If reversal timing is uncertain: some analysts use a middle ground — include it at a discounted value or exclude it from short-term liquidity analysis.
💡 Growing vs. Shrinking Asset Base — The DTL Reversal Question

A company that continuously invests in new PP&E (so its gross asset base is growing or stable) will always have new accelerated depreciation DTLs being created. The net DTL balance may grow indefinitely and never reverse. Conversely, a company reducing its asset base will eventually see DTLs reverse as old assets are fully depreciated. This distinction is critical for whether to treat the DTL as a liability or quasi-equity.

04

Deferred Tax Assets — Deep Dive

A Deferred Tax Asset (DTA) represents taxes already paid (or a tax benefit to be received in the future) that have not yet been recognized as a reduction in tax expense on the income statement. Think of it as "prepaid tax." DTAs arise from deductible temporary differences and from tax loss carryforwards.

DTA Calculation
DTA = (Tax Base − Carrying Amount) × Tax Rate [for an asset where TB > CA]
DTA = (Carrying Amount − Tax Base) × Tax Rate [for a liability where CA > TB]

Tax Loss Carryforward DTA = Unused Tax Loss × Expected Future Tax Rate

Realizability — When Can a DTA Be Recognized?

A DTA can only be recognized if it is probable that sufficient future taxable income will exist to utilize it. This is the key judgment call for DTAs:

  • If the company expects to be profitable → DTA recognized in full
  • If it is doubtful that the tax benefit will be realized → DTA must be written down
IFRS Treatment
  • Recognize DTA only if it is "probable" that sufficient future taxable profit will exist
  • If doubt arises later about realizability → reverse the DTA directly (reduce the asset)
  • No separate "valuation allowance" concept — just recognize or de-recognize the DTA
  • The reversal goes through profit or loss (reduces deferred tax benefit)
US GAAP Treatment
  • Recognize DTA in full initially
  • Then establish a valuation allowance to reduce the DTA to the amount "more likely than not" (>50%) to be realized
  • Net DTA reported on B/S = Gross DTA − Valuation Allowance
  • Increasing the valuation allowance → increases tax expense (reduces DTA). Decreasing it → decreases tax expense (a benefit).
  • Valuation allowance changes are disclosed in the rate reconciliation — important signal for analysts
⚠️ Valuation Allowance — Earnings Management Signal

Valuation allowances involve significant management judgment. A company facing large deferred tax assets (e.g., from accumulated losses) can: (1) Increase the valuation allowance → reduces DTA, increases tax expense → lowers earnings. (2) Release the valuation allowance when prospects improve → increases DTA, reduces tax expense → boosts earnings. Analysts must scrutinize large changes in valuation allowances and the underlying assumptions justifying them.

Tax Loss Carryforwards

When a company has a taxable loss in a given year, tax laws generally allow it to use that loss to reduce taxable income in future years — a tax loss carryforward. This creates a DTA equal to the loss × expected future tax rate.

Key analytical issues: (1) carryforwards expire — if unused within the expiry period (e.g., 20 years in the US), the DTA is lost; (2) the company must generate future profit to use them; (3) large carryforward DTAs with large valuation allowances signal that management is uncertain about future profitability.

05

Worked Example — Reston Partners (Accelerated Depreciation DTL)

Setup: Reston Partners purchases equipment for GBP 20,000 at the start of Year 1. For accounting purposes: straight-line over 10 years → GBP 2,000/year depreciation. For tax purposes: straight-line over 7 years → GBP 2,857/year depreciation. Tax rate = 30%. All other items identical for accounting and tax.

Step 1 — Carrying Amount vs. Tax Base of Equipment
ItemYear 1Year 2Year 3
Original Cost 20,000 20,000 20,000
Carrying Amount (accounting, 2,000/yr) 18,000 16,000 14,000
Tax Base (tax, 2,857/yr) 17,143 14,286 11,429
Carrying Amount − Tax Base (Taxable Temp. Difference) 857 1,714 2,571
DTL = Diff × 30% tax rate 257 514 771

Since carrying amount > tax base in all years → taxable temporary difference → DTL. The DTL grows each year as the depreciation gap accumulates.

Step 2 — Taxes Payable vs. Tax Expense on Income Statement
ItemYear 1Year 2Year 3
Accounting Profit (pretax) 4,700 15,280 26,700
Taxable Income (higher dep. for tax) 3,843 14,423 25,843
Taxes Payable (taxable income × 30%) 1,153 4,327 7,753
Change in DTL (current DTL − prior DTL) 257 257 257
Income Tax Expense on IS (taxes payable + ΔDTL) 1,410 4,584 8,010
Check: Accounting Profit × 30% 1,410 4,584 8,010

Note: Tax expense on IS = 30% × accounting profit exactly, because the depreciation difference is a temporary (not permanent) difference. All that differs is the split between current (taxes payable) and deferred (ΔDTL).

💡 The Key Insight from This Example

Because it's a temporary difference, the income tax expense on the IS is always exactly 30% of accounting profit. The difference between taxes payable and tax expense is purely in the timing — the DTL is essentially "tax owed but deferred." When the tax depreciation runs out (after year 7) and accounting depreciation continues (years 8–10), taxable income will exceed accounting profit → DTL will start reversing → taxes payable will exceed accounting tax expense.

06

The Three Tax Rates Analysts Use

① Statutory Tax Rate
  • The official corporate income tax rate in the country where the company is domiciled
  • Set by law — does not change unless legislation changes
  • US statutory rate: currently 21% (post-Tax Cuts and Jobs Act 2017; was 35% before)
  • The benchmark against which the effective rate is compared
  • Multinational companies face multiple statutory rates across jurisdictions
② Effective Tax Rate
  • ETR = Income Tax Expense ÷ Pretax Income
  • The actual rate paid on accounting profit — reflects all the accounting and tax differences
  • Affected by: permanent differences, tax credits, geographic mix of income, tax planning strategies, valuation allowance changes
  • Use for: projecting future IS tax expense and after-tax earnings
  • Can be significantly above or below statutory — must be explained in the rate reconciliation note
③ Cash Tax Rate
  • Cash Tax Rate = Taxes Paid (cash) ÷ Pretax Income
  • Taxes actually paid during the period (per cash flow statement)
  • Differs from effective rate due to changes in deferred tax balances
  • Use for: projecting future cash tax payments and free cash flow
  • If DTA grows → cash taxes < tax expense → cash tax rate < effective rate
  • If DTL grows → cash taxes < tax expense → same directional effect
Three Tax Rates — Formulas & Relationships
Statutory Rate = Set by law (e.g., 21% US, 19% UK, 25% China)
Effective Tax Rate = Income Tax Expense (IS) ÷ Pretax Income
Cash Tax Rate = Taxes Paid (CFS) ÷ Pretax Income

Relationship:
Cash Tax = Tax Expense ± Change in Net DTA/DTL
If Net DTL ↑ (deferred tax building up): Cash Tax < Tax Expense → Cash Rate < Effective Rate
If Net DTA ↑ (prepaying taxes): Cash Tax > Tax Expense → Cash Rate > Effective Rate
Use effective tax rate for forecasting income statement tax expense. Use cash tax rate for forecasting cash flows and free cash flow. They can diverge substantially, especially for capital-intensive companies with large accelerated depreciation benefits.
Examiner Focus — Three Rates

(1) Know the formula for each. (2) Effective rate is for IS forecasting. Cash rate is for cash flow forecasting. (3) ETR < statutory → common causes: tax-exempt income, foreign operations in low-tax jurisdictions, R&D credits, equity method investee income. (4) ETR > statutory → common causes: non-deductible expenses (fines, penalties), high-tax foreign jurisdictions. (5) Cash rate < effective rate → company is building up DTLs (deferring taxes). (6) Analysts should use a normalized effective rate that excludes one-time items and equity-method income for forward estimates.

07

Effective Tax Rate Reconciliation

Both IFRS and US GAAP require companies to disclose a reconciliation from the statutory tax rate to the effective tax rate. This is one of the most analytically rich disclosures in the financial statements. It explains every reason why the effective rate differs from the statutory rate — and whether those differences are permanent or temporary.

J&J Example — Statutory to Effective Rate Walk

Johnson & Johnson 2021 — ETR Reconciliation (2021 ETR = 8.3% vs. 21% statutory)
Item202120202019Direction
US Statutory Rate 21.0% 21.0% 21.0% —
International operations (16.4)% (9.9)% (5.9)% Reduces ETR
US taxes on international income (GILTI etc.) +6.7% +2.7% +1.8% Increases ETR
Tax benefits on capital asset losses (1.3)% (1.2)% (0.3)% Reduces ETR
Tax benefits on share-based compensation (1.0)% (1.5)% (0.5)% Reduces ETR
Tax Cuts and Jobs Act related impacts (0.5)% +0.7% (3.9)% Varies
Effective Tax Rate 8.3% 10.8% 12.7% —

J&J's ETR is dramatically lower than 21% because the vast majority of profits are earned in non-US jurisdictions with lower tax rates. The net international benefit is (16.4%) − 6.7% = −9.7% in 2021. This is the primary persistent driver of J&J's low effective rate.

Reading the ETR Reconciliation — What Analysts Look For

  • Identify permanent vs. temporary drivers: Permanent differences (tax-exempt income, non-deductible fines) → persistent divergence. Temporary items → will normalize.
  • Geographic mix shifts: If a company is shifting profits to low-tax jurisdictions (or vice versa), the ETR will trend accordingly. Watch for year-on-year changes in the "international operations" line.
  • One-time items: Large rate changes from tax law changes, audit settlements, or valuation allowance releases are non-recurring. Normalize the ETR by removing these for forecasting.
  • Valuation allowance changes: A large release of valuation allowance → artificially low ETR that year. A large addition → artificially high ETR. Always one-time in nature.
  • R&D and other tax credits: Recurring credits that structurally reduce the ETR. Include in normalized rate.
  • Share-based compensation: If stock price rises, companies can deduct more than the accounting expense → reduces ETR. Volatile and stock-price dependent.
💡 Forecasting the ETR — Analyst Best Practice

Start with a normalized operating ETR — exclude equity-method investee income (often taxed differently), one-time tax benefits/charges, and valuation allowance releases. This gives the structural rate the company will face on its recurring operating earnings. Then layer in known structural changes (e.g., a tax reform, a geographic expansion) to get your forward estimate.

08

Disclosures & Financial Statement Analysis

The income tax note is typically one of the most extensive disclosures in the financial statements. It provides the full detail analysts need to understand the tax position and project future taxes accurately. The key components are:

Structure of the Income Tax Note

Disclosure ComponentWhat It ShowsAnalyst Use
Current vs. Deferred Tax Breakdown Splits total tax expense into current (taxes payable) and deferred (DTA/DTL change) components. Also splits by jurisdiction (domestic/foreign). Compute cash tax rate; understand where taxes are actually being paid
ETR Reconciliation Walk from statutory rate to effective rate, explaining each difference in % or $ terms. Identify permanent vs. temporary, recurring vs. one-time drivers; build normalized ETR
Deferred Tax Assets Schedule Lists all sources of DTAs (loss carryforwards, accrued liabilities, stock compensation, etc.) with gross amounts and valuation allowance offset. Assess realizability; understand remaining carryforward amounts and expiry dates
Deferred Tax Liabilities Schedule Lists all sources of DTLs (accelerated depreciation, intangibles, unremitted earnings, etc.) Assess whether DTLs are likely to reverse; include/exclude from leverage analysis
Unrecognized Tax Benefits Uncertain tax positions where company has taken a position that the tax authority may challenge. Disclosed as a reconciliation of the balance. Contingent tax liabilities; watch for large uncertain tax positions
Carryforward Details Amount of tax loss and credit carryforwards, and their expiry dates. Assess whether carryforwards will actually be used before expiry; check adequacy of valuation allowance

Micron Technology — Applying the Framework

From the Micron Technology (MU) example in the curriculum (FY2017):

  • Pretax income: USD 5,196M → at 35% statutory rate would imply USD 1,819M of tax expense
  • Actual tax provision: USD 114M → ETR of only 2.2% (= 114/5,196) — dramatically below statutory
  • Primary driver: Foreign tax rate differential — MU earns most income outside the US at lower rates, saving ~USD 1,571M vs. what would have been taxed at US rates
  • Gross DTA: USD 3,782M. Valuation allowance: USD (2,321)M. Net DTA: USD 1,461M — MU is uncertain about realizing ~61% of its gross DTAs
  • Largest DTA: Net operating loss carryforwards (USD 3,426M gross) — these expire 2018–2037, creating urgency
  • DTLs: USD 712M — primarily from PP&E timing differences (accelerated depreciation)
  • Net deferred tax position: USD 749M asset (DTA − DTL). Reported as USD 766M DTA (non-current asset) and USD 17M DTL (in other non-current liabilities)

Impact of Tax Rate Changes on DTA/DTL

When a tax rate changes (e.g., the 2017 US Tax Cuts and Jobs Act reduced the rate from 35% to 21%), all existing DTA and DTL balances must be remeasured at the new rate:

  • Rate decrease → DTL decreases → deferred tax benefit → boosts earnings in the period of rate change
  • Rate decrease → DTA decreases → deferred tax expense → reduces earnings in the period of rate change
  • The net impact depends on whether the company is a net DTA or net DTL position. A company with a large net DTL (like a capital-intensive manufacturer) benefits from rate reductions. A company with a large net DTA (like one with loss carryforwards) is hurt.
Examiner Focus — Disclosures & Analysis

(1) Be able to read a DTA/DTL schedule and identify the sources and net position. (2) Understand how the valuation allowance works and what changes in it signal. (3) ETR reconciliation: know how to walk from statutory to effective rate and identify permanent vs. temporary items. (4) Tax rate changes: rate ↓ → DTLs ↓ (benefit), DTAs ↓ (expense). The net effect depends on the company's net deferred tax position. (5) Unremitted foreign earnings: companies may not accrue deferred taxes on foreign earnings they plan to reinvest indefinitely — this is a potential hidden liability. (6) Loss carryforward expiry dates: DTAs from carryforwards have a shelf life — if the company can't generate taxable income before expiry, the DTA evaporates.

Exam Checklist — Module 9

  • ✅ Accounting profit ≠ taxable income. Accounting profit is per IFRS/US GAAP (on IS, before tax). Taxable income is per tax law (on tax return). Neither includes the other's adjustments directly.
  • ✅ Taxes payable = taxable income × statutory rate. This is what goes to the government. It is a current liability on the B/S.
  • ✅ Income tax expense (IS) = taxes payable + ΔDTL − ΔDTA. Tax expense follows accounting profit (matching principle). Always equals accounting profit × effective rate.
  • ✅ Temporary differences reverse in future → create DTA or DTL. Permanent differences never reverse → no DTA or DTL, but cause permanent wedge between ETR and statutory rate.
  • ✅ DTL: Carrying amount > tax base (for assets), or Tax base > carrying amount (for liabilities). Company paying less tax now → owes more later. DTL = (CA − TB) × tax rate.
  • ✅ DTA: Tax base > carrying amount (for assets), or Carrying amount > tax base (for liabilities). Company paying more tax now → gets benefit later. Also created by tax loss carryforwards.
  • ✅ IFRS DTA realizability: Recognize only if probable future taxable profit. If doubtful → reverse the DTA directly. No separate valuation allowance concept.
  • ✅ US GAAP DTA realizability: Recognize gross DTA, then establish a valuation allowance for any portion "not more likely than not" to be realized. Net DTA reported = Gross DTA − Valuation Allowance.
  • ✅ Valuation allowance increase → reduces net DTA → increases tax expense → signals management uncertainty about future profitability. Valuation allowance decrease → releases DTA → reduces tax expense → potential earnings boost. Both are subject to significant management judgment.
  • ✅ Tax rate change effects: Rate ↓ → DTL ↓ (deferred tax benefit on IS, helps earnings) AND DTA ↓ (deferred tax expense on IS, hurts earnings). Net effect depends on net DTA vs. DTL position.
  • ✅ DTL analyst treatment: If reversal is likely → treat as real liability. If unlikely (growing asset base creates perpetual new DTLs) → treat as quasi-equity or discount heavily.
  • ✅ Statutory rate: Legal rate in domicile jurisdiction. For US companies = 21%.
  • ✅ Effective tax rate = Income Tax Expense ÷ Pretax Income. Use for projecting IS tax expense and after-tax earnings.
  • ✅ Cash tax rate = Taxes Paid (CFS) ÷ Pretax Income. Use for projecting cash tax payments and free cash flow.
  • ✅ ETR < statutory: Common causes: foreign operations in low-tax jurisdictions (most important), tax-exempt income, R&D credits, tax credits. ETR > statutory: non-deductible expenses, high-tax foreign operations.
  • ✅ ETR reconciliation note: Walks from statutory to effective rate. Explains each permanent and temporary driver. Key tool for normalizing the effective rate for forecasting. Look for one-time items (valuation allowance releases, tax law changes, audit settlements) to exclude.
  • ✅ Tax loss carryforwards: Create DTA = loss × expected future tax rate. Have expiry dates — if unused before expiry → DTA lost. Large carryforward DTAs with large valuation allowances → management uncertainty about profitability.
  • ✅ Unremitted foreign earnings: Companies may defer taxes on foreign subsidiary earnings they plan to reinvest indefinitely → no deferred tax accrued. This is a contingent liability — if they repatriate, taxes will be owed. The disclosure notes this omission.
  • ✅ For forecasting: Use normalized ETR (exclude equity-method investee income, one-time items, valuation allowance changes) for IS projections. Reconcile to cash tax rate using expected DTA/DTL changes for cash flow projections.

Top exam traps for Module 9:
(1) Tax expense ≠ taxes payable. Tax expense follows accounting profit (matching). Taxes payable follows the tax return. The bridge between them is the change in deferred tax.
(2) Temporary differences → DTA/DTL. Permanent differences → nothing on the B/S but a permanent wedge in the ETR reconciliation.
(3) Accelerated depreciation for tax: early years → CA > TB → DTL (taxable temp. difference). Later years reverse.
(4) Warranty liabilities: liability CA > TB → DTA (deductible temp. difference). Tax deduction comes when warranty is paid, not when accrued.
(5) US GAAP valuation allowance: management judgment tool that can be used to manage earnings. Large changes require scrutiny.
(6) IFRS: just de-recognize the DTA if not probable. No separate valuation allowance concept.
(7) Effective rate for IS forecasting. Cash rate for cash flow forecasting. Know which is which.
(8) ETR < statutory most commonly because of low-tax foreign operations — not tax avoidance per se, but geographic income allocation.
(9) Rate decrease: net DTL company benefits (DTLs shrink). Net DTA company is hurt (DTAs shrink).
(10) Loss carryforward DTAs expire. If the company can't generate taxable income before expiry, the DTA and its tax benefit are lost forever.
