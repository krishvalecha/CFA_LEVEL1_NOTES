Intangible Asset Acquisition — Three Routes

How an intangible asset is acquired determines its accounting treatment. The same underlying asset — say, a patent or a brand — is treated very differently depending on whether it was (1) purchased outright, (2) developed internally, or (3) acquired as part of a business combination. This asymmetry creates major comparability issues for analysts comparing companies with different growth strategies.

① Purchased (Standalone)
  • Recorded at fair value at acquisition (= purchase price for standalone)
  • If multiple intangibles acquired together, allocate purchase price to each based on FV
  • Appears on balance sheet as a separately identifiable intangible asset
  • Example: buying a patent, license, or franchise from another company
  • Both IFRS and US GAAP: capitalize at cost
② Internally Developed
  • Generally expensed when incurred — not capitalized
  • IFRS exception: development phase costs can be capitalized if 6 criteria met
  • US GAAP exception: only certain software development costs capitalized (post-feasibility)
  • Operating cash outflow (not investing)
  • Creates major comparability issue vs. companies that buy vs. build
③ Business Combination
  • Acquired via acquisition method — allocated from purchase price at FV
  • Any identifiable intangible must be separated from goodwill and recognized individually
  • Residual excess of purchase price over FV of net identifiable assets = Goodwill
  • Goodwill: not amortized, tested annually for impairment
  • Same asset may be unrecognized in seller's books but recognized by acquirer
⚠️ The Buy vs. Build Asymmetry — Critical for Analysts

A company that builds its brand through advertising (internally generated) has zero brand value on its balance sheet — all costs expensed. A company that acquires the same brand in an acquisition shows it as an intangible asset (and records goodwill). Both may have economically identical brand value, but the accounting treatment is completely different. This creates a false impression that the acquirer has more assets. The buyer-built company also has lower reported assets → higher asset turnover ratios. Analysts must understand this asymmetry when comparing companies across growth strategies (organic vs. acquisition). Similarly, R&D-intensive companies under US GAAP expense all R&D while competitors using IFRS may capitalize development costs — comparing their margins directly is misleading.

IFRS Criteria for Recognizing Identifiable Intangible Assets

Under IFRS, an identifiable intangible asset must meet three definitional criteria AND two recognition criteria:

Definitional Criteria (3):

  • Identifiable: Separable from the entity OR arises from contractual/legal rights
  • Control: Company has power to obtain future economic benefits and restrict others
  • Future economic benefits: Revenues, cost savings, or other benefits expected

Recognition Criteria (2):

  • Probability: It is probable that the expected future economic benefits will flow to the company
  • Reliable measurement: The cost of the asset can be reliably measured
02

Internally Developed Intangibles — IFRS vs. US GAAP

The treatment of internally developed intangible costs is one of the most significant IFRS vs. US GAAP differences in this module. Under IFRS, the research/development split creates a more nuanced rule — some development costs CAN be capitalized. Under US GAAP, the default is to expense everything (with a narrow exception for software).

IFRS — Research vs. Development Phase
  • Research phase: Original investigation seeking new scientific/technical knowledge. Outcome highly uncertain. → ALWAYS expensed. No exceptions.
  • Development phase: Application of research findings to design/test products. Follows the research phase. → May be capitalized if ALL 6 criteria met simultaneously:
    1. Technical feasibility of completing the asset
    2. Intention to complete and use or sell the asset
    3. Ability to use or sell the asset
    4. Probable future economic benefits
    5. Adequate technical, financial, and other resources to complete
    6. Ability to reliably measure the expenditure
  • Once criteria are met, ALL subsequent development costs → capitalize as intangible asset
US GAAP — General Rule + Software Exception
  • General rule: Both research and development costs → expensed as incurred.
  • Software for sale exception: Expenses until technological feasibility is established. After feasibility → capitalize. Feasibility = completion of a detailed program design or working model.
  • Software for internal use: Expenses until it is probable the project will be completed AND the software will be used as intended. After → capitalize. (Note: easier threshold than "technological feasibility".)
  • Capitalized software costs include: employee costs of building and testing the software (direct labor).
  • Microsoft example: sets technological feasibility very late (just before production) → effectively expenses all software R&D. This is a management judgment choice — different companies set feasibility at different points.

Cash Flow Statement Impact — Develop vs. Acquire

The strategy of growing through internal development vs. external acquisition has a material impact on cash flow classification:

Internal Development (Expensed)

Costs expensed → Operating cash outflow (CFO). This reduces CFO. A company growing organically via R&D may show weaker CFO than an acquirer — even if their underlying economics are equivalent.

External Acquisition

Purchase price paid → Investing cash outflow (CFI). CFO is not impacted. An acquirer may appear to have stronger CFO than an organic grower purely due to how costs are classified.

This means: two companies spending the same amount on IP-building activities may show completely different CFO figures depending on whether they buy vs. build. Analysts must adjust for this when comparing cash flow across growth strategies.

Examiner Focus — Internally Developed Intangibles

The exam tests: (1) IFRS: research phase ALWAYS expensed, development phase MAY be capitalized if 6 criteria met; (2) US GAAP: generally expense all R&D, except post-feasibility software development costs (the narrow software exception); (3) The cash flow classification difference: expensed development = CFO outflow; acquired intangible = CFI outflow; (4) Balance sheet asymmetry: internally generated brand worth billions = zero on balance sheet. Acquired brand = recognized asset. Never compare total assets between organic growers and acquirers without adjusting for this.

Business Combination — Acquisition Method

When a company acquires another company, it uses the acquisition method (required under both IFRS and US GAAP). The acquirer allocates the entire purchase price to all identifiable assets and liabilities at fair value — including identifiable intangibles that were never on the acquiree's own balance sheet. Any residual excess is goodwill.

Identifiable intangibles MUST be separated from goodwill if:
IFRS: Meets definitional and recognition criteria
US GAAP: Arises from contractual/legal rights OR can be separated from the acquiree
Key implication: intangibles that were previously unrecognized in the target's books (e.g., internally developed customer lists, brands, distribution systems) ARE now recognized at fair value on the acquirer's consolidated balance sheet. The same brand that was zero on the seller's books becomes a measurable intangible on the buyer's balance sheet — because now there's a transaction to establish fair value.

What Drives Goodwill in an Acquisition?

Unrecognized Assets

Target has valuable assets not recorded (workforce, know-how, distribution network, customer relationships). Acquirer pays for these even though they never appeared on the target's balance sheet.

R&D Value

R&D expensed by target under US GAAP (or in research phase under IFRS) may have created real but unrecognized value. Acquirer is paying for the pipeline, drug candidates, technology that was expensed.

Strategic Synergies

Cost savings, revenue enhancement from combining operations. Improved strategic positioning. Assembled workforce. Tax benefits. These synergies don't exist in the standalone target's balance sheet.

💡 IFRS vs. US GAAP — Recognizing Intangibles in Business Combinations

IFRS: An identifiable intangible acquired in a business combination is recognized separately from goodwill if it meets the definitional criteria (identifiable, controlled, future benefits) AND recognition criteria (probable benefits, reliable measurement). US GAAP: Two-pronged test — recognize separately from goodwill if the intangible (1) arises from contractual or legal rights, OR (2) can be separated from the acquired company. US GAAP's criteria are somewhat broader — more items are separated from goodwill. In practice, both standards result in similar outcomes for most intangibles (patents, licenses, brands, customer lists, software).

Impairment of Property, Plant and Equipment

Depreciation/amortization = systematic allocation of cost over useful life. It's planned, predictable, and does NOT reflect actual declines in value. Impairment = an unexpected decline in the fair value of an asset below its carrying amount. Impairment charges are non-cash, reduce earnings and assets, and are separately disclosed.

PP&E is NOT tested annually for impairment — only when indicators of impairment exist. Indicators include: significant decline in market value, evidence of obsolescence, declining demand for products, significant adverse changes in legal or technological environment, or physical damage.

IFRS vs. US GAAP — Impairment Recognition and Measurement

IFRS — IAS 36
  • Impairment exists when: Carrying Amount > Recoverable Amount
  • Recoverable Amount = Higher of:
    • Fair Value less Costs to Sell (market value − disposal costs)
    • Value in Use (PV of expected future cash flows from use)
  • Impairment loss = Carrying Amount − Recoverable Amount
  • Impairment loss recognized immediately in profit or loss
  • After impairment: Carrying amount = Recoverable Amount (the new basis)
  • Future depreciation is recalculated on the new (impaired) carrying amount
  • Reversals permitted — see Section 6
US GAAP — Two-Step Process
  • Step 1 — Recoverability Test: Carrying Amount > Sum of Undiscounted expected future cash flows? If NO → no impairment. If YES → proceed to Step 2.
  • Step 2 — Measurement: Impairment loss = Carrying Amount − Fair Value of asset
  • Note: Step 1 uses undiscounted cash flows (easier to pass). Step 2 uses fair value (not PV of cash flows — usually lower than IFRS's value in use)
  • Implication: US GAAP may recognize impairment less frequently (passes Step 1 more easily) but when it IS recognized, it can be larger (fair value vs. value in use)
  • Reversals NOT permitted for assets held for use
IFRS vs. US GAAP Impairment — Scenario Comparison
Asset DetailIFRS ResultUS GAAP Result
Carrying amount: $100; Fair value less costs to sell: $80; Value in use: $95; Undiscounted future CF: $105 Recoverable = max($80, $95) = $95. Impairment = $100 − $95 = $5 loss Step 1: $100 < $105 undiscounted CF → No impairment
Carrying amount: $100; Fair value less costs to sell: $70; Value in use: $75; Undiscounted future CF: $90 Recoverable = max($70, $75) = $75. Impairment = $100 − $75 = $25 loss Step 1: $100 > $90 → Impaired. Step 2: $100 − Fair Value $70 = $30 loss

Key insight: In the first scenario, IFRS recognizes an impairment ($5) but US GAAP does not (undiscounted CF of $105 exceeds carrying amount of $100 → passes recoverability test). In the second scenario, both recognize impairment, but IFRS's loss is smaller ($25 vs. $30) because IFRS uses the higher "value in use" ($75) while US GAAP uses "fair value" ($70). These differences can make IFRS companies appear more or less impaired than equivalent US GAAP companies.

Financial Statement Impact of Impairment

  • Income statement: Impairment loss reduces net income. Non-cash — no cash flow impact in the period recognized.
  • Balance sheet: Asset carrying amount reduced. Equity (retained earnings) reduced.
  • CFS (indirect method): Impairment loss added back to net income in CFO (it's a non-cash charge — same treatment as depreciation).
  • Future depreciation: Lower carrying amount → lower future depreciation expense → higher future earnings (the "earnings bath" effect — take a big write-down now, improve future earnings).
  • Ratio effects: Total assets ↓ → future asset turnover may ↑. ROA: NI falls (bad) but asset base also falls → future ROA may recover or improve. D/E: equity ↓ → D/E ↑.
05

Impairment of Intangible Assets

Finite-Life Intangibles — Same as PP&E

Intangible assets with finite useful lives are amortized AND may become impaired. Like PP&E, they are NOT tested annually. Instead, they are tested only when significant events suggest the need — e.g., significant decrease in market price, significant adverse change in legal or economic factors, or a major technological shift. Impairment accounting is identical to PP&E (IFRS: carrying amount vs. recoverable amount; US GAAP: two-step process).

Indefinite-Life Intangibles — Annual Test Required

Indefinite-Life Intangibles & Goodwill — Annual Impairment Testing
  • Intangibles with indefinite lives (e.g., broadcast licenses, certain trademarks) and Goodwill are NOT amortized
  • Instead, tested for impairment at least annually (and more frequently if indicators exist)
  • Impairment exists when: Carrying Amount > Fair Value (both IFRS and US GAAP)
  • Under IFRS: Goodwill tested at the Cash Generating Unit (CGU) level — the smallest group of assets generating independent cash flows
  • Under US GAAP: Goodwill tested at the reporting unit level (similar concept to CGU)
  • Management assumptions (perpetuity growth rate, discount rate) drive the fair value calculation → significant management judgment and risk of manipulation
  • Orange SA example: France CGU uses 5.5% post-tax discount rate and 0.8% perpetuity growth rate. A 1% increase in discount rate would reduce recoverable amount by €11.4B — enormous sensitivity showing how dependent impairment is on assumptions

Assets Held for Sale — Reclassification and Impairment

A long-lived asset is reclassified from "held for use" to "held for sale" when: (1) management intends to sell it, (2) it's available for immediate sale in current condition, and (3) the sale is highly probable. Upon reclassification:

  • Asset is tested for impairment — if carrying amount > fair value less costs to sell → impairment recognized
  • Depreciation and amortization CEASE — no further D&A on held-for-sale assets
  • Asset shown separately on balance sheet as "Non-current assets held for sale" (often shown as current if sale within 12 months)
  • Under US GAAP: if fair value recovers after impairment → reversal IS permitted for held-for-sale assets (unlike held-for-use assets)

Examiner Focus — Reversals

The table above is highly testable. Key points to memorize: (1) IFRS permits reversals for most assets — but NOT goodwill (unique exception). (2) US GAAP prohibits reversals for assets held for use — but PERMITS reversals for assets held for sale (another exception). (3) When an IFRS impairment is reversed, the reversal is reported in profit (income statement). (4) Reversal under IFRS is limited to the original impairment amount — you cannot write an asset UP above its original pre-impairment carrying amount. This is different from revaluation (the revaluation model can take assets above cost; impairment reversals cannot).

07

Derecognition — Sale, Abandonment, Exchange, Spin-off

An asset is derecognized (removed from the balance sheet) when it is disposed of OR when it is expected to provide no future benefits. This can happen through: sale, exchange for another asset, abandonment, or distribution to shareholders (spin-off). Each has a different accounting treatment.

① Sale of Long-Lived Assets

Gain or Loss on Sale of Asset
Gain (Loss) = Sale Proceeds − Carrying Amount (Net Book Value) at time of sale

Carrying Amount = Historical Cost − Accumulated Depreciation
                (adjusted for any impairment or revaluation)

Gain on IS → Non-operating income. Removed from CFO (indirect method).
Cash proceeds → CFI (investing activities).
Asset removed from balance sheet at carrying amount.
The gain/loss on sale is disclosed on the income statement (in other gains/losses or as a separate material line). Under the indirect method of the CFS, the gain is subtracted from net income in CFO (to remove the non-operating element) and the actual cash proceeds appear in CFI. A loss on sale is added back in CFO and the (smaller) proceeds appear in CFI.

② Abandonment

When an asset is abandoned (not sold — just retired with no proceeds): accounting is similar to a sale but with ZERO sale proceeds. A loss equal to the asset's carrying amount is recorded. The asset is removed from the balance sheet. If the asset has been fully depreciated (zero carrying amount), no loss is recognized upon abandonment.

③ Exchange for Another Asset

When assets are exchanged: remove the carrying amount of the asset given up, record the newly acquired asset at its fair value (typically the fair value of the asset given up, unless the asset received has a more clearly determinable fair value). The difference between FV used and carrying amount = gain or loss. If no reliable fair value exists → new asset measured at carrying amount of old asset → no gain or loss.

④ Spin-off / Distribution to Owners

In a spin-off, an entire business segment (with all its assets) is distributed to existing shareholders. The Fiat Chrysler / Ferrari example illustrates the key accounting: FCA distributed Ferrari to shareholders at carrying amount. No gain or loss is recognized in a pure spin-off distribution — the assets leave at book value. The shareholders receive shares in the new entity (Ferrari N.V.) instead of cash. Contrast with the earlier 10% IPO sale: that DID generate a gain (proceeds of €866m vs. carrying amount of €7m = €859m gain).

💡 Held for Sale — Asset Treatment Changes

Once reclassified to "held for sale": (1) depreciation STOPS immediately — no more D&A from reclassification date forward; (2) asset tested for impairment at time of reclassification (FV less costs to sell vs. carrying amount); (3) shown separately on the balance sheet — typically as a current asset if expected sale within 12 months; (4) the related operations may be classified as discontinued operations if the component represents a major line of business or geographical area. This is why analysts pay close attention to the held-for-sale reclassification — it signals an upcoming divestiture and changes how you project future earnings (exclude the divested segment).

Disclosures and Key Analytical Ratios

Required Disclosures — PP&E and Intangibles

PP&E Disclosures (IFRS — per class)
  • Measurement basis (cost model or revaluation model)
  • Depreciation method used
  • Useful life or depreciation rate
  • Gross carrying amount and accumulated depreciation — opening and closing
  • Reconciliation of carrying amount (roll-forward)
  • Restrictions on title, pledges as security
  • Contractual commitments to acquire PP&E
  • If revaluation model: date of revaluation, how FV obtained, carrying amount under cost model, revaluation surplus
Intangible Assets Disclosures (IFRS — per class)
  • Whether useful life is finite or indefinite (and why, if indefinite)
  • Useful lives or amortization rates, amortization methods
  • Gross carrying amount and accumulated amortization — opening and closing
  • Where amortization is included on the income statement
  • Reconciliation of carrying amount
  • Impairment losses and reversals recognized in the period
  • US GAAP adds: estimated amortization expense for each of the next 5 fiscal years; gross amounts by major class

Reading Long-Term Asset Disclosures — Orange SA Illustration

Orange SA's disclosures demonstrate what analysts should look for when reading long-term asset footnotes:

  • Goodwill roll-forward: Gross goodwill was stable at ~€32.7B over 2015–2017. Accumulated impairment losses grew from €5,487m to €5,592m — small annual impairments charged. Net goodwill barely changed (€27B range). This suggests management believes goodwill is largely recoverable — but the sensitivity analysis reveals enormous risk: a 1% increase in France's discount rate reduces recoverable amount by €11.4B. That's almost half of France's goodwill — a sign of how subjective these values are.
  • Intangible mix: Telecom licenses declining (€6,233m from €6,440m) as licenses amortize. Software growing (€3,946m from €3,781m) — consistent with digital transformation investment. Orange brand stable at €3,133m — indefinite life, not amortized.
  • PP&E growth: Network and terminals up from €21,194m (2015) to €22,880m (2017) — ongoing investment in network infrastructure. Total PP&E growing each year — capex exceeding depreciation.
  • Impairment sensitivity disclosures are particularly valuable — they quantify how much a CGU's recoverable amount changes with assumptions. Orange disclosed that a 1% discount rate increase in France → €11.4B impact. This helps analysts stress-test management's assumptions.

Exam Checklist — Module 7: Analysis of Long-Term Assets

  • ✅ Three routes to intangible acquisition: (1) Purchased standalone → capitalize at cost/FV; (2) Internally developed → generally expense (IFRS: development phase may capitalize; US GAAP: expense except post-feasibility software); (3) Business combination → acquisition method, allocate purchase price at FV, residual = goodwill.
  • ✅ IFRS intangible recognition: 3 definitional criteria (identifiable + control + future economic benefits) AND 2 recognition criteria (probable benefits + reliable measurement). All 5 must be met.
  • ✅ IFRS R&D split: Research phase = ALWAYS expensed (seeking new knowledge, high uncertainty). Development phase = CAN capitalize if all 6 criteria met (feasibility, intent, ability to use/sell, probable benefits, adequate resources, reliable measurement).
  • ✅ US GAAP R&D: Generally expense both research and development. Exception: software development costs capitalized after technological feasibility (for software for sale) or after probable completion (software for internal use).
  • ✅ Cash flow classification: Internally developed intangibles expensed → CFO outflow. Externally acquired intangibles → CFI outflow. Organic vs. acquisition strategy creates different CFO profiles even with identical economic spending.
  • ✅ Acquisition method: Purchase price allocated to all identifiable assets (tangible + intangible) and liabilities at fair value. Residual = Goodwill. Identifiable intangibles from the target (previously unrecognized) must be separately recognized and valued.
  • ✅ Depreciation vs. Impairment: Depreciation = planned, systematic allocation of cost over useful life. Impairment = unexpected decline in fair value below carrying amount. Both non-cash; both reduce assets and earnings.
  • ✅ PP&E impairment trigger: NOT tested annually — only when indicators of impairment exist (obsolescence, declining demand, technological change, damage, adverse legal changes).
  • ✅ IFRS impairment (IAS 36): Impairment = Carrying Amount − Recoverable Amount. Recoverable Amount = max(FV less costs to sell, Value in Use). VIU = PV of expected future CFs.
  • ✅ US GAAP impairment — two steps: Step 1: Carrying Amount > Undiscounted future CFs? (If no → no impairment). Step 2 (if failed): Impairment = Carrying Amount − Fair Value.
  • ✅ IFRS may recognize impairment earlier (no undiscounted CF test). US GAAP Step 1 filter means fewer impairments recognized. But when recognized under US GAAP, loss can be larger (FV vs. VIU).
  • ✅ Indefinite-life intangibles and goodwill: NOT amortized. Tested for impairment at least ANNUALLY. IFRS: CGU level. US GAAP: reporting unit level. High management judgment in setting discount rates and growth rates.
  • ✅ Assets held for sale: Reclassified when sale imminent, immediately available, highly probable. Depreciation CEASES upon reclassification. Tested for impairment at reclassification (vs. FV less costs to sell).
  • ✅ Impairment reversal rules: IFRS: reversals PERMITTED for PP&E, finite and indefinite intangibles, held-for-sale assets — but NOT goodwill. US GAAP: reversals NOT PERMITTED for assets held for use. PERMITTED for assets held for sale (the exception).
  • ✅ Gain/loss on sale = Sale Proceeds − Net Carrying Amount (historical cost − accumulated depreciation ± impairment ± revaluation). Gain/loss on IS. Cash proceeds in CFI. Gain subtracted in CFO (indirect method).
  • ✅ Abandonment: No proceeds. Loss = Carrying Amount. Asset removed from B/S.
  • ✅ Exchange: New asset at FV of asset given up (unless asset received has clearer FV). Gain/loss = FV used − Carrying Amount of old asset.
  • ✅ Spin-off: Distribution at book value — no gain or loss (vs. IPO sale which generates a gain/loss).
  • ✅ Fixed Asset Turnover = Revenue ÷ Avg Net PP&E. Higher = more efficient. Caveat: old, fully depreciated assets → low net book value → artificially high ratio.
  • ✅ Asset age ratios: Average age = Accum. Dep ÷ Dep Expense. Remaining life = Net PP&E ÷ Dep Expense. Total life = Gross PP&E ÷ Dep Expense = Age + Remaining Life. Apply only under cost model.
  • ✅ Capex-to-Depreciation ratio: >1 = growing asset base. <1 = underinvesting. Trending below 1 signals long-term capacity risk.

          Step 2: Impairment = Carrying Amount − Fair Value

═══ IMPAIRMENT REVERSALS ═══
IFRS: Permitted for PP&E, intangibles, held-for-sale | NOT goodwill
US GAAP: NOT permitted for held-for-use | PERMITTED for held-for-sale

═══ DERECOGNITION ═══
Sale: Gain/Loss = Proceeds − Net Book Value → G/L on IS; cash in CFI
Abandon: Loss = Net Book Value (zero proceeds)
Exchange: New asset at FV of asset given up; G/L = FV − NBV
Spin-off: No gain/loss (book value distribution)

═══ KEY ASSET RATIOS ═══
Fixed Asset Turnover = Revenue ÷ Avg Net PP&E
Average Age = Accumulated Dep ÷ Annual Dep Expense
Remaining Life = Net PP&E ÷ Annual Dep Expense
Total Useful Life = Gross PP&E ÷ Annual Dep Expense (= Age + Remaining Life)
Capex/Dep ratio = Capital Expenditures ÷ Depreciation Expense
    (>1 = growing capacity; <1 = underinvesting; =1 = maintaining)
Top exam traps for Module 7:
(1) IFRS research phase: ALWAYS expensed. Development phase: MAY capitalize (6 criteria). US GAAP: generally expense all R&D (software exception).
(2) Internally developed brand = ZERO on balance sheet. Acquired brand = recognized asset. Never compare balance sheets of organic vs. acquisition growers without adjusting.
(3) Internal development costs → CFO outflow. Acquisition of intangibles → CFI outflow. Same economic activity, different cash flow classification.
(4) PP&E impairment: NOT tested annually — only when indicators exist. Goodwill and indefinite-life intangibles: tested ANNUALLY.
(5) IFRS impairment = CA − max(FV less costs to sell, VIU). US GAAP Step 1 uses undiscounted CF (easier to pass) → IFRS recognizes more impairments. Step 2 uses FV (not VIU) → US GAAP impairment can be LARGER.
(6) Impairment reversal: IFRS allows (not goodwill). US GAAP prohibits (held for use) but allows (held for sale).
(7) Held for sale: depreciation CEASES. Tested for impairment at reclassification. Shown separately on B/S.
(8) Gain on sale = Proceeds − Net Book Value. Gain appears on IS. Subtract from CFO (indirect). Proceeds in CFI.
(9) Asset age ratios: Average age = AccumDep/DepExp. Remaining life = NetPPE/DepExp. Only valid under cost model — not under revaluation model (carrying amount differs from depreciated cost).
(10) Capex < Depreciation over time → asset base shrinking → underinvestment signal → future capacity/competitive risk.
(11) Goodwill impairment: non-cash charge. Reduces assets and equity. May increase future ROA (smaller denominator). Management judgment in setting discount rates and growth rates creates impairment manipulation risk.
