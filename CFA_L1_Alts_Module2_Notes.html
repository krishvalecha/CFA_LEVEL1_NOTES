01

Why Performance Appraisal of Alternatives Is Complex

Traditional Assets (Simple)
  • Standardized claims; no further capital commitments required
  • Identical periodic cash flows (dividends, coupons)
  • Continuously quoted market prices
  • Large peer groups; common benchmark indexes
  • Returns approximately normally distributed
  • Straightforward performance comparison
Alternative Investments (Complex)
  • Customized investments; staggered capital calls
  • Variable timing of cash inflows and outflows
  • Often no continuous market pricing (illiquid)
  • Use of leverage magnifies gains and losses
  • Complex fee structures affecting different investors differently
  • Returns usually NOT normally distributed
  • Different tax and accounting treatment

Four Key Areas Requiring Additional Scrutiny

  1. Investment life cycle phase — timing and direction of cash flows vary by phase
  2. Use of borrowed funds (leverage) — magnifies both gains and losses
  3. Valuation of assets — often requires Level 3 "mark-to-model" inputs; potential for stale or smoothed valuations
  4. Fee structure — complex, customized, and investor-specific; results vary by when and how an investor participated
💡 The Core Challenge

Unlike public securities where all investors in the same fund earn the same return, alternative investment returns can vary significantly by investor depending on when they invested, what fee structure they negotiated, and what liquidity restrictions they accepted. This makes generalized performance appraisal unreliable.

02

Investment Life Cycle & the J-Curve Effect

J-Curve Effect
The characteristic pattern of alternative investment returns — initial negative returns in the early phase (due to fees and expenses before assets generate income) followed by an acceleration of returns as investments mature. The return profile resembles the letter "J."
Phase 1 — Capital Commitment (Negative Returns)

Manager identifies and selects investments. Capital is committed via a capital call (immediate or delayed).

  • Returns are typically negative — fees and expenses incurred immediately before assets generate income
  • Investments may generate little or no income during this phase
  • Applies to: early-stage companies (VC), mature firms (PE), or properties (real estate)
Phase 2 — Capital Deployment (Cash Outflows Exceed Inflows)

Manager deploys funds for operations: construction or improvements (real estate/infrastructure), turnaround expenses (PE), or startup operations (VC).

  • Cash outflows typically exceed inflows
  • Management fees further reduce returns
  • Investments begin to develop but have not yet reached profitability
Phase 3 — Capital Distribution (Acceleration of Returns)

Turnaround, startup phase, or improvements completed → investments mature.

  • Underlying assets appreciate and/or generate income in excess of costs
  • Fund may realize substantial capital gains from liquidating or exiting investments
  • Exit methods: IPO (VC), sale of properties (real estate), sale of portfolio companies (PE)
  • Returns accelerate, then level off as capital is distributed, investments sold, and fund closed
💡 J-Curve — Why It Matters for Performance Comparison

A private equity fund in Phase 1 will show poor performance vs. a fund in Phase 3. This is why vintage year comparisons are more appropriate than simply comparing current returns. Using a composite benchmark where funds are at different phases is misleading — like comparing a startup vs. a mature company and calling one "better managed."

⚠️ Interim Valuations Are Less Reliable Mid-Life Cycle

After a PE fund has fully drawn capital commitments, interim accounting values become less critical for a period. Accounting conventions often leave longer-lived investments marked at or near initial cost until clear impairment events or realization events occur. These "stale" values may not reflect actual changes in underlying investment value — potentially understating volatility and overstating apparent stability.

03

Return Metrics — IRR vs. MOIC

IRR (Internal Rate of Return)

Preferred measure for alternative investments — especially private equity and real estate.

  • Accounts for the timing and magnitude of all cash flows in and out
  • GP is rewarded or penalized for timing decisions
  • Requires assumptions: (1) financing rate for outgoing cash flows (typically WACC), (2) reinvestment rate for incoming cash flows
  • Cannot be solved algebraically — requires iteration/calculator/Excel
  • Best for assessing longer-term PE and real estate investments
MOIC (Multiple of Invested Capital)

Shortcut measure — easier to calculate and intuitively understand.

  • Also called: money multiple
  • Formula: MOIC = (Realized value + Unrealized value) / Total invested capital
  • Ignores the timing of cash flows — a key limitation
  • Easy to communicate: "We made 2× or 3× our initial investment"
  • Total invested capital = paid-in capital less management fees and fund expenses
MOIC Formula
MOIC = (Realized value of investment + Unrealized value of investment) / Total amount of invested capital

Total invested capital = paid-in capital − management fees − fund expenses
MOIC ignores time value of money — a 2× return in 2 years is far better than 2× in 15 years.
IRR captures the time dimension; MOIC does not.
Unrealized value = residual asset values still awaiting their ultimate sale (not yet exited).
Both IRR and MOIC are commonly reported together for private equity funds.
⚠️ MOIC Exam Trap — Time Blindness

A MOIC of 3× sounds impressive, but you must know how long it took. 3× in 3 years ≈ IRR of ~44%. 3× in 15 years ≈ IRR of ~8%. Same MOIC, radically different IRRs. Always use IRR to assess performance when timing matters — which for alternatives, it always does.

04

Use of Borrowed Funds — Leverage

Leveraged Rate of Return
rL = [r × (Vc + Vb) − (Vb × rb)] / Vc

Rearranged to show relationship clearly:
rL = r + (Vb / Vc) × (r − rb)

where:
rL = leveraged rate of return
r = unleveraged cash portfolio return
Vc = cash (equity) invested by investor
Vb = borrowed funds
rb = periodic borrowing rate
If r > rb → leverage INCREASES returns (positive carry)
If r < rb → leverage DECREASES returns (negative carry)
If r = rb → leverage has no effect
Leverage amplifies BOTH gains AND losses — same formula applies in both directions.

How Hedge Funds Use Leverage

  • Derivatives: Take large notional exposures with small capital outlay
  • Prime broker borrowing: Hedge fund borrows shares, bonds, or derivatives; deposits cash or collateral into a margin account
  • Margin account = hedge fund's net equity in its positions
  • Minimum margin depends on portfolio riskiness and hedge fund creditworthiness
  • Margin call: If margin account falls below minimum → lender demands more collateral → hedge fund may be forced to liquidate positions → can lock in or magnify losses
  • Forced liquidation of large positions can move security prices further against the fund before the position is fully closed
  • Leverage is necessary for meaningful returns from arbitrage, relative value, and quantitative strategies
⚠️ Leverage Risk — Margin Calls and Forced Liquidation

If a hedge fund cannot meet a margin call, it must liquidate positions — potentially at the worst possible time. If the position is large enough to move the market, the fund's forced selling worsens the price further before the position is closed. This is why leverage can turn modest losses into catastrophic ones, and why "access to borrowed funds or lack thereof is an important factor in evaluating the risk of leveraged investments."

05

Valuation & Fair Value Hierarchy

Fair Value
A market-based measure based on observable or derived assumptions to determine the exit price a market participant would use to sell an asset (or transfer a liability) in an orderly transaction at a specific date. Accounting rules require investments to be recorded at fair value for financial reporting purposes.
Level 1 — Observable Market Prices

Quoted prices in active markets for identical assets/liabilities accessible as of the measurement date.

Example: Exchange-traded public equity securities (observed closing market price).

Most reliable; no estimation required.

Level 2 — Observable Inputs (Indirect)

Inputs other than Level 1 quoted prices that are directly or indirectly observable for the asset/liability.

Example: OTC interest rate derivatives (pricing model using quoted market prices).

Model-based but anchored to observable market data.

Level 3 — Unobservable Inputs ("Mark-to-Model")

Unobservable inputs used when there is little or no market activity for the asset/liability.

Example: Private equity or real estate (cash flow projection models with reasonably available market participant assumptions).

Least reliable; highest estimation risk; increased scrutiny required.

⚠️ Level 3 Valuation Risks — Smoothing and Conflicts of Interest

Alternative investments often rely on Level 3 ("mark-to-model") inputs, creating several risks:

  • Smoothed or overstated returns: Stable accounting valuations (no new market price information) give a false impression that returns are less correlated and less volatile than actual underlying asset value changes
  • Understated volatility: Mark-to-model valuations don't fluctuate with market prices → standard deviation of reported returns understates true risk
  • Conflict of interest: When managers self-value their own portfolios (mark-to-model), there is incentive to overstate values → must have independent testing, benchmarking, and calibration to industry standards
  • "More realistic picture" emerges if managers are forced to liquidate a portfolio prematurely — true market prices revealed
  • Rule of thumb: Any investment vehicle heavily involved with Level 3 priced assets deserves increased scrutiny and due diligence
06

Redemption Mechanics — Lockup, Notice Period, Gate

Redemptions (investor withdrawals) are particularly damaging when a hedge fund is performing poorly — forcing the manager to sell positions at disadvantageous prices under pressure, incurring transaction costs, and potentially locking in or magnifying losses. Funds use several mechanisms to manage this risk:

Mechanism Definition Purpose Typical Terms
Lockup Period Minimum holding period before investors can withdraw or redeem shares Allows manager time to implement strategy and realize expected results; prevents premature forced selling Varies; longer lockups → lower fees
Notice Period Advance notice investors must give before redeeming Allows manager to liquidate positions in an orderly manner without distressing prices Typically 30–90 days in advance
Redemption Fee Fee charged on withdrawals Discourages redemptions; offsets transaction costs for remaining investors Varies; typically a % of redeemed amount
Gate A provision that limits or restricts redemptions for a period of time Prevents a "run on the fund" during stressed conditions; preserves orderly operations Usually at discretion of fund manager
💡 Fund of Funds — More Redemption Flexibility

Funds of hedge funds may offer more redemption flexibility than direct hedge fund investments because of: (1) special redemption arrangements with underlying fund managers, (2) maintained cash reserves, (3) access to bridge-loan financing, or (4) avoidance of less-liquid hedge fund strategies. However, they add another layer of fees.

💡 Redemption Terms Should Match Asset Liquidity

Ideally, redemption terms should match the expected liquidity of the assets being invested in. However, even careful planning can fail when assets are illiquid and obscure — initial drawdowns can become much more serious. These left-tailed loss events (rare but catastrophic) are not easily modeled for hedge funds.

07

Custom Fee Arrangements

Alternative investment fees are often customized to vary based on the size, timing, and terms of investor participation. Different investors in the same fund can face different fee structures, leading to different net returns from the same underlying performance.

1 — Fees Based on Liquidity Terms & Asset Size
  • Longer lockup periods → lower management fees
  • Larger investors receive fee discounts
  • Placement agents who introduced investors may also receive fee reductions
  • Large LP management fees: 0.5%–1.5% with incentive fees of 10%–15%
  • Strong small funds may decline business from large investors rather than reduce fees (capacity constraints)
2 — Founders Shares (Founders Class)
  • Incentives for early investors in startup funds — lower fee structure in exchange for early commitment
  • Typical founders share terms: 1.5% management fee / 10% performance fee vs. standard 2% management fee / 20% performance fee
  • Often applies only to the first $100 million in assets invested (cutoff thresholds vary)
  • Alternative: fees reduced once fund achieves a critical mass or performance targets
3 — "Either/Or" Fee Structure
  • Major institutional investors (e.g., Teacher Retirement System of Texas) may negotiate an either/or fee agreement
  • Manager chooses between: (a) 1% fixed management fee (covers expenses in down years) OR (b) 30% incentive fee above an agreed hurdle (rewards performance in up years) — whichever is greater
  • Effectively: if fund performs well → fund charges performance fee only; if fund performs poorly → fund charges management fee only
  • Designed to reward returns in excess of a benchmark while covering expenses when underperforming
  • Typically negotiated by large investors with large commitments; smaller investors (high-net-worth individuals) face more traditional fee structures
💡 Fee Structure Summary — Standard vs. Custom

Standard "2 and 20": 2% management fee + 20% performance fee above hurdle rate (most common traditional hedge fund structure).
Founders shares: ~1.5% + 10% for early investors.
Either/or: max(1% flat, 30% incentive above hurdle) — large institutional investors only.
Because fees differ by investor, the same fund's performance can produce different net returns for different LPs.

Return Calculations — Before and After Fees

GP Return and Investor Net Return Formulas
GP Return in currency terms:
RGP = (P₁ × rm) + max[0, (P₁ − P₀) × p]

Investor's periodic net rate of return:
rᵢ = (P₁ − P₀ − RGP) / P₀

where:
P₀ = beginning-of-period assets (fund value at start)
P₁ = end-of-period assets (fund value at end)
rm = management fee rate (% of AUM, applied to end-of-period assets P₁)
p = performance fee rate (% of positive return)
RGP = total GP compensation in currency terms
The GP earns: (1) management fee on end-of-period fund value + (2) performance fee on positive period return.
Performance fee only earned when (P₁ − P₀) > 0 → max[0, ...] ensures no negative performance fee.
Investor's net return = gross return minus all GP compensation.
Applying fees to gross vs. net basis has different effects — fees calculated on net basis give higher investor returns.

Impact of Hurdle Rate on Investor Returns

When a performance fee includes a hurdle rate, the fee reduction for a specific investor is:

Fee reduction = P₁ × rh × p (product of end-of-period fund value × hurdle rate × performance fee)

This is the same for any investor regardless of when they entered the fund. However, high-water marks produce different results depending on entry timing:

  • Original investor who has been in since inception → benefits from HWM if fund previously fell and is now recovering
  • New investor entering after a fund decline → gets NO HWM benefit because the new investor's HWM starts at their entry price, not the fund's prior peak
  • Result: new investor faces higher performance fees than the original investor for the same period of fund recovery

Effect of Clawback on Investor Returns

Clawback provisions are activated when early success is followed by later losses. The timing of returns has a meaningful impact:

  • GP earns performance fees on early successful deal exits
  • Later deals generate losses
  • Clawback requires GP to return previously paid performance fees to LPs
  • Protects LPs from paying performance fees on an overall losing fund
  • Net effect on investor return: LP recovers some or all performance fees paid on early gains if those gains are offset by later losses at the fund level
💡 Timing-Dependent Returns — An Important Nuance

Two investors with identical fee structures in the same fund can earn different net returns purely because of when they invested: (1) Investor entering after a fund drop may face performance fees on the same recovery period where an earlier investor owes nothing (protected by HWM). (2) Fees generally higher for later entrants in recovering funds. (3) Alternative investment databases report fund performance net of aggregated fees — but if fee structures vary, individual investor returns may differ.

Relative Returns, Survivorship Bias & Backfill Bias

Benchmarking Alternatives

  • Alternative investment returns are compared to benchmarks of investments with similar features
  • A composite benchmark for PE or real estate can be misleading if a specific investment is in a different life cycle phase than most peers
  • Vintage year comparisons (same year investments started) on annual or "since inception" basis lead to more accurate results
  • Key limitation: Lockups and illiquidity prevent investors from reacting to underperformance by selling — unlike public securities
Survivorship Bias

Exclusion of failed or closed funds from a benchmark, leading to overly optimistic return expectations.

Studies suggest: over a quarter of all hedge funds fail within the first 3 years due to performance problems → investor defections → fund closure.

Hedge fund indexes that include only currently operating funds and exclude those no longer available overstate true average returns.

Backfill Bias (Instant History Bias)

The selective inclusion of prior performance data for newly added funds in an index — inflating average reported returns.

Example: Manager launches several funds simultaneously → only includes the most successful ones in an index a few years later → selectively "backfills" favorable history.

The selective backfilling of only successful funds serves to increase reported average returns.

⚠️ Combined Effect of Both Biases

Hedge fund indexes may NOT reflect actual average hedge fund performance — they reflect only the returns of funds that: (1) initially performed best (backfill bias) AND (2) have not failed (survivorship bias). The true average hedge fund return is likely considerably lower than index performance suggests. Investors using hedge fund index returns to set expectations are likely to be disappointed.

Bias What Is Excluded / Distorted Direction of Effect Asset Class Most Affected
Survivorship Bias Failed/closed funds excluded from historical benchmark returns Overstates reported returns (upward bias) Hedge funds (25%+ fail within 3 years)
Backfill Bias Only successful fund histories selectively added to index Overstates reported returns (upward bias) Hedge funds; any index with voluntary reporting
Vintage Year Mismatch Comparing funds at different life cycle phases Misleading performance comparisons PE and real estate
