01

What Are Alternative Investments?

Alternative Investments
Investments other than ownership of public equity securities, fixed-income instruments, or cash. Grouped together not because they are similar to each other, but because they are distinct from traditional assets. Categories: private capital, real assets, and hedge funds.

Key Features That Distinguish Alternatives

Distinctive Characteristics
  • Need for specialized knowledge to value cash flows and risks
  • Typically low correlation with traditional asset classes
  • Illiquidity and long investment time horizons
  • Large capital outlays required
  • Less efficient markets
Resulting Structural Features
  • Different investment structures (LP/GP partnerships)
  • Incentive-based fees to address information asymmetry
  • Performance appraisal challenges
  • More complex, richer compensation structures
  • Longer commitment periods (PE fund life ~10 years)

Why Invest in Alternatives?

  • Greater portfolio diversification — low correlation with public equity and bonds
  • Higher expected returns — illiquidity premium; access to less efficient markets
  • Investors accept: longer investment periods, reduced liquidity, and less efficient markets
  • Most investors limit alternatives to the portion of their portfolio designated for obligations several years in the future
  • Largest allocators: large pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and not-for-profit endowments — have the longest time horizons and highest sophistication
💡 The Three Alternative Investment Categories

Private Capital = private equity + private debt
Real Assets = real estate + infrastructure + natural resources + commodities + collectibles + digital assets
Hedge Funds = distinguished by investment approach (leverage, derivatives, short selling), not asset class

02

Private Capital

Private Capital
Funding provided to companies sourced from neither the public equity nor the public debt markets. Includes private equity (ownership) and private debt (loans/debt instruments).
Private Equity (PE)

Investment in privately owned companies or in public companies with intent to take them private.

  • Leveraged buyouts (LBOs): Key approach for mature or declining firms; uses debt to acquire and restructure companies over several years
  • PE managers use greater control and flexibility (vs. public) to make management/strategy changes, close/sell/reorganize business lines to increase profitability
  • Venture capital (VC): Specialized PE for non-public companies in early life cycle or startup phase; uses ownership capital where an idea/business plan exists with limited operations or customers
  • Both private and public equity investors are company owners with residual claims on future cash flows and dividends
  • Key difference: private equity investors may have full access to company information and can influence day-to-day management; public equity investors only receive publicly available information with voting rights limited to shareholder approval decisions
Private Debt
  • Private loans or bonds — direct lending to private companies without going through public markets
  • Venture debt: Extended to early-stage firms with little or no cash flow (alongside venture capital)
  • Distressed debt: Public or private debt of corporate issuers believed to be close to or in bankruptcy; benefits from investors with capital restructuring skills
💡 PE vs. VC — Life Cycle Stage

Venture capital → early stage / startup (idea or limited operations). Private equity (LBO) → mature or declining stage (restructuring for profitability). Both are forms of private equity — VC is a specialized subset of PE.

03

Real Assets

Real Assets
Generally tangible physical assets (real estate, natural resources) but also include intangibles (patents, intellectual property, goodwill). Either generate current/expected future cash flows AND/OR are considered a store of value.
Category Definition Examples Cash Flow Source
Real Estate Borrowed or ownership capital in buildings or land Commercial (offices, retail), residential, industrial Commercial: private business activity. Residential: rents or mortgage payments
Infrastructure Long-lived fixed assets for public use providing essential services Bridges, toll roads, airports, broadband networks, utilities Fees, leases, access rights OR indirectly via economic growth → government tax revenue
Natural Resources (developed) Less developed land as source of economic value Farmland, timberland, mineral/energy deposits Crop yields, agricultural lease payments, future timber harvests, mineral/drilling rights
Commodities Standardized, traded goods Energy (oil, gas), metals, agriculture, livestock No direct cash flow — return from price changes. Inflation hedge. Countercyclical.
Collectibles Tangible rare assets Fine art, wine, rare coins, watches Price appreciation; no income yield
Digital Assets Assets created, stored, transmitted electronically with ownership/use rights Cryptocurrencies, tokens, NFTs Price appreciation; utility/governance rights

Infrastructure — Key Details

  • Public-Private Partnership (PPP): Agreement between public sector and private sector to finance, build, and operate public infrastructure. Used to attract long-term private investment.
  • Special purpose entity (SPE): Often raises borrowed and ownership capital for construction and operation of a specific asset (road, bridge, etc.)
  • Concession agreement: Contract governing private investor's obligations to construct and maintain infrastructure AND the exclusive right to operate and earn fees for a pre-determined period, after which the asset reverts to the public sector
  • Infrastructure assets are long-lived and provide essential services — relatively stable, predictable cash flows

Commodities — Investment Rationale

  • Commodities themselves do not generate cash flows — ultimate return comes from price changes when sold by producers to consumers
  • Investors benefit from: (1) commodity price appreciation based on future economic use, (2) lower correlation with other asset classes over the economic cycle
  • Commodities serve as a countercyclical holding and an inflation hedge

Publicly Traded Forms of Real Assets

  • REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts): Equity securities — publicly traded form of real estate investment
  • Mortgage-backed securities (MBS): Publicly traded debt form of real estate exposure
  • Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs): Common for real estate or natural resource fund investors; similar to LP structure but more liquid and often publicly traded
  • Other liquid alternatives: commodity funds, ETFs
04

Hedge Funds & Digital Assets

Hedge Funds

Private investment vehicles distinguished by their investment approach rather than by the investments themselves. May invest in public equities, public fixed income, private capital, AND/OR real assets.

  • Frequent use of: leverage, derivatives, short selling, and other strategies
  • Results in a substantially different risk and return profile from simply buying and holding the underlying assets
  • Fund of funds: An investor investing in a portfolio of hedge funds (adds another layer of fees but provides additional diversification)
⚠️ Exam Trap — Hedge Funds vs. Other Alternatives

Hedge funds are distinguished by their investment APPROACH (leverage, derivatives, short selling), NOT by the asset classes they invest in. They may actually hold the same underlying assets as traditional investors — the difference is how they use them. Private capital and real assets are distinguished by the asset type; hedge funds are not.

Digital Assets

Umbrella term for assets that can be created, stored, and transmitted electronically with associated ownership or use rights.

  • Cryptocurrencies: Have their own blockchain (native asset). Examples: Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH). Use cryptography for authenticity.
  • Tokens: Built on an existing blockchain (not their own). Examples: ERC-20 tokens on the Ethereum ecosystem, used in DeFi and NFT marketplaces.
  • NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens): Digital collectibles — unique, non-interchangeable digital assets
  • Key distinction: cryptocurrencies have their own blockchains; tokens do not — they are built on another chain (e.g., Ethereum)

Three Investment Methods

Investors can access alternative investments in three ways. Institutional investors typically begin with funds, then progress to co-investing and direct investing as they gain experience.

Progression: Fund → Co-investment → Direct investment (increasing sophistication, control, and lower fees; but higher oversight costs)

Method 1 — Fund Investment (Indirect)

Investor contributes capital to a fund → fund identifies, selects, and manages investments on the investor's behalf. Most common starting point for institutional investors.

  • Control: Minimal — investment decision is limited to investing in the fund or not; cannot affect underlying investments
  • Fees: Highest — management fee + performance fee
  • Transparency: Lowest — less frequent reporting on returns and positions
  • Expertise required: Lowest — investor relies on manager's specialized knowledge
  • Pre-commitment: Funds require pre-commitment of capital prior to investment selection; extended period during which fund may not be sold
  • Available for: hedge funds, private capital, real estate, infrastructure, natural resources
  • Suited for: investors with limited resources and/or experience
Method 2 — Co-Investment

Investor invests in assets indirectly via the fund AND also possesses co-investment rights to invest directly alongside the fund in the same deals. A middle ground between fund and direct investing.

  • Control: Moderate — investor makes additional direct investment in specific deals the fund identifies
  • Fees: Lower than fund-only — investor access to deals at lower fee than pure fund investment
  • For investors: Expands knowledge and experience; learn from fund's process; prepare for direct investing
  • For managers (GP benefits of offering co-investment):
    • Accelerate investment timing when available funds are insufficient for a specific deal
    • Expand the scope of available new investments
    • Increase diversification of existing fund investment pool
  • Co-investors weigh: benefits of greater control and lower fees vs. higher oversight costs
Method 3 — Direct Investment

Largest, most sophisticated investors invest without using an intermediary fund. Maximum flexibility and control.

  • Control: Maximum — over investment choice, financing methods, and timing
  • Fees: Lowest — no external fund manager or GP
  • Expertise required: Highest — must have internal specialized knowledge, skills, and oversight capabilities
  • In private equity: purchase a direct stake in a private company without using an external fund manager/GP
  • Primarily applies to: private capital and real estate; some very large investors (pension funds, sovereign wealth funds) also invest directly in infrastructure and natural resources
  • The largest, most sophisticated direct investors (some sovereign wealth funds) compete directly with fund managers for the best investment opportunities

Ownership Structures — Limited Partnership (LP/GP)

Limited Partnership Structure
The most common alternative investment ownership structure. Maximizes flexibility in allocating business risk, return, and responsibilities between investors (LPs) and managers (GPs).
General Partner (GP)
  • Manages the fund; theoretically unlimited liability
  • Responsible for all fund operations and decisions
  • Buys/sells assets, borrows funds, establishes reserves, enters contracts
  • May manage multiple funds simultaneously
  • Receives management fee + performance fee (carried interest)
  • Has specialized knowledge and control → information asymmetry vs. LPs
Limited Partners (LPs)
  • Outside investors; liability capped at amount invested
  • Own a fractional interest based on initial investment and LPA terms
  • Play passive roles — not involved in day-to-day management
  • Commit to future capital contributions (upfront cash = small portion of total commitment)
  • Must meet minimum regulatory net worth / institutional requirements (accredited investors)
  • Restricted number allowed per fund
  • Co-investment rights allow LPs to make additional direct investments in portfolio companies

Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA)

The governing document of the limited partnership. Key features:

  • Distribution of profits and losses between GP and LPs
  • Manager roles and responsibilities: investment criteria and restrictions
  • Terms governing transfers, withdrawals, and dissolution

Side Letter: A supplemental document issued between a GP and one or more specific LPs, with terms that override or modify the original LPA terms. May include:

  • Increased ability to transfer investments to a related/successor fund
  • First right of refusal and other preferential terms
  • Excusal right: Ability to forgo a contractual capital contribution
  • Additional investment reporting beyond standard LPA
  • "Most Favored Nation" (MFN) clause: Ensures any more favorable terms negotiated with other LPs will also apply to this LP

Contrast with public bond indenture: LPA and side letters are highly customized per investor; bond indentures are standardized for all bondholders.

Other Alternative Investment Ownership Structures

  • Master Limited Partnership (MLP): Similar to LP but more liquid and often publicly traded. Common for real estate and natural resource fund investors (classified as unitholders).
  • Public-Private Partnership (PPP): Agreement between public and private sectors to finance, build, and operate public infrastructure. Often uses a special purpose entity (SPE) and governed by a concession agreement.
  • Joint Ventures: Common for direct real estate investments. Two or more parties share ownership and management.
  • Trusts and Limited Liability Companies (LLCs): Other legal structures for alternative investments
  • REITs: Equity securities that are publicly traded forms of real estate

Compensation Structures — Fees & Hurdle Rate

Asymmetry of information between GP (specialized knowledge + control) and LPs requires complex, incentive-aligned compensation structures. Traditional public funds: flat % of AUM. Alternative funds: management fee + performance fee.

Standard fee structure for alternatives:

  • Management fee: 1%–2% of assets under management (hedge funds) OR of committed capital (private equity)
  • Performance fee (incentive fee / carried interest): Based on a percentage of periodic fund returns above a hurdle rate
⚠️ Exam Trap — Committed Capital vs. AUM for Management Fees

Private equity funds: Management fee typically based on committed capital (total LPs have promised), NOT invested capital or AUM. Reason: using committed capital (not AUM) reduces the incentive for GPs to deploy capital hastily to maximize near-term fee income — allows GPs to be selective. Also, since GPs have so much influence over asset values, basing fees on AUM would be inappropriate.
Hedge funds and REITs: Management fees based on AUM (assets under management).

Hurdle Rate (Preferred Return)

A minimum fund return that must be achieved before the manager earns a performance fee. Also called the "preferred return" — LPs receive the hurdle rate first before GPs earn performance fees.

Hard Hurdle Rate — GP Performance Fee Formula
rGP = max[0, p × (r − rh)]

where:
r = fund rate of return for the period
rh = hard hurdle rate
p = GP performance fee % (e.g., 20%)

Example: r = 18%, rh = 8%, p = 20%
rGP = max[0, 20% × (18% − 8%)] = max[0, 20% × 10%] = 2.0%
Hard hurdle rate: manager earns performance fee only on returns IN EXCESS of the hurdle rate.
Soft hurdle rate: once the hurdle is exceeded, the fee is calculated on the ENTIRE return (not just the excess). GP can "catch up" once hurdle is exceeded.
Hard Hurdle Rate

Manager earns performance fee only on returns IN EXCESS of the hurdle rate.

Example: Hurdle = 8%, Fund return = 18%, Performance fee = 20%
Fee = 20% × (18% − 8%) = 2% on excess only

More favorable to LPs — GP earns less.

Soft Hurdle Rate

Once the hurdle is exceeded, fee is calculated on the ENTIRE return (not just the excess).

GP can "catch up" — receives 100% of distributions after the hurdle until profit split reaches the agreed ratio (e.g., 80/20).

More favorable to GPs — larger total fee.

Catch-Up Clause, High-Water Mark & Clawback

Catch-Up Clause

The acceleration of performance fees once a fund exceeds the soft hurdle rate. Under a catch-up clause:

  1. LP receives 100% of all distributions until the hurdle rate (e.g., 8%) is met
  2. GP then receives 100% of distributions until the profit split reaches the agreed ratio (e.g., 80/20 LP/GP)
  3. Remaining distributions split at the agreed ratio (e.g., 80/20)
GP Return with Catch-Up Clause
rGP = max[0, rcu + p × (r − rh − rcu)]

where rcu = catch-up return to GP (e.g., 2%)

Example: r = 18%, rh = 8%, rcu = 2%, p = 20%
rGP = max[0, 2.0% + 20% × (18% − 8% − 2%)]
rGP = max[0, 2.0% + 20% × 8%] = 2.0% + 1.6% = 3.6%

Without catch-up: rGP = 2.0% → With catch-up: rGP = 3.6% (+1.6% to GP)
The catch-up clause allows the GP to receive a larger total fee once the hurdle is exceeded, making the soft hurdle more valuable to the GP than the hard hurdle.
Under the catch-up clause: LP gets 100% first (to meet hurdle), then GP gets 100% (to "catch up" to agreed split), then both share at agreed ratio.

High-Water Mark (HWM)

High-Water Mark
The fund's peak value as of a performance calculation date, net of fees. If the fund's value subsequently declines below the HWM, the hedge fund manager may NOT charge performance fees until the fund value exceeds the previous HWM. Purpose: reward sustained performance and protect LPs from paying twice for the same returns.

How HWM works:

  • Fund reaches peak of $120M → HWM = $120M
  • Fund drops to $90M → NO performance fees payable (below HWM)
  • Fund recovers to $95M → still below HWM ($120M) → NO performance fees
  • Fund rises to $125M → now above HWM → performance fees payable on the $5M gain above $120M HWM

Hedge fund nuance: Investor high-water marks generally carry over into new calendar years, but in hedge funds, an investor may no longer claw back incentive fees paid in a prior calendar year if losses are incurred later.

Clawback Provision

Clawback Provision
Grants LPs the right to reclaim a portion of the GP's previously paid performance fees. Activated when GP exits successful deals early but incurs losses on deals later in the fund's life. More common in private equity and real estate (illiquid, longer-term) than hedge funds. Applies for the entire life of the portfolio in PE/real estate.
💡 HWM vs. Clawback — Know the Distinction

High-water mark: Prevents future performance fees until prior losses are recovered — FORWARD-looking protection. Used primarily in hedge funds.
Clawback: Requires GP to return previously paid performance fees if later losses occur — BACKWARD-looking reclaim. Used primarily in private equity and real estate (more illiquid, longer-term). LPs literally get money back from the GP.

Waterfall Structures — Distribution of Cash Flows

Waterfall Structure
Determines the distribution of cash flows between GPs and LPs in alternative investment funds. GPs typically receive a disproportionately large share of profits relative to their initial investment (to incentivize maximizing profitability). Two types: deal-by-deal (American) and whole-of-fund (European).
Deal-by-Deal Waterfall (American)

More advantageous to the GP.

  • Performance fees collected on a per-deal basis
  • GP gets paid before LPs receive both their initial investment AND the hurdle rate on the entire fund
  • GP can earn fees from early successful deals even if later deals underperform
  • Often paired with a clawback provision to address potential LP disadvantage
  • GP faces risk of having to return fees (via clawback) if total fund performance falls short
Whole-of-Fund Waterfall (European)

More advantageous to the LPs.

  • Distributions go to LPs as deals are exited
  • GP does not participate in any profits until LPs receive their initial investment AND the hurdle rate has been met at the aggregate fund level
  • GP only paid after all LP capital returned + hurdle achieved across the entire fund
  • Less need for clawback provisions (GP hasn't been paid early)
  • If fund breaks even: GP receives NO performance payouts
⚠️ Waterfall Exam Example — Fund That Breaks Even

Deal-by-deal waterfall: GP earns $2M, $3M, $8M on early successful deals. Later deals break even or lose. If the fund breaks even overall → GP must return all early payouts via clawback.
Whole-of-fund waterfall: GP receives no payouts until LPs are fully returned + hurdle met. If fund breaks even → GP receives $0 in performance fees.
Same fund, same result — but the timing and mechanism differ: one is a payment + clawback, the other is no payment at all.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Alternative investments definition: NOT public equity, fixed income, or cash. Three categories: (1) Private capital (PE + private debt), (2) Real assets (real estate, infrastructure, natural resources, commodities, collectibles, digital assets), (3) Hedge funds. Grouped by distinction from traditional assets, not similarity to each other.
  • ✅ Key features of alternatives: Specialized knowledge needed, low correlation with traditional assets, illiquidity, long horizons, large capital outlays. Lead to: complex structures, incentive-based fees, appraisal challenges.
  • ✅ Why invest in alternatives: Greater diversification (low correlation), higher expected returns (illiquidity premium). Trade-off: longer periods, lower liquidity, less efficient markets. Largest allocators: large pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, endowments.
  • ✅ Private equity: Investment in private companies OR public companies taken private. LBOs = mature/declining firms. VC = early stage/startup. PE investors: full info access, can influence management. Public equity investors: only public info, voting limited.
  • ✅ VC vs. PE life cycle: VC = early stage / startup. LBO = mature or declining stage.
  • ✅ Private debt types: Private loans/bonds, venture debt (early-stage firms, little/no cash flow), distressed debt (near-bankrupt companies; benefits restructuring-skilled investors).
  • ✅ Real assets: Tangible physical or intangible assets that generate cash flows AND/OR store of value. Real estate (commercial: business activity; residential: rents/mortgages), infrastructure (essential services; concession agreements), natural resources (farmland, timberland, minerals), commodities (no cash flows; inflation hedge; countercyclical), collectibles, digital assets.
  • ✅ Infrastructure: Long-lived, essential public services. PPP = public-private partnership. Concession agreement = private investor builds/operates for set period → reverts to public sector. SPE often used to raise capital for a specific project.
  • ✅ Commodities: Do NOT generate cash flows directly. Return from price changes. Low correlation with other assets → inflation hedge and countercyclical holding.
  • ✅ Publicly traded alternatives: REITs (equity, real estate), MBS (debt, real estate), MLPs (natural resources/real estate, more liquid than standard LP), commodity funds, ETFs.
  • ✅ Hedge funds: Distinguished by INVESTMENT APPROACH (leverage, derivatives, short selling), NOT asset class. May invest in anything. Fund of funds = portfolio of hedge funds.
  • ✅ Digital assets: Cryptocurrencies = have OWN blockchain (Bitcoin, Ethereum). Tokens = built on EXISTING blockchain (ERC-20 tokens on Ethereum). NFTs = non-fungible digital collectibles.
  • ✅ 3 investment methods: Fund (least control, highest fees, least expertise) → Co-investment (moderate; lower fees; learn from fund) → Direct (maximum control, lowest fees, most expertise). Progression: fund → co-invest → direct as sophistication grows.
  • ✅ Co-investment — GP benefits: (1) Accelerate timing when funds insufficient for a deal, (2) Expand scope of new investments, (3) Increase diversification.
  • ✅ Fund investment unique features: Pre-commitment of capital before investment selection; extended lock-up period; higher/more complex fees; less frequent transparency on returns and positions.
  • ✅ LP/GP structure: GP = manager, unlimited liability, controls fund. LPs = passive investors, liability capped at investment amount, accredited investors, restricted number per fund.
  • ✅ LPA (Limited Partnership Agreement): Governs profit/loss distribution, manager roles/restrictions, transfer/withdrawal/dissolution terms. Side letters modify LPA for specific LPs — can include MFN clause, excusal rights, additional reporting. Contrast: LPA is customized (vs. standardized bond indentures).
  • ✅ PE management fees: Typically based on COMMITTED CAPITAL (not AUM). Reason: reduces incentive to deploy capital hastily; GP has too much influence over asset values to use AUM. Hedge fund management fees: based on AUM. PE fund life: typically 10 years. PE capital drawdown: generally over 3–5 years.
  • ✅ Standard alt fee structure: Management fee (1%–2%) + performance fee (carried interest, % of returns above hurdle). Performance fee aligns manager with investor over long periods.
  • ✅ Hard hurdle rate: Performance fee on returns IN EXCESS of hurdle only. rGP = max[0, p(r − rh)]. More favorable to LPs.
  • ✅ Soft hurdle rate: Once hurdle exceeded, fee calculated on ENTIRE return. GP can "catch up." More favorable to GPs.
  • ✅ Catch-up clause formula: rGP = max[0, rcu + p(r − rh − rcu)]. Under catch-up: LP gets 100% to hurdle → GP gets 100% to catch up to agreed split → both share at agreed ratio (e.g., 80/20).
  • ✅ High-water mark: Fund's peak value net of fees. No performance fees until fund exceeds prior HWM. Prevents LPs paying twice for same returns. Primarily used in hedge funds. Carries over into new calendar years; hedge fund HWM may not survive year-end.
  • ✅ Clawback: LPs reclaim previously paid GP performance fees if later losses occur. Activated when early wins → later losses. More common in PE/real estate (illiquid, long-term) than hedge funds. Applies entire fund life in PE/real estate.
  • ✅ Waterfall — deal-by-deal (American): Performance fees per deal. GP paid before LPs fully recovered. More favorable to GP. Often requires clawback provision for protection.
  • ✅ Waterfall — whole-of-fund (European): GP paid ONLY after LPs receive full capital return + hurdle rate at aggregate fund level. More favorable to LPs. Less need for clawback. If fund breaks even: GP gets $0.
