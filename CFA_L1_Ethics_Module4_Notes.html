01

Why GIPS Were Created — The Problem They Solve

Before GIPS, investment firms reported performance however they chose. There was no standardised methodology, no requirement to present complete track records, and no way for a prospective client to make a fair apples-to-apples comparison between two competing firms. Widespread manipulation of performance data was possible — and common.

GIPS are a practitioner-driven set of ethical principles that establish a standardised, industry-wide approach for calculating and presenting historical investment results to prospective clients. They are built on two core principles: fair representation and full disclosure.

GIPS Mission Statement

"To promote ethics and integrity and instill trust through the use of the GIPS standards by achieving universal demand for compliance by asset owners, adoption by asset managers, and support from regulators for the ultimate benefit of the global investment community."

Five Objectives of GIPS

1 · Investor Protection

Promote investor interests and instill investor confidence in reported performance.

2 · Data Accuracy

Ensure accurate and consistent performance data that can be relied upon for decision-making.

3 · Single Global Standard

Obtain worldwide acceptance of one standard for calculating and presenting performance.

4 · Fair Global Competition

Promote fair competition among investment firms globally — regardless of country of domicile.

5 · Industry Self-Regulation

Promote self-regulation within the industry on a global basis — reducing dependence on government mandates.

💡 GIPS in Context of the CFA Standards

GIPS relate directly to Standard III(D) — Performance Presentation. Under III(D), members must make "reasonable efforts to ensure that performance information is fair, accurate, and complete." GIPS compliance is not required by the Standards — but it represents best practice. If a firm claims GIPS compliance, that claim must be accurate (otherwise it's a III(D) or I(C) misrepresentation violation).

02

The Three Pre-GIPS Abuses GIPS Were Designed to Eliminate

These three misleading practices are the entire reason GIPS exists. The exam tests whether you recognise each one and understand exactly which GIPS requirement eliminates it. Learn them as a set — they are frequently presented in scenario form.

Abuse #1
Representative Accounts — Cherry-Picking
Selecting a single top-performing portfolio to represent the firm's overall results for a strategy. A firm managing 20 Global Equity accounts presents only the best-performing one to prospects, creating a false impression of firm-wide results.
✓ GIPS Fix: Composite requirement. ALL fee-paying, discretionary accounts managed under the same strategy must be included in the composite — no selection bias permitted.
Abuse #2
Survivorship Bias — Hiding Poor Performers
Calculating an "average" performance track record that excludes accounts that were terminated because of poor performance. This systematically overstates the firm's typical results — only the "survivors" (better performers) remain in the sample.
✓ GIPS Fix: Historical inclusion requirement. Terminated accounts must remain in historical composite records for the periods they were managed. Cannot retroactively remove failed accounts from history.
Abuse #3
Varying Time Periods — Cherry-Picking Windows
Presenting performance only for a cherry-picked time period during which the strategy performed exceptionally well or beat its benchmark. Comparing firms with different time period selections is impossible.
✓ GIPS Fix: Standardised, continuous, complete performance history. Must present a minimum 5-year history (or since inception if shorter) and build up to 10 years. Consistent methodology across all periods.
🎯 Examiner Focus — Recognising the Three Abuses in Scenarios

The exam will describe a firm's performance reporting practice and ask which abuse it represents, or ask which GIPS requirement eliminates a described problem. Cherry-picking accounts = Representative Accounts / solved by composites. Removing failed accounts from history = Survivorship Bias / solved by mandatory historical inclusion. Best time period only = Varying Time Periods / solved by standardised complete history. These are distinct — do not conflate "picking accounts" with "picking time periods."

03

Who Can (and Cannot) Claim GIPS Compliance

This is one of the most heavily tested areas of this module. The rules about who can claim compliance are very specific and the exam loves to test edge cases — particularly consultants, software vendors, and asset owners.

✓ CAN Claim Compliance
  • Investment management firms that manage actual assets
  • Asset owners that compete for business (same as firms)
  • Asset owners that don't compete but report to an oversight body — using the GIPS Standards for Asset Owners
✗ CANNOT Claim Compliance
  • Consultants — unless they actually manage the assets
  • Software vendors — software cannot "comply"
  • Individuals — compliance is firm-wide, not individual
  • Single composites or products — cannot comply in isolation
◎ Special Cases
  • Consultants can endorse GIPS and require their managers to comply
  • Software can assist firms in achieving compliance
  • Non-competing asset owners use GIPS Standards for Asset Owners (different chapter)
⚠️ The Binary Compliance Rule — No Middle Ground

A firm has exactly two options:

Option 1: Fully comply with ALL requirements of GIPS → claim compliance using the exact GIPS Compliance Statement.

Option 2: Not comply with all requirements → make NO claim of compliance whatsoever and make NO reference to GIPS standards.

There is no "substantially compliant," "mostly compliant," or "compliant with most requirements." Partial compliance = no compliance claim. This is the single most important rule about GIPS compliance and it appears on nearly every exam.

💡 GIPS Compliance is Voluntary — But Not Costless

Compliance with GIPS is voluntary — it is not required by law or regulation in most jurisdictions. However, once a firm chooses to claim compliance, it is bound by all requirements. And if it falsely claims compliance, it violates GIPS and Standard I(C) Misrepresentation and III(D) Performance Presentation simultaneously.

🎯 Examiner Focus — The Consultant and Software Traps

Two edge cases that consistently appear: (1) Can a consultant claim GIPS compliance? Only if the consultant actually manages the assets. A pure advisory/consulting role = cannot claim compliance. They can claim to "endorse" or "require" GIPS compliance by their managers. (2) Can software be GIPS compliant? No — only a firm can be compliant. Software can be designed to assist compliance but the compliance claim rests with the firm, not the tool. Watch for answer choices saying "the firm uses GIPS-compliant software therefore it is compliant" — this is wrong.

Critical nuance the exam exploits: GIPS compliance does not guarantee investment performance, does not eliminate the need for due diligence, and does not ensure that any specific performance report is accurate. Prospective clients still need to evaluate the quality of the manager, not just the integrity of the reporting. Answer choices suggesting "GIPS eliminates the need for due diligence" are always wrong.

05

Composites — The Core Anti-Cherry-Picking Mechanism

The composite requirement is the single most important technical feature of GIPS. Everything about composites is designed to prevent firms from cherry-picking which portfolios represent their strategy. If you understand why composites exist, the specific rules follow logically.

Definition — What Is a Composite?

A composite is an aggregation of one or more portfolios managed according to a similar investment mandate, objective, or strategy.

  • Must include ALL actual, fee-paying, discretionary portfolios managed under that strategy
  • Pooled funds must also be included in any composite for which they meet the composite definition
  • Inclusion criteria must be established ex ante (in advance), not after the fact — prevents retroactive selection
  • Cannot subjectively select which accounts go in or out of the composite
  • Historical composites must include terminated accounts — cannot be removed retroactively

What Must Be Included in a Composite

✓ Must Be Included
  • All actual portfolios (not simulated/back-tested)
  • All fee-paying portfolios (fees actually charged)
  • All discretionary portfolios (firm can implement its strategy)
  • Accounts that were later terminated — for the period they were managed
  • All pooled funds that meet the composite definition
✗ Must Be Excluded
  • Non-fee-paying accounts (e.g., firm's own money — unless firm chooses to include)
  • Non-discretionary portfolios (client restrictions prevent strategy implementation)
  • Back-tested or simulated portfolios
  • Accounts the firm selects after the fact based on performance
🎯 Examiner Focus — The Ex Ante Criterion Rule

This is the most technically precise rule in composites and it's directly testable. Composite membership criteria must be established before the performance period, not after observing performance. Why? Because allowing after-the-fact assignment would enable cherry-picking — a firm could observe which accounts performed best, then retroactively define those as the "composite." The GIPS requirement that criteria be set ex ante eliminates this loophole entirely. Exam scenario: "A firm reviews its Global Equity accounts at year-end and assigns the best performers to the composite." This violates GIPS — assignment must follow pre-established criteria applied at account opening or strategy assignment, not based on observed performance.

⚠️ Terminated Accounts Cannot Be Removed from History

This directly addresses the survivorship bias problem. If a poorly performing account is closed by the client, that account's performance during the period it was managed must remain in the composite's historical record. A firm cannot "clean up" its history by removing failed accounts. The composite's past performance must reflect all accounts that were managed under that strategy — winners and losers alike. Watch for exam scenarios where a firm "excludes closed accounts" — this is a GIPS violation.

🎯 Examiner Focus — Fee-Paying vs. Non-Fee-Paying

The composite requirement applies to fee-paying discretionary accounts. Non-fee-paying accounts (e.g., the firm managing its own money, or a pro bono account for a charitable foundation) are not required to be in composites — though firms may choose to include them with appropriate disclosure. The key: GIPS is about fair presentation to prospective fee-paying clients, so the composite must reflect the experience of actual paying clients, not internal or complimentary accounts which may be managed with different care or constraints.

06

Fundamentals of Compliance

Several core principles form the foundation for GIPS compliance. Beyond composites and firm definition, the GIPS standards require that firms provide GIPS Reports to all prospective clients and certain pooled fund prospective investors, adhere to applicable laws and regulations, and ensure all information presented is not false or misleading.

Key Compliance Principles
  • Firm-wide process: GIPS compliance cannot be achieved on a single composite, product, or strategy — it must apply to the entire firm
  • All requirements must be met: includes the main GIPS standards text, Guidance Statements, interpretations, and Q&As published by CFA Institute and GIPS governing bodies
  • Fair representation and full disclosure are the overarching goals. Meeting minimum requirements may not be sufficient to achieve these goals — firms should also follow GIPS recommendations (best practice), not just requirements (mandatory minimum)
  • GIPS will continue to evolve: the standards do not address every aspect of performance measurement; they are updated periodically
  • Input data integrity: underlying valuations of portfolio holdings drive performance — the quality of input data is critical. Accurate valuations are the foundation of accurate performance.
💡 Requirements vs. Recommendations — A Key GIPS Distinction

GIPS has two tiers of guidance. Requirements are mandatory — firms must follow them to claim compliance. Recommendations are best practice — firms are encouraged but not required to follow them. Meeting only requirements may not be sufficient to fully achieve "fair representation and full disclosure." Exam: if asked whether a firm is GIPS compliant because it follows all requirements, the answer is: yes for compliance purposes — but the spirit of GIPS encourages following recommendations too.

Definition of the Firm

Because GIPS compliance is firm-wide, the first critical question is: what counts as "the firm"? The definition of the firm establishes the boundary within which total firm assets must be counted and across which compliance must apply. Without a clear firm definition, a company could manipulate compliance by claiming only its best-performing division is "the firm."

The GIPS Definition of the Firm

"The firm should adopt the broadest, most meaningful definition of the firm. The scope of this definition should include all geographical (country, regional, etc.) offices operating under the same brand name, regardless of the actual name of the individual investment management company."

  • Broadest, most meaningful definition — the default is to include more, not less
  • All geographical offices under the same brand are included — even if legally separate entities
  • The legal name of the subsidiary does not override the brand — if it operates as "XYZ Asset Management London," it is included with "XYZ Asset Management New York" even if they are different legal entities
  • This prevents a firm from claiming GIPS compliance for only its best-performing regional office or division
✓ Correct Firm Definition
  • All global offices under the same brand included
  • All strategies/mandates offered by the firm included
  • Definition applied consistently and broadly
  • All assets under management counted firm-wide
✗ Manipulated Firm Definition
  • Claiming only the equity division is "the firm"
  • Excluding underperforming regional offices from firm scope
  • Defining "firm" narrowly to match only strong-performing units
  • Separating by legal entity to escape firm-wide requirement
🎯 Examiner Focus — Why Firm Definition Matters

If a firm could define itself narrowly, it would simply exclude its worst-performing divisions or offices and claim compliance only for the "good" part of the business. GIPS prevents this by requiring the broadest reasonable definition. Exam scenario: "Apex Asset Management has offices in London and New York under the same brand name, but they are legally separate companies. Can the New York office claim GIPS compliance independently?" Answer: no — they are both part of "the firm" and compliance must be firm-wide, covering both offices. Legal separation does not override brand-level firm definition.

08

Discretion — Which Portfolios Must Be in a Composite

The composite requirement applies only to discretionary accounts. Discretion determines whether a portfolio must be included in a composite. If client-imposed restrictions prevent the firm from implementing its intended strategy, the portfolio may be classified as non-discretionary and excluded from the composite.

Discretionary Portfolio
  • Firm can implement its investment strategy without material interference
  • Must be included in the appropriate composite
  • Minor client constraints (e.g., "no tobacco stocks") that don't significantly change the strategy = still discretionary
  • The firm exercises meaningful control over the portfolio's construction
Non-Discretionary Portfolio
  • Client-imposed restrictions interfere materially with the intended strategy
  • Must NOT be included in a firm's composites
  • Example: a client says "only hold stocks from this pre-approved list" — the firm cannot implement its strategy
  • Portfolio is no longer representative of the firm's strategy
🎯 Examiner Focus — The Discretion Line

The exam tests where minor restrictions end and non-discretion begins. The standard: if client-imposed restrictions prevent the portfolio from being representative of the firm's intended strategy, it is non-discretionary. Minor constraints that don't materially affect strategy implementation = still discretionary, still must be included in the composite. Major constraints that fundamentally change the portfolio = non-discretionary, must be excluded. Watch for: a firm excluding an account from its composite because the account performed poorly — this is a GIPS violation unless the account was genuinely non-discretionary. Poor performance alone is not grounds for exclusion.

⚠️ Non-Discretionary Exclusion Cannot Be Used to Game the Composite

Firms cannot abuse the non-discretionary classification to exclude underperformers. If a client's restrictions are minor — even if the firm dislikes the constraint — the portfolio remains discretionary and must be in the composite. The firm's definition of discretion must be applied consistently and documented. Using the "non-discretionary" label selectively on poor performers while including outperformers with similar restriction levels = GIPS violation.

Independent Verification

Verification is a separate, voluntary process distinct from the firm's own claim of compliance. It is conducted by an independent third party and tests the firm's policies and procedures at the firm-wide level. Verification is one of the most tested topics in the GIPS module — particularly what it covers, what it doesn't cover, and what it cannot guarantee.

What Verification Is
  • A process by which an independent third-party verifier conducts testing of a firm on a firm-wide basis
  • Tests whether the firm's policies and procedures related to composite/pooled fund maintenance, calculation, presentation, and distribution of performance have been designed in compliance with GIPS and implemented on a firm-wide basis
  • Brings additional credibility to the firm's claim of compliance
  • May improve the firm's internal processes and performance measurement knowledge
  • Provides marketing advantages — tells prospects an independent party has reviewed the firm's compliance framework
  • Verification is voluntary — not required to claim GIPS compliance
⚠️ What Verification Does NOT Do — Exam Critical

Verification does NOT ensure the accuracy of any specific performance report. It verifies the firm-wide policies and procedures — not the numbers in any individual composite or report. A firm can have fully GIPS-compliant procedures but still have an error in a specific composite's reported figures — verification would not necessarily catch this specific error. Verification also does NOT guarantee that the firm is fully compliant in every detail — it provides reasonable assurance about the framework.

DimensionDetail
Who performs itAn independent third party — firms CANNOT perform their own verification. Self-verification is not permitted.
ScopeFirm-wide — not on specific composites or pooled funds individually
What it testsPolicies and procedures for composite maintenance, calculation, presentation, and distribution — i.e., the framework, not individual numbers
What it does NOT doDoes NOT ensure the accuracy of any specific performance report. Does NOT guarantee full compliance in every detail.
Voluntary or requiredVoluntary. A firm can claim GIPS compliance without ever being verified. But verification adds credibility.
ResultVerified firms may communicate the verification to clients/prospects as an additional assurance. Cannot claim "verified to be accurate" — only "policies and procedures verified."
🎯 Examiner Focus — Verification: The Four Key Rules

Rule 1 — Voluntary: GIPS compliance does not require verification. Many compliant firms are not verified. Rule 2 — Firm-wide: verification is performed on the entire firm, not one composite or product. A firm cannot "verify" its best composite and leave others unverified. Rule 3 — Independent: A firm cannot verify itself. Must be an external, independent verifier. Rule 4 — Specific report accuracy NOT guaranteed: The biggest trap. Verification tests policies and procedures. A specific composite report could still have an error even in a verified firm. Exam question: "A verified firm's GIPS Report must therefore be accurate." → FALSE. Verification provides firm-level procedural assurance, not report-level numerical accuracy.

Benefits of Verification (to the Firm)
  • Increased credibility of compliance claim
  • Marketing differentiation — "independently verified"
  • Improved internal knowledge of GIPS requirements
  • Better consistency and quality of performance reporting systems
  • Greater assurance to prospective clients and investors
Limitations of Verification
  • Does not guarantee compliance in every detail
  • Does not ensure accuracy of specific reports
  • Does not guarantee investment performance
  • Does not eliminate client due diligence obligation
  • Does not confirm individual composite figures are error-free
