Fixed-Income Market Segments — Three Dimensions

Fixed-income instruments and markets are typically categorized along three primary dimensions:

  1. Issuer type (sector): government, corporate, securitized, etc.
  2. Credit quality: investment grade vs. high yield vs. default-risk-free
  3. Time to maturity: short-term (<1yr), intermediate (1–10yr), long-term (>10yr)

Sometimes additionally classified by: geography, currency, ESG characteristics.

Key contrast with equities: corporate issuers typically have only 1–2 equity instruments but many fixed-income instruments outstanding. Example: Apple Inc. had over 80 fixed-income instruments vs. 1 equity instrument (common stock) at end of 2021.

Maturity Classification

Short-Term (<1 year)

Also called money market securities. Meet near-term obligations and serve as liquid cash alternatives. Examples: T-bills, commercial paper, repos, ABCP.

Intermediate-Term (1–10 years)

Capital market securities. Balance between yield and duration risk. Examples: Treasury notes, unsecured corporate bonds, ABS, secured corporate bonds.

Long-Term (>10 years)

Highest interest rate risk; highest expected return. Match long-dated liabilities (pensions, insurance). Examples: Treasury bonds, long-duration corporate bonds, MBS.

Credit Ratings — S&P and Moody's Scales

Credit ratings are letter-grade, qualitative measures of an issuer's ability to meet debt obligations, based on both the probability of default AND the expected loss under a default scenario. Provided by credit rating agencies (S&P, Moody's, Fitch). Given for both short-term and long-term debt, and for both issuers AND specific bond issues.

Investment Grade (IG) — BBB- / Baa3 and Above

AAA / Aaa
Extremely strong capacity to meet financial commitments — highest rating. Sovereign DM issuers often at this level — considered "default risk free."
AA / Aa
Very strong capacity to meet financial commitments
A / A
Strong capacity but somewhat susceptible to adverse economic conditions and changes in circumstances
BBB / Baa
Adequate capacity but more subject to adverse economic conditions
BBB- / Baa3
Lowest investment grade — the critical boundary. Below this = speculative/junk.

Speculative Grade / High Yield — BB+ / Ba1 and Below

BB+ / Ba1
Highest speculative grade — "fallen angel" zone entry point or new high-yield issuers
BB / Ba
Less vulnerable near-term but faces major ongoing uncertainties to adverse business, financial, and economic conditions
B / B
More vulnerable to adverse conditions but currently has capacity to meet financial commitments
CCC / Caa
Currently vulnerable — dependent on favorable conditions to meet commitments
CC / Ca
Highly vulnerable; default has not yet occurred but is expected to be a virtual certainty

Default

D

★ Key Credit Rating Boundaries — Must Memorize
Investment Grade (IG): BBB- (S&P) / Baa3 (Moody's) and ABOVE
High Yield / Junk: BB+ (S&P) / Ba1 (Moody's) and BELOW

Fallen Angels = formerly investment-grade issuers downgraded below BBB- / Baa3
Rising Stars = formerly high-yield issuers upgraded to BBB- / Baa3 or above
S&P uses +/− modifiers (e.g., AA+, AA, AA−). Moody's uses 1/2/3 (e.g., Aa1, Aa2, Aa3).
Credit ratings measure BOTH probability of default AND expected loss severity under default.
Ratings apply to both the ISSUER (overall creditworthiness) and specific BOND ISSUES (can differ for the same issuer).
Many investors are constrained to IG instruments with only a small percentage allowed in unrated or HY bonds.
💡 Fallen Angels vs. Rising Stars

Fallen angels: formerly IG issuers whose credit quality deteriorated — downgraded to HY. Often face forced selling because many IG-only mandated investors must sell below BBB−.

Rising stars: formerly HY issuers upgraded to IG status. Often face increased demand as IG-mandated investors can now buy them.

High-yield issuers include both new issuers with less stable cash flows AND fallen angels. New HY issuers are more likely to require collateral (secured bonds) due to less stable operating cash flows.

💡 Commercial Paper — Key Details

Commercial paper (CP) is a type of short-term bond used by corporations to finance working capital needs. Key features: (1) typically <270 days; (2) can be collateralized by specific assets (Asset-Backed Commercial Paper — ABCP); (3) usually issued at a discount; (4) common among investment-grade issuers who can access this cheap short-term funding. NOT available to high-yield issuers who lack the credit quality to attract CP investors.

💡 Pension Funds & Insurance Companies — Long Duration Preference

Pension funds and insurance companies have long investment time horizons and known long-dated future obligations (pension payments, insurance claims). They favor long-duration fixed-income instruments because: (1) fixed periodic coupon cash flows provide predictable income; (2) the maturity profile of their bond portfolios matches their long-term liabilities (asset-liability matching / immunization strategy). They are therefore the natural buyers of long-dated sovereign and investment-grade corporate bonds.

Fixed-Income Indexes — Purpose, Types & Differences from Equity

Fixed-income indexes track the returns of groups of securities meeting inclusion criteria. They serve three main functions:

  1. Evaluate market performance — measure how the overall bond market (or a segment) performed
  2. Benchmark investment managers — compare a fund's or manager's returns against a relevant index
  3. Basis for indexed investment strategies — passive bond funds/ETFs that replicate index returns

Index choice must match the investment strategy: a short-term IG bond fund should be benchmarked against a short-term IG bond index, NOT a broad aggregate index.

Three Key Differences from Equity Indexes

★ Fixed-Income vs. Equity Indexes — Key Differences
Difference 1 — NUMBER OF CONSTITUENTS:
A single issuer can have MANY bond issues (vs. 1-2 equity instruments)
→ All eligible bonds included → fixed-income indexes far larger than equity indexes
→ Some have 10,000+ constituents

Difference 2 — TURNOVER (REBALANCING FREQUENCY):
Finite bond maturities + higher new issuance frequency → much higher turnover
→ Bond indexes usually rebalanced MONTHLY (not annually like some equity indexes)
→ Each month: new issues added, bonds below minimum maturity removed

Difference 3 — WEIGHTING METHODOLOGY:
Bond indexes weighted by MARKET VALUE OF DEBT OUTSTANDING
(analogous to equity market-cap weighting)
→ Large government issuers → high government debt weights in broad indexes
→ Composition changes over time as new debt is issued or retired
Key implication of high constituent count: bond funds tracking an index typically hold a REPRESENTATIVE SAMPLE (not all constituents) because it is impossible to purchase every bond in large indexes.
Monthly rebalancing: bonds failing to meet maturity, rating, or size criteria are removed; new issues meeting criteria are added.

Types of Fixed-Income Indexes

Broad Aggregate Indexes
  • Very large number of constituents (thousands)
  • Cover many sectors, maturities, currencies
  • Usually investment grade only
  • Example: Bloomberg Barclays Global Aggregate Index
  • Suitable benchmark for diversified IG bond funds
Narrow / Specialized Indexes
  • Filter by one or more specific criteria
  • Sector (corporate, government, securitized)
  • Credit quality (IG only, HY only, EM sovereign)
  • Geography/currency (US only, euro only)
  • Maturity (short-term, long-term)
  • ESG (sustainable, SRI bond indexes)
  • Example: J.P. Morgan EMBI+, Bloomberg MSCI SRI Index
⚠️ Government Bias in Broad Indexes

Because government issuers are among the largest bond issuers in the world, broad bond indexes typically have high weights in government debt. An investor seeking primarily corporate bond exposure would need a narrower, sector-specific index rather than a broad aggregate index as their benchmark.

💡 Index Hedged vs. Unhedged Returns

For international bond indexes, returns can be calculated in two ways:
Unhedged returns: include currency gains/losses from exchange rate fluctuations (investor bears FX risk).
Hedged returns: offset currency changes using currency forward contracts on rebalancing dates (FX risk eliminated; only local bond market returns captured).

Choice depends on the investor's currency exposure strategy. Most global institutional investors can choose either format.

07

Primary Fixed-Income Markets

Primary bond markets: markets in which issuers sell NEW bonds to investors to raise financing.

Secondary bond markets: markets in which EXISTING bonds are traded among investors.

A debut issuer (first time in the bond market) is analogous to an IPO — the first opportunity for investors to buy that issuer's bonds. Often replaces private debt (e.g., bank loan) with publicly traded bonds.

Types of Debut Issuers

  • New corporate legal entities formed after a merger, acquisition, or divestiture — refinance all existing debt
  • Mature companies that reach a stage with more predictable cash flows and begin issuing bonds (replacing bank loans)
  • Sovereign governments raising external foreign currency debt for the first time

For debut issuers: underwriters conduct roadshows and information sessions for several weeks prior to issuance to familiarize investors with the new entity.

Public Offering vs. Private Placement

Public Offering
  • Any member of the public may buy
  • Full registration and disclosure required
  • Larger, more standardized issues
  • More liquid in secondary market
  • Underwritten offering (common for IG) or best-efforts (common for HY)
  • Sovereign debt: usually public auction
Private Placement
  • Only selected investor(s) may buy
  • Fewer registration requirements
  • Smaller, more customized terms
  • Less liquid in secondary market
  • Used when: bond size is small, issuer less well known, or terms are customized
  • Example: Eurobonds available to US investors via Rule 144A (qualified institutional buyers only)

Underwritten vs. Best-Efforts Offering

Underwritten Offering
  • Underwriter guarantees the sale at a negotiated offering price
  • Underwriter bears risk if bonds are unsold
  • Typical for investment-grade corporate bonds
  • Issuers have ready market access; choose timing opportunistically
  • Shelf registration used by frequent issuers (broad offering circular updated regularly)
Best-Efforts Offering
  • Financial intermediary acts as broker only on a commission basis
  • Does NOT guarantee the sale
  • Tries to sell at negotiated price only if it can do so
  • Typical for lower credit quality issuers (high yield, secured bonds)
  • Issuer bears the risk if bonds are undersubscribed

Repeat Issuers vs. Debut Issuers

For repeat issuers, the bond issuance process is far more abbreviated than for debut issuers (and far quicker than follow-on equity offerings):

  • Frequent IG corporate bond issuances can be completed within a single business day (few hours)
  • Repeat issuers usually issue a new security priced at or close to par
  • Less common: reopening = increasing the size of an existing bond (used when the existing bond trades significantly above/below par — creates a bond with a price different from par)
  • Shelf registration (or broad offering circular): pre-approved filing updated regularly, allowing rapid issuance without a full registration each time
08

Investment-Grade Corporate Bond Issuance Timeline

Unsecured IG corporate bond issuance typically takes place within a single business day — usually a few hours. This is dramatically faster than equity follow-on offerings or debut bond issuances (which take weeks).

9:00 AM
Underwriters and issuer agree to launch the transaction
9:15 AM
Transaction announced to market — size, spread, and maturities "TBD" (to be determined). Electronic roadshow released. Order book opens to investors.
10:30 AM
Investor conference call with issuer and underwriters — Q&A, clarification of terms
12:00 PM
Price guidance announced — indicative spread and size (still subject to change). Order book closed to new investor orders.
1:00 PM
Transaction launched with final size, pricing, and maturities. Underwriters allocate the bonds to investors (1:00–3:00 PM).
3:00 PM
Transaction priced — government benchmark and coupon set (finalized)
4:00 PM
Final term sheet delivered. Bonds free to trade same day or next day in secondary market.
💡 Why IG Issuance Is So Fast

IG corporate bond issuance is fast because: (1) investors are already very familiar with the issuer's financial statements and bond indenture; (2) frequent issuers use shelf registrations (pre-approved documentation); (3) underwriters target large institutional investors who can evaluate and commit quickly; (4) underwriters manage the order book efficiently to ensure wide distribution at the lowest spread across maturities. The same process for a high-yield or debut issuer takes significantly longer due to more complex covenants and the need for investor education.

Secondary Fixed-Income Markets

Secondary bond markets are where existing bonds are traded among investors (vs. primary markets where new bonds are issued). Key characteristics that differ from equity secondary markets:

OTC vs. Exchange Trading

Most Bond Markets — OTC (Over-the-Counter)
  • Quote-driven (dealer markets): dealers quote bid and offer prices
  • Electronic platforms: MarketAxess, Tradeweb
  • Institutional investors trade bilaterally with dealers
  • Less transparent than exchange markets
  • Wide variation in liquidity across segments
Some Bond Markets — Exchange Traded
  • Less common for bonds than for equities
  • Example: Australian domestic sovereign bonds trade on an exchange
  • Bond ETFs trade on exchanges (but underlying bonds trade OTC)
  • Greater price transparency

Major Secondary Market Participants

Institutional Investors

Pension funds, insurance companies, bond funds, ETFs, asset managers. Largest buyers and sellers of secondary market bonds.

Financial Intermediaries (Dealers)

Banks and broker-dealers. Make markets by quoting bid-offer spreads. Hold trading inventory. Primary dealers make active markets in sovereign bonds.

Central Banks

Buy and sell government bonds in open market operations (monetary policy). Major force in sovereign bond secondary markets.

Bid-Offer Spread
The difference between the price at which a dealer will BUY (bid) from a customer and the price at which a dealer will SELL (offer/ask) to a customer. A key measure of market liquidity. Smaller spread = more liquid. Expressed in basis points in fixed-income markets. Investor always buys at the higher offer price and sells at the lower bid price.

Liquidity Spectrum & Distressed Debt

Liquidity Spectrum — Highest to Lowest

Bond TypeBid-Offer SpreadWhy Most/Least Liquid
On-the-run DM sovereign bonds (most recently issued) Fraction of 1 bps Primary dealers actively make markets; highest demand; largest issuance size; benchmark for all other bonds
Recently issued IG corporate bonds (frequent issuers) Few bps Underwriters hold recent inventory; widely known issuers; investor familiarity high
Seasoned IG bonds (same frequent issuer, older) 10–20+ bps Dealers less likely to hold inventory; investors have moved to newer on-the-run issues; rarely traded
Less frequent issuer bonds 10–20+ bps (even for small sizes) Fewer natural buyers/sellers; lower investor familiarity; dealers reluctant to hold inventory
Distressed debt (CCC or in default) Very wide; trades on price (cents per dollar of par), not spread Forced selling by IG-constrained investors; uncertainty about recovery value; few specialized buyers
Illiquid bonds (small issues, rare issuers) Not regularly traded; estimated prices from comparable bonds No natural dealer market; prices are matrix-priced from liquid comparables
On-the-Run vs. Off-the-Run
On-the-run: the most recently issued bond of a particular maturity — highest liquidity, tightest spreads, used as benchmark. Off-the-run: older bonds of the same maturity that have been superseded by newer issues — less liquid, wider spreads.
Distressed Debt
Bonds of issuers very close to or in bankruptcy, typically trading well below par (e.g., <50% of par, sometimes under 10%). Bondholders unlikely to receive all promised payments. Trades until either: (1) issuer liquidates assets, or (2) outstanding bonds are restructured. Key trading dynamic: IG-mandated investors FORCED TO SELL; hedge funds and distressed debt investors who seek equity-like returns are the BUYERS.
💡 Distressed Debt vs. Delisted Equity — Key Distinction

Distressed debt continues to trade in secondary markets even as an issuer enters bankruptcy (until resolution). Bonds of Hertz, for example, fell to under 10% of par during COVID-19 but then rose back to pre-pandemic prices when the company exited bankruptcy.

Equity securities are removed (delisted) from exchange trading if they fail listing requirements (minimum share price, net worth, free float). By the time an issuer's bonds are distressed, its equity has almost certainly already been delisted.

💡 Credit Quality Deterioration Can Increase Trading Volume

An exception to the normal pattern of low trading frequency for seasoned bonds: a significant deterioration in credit quality can dramatically increase trading volume — even for older issues. Forced sellers (IG-constrained investors) must sell, while opportunistic buyers (distressed investors) are attracted. This can briefly make a previously illiquid bond one of the most actively traded in its segment.

⚠️ Illiquid Bond Pricing — Matrix Pricing

Many bond issues do not trade regularly. Price quotes for illiquid bonds on platforms like Bloomberg often rely on estimates derived from more liquid bonds of similar credit quality and maturity — a process called matrix pricing or comparable pricing. This means the "price" shown for an illiquid bond is an estimate, not an actual transaction price. This introduces pricing uncertainty and is covered in detail in later modules.
