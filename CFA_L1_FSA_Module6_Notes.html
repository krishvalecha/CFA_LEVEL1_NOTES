Inventory Cost Methods — Overview

When a company sells goods, it must decide which costs to assign to the goods sold (→ Cost of Goods Sold on the income statement) and which costs remain in ending inventory (→ Balance Sheet). In practice, goods often arrive at different prices over time. The cost flow assumption (inventory valuation method) determines how those costs are allocated — and significantly affects both the income statement and balance sheet.

The key insight: Cost of Goods Available for Sale = COGS + Ending Inventory. This is a fixed total — how you split it between COGS and ending inventory is what changes across methods.

The Fundamental Inventory Identity
Beginning Inventory + Purchases = Goods Available for Sale
Goods Available for Sale − Ending Inventory = Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

→ COGS ↑ implies Ending Inventory ↓ (and vice versa) — they always sum to Goods Available
This identity is the key to understanding how all inventory methods work. Every dollar of cost must end up in either COGS (income statement) or ending inventory (balance sheet). The choice of cost flow assumption determines which costs go where — it does NOT change the total pool of costs.

The Three Cost Flow Methods

FIFO — First-In, First-Out
  • Assumes the oldest inventory is sold first. The first units purchased are the first to leave as COGS.
  • COGS reflects the oldest (earliest) prices purchased
  • Ending Inventory reflects the most recently purchased (newest) prices — closest to current replacement cost
  • Permitted under both IFRS and US GAAP
  • In rising price environment: COGS is lower → higher gross profit → higher net income → higher tax burden
  • Balance sheet inventory is most current (realistic) — higher asset values than LIFO
  • Most common globally; required under IFRS (LIFO not permitted)
LIFO — Last-In, First-Out
  • Assumes the most recently purchased inventory is sold first. Newest units go to COGS.
  • COGS reflects the most recent (newest) prices — more relevant economically in rising prices
  • Ending Inventory reflects the oldest prices — potentially very stale (far from current replacement cost)
  • Permitted only under US GAAP — PROHIBITED under IFRS
  • In rising price environment: COGS is higher → lower gross profit → lower net income → lower taxes (main US tax motivation for using LIFO)
  • Often chosen by US companies for the tax advantage (higher COGS → lower taxable income → lower taxes payable in rising price environment)
  • Can result in LIFO layers of old, low-cost inventory on the balance sheet — severely understating current value
Weighted Average Cost
  • Assigns the average cost of all units available for sale to both COGS and ending inventory
  • Weighted average cost per unit = Total cost of goods available ÷ Total units available
  • Results typically fall between FIFO and LIFO for both COGS and ending inventory in a rising price environment
  • Permitted under both IFRS and US GAAP
  • Also called the "average cost method." Smooths out price fluctuations over time.
  • Most commonly used internationally alongside FIFO; practical for companies with large volumes of interchangeable goods
💡 LIFO Not Permitted Under IFRS — Critical Exam Fact

IFRS prohibits the LIFO method entirely. This is one of the most tested IFRS vs. US GAAP differences in FSA. Under IFRS, only FIFO and weighted average cost are permitted (plus specific identification for unique items). Under US GAAP, all three (FIFO, LIFO, weighted average) are permitted. This means any cross-border comparison between a US company using LIFO and a non-US company using FIFO requires adjustments before the comparison is meaningful.

Specific Identification — The Fourth Method

Specific identification assigns the actual cost of each specific item sold to COGS. Each unit is tracked individually. Used when inventory items are unique or of high value and individually distinguishable — e.g., luxury automobiles, fine art, custom jewelry, real estate. Permitted under both IFRS and US GAAP. Provides the most accurate cost assignment but is impractical for homogeneous, high-volume inventory.

02

Lower of Cost and Net Realizable Value (NRV)

Inventory can decline in value due to obsolescence, damage, physical deterioration, declining selling prices, or estimation errors. Accounting standards require inventory to be written down when its market value falls below its carrying cost — applying a conservatism principle. The write-down rules differ between IFRS and US GAAP.

IFRS — Lower of Cost and NRV

IFRS: Lower of Cost and NRV (IAS 2)
  • NRV = Estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business − Estimated costs of completion − Estimated costs necessary to make the sale
  • If carrying value > NRV → write down to NRV; recognize loss as expense (in COGS or separately)
  • Assessment done item-by-item or by groups of similar/related items
  • Reversals ARE permitted under IFRS (limited to the amount of the original write-down). If NRV subsequently recovers → reverse prior write-down → recognize as a reduction in cost of sales
  • Does NOT apply to producers of agricultural products, forest products, mineral products, or commodity broker-traders (these may use fair value less costs to sell)

US GAAP — Lower of Cost, Market, or NRV

US GAAP: Two-Track System
  • Non-LIFO / Non-Retail inventories (effective 2017): Lower of cost or NRV — broadly consistent with IFRS. NRV = estimated selling price − costs of completion and disposal.
  • LIFO and Retail Inventory Method: Lower of cost or market value. Market value = current replacement cost, subject to:
    • Upper limit: NRV (cannot exceed selling price − costs to sell)
    • Lower limit: NRV − Normal profit margin
  • Reversals are PROHIBITED under US GAAP — once written down, inventory stays at the lower value. No recovery allowed even if prices recover.
  • Write-downs are generally reflected in COGS on the income statement

Impact of an Inventory Write-Down on Financial Statements

An inventory write-down reduces both inventory on the balance sheet and increases COGS (or is separately expensed) on the income statement. The ripple effects across financial statements and ratios:

Negative Effects of Write-Down
  • COGS ↑ → Gross profit ↓ → Net income ↓
  • Inventory ↓ → Current assets ↓ → Total assets ↓
  • Net income ↓ → Retained earnings ↓ → Equity ↓
  • Current ratio ↓ (current assets decrease)
  • Return on assets ↓ (lower NI, smaller denominator benefit doesn't fully offset)
  • Gross profit margin ↓
  • Debt-to-equity ratio ↑ (lower equity)
Positive Effects of Write-Down (Counterintuitive)
  • Inventory turnover ↑ (lower denominator: avg inventory down)
  • Days of inventory on hand ↓ (inverse of turnover)
  • Total asset turnover ↑ (lower denominator: total assets down)
  • Future COGS will be lower → future gross profit will be higher (asset already written down)
  • Activity ratios improve because the asset base (denominator) shrinks
Examiner Focus — Write-Downs and Reversals

The exam loves asking about the IFRS vs. US GAAP reversal difference: IFRS allows reversals; US GAAP prohibits them. This is the single most important rule in this area. Also tested: the effect of write-downs on ratios. Common trap: students think write-downs always hurt all ratios. In fact, activity ratios (inventory turnover, asset turnover) improve after a write-down because the asset base shrinks. Profitability ratios and liquidity ratios deteriorate. The exam will often say "A company writes down inventory. Which ratio is MOST LIKELY to improve?" — answer: inventory turnover (lower average inventory → higher turnover).

Inflation Effects on Inventory Methods

The choice of inventory method matters most when prices are changing. In a rising price (inflationary) environment with stable or growing inventory quantities, the three methods produce significantly different income statement and balance sheet results. Understanding the direction of impact is the core testable content for this section.

Rising Prices + Stable/Growing Quantities — The Key Scenario

💡 The Core Trade-Off — FIFO vs. LIFO in Inflation

Under inflation: FIFO gives a better balance sheet (inventory at current prices = realistic asset value) but a worse income statement match (COGS is stale old prices, not current replacement cost). LIFO gives a better income statement match (COGS reflects current replacement cost) but a worse balance sheet (inventory is stale old prices). Neither method is perfect — they each excel on one dimension and fail on the other. This is the economic logic behind the LIFO reserve adjustment (discussed next section).

*ROA: FIFO has higher net income (numerator) AND higher assets (denominator), but the proportional increase in numerator exceeds the increase in denominator → FIFO ROA > LIFO ROA in rising prices.

04

Deflation Effects — When Prices Fall

In a falling price (deflationary) environment with stable or growing inventory quantities, the relationships between FIFO, LIFO, and weighted average completely reverse. What FIFO does in inflation, LIFO does in deflation — and vice versa.

MetricInflation (Rising Prices)Deflation (Falling Prices)
COGS: which method is higher?LIFO (newest = most expensive)FIFO (oldest = most expensive)
Gross Profit: which method is higher?FIFOLIFO
Net Income: which method is higher?FIFOLIFO
Ending Inventory: which is higher?FIFO (newest = most expensive)LIFO (newest = cheapest — wait, opposite)
CFO after-tax: which is higher?LIFO (lower taxes)FIFO (lower taxes in deflation)
⚠️ Deflation Reversal — Don't Get Tripped Up

In deflation: oldest prices are HIGHER than newest prices. So FIFO (which assigns oldest prices to COGS) now produces HIGHER COGS and LOWER profits than LIFO. The exam can ask about deflation specifically to test whether you understand the reversal. Always think: "oldest prices vs. newest prices — which is higher given the price environment?" Then apply FIFO (oldest to COGS) and LIFO (newest to COGS) accordingly.

💡 Shortcut — The Two Key "Match" Rules

FIFO balance sheet matches current reality (ending inventory = most recent prices) regardless of whether prices are rising or falling. LIFO income statement matches current reality (COGS = most recent prices) regardless of direction. Weighted average always produces results between the two extremes. Under stable prices: all three methods produce the same results — differences only emerge when prices change.

LIFO Reserve and LIFO Liquidation

Because LIFO is used by many US companies but not permitted under IFRS, cross-border comparisons require analytical adjustments. US GAAP requires LIFO companies to disclose the LIFO reserve — the cumulative difference between what inventory would be under FIFO and what it actually is under LIFO. This disclosure allows analysts to convert LIFO statements to FIFO-equivalent for comparison.

The LIFO Reserve

LIFO Reserve — Definition and Adjustments
LIFO Reserve = FIFO Inventory − LIFO Inventory
(the cumulative difference — always positive in a rising price environment)

Converting LIFO Balance Sheet → FIFO Equivalent:
FIFO Inventory = LIFO Inventory + LIFO Reserve
FIFO Total Assets = LIFO Total Assets + LIFO Reserve × (1 − tax rate)
Wait — more precisely:
FIFO Equity = LIFO Equity + LIFO Reserve × (1 − tax rate)
(because if inventory were higher, retained earnings would be higher, but taxes would have been paid)

Converting LIFO COGS → FIFO Equivalent:
FIFO COGS = LIFO COGS − ∆LIFO Reserve (decrease in reserve → lower FIFO COGS)
FIFO COGS = LIFO COGS − (Ending LIFO Reserve − Beginning LIFO Reserve)
The LIFO reserve is disclosed in US GAAP footnotes. In a rising price environment with growing inventory quantities, the LIFO reserve grows over time. An analyst can use the LIFO reserve to convert a LIFO company's balance sheet and income statement to FIFO-equivalent, enabling comparison with FIFO companies (including all IFRS companies).

LIFO Liquidation

⚠️ LIFO Liquidation — The Earnings Quality Trap

A LIFO liquidation occurs when a LIFO company's inventory quantities decline (sells more than it purchases in a period). This forces the company to dip into older, lower-cost LIFO inventory layers. The old low-cost inventory goes to COGS — which is much lower than current replacement cost — creating an artificial boost to gross profit and net income. This is a transitory, non-recurring source of earnings that does NOT reflect current operating performance. Under US GAAP, companies must disclose the material income impact of LIFO liquidations. Analysts should strip out this effect when assessing sustainable earnings. Key signal: LIFO company with rising gross margins + declining inventory quantities = likely LIFO liquidation effect.

Inventory Ratios — Calculation and Interpretation

The Three Core Inventory Ratios
Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory
Days Inventory on Hand = 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover
                    (or: Ending Inventory ÷ COGS × 365)

Gross Profit Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue
                    = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue

Note: Inventory turnover uses AVERAGE inventory in denominator;
Days on hand sometimes uses ENDING inventory — check which is specified.
Important: COGS (not revenue) is the numerator for inventory turnover. A common error is using revenue instead of COGS. Revenue ÷ inventory gives total asset intensity, not inventory efficiency. The standard formula uses COGS because that's what was consumed from inventory.

Interpreting Inventory Turnover and Days on Hand

High Turnover / Low Days — Could Mean:
  • Efficient inventory management — lean, just-in-time operations. Positive interpretation.
  • Inadequate inventory levels — company doesn't carry enough stock → stockouts, lost sales, production disruptions. Negative.
  • Inventory write-downs — written-down inventory reduces the denominator artificially, boosting the ratio. Negative quality signal.
  • Distinguish by: comparing with industry peers + examining sales growth. Turnover ↑ + sales growth in line with peers → efficient. Turnover ↑ + sales growth lagging → inadequate inventory.
Low Turnover / High Days — Could Mean:
  • Slow-moving or obsolete inventory — goods not selling, piling up. Major concern.
  • Strategic build-up — company building inventory ahead of expected demand surge or supply disruption. Positive.
  • Luxury/specialty goods — inherently low turnover (e.g., fine jewelers vs. grocery stores). Not necessarily bad — compare within industry.
  • Always benchmark against industry norms and historical trend. Never interpret in isolation.

Gross profit margin measures how much of each revenue dollar remains after covering COGS. Higher = better (more pricing power, lower input costs, or more value-added products). Key considerations:

  • Highly competitive industries → lower gross margins (commoditized). Fewer competitors → higher margins.
  • Luxury goods: high gross margins but low turnover. Staples: low gross margins but high turnover. Both can be profitable — compare within sector.
  • Inventory method choice significantly affects gross margin. FIFO in inflation → higher gross margin. LIFO → lower. Cross-company comparison requires same method or adjustment.
Examiner Focus — Inventory Ratio Interpretation

The exam frequently presents a scenario where turnover increases and asks for the most likely explanation. The answer depends on context: if sales are also growing → efficient management. If sales are flat or declining → possibly inadequate inventory OR write-downs. Another common question: "Which method produces a higher inventory turnover in rising prices?" → LIFO (higher COGS in numerator AND lower avg inventory in denominator = higher turnover). The exam also tests: write-down → turnover ↑ (artificially, via lower inventory denominator) — an activity ratio improvement that actually signals a problem, not a success.

Inventory Disclosures and Analyst Issues

Inventory disclosures in the footnotes to financial statements provide critical information that goes beyond the balance sheet and income statement figures. Reading the notes on inventory is essential for understanding the accounting policies and any quality concerns.

Required Disclosures Under IFRS (IAS 2)

IFRS Required Disclosures
  • Accounting policies adopted (cost formula used — FIFO or weighted average)
  • Total carrying amount and breakdown by classification (raw materials, WIP, finished goods, merchandise)
  • Carrying amount of inventories at fair value less costs to sell
  • Amount of inventories recognized as expense (COGS) during the period
  • Amount of any write-downs recognized in the period
  • Amount of any reversal of write-downs (IFRS unique — US GAAP does not have this)
  • Circumstances/events leading to reversal of write-down
  • Carrying amount of inventories pledged as collateral

Additional US GAAP Disclosures

US GAAP Additional Requirements
  • Same as IFRS except no reversal disclosures (prohibited)
  • Significant estimates applicable to inventories
  • Material income from LIFO liquidation must be disclosed — a key alert for analysts
  • LIFO reserve disclosure (allows FIFO conversion)

Key Analyst Issues When Examining Inventory

  • Compare inventory method across peers. Before comparing ratios, identify whether companies use FIFO, LIFO, or weighted average. Adjust using the LIFO reserve for US GAAP LIFO companies when comparing to FIFO or IFRS companies.
  • Examine changes in inventory classifications. Changes in the mix of raw materials, WIP, and finished goods can signal future business trends. Rising WIP relative to finished goods may signal production issues. Rising finished goods relative to sales may signal demand weakness.
  • Track the valuation allowance (write-down trend). Growing write-down reserves signal worsening inventory quality or obsolescence risk (see Jollof — allowance grew from €318m to €432m to €549m over three years while revenues were falling).
  • Watch for LIFO liquidation signals. LIFO company + declining inventory quantities + unusual gross margin improvement = potential LIFO liquidation boosting reported profits. Check the US GAAP footnote disclosure.
  • Assess inventory as % of current assets over time. Rising concentration of current assets in inventory (less liquid than cash or receivables) can worsen effective liquidity even if the current ratio holds steady.
  • Consider debt covenant implications. Companies with tight debt covenants (financial ratio requirements) may be reluctant to write down inventory — even when warranted — because write-downs hurt profitability and equity ratios. Analysts should be especially skeptical of companies that have NOT taken write-downs in industries with obvious obsolescence risk (e.g., tech hardware, fashion, seasonal goods).
  • Tangible book value from notes: Historical cost of inventory = Reported inventory + Valuation allowance (from the notes). This tells you the gross cost before write-downs.

Exam Checklist — Module 6: Analysis of Inventories

  • ✅ Three permitted methods: FIFO (both standards), Weighted Average (both), LIFO (US GAAP only — PROHIBITED under IFRS). Specific identification for unique items (both).
  • ✅ LIFO is NOT permitted under IFRS. This is the most important IFRS/US GAAP difference in this module.
  • ✅ The fundamental identity: Beg. Inventory + Purchases = Goods Available. COGS + End. Inventory = Goods Available. Higher COGS always means lower ending inventory (and vice versa).
  • ✅ FIFO in rising prices: Oldest (cheaper) costs → COGS. Newest (pricier) costs → ending inventory. Result: lower COGS, higher gross profit, higher NI, higher taxes, lower CFO, higher inventory on B/S.
  • ✅ LIFO in rising prices: Newest (pricier) costs → COGS. Oldest (cheaper) costs → ending inventory. Result: higher COGS, lower gross profit, lower NI, lower taxes (tax advantage!), higher CFO, lower (stale) inventory on B/S.
  • ✅ WAVG in rising prices: Results always fall between FIFO and LIFO for all measures.
  • ✅ Deflation: all relationships reverse. FIFO COGS becomes highest (oldest = most expensive). LIFO profit becomes highest.
  • ✅ FIFO gives better balance sheet; inventory at current (replacement) prices. LIFO gives better income statement match; COGS reflects current replacement cost.
  • ✅ Inflation, FIFO vs. LIFO direction of ratios: FIFO → higher gross margin, higher ROA, higher current ratio, lower D/E, lower inventory turnover. LIFO → lower gross margin, lower ROA, lower current ratio, higher D/E, HIGHER inventory turnover.
  • ✅ IFRS write-down basis: Lower of cost and NRV. NRV = selling price − completion costs − selling costs. Write-downs ARE reversible (up to original write-down). Reversal = reduction in COGS.
  • ✅ US GAAP write-down basis (non-LIFO): Lower of cost or NRV. Write-downs are NOT reversible.
  • ✅ US GAAP LIFO: Lower of cost or Market (bounded NRV). Market = replacement cost, bounded above by NRV and below by NRV − normal profit. No reversals.
  • ✅ Write-down effects: Inventory ↓, COGS ↑, NI ↓, equity ↓, total assets ↓. Current ratio ↓, ROA ↓, gross margin ↓. BUT: inventory turnover ↑ and asset turnover ↑ (lower denominator). Activity ratios IMPROVE.
  • ✅ LIFO Reserve = FIFO inventory − LIFO inventory. Positive in rising prices. Disclosed in US GAAP footnotes. Used by analysts to convert LIFO → FIFO.
  • ✅ FIFO adjustment from LIFO: FIFO Inventory = LIFO Inventory + LIFO Reserve. FIFO COGS = LIFO COGS − ∆LIFO Reserve. FIFO Equity = LIFO Equity + LIFO Reserve × (1 − tax rate).
  • ✅ LIFO liquidation: When inventory quantities fall, old low-cost layers emerge in COGS → artificially low COGS → inflated gross profit. Transitory, non-recurring. Must be disclosed under US GAAP. Analyst should strip out when assessing sustainable earnings.
  • ✅ Inventory turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory. Days on hand = 365 ÷ Turnover. Higher turnover is usually good — but check for write-downs or inadequate inventory as alternative explanations.
  • ✅ Gross profit margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue. Directly affected by inventory method. Always compare within same inventory method group.
  • ✅ Valuation allowance (IFRS term) = cumulative write-downs on gross inventory. Gross inventory = Net inventory + Valuation allowance. Growing allowance trend = worsening inventory quality.
  • ✅ Debt covenant risk: Companies may resist taking write-downs to avoid ratio deterioration that could breach covenants. Skepticism warranted in high-obsolescence industries with no write-downs taken.

D/E Ratio: LOWEST Middle HIGHEST
ROA: HIGHEST Middle LOWEST

═══ DEFLATION: All relationships REVERSE ═══

═══ WRITE-DOWN EFFECTS ═══
Inventory ↓, COGS ↑, NI ↓, Equity ↓, Total Assets ↓
Current ratio ↓, Gross margin ↓, ROA ↓
BUT: Inventory turnover ↑, Total asset turnover ↑ (lower denominators)
IFRS: write-down reversals PERMITTED (up to original amount)
US GAAP: write-down reversals PROHIBITED

═══ LIFO RESERVE ADJUSTMENTS ═══
FIFO Inventory = LIFO Inventory + LIFO Reserve
FIFO COGS = LIFO COGS − ∆LIFO Reserve (change in reserve during year)
FIFO Equity = LIFO Equity + LIFO Reserve × (1 − tax rate)

═══ INVENTORY RATIOS ═══
Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory
Days on Hand = 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover
Gross Profit Margin = (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue
Top exam traps for Module 6:
(1) LIFO is PROHIBITED under IFRS. Any question asking about IFRS companies using LIFO is a trap — they can't.
(2) Rising prices: FIFO → higher NI, lower CFO (more taxes). LIFO → lower NI, higher CFO (tax advantage). Both have same total lifetime income/CF — only timing differs.
(3) Write-down → activity ratios IMPROVE (turnover, asset turnover) because denominator shrinks. Profitability and liquidity ratios deteriorate.
(4) IFRS allows reversals; US GAAP prohibits them. If inventory price recovers: IFRS reverses write-down → reduces COGS (increases profit). US GAAP: no change — inventory stays at written-down value.
(5) LIFO liquidation: inventory quantities fall → old cheap layers hit COGS → artificially high gross profit. Transitory and must be disclosed under US GAAP. Strip from sustainable earnings analysis.
(6) FIFO ending inventory = current (realistic) prices. LIFO ending inventory = old stale prices. For balance sheet realism: FIFO wins. For income statement (COGS) realism in rising prices: LIFO wins.
(7) Converting LIFO COGS to FIFO: subtract the CHANGE (∆) in LIFO reserve, NOT the total LIFO reserve. The total LIFO reserve adjusts the balance sheet (inventory and equity). The annual change adjusts the income statement (COGS).
(8) Inventory turnover uses COGS (not revenue) as numerator. Revenue/inventory is a different ratio.
(9) High turnover could mean efficiency OR inadequate inventory OR write-downs. Always check context and compare to peers and sales growth.
(10) Gross profit margin is directly affected by inventory method — never compare across methods without adjustment.
