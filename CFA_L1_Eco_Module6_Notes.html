01

Benefits of International Trade

Five Core Benefits

  1. Gains from exchange and specialization — Each country can receive a higher price for its exports OR pay a lower price for imported goods than it would cost to produce them domestically. Enables more efficient resource allocation → larger consumption bundle → higher welfare.
  2. Economies of scale — Industries with increasing returns to scale (autos, steel) benefit from larger market size. Average cost falls as output rises. Scale economies from global markets → lower costs for all.
  3. Greater product variety — Consumers gain access to differentiated goods not produced domestically. Explains intra-industry trade (countries export AND import goods in the same product category, e.g., EU exports and imports different car types).
  4. Increased competition — Foreign competition reduces monopoly power of domestic firms, forcing them to be more efficient. Open economy = more competitive than closed economy.
  5. More efficient resource allocation — Resources flow to their most productive use according to comparative advantage. Learning by doing (e.g., semiconductors), knowledge spillovers, technology transfer, and institutional improvements all enhance productivity.

Traditional vs. Newer Trade Models

Traditional Models
  • Ricardian Model: Trade based on comparative advantage arising from differences in technology
  • Heckscher-Ohlin Model: Trade based on comparative advantage arising from differences in factor endowments (land, labor, capital)
  • Focus: specialization in production and export of goods where you have comparative advantage
Newer Models
  • Focus on gains from economies of scale, product variety, and competition
  • Explain intra-industry trade — why similar countries trade similar goods (monopolistic competition model)
  • Even countries with similar factor endowments and technology gain from trade through specialization in varieties

Intra-Industry Trade — Key Concept

A country both exports and imports goods in the same product category. Example: Germany exports BMWs and imports Hondas. Explained by monopolistic competition: many firms producing differentiated products; no barriers to entry; zero long-run economic profit. Countries specialize in one or more varieties and trade other varieties → consumers gain variety; firms gain scale economies.

💡 Key Principle — Winners Can Compensate Losers

Trade increases overall welfare — but not every individual is better off. The critical insight: the winners from trade gain enough to theoretically compensate the losers and still be better off. This is the economic case for trade even when some groups suffer. Whether compensation actually occurs is a political/policy question — it often doesn't, which fuels anti-trade sentiment.

02

Costs & Arguments Against Free Trade

  • Income inequality: Trade benefits are distributed unequally — skilled workers, capital owners, and efficient firms gain; low-skilled workers and import-competing industries lose.
  • Job losses in developed countries: Import competition from lower-cost countries forces inefficient domestic firms to exit → unemployment and need for worker retraining. Short-to-medium-term disruption.
  • Resource reallocation costs: Capital and labor must move from contracting industries to expanding industries. This is costly and not frictionless.
  • Permanent displacement: Some workers (especially older workers in declining industries like autos) may never find comparable replacement jobs.

Why Countries Impose Trade Restrictions

  1. Protect established domestic industries from foreign competition
  2. Infant industry argument: Protect new industries until they mature and become competitive
  3. Protect and increase domestic employment
  4. National security: Protect strategic industries (defense, energy)
  5. Revenue generation: Especially important for developing countries with limited tax bases
  6. Retaliation: Respond to trade restrictions imposed by other countries
🧠 Mnemonic — "PINRR" = 5 Reasons for Trade Restrictions

Protect established industries · Infant industry protection · National security · Revenue (developing countries) · Retaliation

Tariffs — Welfare Analysis

Definition & Mechanics

Tariff: A tax levied by the government on imported goods. Primary objective: protect domestic industries. Secondary: reduce trade deficit.

Small country vs. large country: A small country is a price taker in world markets (cannot influence world price). A large country is a large enough importer that a tariff causes exporters to lower their price to retain market share → changes the terms of trade in the importer's favor.

Tariff Welfare Analysis — The ABCD Framework

Under free trade: world price = P*. Domestic supply = Q1, domestic consumption = Q4, imports = Q1Q4.

After tariff t: domestic price rises to Pt = P* + t. Domestic production rises to Q2, domestic consumption falls to Q3, imports fall to Q2Q3.

B = production DWL (inefficient domestic production). D = consumption DWL (blocked mutually beneficial trades). Both B and D are lost to society — not redistributed to anyone. This is why free trade is more efficient.

Large Country Case — Special Consideration

When a large country imposes a tariff, exporters reduce their price to retain market share. This improves the terms of trade for the importing country (pays less per unit). In theory, a large country can increase its own welfare from a tariff IF: (1) trading partner doesn't retaliate, AND (2) terms-of-trade gain exceeds the deadweight loss. However, global welfare still falls because the importer's gain comes at the exporter's expense (zero-sum redistribution + DWL).

⚠ Exam Trap — Large Country Tariff

A large country can improve its own welfare with a tariff (terms-of-trade gain > DWL, no retaliation). But global welfare always falls. The large country gains only by imposing an even larger loss on its trading partner. Retaliation (trade wars) eliminates even the domestic gain.

04

Quotas & Voluntary Export Restraints (VERs)

Import Quota

A quota restricts the quantity of a good that can be imported (usually for a specified period). An import license specifies the quantity that can be imported.

Key difference from a tariff: An equivalent quota produces the same domestic price (Pt) and the same domestic quantity effects as a tariff — BUT the Area C (revenue/profit) goes to the foreign producer, not the domestic government. These profits are called quota rents.

Quota Welfare — Two Scenarios
Foreign producer captures quota rent (C): DWL = B + D + C   (worse than tariff)
Govt auctions import licenses and captures C: DWL = B + D   (same as tariff)
Under a quota, welfare is usually worse than under an equivalent tariff because the domestic government typically does NOT capture the quota rents. The foreign exporter earns higher profits from the restricted supply.

Voluntary Export Restraint (VER)

A VER is a quota imposed by the exporting country (not the importing country). The exporting country voluntarily agrees to limit its exports to a specified number of units.

Key difference from quota: In a VER, the quota rents (Area C) are captured by the exporter (or exporting country government) by design — since the exporter controls the restriction. This guarantees the importing country suffers the welfare loss B + D + C.

Example: 1981 — Japanese government imposed VERs on automobile exports to the United States.

05

Export Subsidies

An export subsidy is a payment by the government to a domestic firm for each unit of a good exported. Goal: stimulate exports. Effect: distorts trade away from comparative advantage → reduces global welfare.

The exporter receives the international price plus the per-unit subsidy → incentive to shift sales from domestic to export market → domestic price rises by the amount of the subsidy (in small country case) → domestic consumers suffer higher prices while producers gain.

Export Subsidy Effects
Small country: domestic price rises by subsidy amount. Net welfare = negative.
Large country: world price declines (↑ supply → ↓ world price). Part of subsidy is transferred to foreign buyers. Net welfare loss is LARGER than in small country case.
Countervailing duties: duties levied by the IMPORTING country against subsidized exports to offset the subsidy. A legitimate WTO-recognized response.
⚠ Exam Point — Export Subsidy Affects the Exporting Country, Not the Importing Country

Unlike tariffs and import quotas (which affect the importing country), export subsidies primarily impact the exporting country. The government of the exporting country bears the cost; domestic producers gain but domestic consumers pay higher prices. Net welfare effect is negative for the exporting country in all cases.

💡 Universal Rule for ALL Trade Restrictions

Every trade restriction increases domestic price, increases domestic production, decreases domestic consumption, and increases producer surplus at the expense of consumers. The only differences are: (1) who captures the "Area C" revenue/rents, and (2) who bears the cost. National welfare always falls in the small country case. Global welfare always falls in all cases.

Capital Restrictions — Note

Capital restrictions = controls on foreigners' ability to own domestic assets or domestic residents' ability to own foreign assets. These limit the openness of financial markets (contrast with trade restrictions, which limit goods markets). Covered separately in the curriculum.

Trading Blocs & Regional Integration

Levels of Integration — Ladder of Depth

1
Free Trade Area (FTA)
Added: Eliminates barriers to trade in goods and services among members
Each member keeps its OWN trade policies vs. non-members. Lowest level of integration.
Examples: USMCA (US, Canada, Mexico)
2
Customs Union
Added: COMMON external trade policy against non-members (unified tariffs)
FTA + harmonized trade barriers toward outsiders. Stronger coordination required.
Examples: Benelux (Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg) — 1947; now part of EU
3
Common Market
Added: FREE MOVEMENT OF FACTORS OF PRODUCTION (labor, capital) among members
Customs union + free movement of people and capital across member borders.
Examples: MERCOSUR (Argentina, Brazil, Paraguay, Uruguay)
4
Economic Union
Added: COMMON ECONOMIC INSTITUTIONS + coordination of economic policies
Common market + harmonized fiscal, regulatory, and economic policies. Individual monetary policy still possible.
Examples: European Union (EU) — became EU in 1993
5
Monetary Union
Added: COMMON CURRENCY — individual monetary policy surrendered
Economic union + single currency and central bank. No exchange rate tool to correct imbalances. Deepest integration.
Examples: Eurozone (19 EU members with euro)
🧠 Mnemonic — "FC³EM" = FTA → Customs → Common Market → Economic Union → Monetary Union

Each level ADDS the previous level's features PLUS one more dimension of integration. The key additions at each step: FTA (free trade in goods) → Customs (common external policy) → Common (free factor movement) → Economic (policy coordination) → Monetary (common currency).

Why Regional Integration Instead of WTO?

Regional integration is popular because: (1) easier and quicker among a small group, (2) politically less contentious than global multilateral negotiations, (3) policy harmonization is simpler among fewer members. The WTO's Doha Round (launched 2001) has made limited progress on contentious issues like agricultural subsidies and developing country market access — driving the shift toward bilateral and regional deals.

Trade Creation vs. Trade Diversion

Trade Creation ✅
  • Higher-cost domestic production is replaced by lower-cost imports from member countries
  • Resources reallocated to more efficient use
  • Improves welfare — more efficient allocation
  • Example: Qualor replaces domestic shirt production with cheaper imports from member Vulcan after forming customs union
Trade Diversion ❌
  • Lower-cost imports from non-member countries are replaced by higher-cost imports from member countries (due to tariff preference)
  • Resources allocated less efficiently than under free trade
  • Reduces welfare — trade diverted from most efficient producer
  • Example: Qualor stops importing from lowest-cost Aurelia (non-member, taxed) and switches to higher-cost Vulcan (member, tariff-free)
Net Welfare Effect of Regional Trading Bloc
Net welfare = Trade Creation – Trade Diversion
If trade creation > trade diversion → net positive welfare effect. If trade diversion > trade creation → net negative welfare effect. The welfare outcome depends on which effect dominates — not guaranteed to be positive.
⚠ Exam Trap — Trade Creation vs. Diversion

Trade creation = replacing domestic (high-cost) production with member imports (good). Trade diversion = replacing non-member (lowest-cost) imports with member (higher-cost) imports (bad). An RTA does NOT automatically increase welfare — it depends on whether trade creation dominates. This is a frequently tested distinction.

Benefits of Trading Blocs

  • All free trade benefits: specialization, scale economies, competition, variety, technology transfer
  • Greater interdependence → reduces potential for conflict among members
  • Greater bargaining power in global economy (acting as a bloc vs. individual countries)
  • Growth spillovers among integrated members — strong growth in one country boosts others
  • Convergence in living standards through policy harmonization
  • Lower cost of doing business in a large, single regional market

Costs & Challenges of Integration

  • Adjustment costs: Workers displaced as inefficient domestic firms exit. Long-term job losses in some sectors (e.g., US auto industry under USMCA).
  • Cultural/historical impediments: Past conflicts and cultural differences complicate social and political integration.
  • Loss of policy autonomy: Members cannot use exchange rate devaluation or independent monetary policy to correct imbalances (especially in monetary union). The Greek crisis (2010) is the prime example.
  • Contagion risk: Problems in one member country can quickly spread to others (Greek crisis threatened EU banking system; Ireland needed bailout too).
  • National sovereignty concerns: Especially where large and small economies coexist (e.g., India in South Asian bloc proposals).
💡 Monetary Union Trap — Greece 2010

In a monetary union, individual countries cannot devalue their currency or set independent interest rates. When Greece ran persistent deficits, it couldn't use exchange rate depreciation to restore competitiveness — it needed fiscal austerity (painful). This is the core structural weakness of monetary unions without fiscal union: asymmetric shocks can't be corrected through normal monetary tools. The EU/IMF bailout (€110bn) with strict austerity conditions was the only option.
