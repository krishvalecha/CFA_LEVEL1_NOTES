Evaluating Sources and Uses of Cash — The 4-Step Framework

Analyzing a cash flow statement requires a structured approach: start with the big picture (where does cash come from and go?), then drill into each section to understand the drivers. This 4-step framework is the systematic approach the LES presents — and the lens through which exam questions about CFS analysis are structured.

STEP 1
Evaluate Major Sources and Uses of Cash (CFO, CFI, CFF)
Identify which section is the PRIMARY source of cash and which sections are uses. Ask:
  • Is CFO positive? Mature, healthy companies should generate positive CFO consistently.
  • Is CFO sufficient to cover capital expenditures (i.e., does the company have positive free cash flow)?
  • Is the company funding operations with external financing (debt/equity issuance)? This is a red flag if persistent.
  • What stage of growth is the company in? Growth-stage companies may have negative CFO temporarily — that's expected but not sustainable.
STEP 2
Evaluate Primary Determinants of Operating Cash Flow
Drill into the CFO section. For the indirect method, examine each working capital adjustment. Ask:
  • Is CFO > Net Income? Expected and healthy for a mature company (D&A is non-cash; CFO should exceed NI).
  • Is CFO < Net Income? Could signal aggressive revenue recognition (receivables building), excessive accruals, or other earnings quality issues.
  • Are working capital changes consistent with the business narrative? (e.g., inventory growing faster than sales is a potential red flag).
  • How consistent are operating cash flows over time? Volatile CFO = higher risk and harder to forecast for valuation.
STEP 3
Evaluate Primary Determinants of Investing Cash Flow
Examine each CFI line item. Ask:
  • How much is being invested in PP&E (maintenance vs. growth capex)? Is it funded by excess CFO?
  • Are assets being acquired (acquisitions)? At what scale? Does CFO cover it, or is the company relying on financing?
  • Are assets being sold? Why? Is it a strategic divestiture or a distressed sale of assets needed for operations?
  • Are investments in securities being made? (Significant for cash-rich companies like Apple.)
STEP 4
Evaluate Primary Determinants of Financing Cash Flow
Examine each CFF line item. Ask:
  • Is the company raising or repaying capital? Is debt increasing or decreasing?
  • When does debt mature? Is refinancing risk significant?
  • Is capital being returned to shareholders (dividends + buybacks)? Is it funded from CFO or from new borrowing?
  • Is the mix of debt vs. equity financing changing? What does this imply for financial risk?

Expected CFO/CFI/CFF Patterns by Company Stage

💡 The Danone 2017 Case — Seeing the 4-Step Framework in Action

Step 1: In 2016, CFO ($2,652m) was the primary source. In 2017, CFF ($8,289m) was primary — a massive shift driven by bond issuance. Step 2: CFO grew from $2,652m to $2,958m — solid, growing operating cash generation. Net income also grew ($1,827m → $2,563m). CFO > NI both years — good earnings quality. Step 3: CFI was −$11,437m in 2017 (vs. −$848m in 2016) — dominated by the WhiteWave acquisition ($10,949m in subsidiary purchases). The company spent ~10× normal investing levels. Step 4: CFF swung from −$1,616m to +$8,289m as Danone issued bonds to fund the WhiteWave deal. The 2017 picture: major acquisition funded entirely by new debt. The analyst question: will post-acquisition CFO growth justify the leverage taken on?

Earnings Quality — The CFO vs. Net Income Relationship

Comparing CFO to net income is one of the most powerful tools for assessing earnings quality. For a mature, profitable company operating normally, CFO should consistently exceed net income because of non-cash charges (primarily depreciation and amortization) that reduce net income but not cash flow. When CFO persistently lags net income, something is wrong.

CFO > Net Income — The Healthy Pattern
  • D&A reduces net income but NOT cash → CFO should be higher
  • Other non-cash charges (impairment, stock comp) further inflate CFO vs. NI
  • Strong signal of high earnings quality — accrual income is backed by actual cash
  • The larger the gap (D&A relative to asset base), the more CFO exceeds NI — common in capital-intensive businesses
CFO < Net Income — The Warning Signal
  • Receivables building faster than revenue → possible aggressive revenue recognition
  • Inventory building → possible overstocking or slow-moving goods
  • One-time non-operating gains inflated NI (e.g., asset sale gains) — these bypass CFO
  • May indicate company is generating "paper profits" without real cash flow
  • Persistent pattern of CFO < NI is a serious earnings quality red flag
⚠️ The Classic Earnings Manipulation Signal

A company reports strong and growing net income, but operating cash flow is flat or declining, and accounts receivable are growing rapidly. This classic pattern suggests the company is recognizing revenue before it should be — booking sales that haven't collected in cash. Eventually, either the receivables must be written off (hitting future income) or the manipulation is exposed. The CFS is the hardest financial statement to manipulate because cash is cash. Comparing revenue growth, A/R growth, and CFO growth together is one of the most powerful fraud-detection tools in financial analysis.

Examiner Focus — Earnings Quality via CFO/NI Comparison

The exam will present a scenario where a company reports high net income but low (or negative) CFO and ask what this suggests. The correct answer: it may indicate low earnings quality — the company may be using aggressive accounting to boost reported profits without generating real cash. Key phrase the exam uses: "the relationship between net income and operating cash flow is an indicator of earnings quality." Also important: because D&A is non-cash, the exam may ask why CFO should exceed NI for a mature company — answer: non-cash expenses (D&A) reduce NI but not CFO.

Common-Size Cash Flow Statement Analysis

Common-size analysis standardizes the cash flow statement to enable comparison across companies of different sizes and across time periods. Unlike the income statement (% of revenue) and balance sheet (% of total assets), the CFS has two different common-size approaches — and knowing the difference is testable.

Method 1: Total Inflows/Total Outflows Approach

Inflows/Outflows Approach — How It Works
  • Each cash inflow is expressed as a percentage of total cash inflows
  • Each cash outflow is expressed as a percentage of total cash outflows
  • Best used with the direct method — because operating inflows and outflows are separately presented, allowing meaningful percentage breakdown of each source/use
  • With the indirect method, only the net CFO (not its components) can be expressed as a % of total inflows/outflows — less granular
  • Useful for: identifying the dominant sources and uses of cash; seeing if a company is over-reliant on one inflow source (e.g., 97% of inflows from customers = healthy; 60% from asset sales = concern)

Method 2: Net Revenue Approach

Net Revenue Approach — How It Works
  • Every line item on the cash flow statement is expressed as a percentage of net revenue
  • Works for both direct and indirect format statements
  • The common-size denominator is net revenue (net sales) — same as the income statement common-size
  • Key advantage: enables direct forecasting. Once you forecast revenue, individual items expressed as % of revenue (capex, D&A, working capital changes) provide a basis for projecting cash flows
  • Enables comparison of CFO as a % of revenue across companies — a profitability measure (cash conversion from sales)

💡 Which Common-Size Method for Which Purpose?

Inflows/Outflows approach: Best for understanding the relative importance of each cash source and use. "What % of my cash comes from customers vs. asset sales?" Net Revenue approach: Best for trend analysis, forecasting, and cross-company comparison. "How efficiently does this company convert revenue into operating cash? How does capex compare to peers as a % of sales?" The Apple common-size example uses net revenue — note how Apple's CFO margin declined from 34.8% (2015) to 27.7% (2017) while Microsoft's rose from 31.7% to 43.9% over the same period. That trend comparison is only visible via the net revenue approach.

Examiner Focus — Common-Size Cash Flow

The exam will test whether you know: (1) the two methods (inflows/outflows vs. net revenue); (2) what each is used for; (3) with the indirect method, only net CFO (not its components) can be expressed as % of total inflows — because individual operating inflows and outflows are not shown; (4) the net revenue approach enables forecasting because each item is expressed as % of a projected revenue base. A typical exam question: "Which common-size approach would be most useful for comparing a company's capital expenditure intensity to industry peers?" Answer: net revenue approach (capex ÷ net revenue).

Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF)

Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF) is the cash flow available to all capital providers — both debt holders and equity holders — after all operating expenses (including taxes) have been paid and all necessary investments in working capital and fixed capital have been made. It is the cash available to service both debt and equity. FCFF is used in valuation models (specifically the FCFF discounted cash flow model where you discount at WACC).

FCFF — Two Derivation Approaches
Starting from Net Income:
FCFF = NI + NCC + Int(1 − Tax rate) − FCInv − WCInv

Starting from CFO (simpler — preferred when CFO is given):
FCFF = CFO + Int(1 − Tax rate) − FCInv
FCFF — Component Definitions & Logic
NI = Net income
NCC = Non-cash charges (depreciation, amortization — add back because no cash out)
Int = Interest expense (add back after-tax because FCFF is pre-debt-service)
Tax rate = Effective tax rate = Tax expense ÷ Pretax income
FCInv = Fixed capital investment (net capex = capex − proceeds from asset sales)
WCInv = Net investment in working capital = ∆WC operating assets − ∆WC operating liabilities

Why add back after-tax interest?
FCFF is available to BOTH debt AND equity holders. Interest is a payment TO debt holders.
Adding it back gives us cash flow BEFORE paying debt holders (pre-interest).
We use after-tax interest because interest expense is tax-deductible → tax benefit reduces cost.

Why subtract FCInv?
These are required investments to maintain and grow the business — not discretionary.
FCInv is NET: if company sold assets, those proceeds reduce the net investment.

The CFO-based formula is cleaner because CFO already includes NCC and WCInv adjustments.
Only need to add back after-tax interest (which CFO deducted under US GAAP) and subtract FCInv.
IFRS nuance: If a company classified interest paid in FINANCING activities (not CFO), then CFO already does NOT include interest. In that case, do NOT add back Int(1-t) — the adjustment is only needed when interest was subtracted in CFO (US GAAP treatment). Similarly, if dividends received or interest received were placed in CFI, add those back to CFO before computing FCFF.
Acme Corp — FCFF Calculation (Tax rate = 34%, CFO = $2,606; Interest paid = $258; Net FCInv = $538)

💡 Why Net Capex (Not Gross Capex) for FCInv?

FCInv = Net investment in fixed capital = Capex − Proceeds from asset sales. When a company sells old equipment, it raises cash from those assets. The net figure captures the true change in fixed capital investment: if you buy $1,300 of new equipment but sell $762 of old equipment, your net fixed capital investment is only $538. Using gross capex ($1,300) would overstate how much cash was consumed by investing activities.

Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE)

Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) is the cash flow available to the company's common stockholders — after all operating expenses, taxes, debt service (interest and principal repayment), and necessary capital investments have been made. It is the cash that could theoretically be distributed to equity holders without impairing the business. FCFE is used in equity valuation (discount at required return on equity, not WACC).

FCFE Formula
FCFE = CFO − FCInv + Net Borrowing

Where: Net Borrowing = New debt issued − Debt repaid
If net borrowing is negative (repaid more than borrowed):
FCFE = CFO − FCInv − Net Debt Repayment
FCFE — Logic Behind Each Component
CFO: Cash generated from operations (already after interest and taxes)
− FCInv: Subtract net capex (required investment; same as FCFF)
+ Net Borrowing: Debt raised ADDS to equity-holder cash (lenders funded some capex)
− Net Repayment: Debt repaid REDUCES equity-holder cash (cash used to repay lenders)

Key difference from FCFF:
FCFF: adds back interest (pre-debt service) → Available to debt + equity
FCFE: does NOT add back interest (already deducted in CFO) → Available to equity only
FCFE accounts for debt financing: if company borrows $X to fund capex, equity holders have
more cash available (lenders paid for part of the capex).

FCFE ≤ FCFF when company has net debt repayment (using equity-holder cash to pay back debt)
FCFE ≥ FCFF when company has net new borrowing (lenders adding to equity's cash pool)
FCFE is the maximum amount that could be paid as dividends without reducing the company's asset base or borrowing capacity. A company paying dividends greater than FCFE must either borrow or sell assets to fund those dividends — a potential solvency concern over time.

Examiner Focus — FCFF and FCFE

Both formulas are heavily tested. Key distinctions: (1) FCFF adds back after-tax interest — not pre-tax. Using pre-tax interest is a common error. (2) FCFE uses net borrowing (new debt minus repayments) — for Acme, net borrowing = −$500 because they repaid $500 and issued nothing. (3) Under IFRS, if interest paid was classified as CFF (not CFO), you do NOT add it back in the FCFF formula from CFO — because CFO doesn't already include it. (4) FCInv = net investment in fixed assets = capex − proceeds from asset sales. (5) FCFF is the pre-leverage measure (available to all); FCFE is the post-leverage measure (available to equity only). Higher leverage → larger gap between FCFF and FCFE.

Cash Flow Performance Ratios

Cash flow performance ratios measure the company's profitability and cash-generating ability using CFO rather than accrual-based income measures. Because CFO is harder to manipulate than net income, these ratios often provide a cleaner view of underlying performance. They are used for both time-series analysis (tracking a company over time) and cross-sectional analysis (comparing companies).

Performance Ratios — All 5 Formulas
Cash Flow to Revenue = CFO ÷ Net Revenue
Cash Return on Assets = CFO ÷ Average Total Assets
Cash Return on Equity = CFO ÷ Average Shareholders' Equity
Cash to Income = CFO ÷ Operating Income
Cash Flow per Share = (CFO − Preferred Dividends) ÷ Shares Outstanding
RatioFormulaWhat It MeasuresInterpretation
Cash Flow to Revenue CFO ÷ Net Revenue Operating cash generated per dollar of revenue Cash-based version of net profit margin. Higher = company converts more revenue into actual cash. Declining trend = possible margin compression or earnings quality deterioration.
Cash Return on Assets CFO ÷ Average Total Assets Operating cash generated per dollar of assets Cash-based ROA. Measures asset utilization efficiency. Average total assets = (Beg + End) ÷ 2. Compares how well assets generate cash vs. accrual income.
Cash Return on Equity CFO ÷ Average Shareholders' Equity Operating cash generated per dollar of equity Cash-based ROE. Higher leverage amplifies this ratio (same CFO over smaller equity base). Must compare to industry norms for meaningful interpretation.
Cash to Income CFO ÷ Operating Income Cash-generating ability of operations Converts operating income to a cash flow multiple. Ratio > 1 expected (D&A adds to CFO without reducing operating income). Ratio < 1 = warning signal (operations generating paper income but not cash).
Cash Flow per Share (CFO − Pref. Div.) ÷ Common Shares Operating cash on a per-share basis Cash-based EPS equivalent. Used to assess if share price is supported by cash generation. IFRS note: if dividends paid are in CFO, add them back before subtracting preferred dividends.

This illustrates why interpretation of ratios requires understanding the underlying business model.

Cash Flow Coverage Ratios

Cash flow coverage ratios measure the company's ability to meet specific obligations — interest payments, debt repayment, capital expenditures, dividend payments — using operating cash flow. They are solvency-focused: can the company service its commitments with cash from operations?

Coverage Ratios — All 6 Formulas
Debt Coverage = CFO ÷ Total Debt
Interest Coverage = (CFO + Interest paid + Taxes paid) ÷ Interest paid
Reinvestment = CFO ÷ Cash paid for long-term assets
Debt Payment = CFO ÷ Cash paid for long-term debt repayment
Dividend Payment = CFO ÷ Dividends paid
Investing & Financing = CFO ÷ Cash outflows for investing and financing activities
RatioFormulaWhat It MeasuresHigher = ?
Debt Coverage CFO ÷ Total Debt Financial risk and leverage. How quickly can the company repay total debt from CFO? Lower financial risk. If = 0.25, takes ~4 years to repay debt from CFO.
Interest Coverage (CFO + Int paid + Tax paid) ÷ Int paid Ability to meet interest obligations from cash flow (cash-based coverage). Higher = more comfortable interest coverage. Numerator restores pre-interest, pre-tax CFO.
Reinvestment CFO ÷ Capex (cash paid for LT assets) Ability to fund capital expenditures from operating cash flow. >1 = self-funding capex from operations. <1 = needs external funding for capex.
Debt Payment CFO ÷ Cash paid for LT debt repayment Ability to repay long-term debt from operating cash flows. Higher = more able to service debt obligations from operations.
Dividend Payment CFO ÷ Dividends paid Ability to pay dividends from operating cash flow. >1 = dividends covered by CFO. <1 = borrowing or selling assets to pay dividends.
Investing & Financing CFO ÷ (CFI outflows + CFF outflows) Overall ability to cover all investing and financing obligations from operations. Higher = operations generate sufficient cash for all obligations.

⚠️ Interest Coverage Ratio — IFRS Nuance

The cash-based interest coverage ratio formula adds back interest paid and taxes paid to CFO: (CFO + Interest paid + Taxes paid) ÷ Interest paid. This restores CFO to a pre-interest, pre-tax basis, making it comparable to an EBIT-based interest coverage ratio. IFRS exception: If the company classified interest paid in CFF (not CFO), then interest paid should NOT be added back to the numerator — because it wasn't deducted from CFO in the first place. The LES footnote specifically calls this out. Always check where interest was classified before applying the formula.

Examiner Focus — Cash Flow Ratios

The exam will test you on: (1) knowing which formula to use for which ratio — the names are intuitive but formulas must be memorized; (2) the interest coverage numerator: CFO + interest paid + taxes paid (not just CFO ÷ interest). This unusual numerator reconstructs pre-interest, pre-tax CFO to match an EBIT-equivalent; (3) the reinvestment ratio interpretation: >1 means CFO exceeds capex (company self-funds). The exam may give you a company with reinvestment ratio <1 and ask what this implies (company must borrow or sell assets to fund capital expenditures); (4) dividend coverage: if this ratio is <1, the company is paying dividends it cannot fund from operations — a sustainability concern; (5) IFRS nuance for interest coverage — don't add back interest if it was in CFF.

  • ✅ Dividend coverage < 1: Dividends not supported by CFO — unsustainable without borrowing or asset sales.

═══ CFO vs. NI — EARNINGS QUALITY ═══
CFO > NI → Expected (D&A non-cash) → High earnings quality
CFO < NI → Warning signal → Possible aggressive revenue recognition or accrual manipulation

═══ COMMON-SIZE APPROACHES ═══
Method 1 (Inflows/Outflows): Each inflow ÷ Total inflows; each outflow ÷ Total outflows
  → Best with direct method; with indirect, only net CFO is shown
Method 2 (Net Revenue): Each item ÷ Net revenue
  → Works for all; best for forecasting and cross-company comparison

═══ FCFF ═══
From CFO: FCFF = CFO + Int(1 − t) − FCInv
From NI: FCFF = NI + NCC + Int(1 − t) − FCInv − WCInv
FCInv = Capex − Proceeds from asset sales (NET investment in fixed capital)
t = effective tax rate = Tax expense ÷ Pretax income
IFRS: if Int paid in CFF → do NOT add back Int(1−t) in CFO-based formula

═══ FCFE ═══
FCFE = CFO − FCInv + Net Borrowing
Net Borrowing = New debt − Debt repaid (negative if net repayer)
No interest add-back (interest already subtracted in CFO)
FCFF − FCFE = Int(1−t) − Net Borrowing (the leverage difference)

═══ PERFORMANCE RATIOS ═══
CFO/Revenue → Cash profit margin
CFO/Avg Total Assets → Cash ROA
CFO/Avg Equity → Cash ROE
CFO/Operating Income → Cash conversion
(CFO − Pref Div)/Shares → CFO per share

═══ COVERAGE RATIOS ═══
Debt coverage = CFO ÷ Total Debt
Interest coverage = (CFO + Int paid + Taxes paid) ÷ Int paid
Reinvestment = CFO ÷ Capex
Debt payment = CFO ÷ LT Debt repaid
Dividend payment = CFO ÷ Dividends paid
Investing & Financing = CFO ÷ (CFI outflows + CFF outflows)
Top exam traps for Module 5:
(1) FCFF adds back AFTER-TAX interest (not pre-tax). Int(1−t), not Int. Tax benefit from interest reduces its true cost.
(2) FCFE does NOT add back interest — CFO already deducted it. The add-back is only in FCFF.
(3) IFRS companies classifying interest in CFF: do NOT add Int(1−t) to CFO in the FCFF formula — it wasn't subtracted from CFO.
(4) FCInv = NET capital investment = capex − proceeds from asset sales. Not gross capex.
(5) Net borrowing in FCFE: positive (new borrowing) ADDS to FCFE. Negative (net repayment) SUBTRACTS.
(6) Interest coverage numerator = CFO + Int paid + Taxes paid (restores pre-tax, pre-interest CFO). It's NOT just CFO ÷ Int.
(7) Common-size with indirect method: only NET CFO (not individual operating inflows/outflows) expressed as % — components not separately disclosed.
(8) CFO < NI persistently → low earnings quality. D&A should make CFO > NI normally.
(9) Reinvestment ratio <1 = CFO cannot cover capex → must use external funding.
(10) Dividend coverage < 1 = dividends exceed CFO → funded by borrowing or asset sales → sustainability risk.
