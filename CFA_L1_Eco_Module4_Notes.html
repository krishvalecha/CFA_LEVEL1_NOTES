Roles & Objectives of Central Banks

Six Core Roles

  1. Monopoly supplier of the currency — sole issuer of legal tender (fiat money). Fiat money = not convertible to any commodity; derives value from government decree and acceptance.
  2. Banker to the government and the bankers' bank — holds government accounts; holds reserves of commercial banks.
  3. Lender of last resort — can supply liquidity to banks facing crisis. Knowledge of this role helps prevent bank runs.
  4. Regulator and supervisor of the payments system — ensures robustness, standardization, and international coordination.
  5. Conductor of monetary policy — the highest profile role. Manages money supply and credit to influence economic activity.
  6. Supervisor of the banking system — may be shared with or delegated to a separate authority (e.g., US has multiple supervisors; UK's Bank of England regained this role in 2013 after the FSA period).
🧠 Mnemonic — "MBLRCS" = 6 Central Bank Roles

Monopoly currency supplier · Banker (to govt & banks) · Lender of last resort · Regulator (payments system) · Conductor (monetary policy) · Supervisor (banking system)

Fiat Money — Key Concept

Fiat money = money not convertible into any commodity (e.g., gold abandoned in 20th century). Its value comes from government decree + general acceptance as medium of exchange and unit of account.

Since fiat money can theoretically be created indefinitely, the central bank's role as guardian of the currency's value becomes paramount — hence the primary objective of price stability.

Primary Objective of Monetary Policy

Overarching Goal
Price Stability → Controlling Inflation
Most central banks specify price stability as their primary objective. Some also have secondary mandates: full employment (US Fed — "maximum employment, stable prices, moderate long-term rates"), financial system stability, output growth. The Fed's dual mandate makes it unique among major central banks.
💡 Gold Standard vs. Fiat Money

Under the gold standard, currencies were convertible to gold at fixed rates (UK abandoned this in 1931). Under fiat money, no such backing exists. This is why central bank credibility and independence are so critical — without the discipline of convertibility, inflation control relies entirely on institutional commitment.

⚠ Exam Trap — Fed Funds Rate vs. Discount Rate

The Fed Funds Rate is the interbank overnight lending rate — it is the primary policy instrument. The Discount Rate is what banks pay to borrow directly from the Fed. Both are US-specific. The Fed Funds Rate is the more important and widely watched of the two.

The Monetary Transmission Mechanism

Definition

The monetary transmission mechanism is the process by which a central bank's policy rate change is transmitted through the economy and ultimately affects inflation. Policy rates target very short-term rates (often overnight), but the real economy borrows and lends over much longer horizons — transmission bridges this gap.

Four Channels of Transmission

Example: Central bank raises policy rate → works through 4 interconnected channels simultaneously:

1
Bank Lending Rates (Short-term interest rates)
Commercial banks raise their base rates → borrowing costs rise for households and businesses → less borrowing → less spending on consumption and investment → ↓ Domestic Demand
↓
2
Asset Prices
Higher discount rate lowers present value of future cash flows → bond prices fall, equity prices fall, capital project values fall → ↓ household wealth → ↓ consumption (wealth effect) → ↓ Domestic Demand
↓
3
Expectations / Confidence
Market participants expect slower growth → reduce investment and consumption decisions ahead of actual slowdown → self-fulfilling anticipation → ↓ demand. Also: if rate hike is expected to be followed by more hikes, forward guidance amplifies the effect.
↓
4
Exchange Rate
Higher interest rates attract foreign capital → domestic currency appreciates → exports become more expensive to foreign buyers → ↓ export demand → ↓ Net External Demand. Also: stronger currency → ↓ import prices → direct downward pressure on inflation.
Final Outcome of Rate Hike
↓ Domestic Demand + ↓ Net External Demand + ↓ Import Prices → ↓ Total Demand → ↓ Inflation
All four channels work in the same direction (reinforcing each other) when policy rate rises. For a rate cut, all arrows reverse → ↑ demand → ↑ inflation.
💡 Why Does Changing Short-Term Rates Affect Long-Term Rates?

Long-term interest rates reflect the expected path of future short-term rates. If the market expects a series of rate hikes, long-term yields will rise immediately, even before those hikes occur. This is why central bank communication (forward guidance) is so powerful — credible signals about future policy move long rates today.

04

Monetary Policy Objectives & Targeting

Three Qualities of Effective Central Banks

Independence
  • Shielded from political pressure → avoid keeping rates too low before elections
  • Operational independence: CB sets rates; govt sets the target (UK, New Zealand, Sweden)
  • Target + operational independence: CB sets both the target AND rates (ECB)
  • Heads still appointed by government → perfect independence impossible
Credibility
  • Belief that CB will actually hit its target
  • If credible → inflation expectations become self-fulfilling (2% expected = 2% in wage contracts)
  • Governments have lower credibility (incentive to inflate away debt)
  • Critical for anchoring long-run inflation expectations
Transparency
  • Quarterly inflation reports published — review broad money, financial markets, real economy, prices → followed by growth/inflation forecast (2-year horizon)
  • Transparent decision-making builds reputation and credibility
  • Helps CB influence inflation expectations → easier to hit target
  • Monthly interest rate decisions are publicly explained
🧠 Mnemonic — "ICT" = Independence, Credibility, Transparency

The three pillars of effective central banking. New Zealand pioneered this model in 1989 (Reserve Bank of New Zealand Act) — now widely copied globally.

Inflation Targeting Framework

Typical target: ~2% CPI inflation with a ±1% tolerance band (1%–3% range). Why 2% and not 0%?

  • 0% target risks deflation — which is very hard to escape (Japan example)
  • 10% target → price instability and volatility
  • 2% = far enough from deflation risk, low enough for price stability
  • Forward-looking: CB targets inflation 2 years ahead, not current inflation, because policy changes take time to transmit through the economy

Notable Exceptions to Inflation Targeting

Central BankApproachReason
Bank of Japan (BoJ) No explicit inflation target Fighting deflation for decades — inflation targeting is a tool to fight inflation, not deflation. Low credibility in achieving positive inflation.
US Federal Reserve No single explicit inflation target; dual mandate Statutory goal: maximum employment + stable prices + moderate long-term rates. Watches PCE (personal consumption expenditure) deflator ~2% as informal guide.
ECB Explicit ~2% target; target + operational independence Most independent of the major central banks — sets both the definition of inflation and the rate level independently of governments.
⚠ Exam Trap — Fed's Inflation Gauge

The US Federal Reserve monitors the PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditure) deflator as its preferred inflation gauge — not the CPI. The informal target is ~2% core PCE. Markets watch this closely to anticipate Fed rate moves.

Exchange Rate Targeting (Developing Countries)

Many developing economies target their currency's exchange rate (vs. a major currency like USD) rather than inflation. The logic: by pegging to a low-inflation currency, the domestic economy "imports" the inflation of the target economy.

Mechanism when domestic inflation rises above target: Currency weakens → CB sells foreign reserves and buys domestic currency → ↓ domestic money supply → ↑ domestic interest rates → monetary tightening → inflation falls → currency stabilizes.

Key risk: Must be credible and supported by adequate reserves. If not → speculative attack (e.g., UK sterling forced out of European ERM in 1992; Thai baht crisis 1997 — CB ran out of reserves in July 1997 → debt crisis + Asian contagion).

Dollarization: Stronger than pegging — the domestic currency is completely replaced by USD. No domestic monetary policy possible.

💡 Exchange Rate Targeting Trade-Off

When a country fixes its exchange rate, it loses independent control of monetary policy. Interest rates must adjust to defend the peg, not to stabilize the domestic economy. This can cause domestic interest rate volatility. The country imports the monetary policy of the anchor economy — good if the anchor is credible, dangerous if domestic conditions diverge.

05

Neutral Rate & Contractionary / Expansionary Policy

The Neutral Rate of Interest

The neutral rate (or natural rate) is the interest rate that neither stimulates nor restrains the economy — it corresponds to the average policy rate over a full business cycle.

Neutral Rate Formula
Neutral Rate = Real Trend Growth Rate + Long-Run Expected Inflation
Example: Real trend growth = 2.5%, Inflation target = 2.0% → Neutral Rate = 4.5%
Policy rate > Neutral Rate → Contractionary monetary policy
Policy rate < Neutral Rate → Expansionary monetary policy
Policy rate = Neutral Rate → Neutral (neither tightening nor loosening)
⚠ Key Limitation

The neutral rate is unobservable — it must be estimated. Economists disagree on its value. It changes over time (e.g., post-2008 GFC, many economists revised down trend growth estimates for western economies → neutral rate fell). Policy described as "contractionary" or "expansionary" depends entirely on how the neutral rate is estimated.

Demand Shock vs. Supply Shock — Different Policy Response

Demand Shock (Inflation)
  • Caused by: excess consumer/business confidence → ↑ consumption & investment
  • Appropriate response: Tighten monetary policy (raise rates)
  • Reduces demand pressure → brings inflation down
  • Classic case for contractionary monetary policy
Supply Shock (Inflation)
  • Caused by: ↑ oil prices → cost-push inflation
  • Economy already hurting: profits fall, consumption falls, unemployment rises
  • Tightening policy here would exacerbate the downturn
  • Central bank must identify the shock source before responding
💡 Key Exam Insight — Source of Shock Matters

Inflation from a demand shock → tighten. Inflation from a supply shock → tightening may make things worse. This distinction is crucial for monetary policy analysis. An oil price spike causes both inflation AND economic weakness simultaneously — a central bank that blindly tightens risks a severe recession.

06

Limitations of Monetary Policy & Quantitative Easing

Problems in the Transmission Mechanism

  1. Long-rate paradox: CB raises short rates to cool inflation, but bond market participants think it's overdoing it → long-term rates fall (bond vigilantes bid up bonds) → cheaper long-term borrowing actually stimulates rather than contracts. A less credible CB is more vulnerable to this.
  2. Deposit and credit control limits: CB cannot control how much households and corporations deposit in banks, nor can it force banks to lend. Banks may sit on excess reserves rather than extend credit, especially in difficult economic conditions.
  3. Liquidity trap: In extreme conditions, demand for money becomes infinitely elastic — people hold any additional money supply without reducing interest rates further (horizontal money demand curve). Further monetary injections have no effect. Associated with deflation.

Deflation — Why It's So Hard to Fight

Deflation = pervasive, persistent fall in the general price level. Problems:

  • Real debt rises — deflation increases the real value of outstanding debt → borrowers in distress
  • Delayed consumption — consumers wait to buy (prices will be lower tomorrow) → demand falls → further deflation
  • Zero lower bound: Nominal interest rates can't easily go much below zero (though some European countries and Japan did cut below zero). At or near zero, conventional monetary stimulus runs out.
  • Deflationary trap: weak consumption + falling prices + rising real debt = self-reinforcing spiral. Japan experienced this post-1990 property bubble.

Quantitative Easing (QE)

QE is an unconventional monetary policy tool used when conventional interest rate cuts are exhausted (near zero nominal rates). Operationally similar to open market operations but on a much larger scale.

Mechanism: Central bank creates money and uses it to purchase assets (government bonds, mortgage-backed securities) from banks, institutions, or the public. This injects reserves into the banking system, hoping to kick-start lending and broad money growth → ↑ economic activity.

QE ProgramCountryAssets PurchasedPurpose
QE1 (Bank of England) UK Gilts (3–5 year maturity) Kick-start lending; expand broad money growth
QE1 (Federal Reserve) US Mortgage bonds (Freddie Mac, Fannie Mae); later interbank/commercial paper Support housing market; restore liquidity to failed markets
QE2 (Federal Reserve) US $600bn long-dated US Treasuries (Nov 2010) Keep long bond yields low → encourage business/household borrowing
QE3 (Federal Reserve) US $40bn/month agency MBS (Sep 2012) "Until labor market improved substantially." Tapered Dec 2013; ended Oct 2014.
⚠ QE Risk — Monetization of Deficit

If the central bank purchases government securities on a large scale, it effectively funds the government's budget deficit — the independence of monetary policy becomes an illusion. This is called the monetization of the government deficit. Many economists fear this as it could lead to uncontrolled inflation if the CB loses credibility. A CB that accumulates bad assets risks a loss of confidence in its fiat currency.

Summary of Monetary Policy Limitations

  • Cannot control how much households/firms deposit in banks
  • Cannot easily control banks' willingness to lend (credit expansion)
  • Transmission mechanism can be disrupted (bond vigilantes, expectations divergence)
  • Liquidity trap: policy becomes ineffective when demand for money is infinitely elastic
  • Near-zero nominal rates limit further conventional easing
  • QE has no guarantee of working if banks prefer to hold excess reserves
  • Developing countries face additional challenges: no liquid bond market, poor credibility, rapid financial innovation, government interference

Interaction of Monetary & Fiscal Policy

Key Principle

Both policies can alter aggregate demand, but through different channels with different impacts on the composition of AD. They are not interchangeable. Crucially, the fiscal multiplier is larger when monetary policy is accommodative (IMF research finding).

Four Policy Mix Combinations

Easy Fiscal + Tight Monetary

↑G or ↓T raises output. Tight money (↓money supply) raises interest rates → crowding out private sector. Result: Higher output, higher interest rates, larger government share of GDP.

Tight Fiscal + Easy Monetary

↓G or ↑T shrinks public sector. Low interest rates stimulate private sector. Result: Private sector rises as % of GDP, public sector shrinks, lower interest rates. Encourages private investment.

Easy Fiscal + Easy Monetary

Both expansionary simultaneously. Result: Highly expansionary — large ↑ AD, lower interest rates (if monetary dominates), growing public AND private sectors. Risk of inflation if at full employment.

Tight Fiscal + Tight Monetary

Both contractionary. ↑ interest rates reduce private demand. ↑ taxes / ↓G reduce aggregate demand from public and private sectors. Result: Strong overall demand contraction.

Fiscal Multiplier with vs. without Monetary Accommodation (IMF Research)

Fiscal ToolMultiplier: No Monetary AccommodationMultiplier: With Monetary AccommodationKey Takeaway
Government Expenditure 1.6 3.9 Accommodation makes multiplier 2.4× larger. Real rates fall → private spending surges.
Targeted Social Transfers (poorest) 0.5 1.7 High MPC recipients → strong effect when monetary supports it.
Non-targeted Transfers 0.25 0.85 Not seen as permanent → weaker effect.
Labor Tax Cuts Moderate Less positive (exception) Supply-side effects weaker; less amplified by monetary accommodation.
💡 Why Does Monetary Accommodation Amplify the Fiscal Multiplier?

When the central bank keeps rates unchanged (accommodative) despite the fiscal expansion → rising demand and inflation → real interest rates actually fall (nominal rate fixed, inflation rising) → additional private sector spending on investment and consumption → further multiplier boost. Without accommodation, the CB raises rates to offset the fiscal stimulus → crowds out private spending → smaller multiplier.

FeatureFiscal PolicyMonetary Policy

Information problemBoth suffer from data lags and revision. Fiscal policy has additional implementation lags for capital spending.
⚠ Ricardian Equivalence — The Link to Monetary Policy

If Ricardian equivalence holds perfectly — tax cuts have no impact on private spending (households save the tax cut to pay future taxes) — fiscal policy becomes ineffective. This strengthens the case for using monetary policy instead. In practice, Ricardian equivalence rarely holds fully, but it does weaken the fiscal multiplier.
