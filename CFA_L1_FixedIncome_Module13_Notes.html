Yield Duration vs. Curve Duration — The Gap That Requires New Measures

Modules 11 and 12 covered yield duration measures (Macaulay, modified, money duration, PVBP) — all of which assume a bond's cash flows are certain and measure sensitivity to changes in the bond's own YTM. These work well for standard option-free, investment-grade bonds.

However, for bonds with embedded options (callable, putable), MBS, and other complex instruments, cash flows are uncertain — they depend on whether and when the embedded option is exercised, which depends on future interest rates. For such bonds: (1) the YTM may not be well-defined (callable bonds have a "yield-to-worst," not a single clean YTM); (2) Macaulay and modified durations are not appropriate; (3) a different approach is needed.

Yield Duration — When Applicable
  • Option-free fixed-rate bonds
  • Investment-grade bonds with certain cash flows
  • Measures sensitivity to bond's OWN YTM
  • Assumes cash flows are fixed and known
  • MacDur, ModDur, MoneyDur, PVBP
Curve Duration — When Needed
  • Callable bonds, putable bonds
  • Mortgage-backed securities (MBS)
  • Asset-backed securities (ABS)
  • Measures sensitivity to BENCHMARK YIELD CURVE shifts
  • Allows for uncertain cash flows via option pricing models
  • Effective duration, effective convexity, key rate duration
⚠️ Why Macaulay/Modified Duration Fail for Callable Bonds

A callable bond has multiple possible lives — it may be called early (short life) or held to maturity (full life). The "yield-to-worst" is only one of many possible outcomes. The modified duration of a callable bond does not reflect which scenario will actually occur. More critically, when interest rates fall (the most likely time for the bond to be called), the callable bond's price doesn't rise as much as an equivalent non-callable bond — the embedded call option limits price appreciation. Modified duration cannot capture this cap on price appreciation. Effective duration, derived from actual (option-model-derived) prices, correctly captures this behavior.

Effective Duration — Formula, Inputs & Key Differences (Equation 1)

Effective Duration (EffDur)
The sensitivity of a bond's full price to a parallel shift in the benchmark yield curve (e.g., the government par curve), rather than a change in the bond's own YTM. It is a curve duration statistic — appropriate for all bonds, including those with embedded options, where cash flows are uncertain. PV+ and PV− are derived from option pricing models, not simply from discounting fixed cash flows.
Equation 1 — Effective Duration
EffDur = (PV− − PV+) / (2 × ΔCurve × PV₀)

WHERE:
PV₀ = current full price (at current benchmark curve level)
PV+ = full price when benchmark curve shifts UP by ΔCurve
PV− = full price when benchmark curve shifts DOWN by ΔCurve
ΔCurve = size of parallel benchmark yield curve shift (e.g., 0.0005 for 5bps)

KEY DIFFERENCE from ApproxModDur (Eq. 4, Module 11):
ApproxModDur: denominator uses ΔYield (bond's own YTM change)
EffDur: denominator uses ΔCurve (benchmark yield curve shift)

ApproxModDur: PV+/PV− from discounting fixed cash flows at new YTM
EffDur: PV+/PV− from OPTION PRICING MODEL at new benchmark level
Option pricing model inputs (held constant except benchmark level):
(1) Length of the call protection period
(2) Schedule of call prices and call dates
(3) Assumption about credit spreads over benchmark yields (including liquidity spread)
(4) Assumption about future interest rate volatility
(5) Level of market interest rates (the government par curve) → THIS is what shifts

The analyst holds (1)–(4) constant and shifts (5) up and down to generate PV+ and PV−. This isolates the pure interest rate effect on the uncertain cash flows.

BRWA Bond — EffDur vs. ApproxModDur Worked Comparison

BRWA 5yr, 3.2% semi, PV₀=100.00. ΔYield = ΔCurve = 5bps = 0.0005.
YIELD-BASED (ApproxModDur) — using bond's own YTM:
PV+ = 99.770965 (YTM rises from 3.20% to 3.25%)
PV− = 100.229641 (YTM falls from 3.20% to 3.15%)
ApproxModDur = (100.229641 − 99.770965) / (2 × 0.0005 × 100.00) = 4.587

CURVE-BASED (EffDur) — benchmark par curve shifts non-parallel to spot rates:
PV+ = 99.760 (benchmark curve shifts UP 5bps; spot rates shift non-parallel)
PV− = 100.241 (benchmark curve shifts DOWN 5bps)
EffDur = (100.241 − 99.760) / (2 × 0.0005 × 100.00)
= 0.481 / 0.100
= 4.816

DIFFERENCE: EffDur = 4.816 > ApproxModDur = 4.587
Reason: A parallel shift in the par curve causes a NON-PARALLEL shift in spot rates,
resulting in a slightly larger price change → higher measured sensitivity.

Effective Convexity — Formula & Key Fact (Equation 2)

Equation 2 — Effective Convexity
EffCon = [(PV−) + (PV+) − 2×PV₀] / ((ΔCurve)² × PV₀)

SAME STRUCTURE as ApproxCon (Eq. 2, Module 12) — but uses ΔCurve in denominator
and PV+/PV− from option pricing models (same as for EffDur)

BRWA bond example (5bps ΔCurve, PV+=99.760, PV−=100.241, PV₀=100.00):
EffCon = (100.241 + 99.760 − 2×100.00) / ((0.0005)² × 100.00)
= (200.001 − 200.000) / (0.00000025 × 100)
= 0.001 / 0.000025
= 40.000

Compare to ApproxCon = 24.239 (using bond's own yield)
EffCon = 40.000 > ApproxCon = 24.239 (same reason as EffDur > ApproxModDur)
Critical fact about effective convexity:
• Non-callable option-free bonds: EffCon is always positive
• Callable bonds: EffCon can turn NEGATIVE when yields are low (call option limits price appreciation → curvature goes negative — the bond's price–yield curve bends downward at low yields)
• Putable bonds: EffCon is ALWAYS positive (the put floor protects price → curvature always helpful to investor)

Why not choose smaller ΔCurve for better accuracy? Unlike with ModDur where smaller ΔYield improves accuracy, for complex bonds (callable, MBS) the pricing model inputs (issuer call behavior, prepayment behavior) change with interest rates in complex ways. Making ΔCurve smaller doesn't necessarily improve the estimate because the option exercise behavior may not be well-defined at very small rate changes. The choice of ΔCurve involves judgment specific to the instrument.

Callable Bond Price–Yield Profile — Exhibit 1 (Full Analysis)

Exhibit 1 compares a callable bond with an otherwise identical non-callable bond (same coupon, maturity, payment frequency, credit risk). The difference between their prices at any point is the value of the embedded call option — held by the issuer, not the investor.

Non-Callable Bond
  • Price rises fully as yields fall (standard convex shape)
  • Positive effective convexity always
  • Full price appreciation when benchmark yields decline
  • Benchmark: the "full value" — callable bond always priced below this
Callable Bond
  • Price rises normally when yields are HIGH (call option = low value)
  • Price appreciation LIMITED when yields are LOW (call likely to be exercised)
  • Price approaches and then is capped near the call price
  • Effective convexity becomes NEGATIVE when yields are low
  • Always priced BELOW the non-callable equivalent
Value of Embedded Call Option

= Non-callable price − Callable price

  • Yields HIGH: option value is LOW (unlikely to be exercised — refinancing at high rates makes no sense)
  • Yields LOW: option value is HIGH (issuer will refinance at lower rates)
  • The investor SOLD this option to the issuer — bears "call risk"

Callable Bond — Effective Duration and Effective Convexity Behavior

Benchmark Yield LevelCall Option ValueEffDur vs. Non-CallableEffConInvestor Impact
HIGH yields (ΔCurve positive) Low — call unlikely ≈ Same as non-callable Positive (≈ same) Similar to owning a non-callable bond
LOW yields (ΔCurve negative) High — call likely LOWER than non-callable Can turn NEGATIVE Price appreciation severely limited; investor must reinvest at lower rates if called
⚠️ Negative Convexity — The Key Callable Bond Characteristic

When a callable bond enters the range of negative effective convexity, the price–yield curve bends downward (concave rather than convex). This means the bond behaves perversely: a further decline in yields produces smaller and smaller price gains (and eventually no gain at all as the price is capped near the call price). This is the worst property for an investor — you lose both the price appreciation (compared to a non-callable) AND bear the risk of having the bond called away. Investors demand a higher yield (call premium) to compensate for this feature.

05

Putable Bond Price–Yield Profile — Exhibit 2 (Full Analysis)

A putable bond gives the investor (not the issuer) the right to sell the bond back to the issuer at a specified price (typically par) at specified dates. This protects the investor when rates rise (bond price falls), because they can put the bond back at par rather than selling at a market loss.

Non-Putable Bond
  • Price falls fully as yields rise (standard shape)
  • No protection on the downside
  • Investor bears full price depreciation risk
  • Lower price than putable equivalent when yields are high
Putable Bond
  • Price rises normally when yields are LOW (put option = low value)
  • Price SUPPORTED / FLOORED when yields are HIGH (put likely exercised)
  • Price stays near (or above) par when yields are very high
  • Effective convexity is ALWAYS positive (put floor prevents concavity)
  • Always priced ABOVE the non-putable equivalent
Value of Embedded Put Option

= Putable price − Non-putable price

  • Yields LOW: option value is LOW (investor won't put the bond when it's trading above par)
  • Yields HIGH: option value is HIGH (investor will sell back at par to avoid losses)
  • The investor OWNS this option — pays for it via lower yield

Putable Bond — Effective Duration and Convexity

Benchmark Yield LevelPut Option ValueEffDur vs. Non-PutableEffConInvestor Impact
LOW yields (ΔCurve negative) Low — put unlikely ≈ Same as non-putable Positive (≈ same) Behaves like a non-putable bond
HIGH yields (ΔCurve positive) High — put likely LOWER than non-putable Still Positive (always) Investor can put bond at par → protected from price fall; can reinvest at higher rates
💡 Why Putable Bonds Always Have Positive Convexity

The put floor creates a support level for the bond's price — it cannot fall indefinitely below the put price. This floor means the price–yield curve always bends upward (toward the investor) on the downside: as yields rise past the point where the put becomes valuable, price stops falling and stabilizes. This upward curvature is positive convexity. In contrast, the callable bond's cap creates a downward bend (negative convexity) on the upside. The investor's option (put) always creates positive convexity; the issuer's option (call) creates negative convexity when in the money.

★ Summary — Callable vs. Putable: Who Benefits From What?

The key question: who holds the option? The option holder benefits when they exercise; the option writer loses.

Callable bond:
Issuer holds the call option. When rates fall → issuer calls → investor loses upside and must reinvest at lower rates. Reduces EffDur when rates fall. Can create negative EffCon.

Putable bond:
Investor holds the put option. When rates rise → investor puts → issuer must repurchase at par. Reduces EffDur when rates rise. EffCon remains always positive.

MBS — Prepayment Option = Like a Callable Bond

Mortgage-backed securities (MBS) cash flows depend on homeowners' prepayment decisions — when rates fall, homeowners refinance their mortgages (effectively "calling" the MBS). This makes MBS behave like callable bonds: price appreciation is limited when rates fall (negative convexity at low rates). Effective duration and effective convexity are the only appropriate risk measures for MBS, because standard yield duration cannot capture this prepayment optionality.

Price Change Formula Using EffDur + EffCon — Equation 3

Equation 3 — Percentage Full Price Change Using Effective Measures
%ΔPV^Full ≈ (−EffDur × ΔCurve) + [½ × EffCon × (ΔCurve)²]

IDENTICAL STRUCTURE to Equation 1 of Module 12 — but:
• AnnModDur replaced by EffDur
• AnnConvexity replaced by EffCon
• ΔYield replaced by ΔCurve (parallel benchmark shift)

For callable bond in low-rate environment:
EffDur is smaller (shorter expected life due to likely call)
EffCon may be negative → convexity term SUBTRACTS from gain
→ Price gain from falling rates is doubly limited

For putable bond in high-rate environment:
EffDur is smaller (put provides protection)
EffCon still positive → convexity term adds to estimate
→ Price loss from rising rates is limited on both counts

ModDur vs. EffDur on Option-Free Bonds — When and Why They Differ

Even for standard option-free bonds (where EffDur is not "needed"), ModDur and EffDur will generally not be identical. This is because they measure different things:

  • ModDur: Sensitivity to a change in the bond's own YTM (a single yield)
  • EffDur: Sensitivity to a parallel shift in the benchmark government par curve (affects multiple rates)

When the government par curve shifts in parallel, the government spot (zero) rates shift in a non-parallel manner — long spot rates change by different amounts than short spot rates. This non-parallel spot rate shift causes a slightly different bond price change than a pure YTM change would produce. Hence, EffDur ≠ ModDur in general.

ConditionModDur vs. EffDur Gap
Flatter yield curveGap is SMALLER (parallel par shift ≈ parallel spot shift when curve is flat)
Steeper yield curveGap is LARGER
Shorter time-to-maturityGap is SMALLER
Longer time-to-maturityGap is LARGER
Bond priced closer to parGap is SMALLER
Bond priced far from parGap is LARGER
Perfectly flat yield curveGap = ZERO (the only case where they are exactly equal)
💡 BRWA Comparison: ModDur=4.587 vs. EffDur=4.816

For the BRWA bond, EffDur (4.816) is higher than ApproxModDur (4.587). This means the bond is slightly MORE sensitive to a benchmark yield curve shift than to a pure YTM change of the same magnitude. The reason: a 5bp par curve shift causes the spot rates to shift in a non-parallel way — specifically, spot rates at longer maturities shift by slightly more than 5bps, amplifying the price effect. This is a nuance specific to how yield curves work, not a contradiction.

Key Rate Duration — Measuring Non-Parallel Yield Curve Sensitivity

Key Rate Duration (KeyRateDur_k) — also called Partial Duration
A measure of a bond's (or portfolio's) sensitivity to a change in the benchmark yield at one specific maturity point on the yield curve, holding all other maturities unchanged. Key rate durations capture "shaping risk" — the risk that the yield curve changes shape (steepens, flattens, twists) rather than shifting in parallel. The sum of all key rate durations equals the bond's effective duration.

The problem effective duration doesn't solve: Effective duration assumes ALL yields change by the same amount simultaneously (parallel shift). In reality, the 2-year yield might rise while the 10-year yield stays flat (curve flattening). Effective duration cannot distinguish these scenarios. Key rate duration can — it tells you exactly how sensitive your bond/portfolio is to each specific point on the yield curve.

Equations 4, 5 & 6 — Key Rate Duration Formulas
KeyRateDur_k = −(1/PV) × (ΔPV/Δr_k) [Equation 4]

Sum property: Σ KeyRateDur_k = EffDur [Equation 5]

For estimating price change (Equation 6):
%ΔPV/PV = −KeyRateDur_k × Δr_k

WHERE:
k = specific maturity point (e.g., 0.5yr, 2yr, 5yr, 10yr, 20yr, 30yr)
r_k = benchmark yield at maturity k
Δr_k = change in the benchmark yield at maturity k (holding others constant)
ΔPV = change in bond price from that single-maturity shift

Calculation procedure:
Step 1: Shift only the k-th benchmark rate up by 1bp
Step 2: Calculate new bond price PV+
Step 3: Shift only the k-th rate down by 1bp
Step 4: Calculate PV−
Step 5: KeyRateDur_k = (PV− − PV+) / (2 × 0.0001 × PV₀)
Step 6: Repeat for each key maturity (0.5, 2, 5, 10, 20, 30 years)
The tent shape of a key rate shift (Exhibit 3): When the k-th key rate is shifted, the yield curve is perturbed only in a triangular region around maturity k — it rises linearly from k−1 to k, then falls linearly from k to k+1, and is unchanged outside this range. This triangular tent shape ensures that individual key rate shifts sum to a parallel shift, so the key rate durations sum exactly to the effective duration.

Typical key maturities: 0.5yr, 2yr, 5yr, 10yr, 20yr, 30yr — the major benchmark tenors. Some implementations use more granular maturities (1yr, 2yr, 3yr, 5yr, 7yr, 10yr, 20yr, 30yr).
💡 Key Rate Duration — Practical Uses

Measuring shaping risk: If a bond has high key rate duration at the 10-year point and very low key rate duration at the 2-year point, it is highly sensitive to changes in long rates but not short rates. A flattening yield curve (short rates rise, long rates unchanged) would have little impact; a steepening (long rates rise) would hurt it significantly.

Portfolio positioning: A manager expecting curve flattening (short rates to rise relative to long rates) would tilt the portfolio toward bonds with low short-maturity key rate durations and high long-maturity key rate durations. Effective duration alone doesn't guide this decision — key rate durations do.

Portfolio Key Rate Duration — Exhibit 5 Worked Example

Portfolio key rate durations work the same way as portfolio effective duration — weighted averages using market value weights. And the key rate durations of individual bonds sum to each bond's effective duration; the portfolio's key rate durations sum to the portfolio's effective duration.

= [1.990 × (99,006/276,975)] + [4.938 × (93,960/276,975)] + [9.828 × (84,010/276,975)]

💡 Portfolio Tilting Using Key Rate Durations

If a portfolio manager's view is that the 5yr point of the yield curve will rise more than 2yr and 10yr rates (a butterfly hump in the middle), she would want to reduce the portfolio's 5yr key rate duration relative to the benchmark — by reducing the 5yr bond position and reallocating to 2yr and 10yr bonds. This preserves the same effective duration (parallel shift sensitivity) while repositioning the curve-shape exposure. Key rate durations are the tool that makes this kind of active management precise.

Empirical Duration vs. Analytical Duration — The Credit Spread Factor

Analytical Duration
  • Estimated using mathematical formulas
  • All measures covered so far: MacDur, ModDur, EffDur, KeyRateDur
  • Implicitly assumes benchmark yields and credit spreads are independent and uncorrelated
  • If benchmark yield changes → bond price changes only through that effect
  • Credit spread stays constant when benchmark yield changes
  • Works well for government bonds and when spreads are stable
Empirical Duration
  • Estimated using historical data in statistical models
  • Incorporates various factors affecting bond prices across economic scenarios
  • Accounts for the correlation between benchmark yields and credit spreads
  • Does NOT assume independence of yields and spreads
  • Can produce duration estimates very different from analytical when correlation is significant
  • Most relevant for credit-risky bonds during periods of market stress

The Critical Insight — Flight to Quality and Negative Correlation

★ During Market Crises (e.g., COVID-19 onset, Feb 2020)

Investors sell risky assets and buy government bonds ("flight to quality"). The result:

Government benchmark yields: Fall (bond prices rise as demand surges)

Credit spreads: Widen (risky bonds are sold → prices fall → spreads vs. government widen)

Key observation: Benchmark yields and credit spreads are negatively correlated in this scenario — when yields fall, spreads widen. For a corporate bond: the falling benchmark yield increases price (analytical effect), but the widening credit spread decreases price (partially or fully offsetting). Net result: the corporate bond price rises less than analytical duration would predict.

Empirical duration < Analytical duration for corporate bonds during crisis periods. The spread widening partially offsets the benchmark yield decline, so the bond is less sensitive to benchmark moves than the formula suggests.

Bond TypeBenchmark Yield ChangeCredit Spread ChangeNet Price EffectEmpirical vs. Analytical Duration
Government Bond (no credit risk) Falls (flight to quality) No spread (risk-free) Full price gain Empirical ≈ Analytical
Corporate Bond (credit risk) Falls (flight to quality) Widens (credit stress) Partial price gain (offset by spread) Empirical < Analytical
Corporate Bond (stable market) Rises/falls normally Stable (no correlation) Normal price change Empirical ≈ Analytical
⚠️ When to Use Empirical vs. Analytical Duration

The choice depends on the correlation between benchmark yields and credit spreads in the relevant scenario. For government bonds: analytical is fine (no credit spread). For investment-grade corporates in stable markets: analytical is a reasonable approximation (spread correlation is low). For high-yield or distressed bonds in volatile markets: empirical duration is more appropriate because the negative correlation between benchmark yields and spreads is significant — analytical duration substantially overstates true price sensitivity to benchmark yield changes. Risk managers at large firms often use empirical duration alongside analytical duration for credit portfolios.

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Why EffDur and EffCon are needed: Bonds with embedded options (callable, putable), MBS, ABS have uncertain cash flows — no well-defined YTM. Macaulay and modified duration are not appropriate. Effective duration (a curve duration) measures sensitivity to the benchmark yield curve and handles uncertain CFs via option pricing models.
  • ✅ Effective Duration formula (Eq. 1): EffDur = (PV− − PV+) / (2 × ΔCurve × PV₀). Same structure as ApproxModDur but uses ΔCurve in denominator and PV+/PV− from option pricing models.
  • ✅ Effective Convexity formula (Eq. 2): EffCon = [(PV−) + (PV+) − 2×PV₀] / ((ΔCurve)² × PV₀). Same structure as ApproxCon but with ΔCurve.
  • ✅ Callable bond price behavior (Exhibit 1): At HIGH yields: call option has low value → callable ≈ non-callable → similar EffDur, positive EffCon. At LOW yields: call option has HIGH value → issuer calls → callable price capped → EffDur LOWER than non-callable → EffCon can turn NEGATIVE. Investor bears call risk.
  • ✅ Callable bond: negative effective convexity. When yields are low and call is likely, the callable bond's price–yield curve bends downward (concave). This means price gains from further yield declines are increasingly limited. Negative EffCon is the hallmark of callable/MBS bonds in low-rate environments.
  • ✅ Putable bond price behavior (Exhibit 2): At LOW yields: put has low value → putable ≈ non-putable → similar EffDur, positive EffCon. At HIGH yields: put is valuable → investor puts at par → price floored → EffDur LOWER than non-putable. Putable bonds ALWAYS have positive EffCon (put floor prevents negative convexity).
  • ✅ Summary: callable = issuer's option, putable = investor's option. Option holder always benefits from exercise; option writer (the other party) bears the risk. Callable → investor sells the call → gets higher yield but loses upside. Putable → investor buys the put → accepts lower yield but gets downside protection.
  • ✅ Price change formula (Eq. 3): %ΔPV^Full ≈ (−EffDur × ΔCurve) + [½ × EffCon × (ΔCurve)²]. Identical structure to Eq. 1 of Module 12 — just substitute EffDur for ModDur and EffCon for AnnConvexity, and ΔCurve for ΔYield.
  • ✅ ModDur ≠ EffDur for option-free bonds (generally). Parallel par curve shift → non-parallel spot rate shift → different price change than a YTM change. Difference is smaller when: yield curve is flatter, maturity is shorter, bond is near par. Difference = 0 only for perfectly flat yield curve.
  • ✅ ΔCurve choice for complex bonds: Unlike ModDur where smaller ΔY improves accuracy, for callable bonds and MBS the option exercise behavior is complex — smaller ΔCurve doesn't necessarily improve EffDur/EffCon estimates. The choice of ΔCurve involves judgment.
  • ✅ Key rate duration (KeyRateDur_k): Sensitivity of bond price to a change in the benchmark yield at a SINGLE specific maturity k, with all other maturities unchanged. Also called "partial duration." Captures "shaping risk" — sensitivity to non-parallel curve changes.
  • ✅ Key rate duration sum property (Eq. 5): Σ KeyRateDur_k = EffDur. Individual key rate durations always sum to the bond's (or portfolio's) effective duration.
  • ✅ Portfolio key rate duration: KeyRateDur_k (portfolio) = Σ [w_i × KeyRateDur_k,i] where w_i = market value weight. The three-bond government portfolio: 2yr KRD=0.711, 5yr KRD=1.675, 10yr KRD=2.982, sum=5.368=EffDur ✓.
  • ✅ Key rate duration use case: If 2yr yield rises 25bps only, %ΔPV = −KRD₂ × 0.0025 = −1.990 × 0.0025 = −0.498%. Portfolio managers use KRDs to position for expected curve shape changes (steepening, flattening, butterfly) beyond just overall level shifts.
  • ✅ Analytical duration: All duration measures computed from mathematical formulas. Assumes benchmark yields and credit spreads are INDEPENDENT (uncorrelated).
  • ✅ Empirical duration: Computed from historical price/yield data using statistical models. Accounts for the CORRELATION between benchmark yields and credit spreads. Produces different (usually lower) estimates than analytical duration when yields and spreads are negatively correlated.
  • ✅ Flight to quality (e.g., COVID-19 Feb 2020): Investors sell risky assets → buy government bonds → government yields fall AND credit spreads widen. Benchmark yields and credit spreads are NEGATIVELY correlated. For corporate bonds: falling yields → price up (good), but widening spreads → price down (bad). Net effect is muted. Empirical duration < Analytical duration for corporate bonds.
  • ✅ When to use empirical duration: When spreads and benchmark yields are significantly negatively correlated — especially for credit-risky bonds (high-yield, emerging market) during volatile/crisis periods. Analytical is sufficient for government bonds and stable investment-grade in normal markets.

═══ EFFECTIVE DURATION & CONVEXITY ═══
EffDur = (PV− − PV+) / (2 × ΔCurve × PV₀) [Eq. 1]
EffCon = [(PV− + PV+) − 2×PV₀] / ((ΔCurve)² × PV₀) [Eq. 2]
%ΔPV^Full ≈ (−EffDur × ΔCurve) + [½ × EffCon × (ΔCurve)²] [Eq. 3]

KEY: PV+/PV− from option pricing model (not simple discounting)
KEY: ΔCurve = benchmark par curve shift (not bond's own YTM change)

═══ CALLABLE BOND ═══
High yields: EffDur ≈ non-callable; EffCon positive
Low yields: EffDur LOWER than non-callable; EffCon → NEGATIVE
Always priced BELOW non-callable (call option held by issuer)

═══ PUTABLE BOND ═══
Low yields: EffDur ≈ non-putable; EffCon positive
High yields: EffDur LOWER than non-putable; EffCon still POSITIVE (always)
Always priced ABOVE non-putable (put option held by investor)

═══ KEY RATE DURATION ═══
KeyRateDur_k = −(1/PV) × (ΔPV/Δr_k) [Eq. 4]
Σ KeyRateDur_k = EffDur [Eq. 5]
%ΔPV/PV = −KeyRateDur_k × Δr_k [Eq. 6]
Portfolio KRD_k = Σ (w_i × KRD_k,i)

═══ EMPIRICAL vs. ANALYTICAL ═══
Analytical: formula-based; assumes yields and spreads uncorrelated
Empirical: historical data; accounts for yield-spread correlation
Negative correlation (crisis): empirical < analytical for corporate bonds
Flight to quality: govt yields fall + credit spreads widen simultaneously

═══ MODDUR vs. EFFDUR ═══
Not equal for option-free bonds (except flat yield curve)
BRWA example: ModDur=4.587, EffDur=4.816
Gap smaller when: curve flatter, shorter maturity, bond near par
The key exam distinction between yield-based and curve-based measures:
Yield-based (ModDur, PVBP): sensitivity to bond's OWN yield. Assumes certain CFs. Not valid for bonds with options.
Curve-based (EffDur, KRD): sensitivity to BENCHMARK curve shift. Works with uncertain CFs. Valid for all bonds. Requires ΔCurve (not ΔYield) in denominator and option-model prices for PV+/PV−.

The key exam distinction between EffDur and KRD:
EffDur: ALL benchmark yields shift simultaneously (parallel). One number characterizes full sensitivity.
KRD: ONLY the k-th benchmark yield shifts. A vector of numbers characterizes shape sensitivity. Σ KRD = EffDur always.
