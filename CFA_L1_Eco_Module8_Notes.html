01

Cross-Rate Calculations

What Is a Cross-Rate?

A cross-rate is an exchange rate between two currencies that are NOT directly quoted in the market, derived by using a third (intermediate) currency. Given any two exchange rates involving three currencies, you can always back out the third cross-rate.

The key principle: the intermediate currency must cancel out. You set up the multiplication so that the currency you want to eliminate appears once in the numerator and once in the denominator.

Method 1 — Direct Multiplication (No Inversion Needed)

Use this when the two given quotes can be directly multiplied so that the intermediate currency cancels.

Cross-Rate: CAD/EUR (Euro–Canada)
CAD/USD × USD/EUR = CAD/EUR
USD cancels in the middle. Given: CAD/USD = 1.3020 and USD/EUR = 1.1701
Worked Example — CAD/EUR Cross Rate

Given: Dollar-Canada (CAD/USD) = 1.3020  |  Euro-Dollar (USD/EUR) = 1.1701

CAD/EUR = 1.3020 × 1.1701 = 1.5235

→ 1 Euro buys 1.5235 Canadian Dollars

Note how USD appears as numerator (in CAD/USD) and denominator (in USD/EUR) → USD cancels → left with CAD/EUR ✓

02

Cross-Rate with Inversion

When You Need to Invert

Sometimes the two available market quotes have the intermediate currency in the same position (both numerators or both denominators). You must invert one quote first so the intermediate currency appears on both top and bottom and can cancel.

To invert a rate A/B → B/A, simply take the reciprocal: 1 ÷ (A/B).

Cross-Rate: JPY/CAD (Canada–Yen) — Requires Inversion
Market quotes available: CAD/USD = 1.3020  &  JPY/USD = 111.94
Need: JPY/CAD
Invert CAD/USD → USD/CAD = 1/1.3020 = 0.7680
Then: USD/CAD × JPY/USD = JPY/CAD (USD cancels) ✓
Rule: You must invert until the intermediate currency appears once on each side so it cancels cleanly.
Worked Example — JPY/CAD Cross Rate (Canada–Yen)

Given: CAD/USD = 1.3020  |  JPY/USD = 111.94  |  Target: JPY/CAD

Step 1: Invert CAD/USD → USD/CAD = 1/1.3020 = 0.7680 Step 2: JPY/CAD = USD/CAD × JPY/USD = 0.7680 × 111.94

→ JPY/CAD = 85.97 (1 CAD buys 85.97 Japanese Yen)

JPY is quoted to 2 decimal places (not 4) because the value per unit is much larger. Forward points for JPY are scaled by 100 (not 10,000).

Step-by-Step Cross-Rate Method

1
Identify the target cross-rate you need (e.g., JPY/CAD)
2
Find two market quotes involving the same intermediate currency (e.g., CAD/USD and JPY/USD both involve USD)
3
Check if USD cancels directly. If USD appears in the same position in both quotes (e.g., denominator of both), invert one.
4
Multiply the two rates. The intermediate currency cancels, leaving the desired cross-rate.
5
Verify the answer makes sense — check decimal places (JPY: 2 places; most others: 4 places).
💡 Quick Check — Does the Math Work?

Write out the rate fractions like fractions in algebra. The intermediate currency should appear once in the numerator and once in the denominator so it cancels, leaving you with the ratio you want. If it doesn't cancel, invert one of the quotes. This is the same as unit conversion in physics or chemistry.

03

Triangular Arbitrage

The Concept

If the quoted cross-rate is inconsistent with the underlying exchange rate quotes, a riskless triangular arbitrage opportunity exists. The arbitrage involves three currencies (hence "triangular") and exploits the mispricing.

In practice, these discrepancies are extremely rare because both human traders and automatic trading algorithms continuously monitor for pricing inefficiencies.

Worked Example — Triangular Arbitrage (JPY/CAD)

Given: CAD/USD = 1.3020  |  JPY/USD = 111.94

Correctly calculated: JPY/CAD = 85.97

Misquoted dealer rate: JPY/CAD = 86.20

The dealer is quoting too many JPY per CAD — CAD is overpriced in the cross market.

Arbitrage: Buy CAD1 at the cheap rate of 85.97 JPY/CAD Immediately sell CAD1 at the misquoted rate of 86.20 JPY/CAD

→ Riskless profit = 86.20 − 85.97 = JPY 0.23 per CAD1

This arbitrage continues until the price discrepancy is eliminated by the market. In modern electronic markets, this happens nearly instantaneously.

⚠ Exam Point — Triangular Arbitrage Is Rare

The exam may ask you to identify whether an arbitrage opportunity exists by comparing the calculated cross-rate with a quoted cross-rate. If they differ, arbitrage exists. The profit = quoted rate − correct calculated rate (or vice versa), per unit of the base currency. In practice, electronic systems eliminate these instantly.

Covered Interest Rate Parity (CIP) & Forward Rate Formula

The Arbitrage Logic

Forward exchange rates are determined by an arbitrage relationship that equates the return on two equivalent risk-free investments over the same period:

  1. Path 1 (Domestic): Invest 1 unit of domestic currency at the domestic risk-free rate rd → get (1 + rd) at maturity
  2. Path 2 (Foreign, FX-hedged): Convert domestic to foreign at spot rate Sf/d → invest at foreign risk-free rate rf → convert back to domestic at the pre-contracted forward rate Ff/d (eliminating FX risk)

Both paths are risk-free. They must earn the same return — otherwise riskless arbitrage exists. This is called "swap financing."

Covered Interest Rate Parity (CIP) — Core Arbitrage Equation
(1 + rd) = Sf/d × (1 + rf) × (1/Ff/d)
f/d convention: Sf/d = spot (foreign currency per 1 domestic currency unit)
Ff/d = forward rate (same convention)
rd = domestic risk-free rate  |  rf = foreign risk-free rate

Alternatively, if quotes are in d/f form:
(1 + rd) = (1/Sd/f) × (1 + rf) × Fd/f
Forward Rate Formula (rearranged from CIP)
Ff/d = Sf/d × [(1 + rf) / (1 + rd)]
If rf > rd → Ff/d > Sf/d → foreign currency at FORWARD PREMIUM (worth more in forward market)
If rf < rd → Ff/d < Sf/d → foreign currency at FORWARD DISCOUNT

General rule (regardless of quoting convention): Currency with higher interest rate trades at a DISCOUNT in the forward market
Worked Example — 12-Month Forward Rate

Given: Spot Sf/d = 1.6535  |  rd = 3.50%  |  rf = 5.00%

Ff/d = 1.6535 × (1.0500/1.0350) = 1.6535 × 1.01449 = 1.6775

→ 12-month forward rate = 1.6775

Since rf (5%) > rd (3.5%), the foreign currency (f) trades at a forward PREMIUM — the forward rate (1.6775) is higher than the spot (1.6535). The foreign currency is "worth more" in the future market.

Detecting Arbitrage from a Misquoted Forward Rate

Worked Example — Misquoted Forward Rate Arbitrage

Given: Spot Sf/d = 1.6535  |  rd = 3.50%  |  rf = 5.00%  |  Misquoted Ff/d = 1.6900

Correct forward should be: 1.6775 (from above). Misquoted is 1.6900 → foreign currency overpriced in forward market.

Return on hedged foreign investment at misquoted rate: = Sf/d × (1 + rf) × (1/Ff/d) = 1.6535 × 1.05 × (1/1.6900) = 1.0273

Return on domestic: 1.0350 (3.50%). Return on hedged foreign: 1.0273 (2.73%).

Arbitrage profit: Borrow at foreign rate (5%), sell foreign at spot, hedge at misquoted forward, invest domestically at 3.5% → profit of ~77 bps per unit with no upfront capital.

🧠 Mnemonic — CIP "3-Step Swap Financing" (Right Side of Equation)

Step 1: Convert domestic → foreign at spot (×Sf/d)
Step 2: Invest at foreign risk-free rate (×(1+rf))
Step 3: Convert back domestic at forward rate (×1/Ff/d)
This equals the domestic investment (1+rd). Set equal, solve for any unknown.

Forward Rate as Expected Future Spot Rate (With Caution)

If forward rates are interpreted as expected future spot rates (Ft = S(t+1)), then the expected % change in the spot rate is approximately (rf − rd) / (1 + rd) ≈ rf − rd.

Counterintuitive result: Higher domestic rate (rd) → implies expected depreciation of domestic currency (in f/d convention). In practice, higher rates often attract capital and cause appreciation. This is the "forward rate bias" puzzle in FX markets.

Key warning: Forward rates are poor predictors of future spot rates — large margins of error. Do NOT interpret forward rates as the "market forecast." They are simply the product of the arbitrage equation (CIP).

05

Forward Points (Pips/Swap Points)

Definition & Scaling

Forward points (also called pips or swap points) = Forward rate − Spot rate, scaled to the last decimal place of the spot quote.

  • Most currencies quoted to 4 decimal places → multiply difference by 10,000 to get points
  • Japanese Yen quoted to 2 decimal places → multiply difference by 100 to get points
  • Forward points are quoted to one or more decimal places → forward rate has 5+ decimal places
  • Called "swap points" because FX swaps = simultaneous spot + forward transactions
Forward Points Calculation
Forward Points = (Forward Rate − Spot Rate) × 10,000 (for 4dp currencies)
Forward Points = (Forward Rate − Spot Rate) × 100 (for JPY)
Positive points → base currency at FORWARD PREMIUM (forward > spot)
Negative points → base currency at FORWARD DISCOUNT (forward < spot)
Worked Example — USD/EUR Forward Points

Given: Spot USD/EUR = 1.15885  |  1-year forward USD/EUR = 1.19532

Forward Points = (1.19532 − 1.15885) × 10,000 = 0.03647 × 10,000 = +364.7

→ One-year forward points = +364.7 (base currency EUR at forward PREMIUM)

Converting Forward Points → Forward Rate
Forward Rate = Spot Rate + (Forward Points / 10,000)   [for 4dp currencies]
Forward Rate = Spot Rate + (Forward Points / 100)   [for JPY]
Example: 3-month forward = 1.15885 + (80.9/10,000) = 1.15885 + 0.00809 = 1.16694

What Determines the Magnitude of Forward Points?

Forward points are proportional to three things:

  1. The spot exchange rate (Sf/d) — larger spot rates → more pips
  2. The interest rate differential (rf − rd) — wider spread → more pips
  3. The term to maturity (τ) — longer horizon → more pips (approximately proportional, not exactly)
Forward Points (Swap Points) Formula with Maturity
Ff/d − Sf/d = Sf/d × [(rf − rd) / (1 + rd × τ)] × τ
τ = fraction of year (e.g., 30/360 for 30-day forward using Libor day count actual/360)
Approximately: Forward Points ≈ Sf/d × (rf − rd) × τ   (simplified approximation)
Doubling τ → approximately doubles forward points. Doubling (rf − rd) → approximately doubles forward points.
Worked Example — 30-day and 180-day Forward Rates

Given: Sf/d = 1.6555  |  rd = 2.00%/year  |  rf = 3.00%/year  |  Day count: actual/360

30-day forward: Ff/d = 1.6555 × (1 + 0.03 × 30/360) / (1 + 0.02 × 30/360) = 1.6569 → Forward premium of 14 pips (1.6569 − 1.6555 = 0.0014 × 10,000 = 14) 180-day forward: swap points = 0.0082 → 82 pips (approx 6× the 30-day, not exactly)

Note: 180-day has 6× the maturity but only ~5.86× the pips — not exactly proportional due to compounding.

Forward Premium & Discount

Definitions

Forward Premium
  • Forward rate > Spot rate
  • Forward points are positive
  • Base currency is worth MORE in the future (forward market)
  • Occurs when foreign interest rate (rf) > domestic interest rate (rd) in f/d convention
Forward Discount
  • Forward rate < Spot rate
  • Forward points are negative
  • Base currency is worth LESS in the future (forward market)
  • Occurs when foreign interest rate (rf) < domestic interest rate (rd) in f/d convention
Forward Premium/Discount as % of Spot
% Forward Premium/Discount = (Forward Rate − Spot Rate) / Spot Rate
Equivalently: % Premium/Discount = (Forward Points / 10,000) / Spot Rate

Example: 3-month USD/EUR: (1.16694 − 1.15885) / 1.15885 = 0.00809 / 1.15885 = +0.698%
Converting spot to forward using %: 1.15885 × (1 + 0.00698) = 1.16694

The Critical Rule — Higher Rate = Forward Discount

Universal Rule (Regardless of Quoting Convention)
Currency with HIGHER interest rate → trades at FORWARD DISCOUNT
Currency with LOWER interest rate → trades at FORWARD PREMIUM
Intuition: The forward rate compensates for the interest rate differential. If you can earn more in foreign country, the FX market prices the forward so that your total return (FX-hedged) equals the domestic rate.

In f/d convention: rf > rd → Ff/d > Sf/d → foreign currency at PREMIUM, domestic at DISCOUNT
In d/f convention: rd > rf → Fd/f > Sd/f → domestic at PREMIUM, foreign at DISCOUNT
Both statements say: the high-rate currency is at a forward discount ← this is always true.
⚠ Exam Trap — "Premium" vs. "Discount" Direction

If the USD has a higher interest rate than the EUR → USD trades at a forward DISCOUNT vs. EUR. This seems counterintuitive (higher rate → currency "cheaper" in forward market) but is required for no-arbitrage. The forward discount exactly offsets the interest rate advantage, equating total returns. Remember: High rate = forward discount. Low rate = forward premium.

🧠 Mnemonic — "High-Rate Currency = Discounted Forward"

Think of it as the market "charging you" for the higher yield. If a currency offers higher interest, the forward market marks it down — otherwise everyone would borrow in the low-rate currency and invest in the high-rate currency at zero risk, creating unlimited arbitrage profits.

Forward Rates vs. Expected Future Spot Rates

Although it is tempting to interpret the forward rate as the market's expectation of the future spot rate, historical data shows forward rates are poor predictors of future spot rates. The margin of error is very large. FX markets are influenced by many factors — risk premia, market sentiment, capital flows, central bank policy — beyond just interest rate differentials. FX traders do not base trading strategies solely on forward rates.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Cross-rate: set up fractions so intermediate currency cancels. If needed, invert one quote (take reciprocal) first.
  • ✅ CAD/EUR = CAD/USD × USD/EUR (USD cancels directly). JPY/CAD requires inverting CAD/USD first.
  • ✅ JPY quoted to 2 decimal places; all others 4 decimal places. Scale forward points accordingly (÷100 for JPY; ÷10,000 for others).
  • ✅ Triangular arbitrage: if quoted cross-rate ≠ calculated cross-rate → arbitrage profit = |difference| per unit base currency. Rare in practice due to algorithmic monitoring.
  • ✅ CIP: (1+rd) = Sf/d × (1+rf) × (1/Ff/d) → rearranges to Ff/d = Sf/d × (1+rf)/(1+rd).
  • ✅ Forward rate with maturity: Ff/d = Sf/d × [(1+rf×τ)/(1+rd×τ)] where τ = days/360 (Libor actual/360 convention).
  • ✅ Forward points = (Forward − Spot) × 10,000 (or ×100 for JPY). Positive points = forward premium. Negative points = forward discount.
  • ✅ Forward premium → Forward rate > Spot rate → base currency more expensive in future.
  • ✅ Forward discount → Forward rate < Spot rate → base currency cheaper in future.
  • ✅ CRITICAL RULE: Currency with HIGHER interest rate trades at a FORWARD DISCOUNT. Currency with LOWER rate trades at FORWARD PREMIUM. True regardless of quoting convention.
  • ✅ Forward points are proportional to: (1) spot rate, (2) interest rate differential (rf−rd), and (3) maturity — approximately proportional to each (not exactly due to compounding).
  • ✅ Doubling the maturity → approximately doubles forward points. Doubling the interest rate differential → approximately doubles forward points.
  • ✅ Converting % premium to forward rate: Spot × (1 + %premium). Converting forward rate to %: (Forward/Spot) − 1.
  • ✅ Forward rates are poor predictors of future spot rates — do NOT treat them as the "market forecast." They are simply a product of the CIP arbitrage equation.
