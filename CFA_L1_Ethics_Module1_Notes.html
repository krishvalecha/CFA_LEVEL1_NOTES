What Is Ethics?

Ethics
A set of moral principles and rules of conduct that provide guidance for our behavior. From the Greek word "ethos" meaning character. The study of ethics examines the role of consequences and personal character in defining what is considered good, or ethical, conduct.
Ethical Conduct
Behavior that follows moral principles and balances self-interest with both the direct and indirect consequences of the behavior on others. Ethical actions are those perceived as beneficial and conforming to the ethical expectations of society.
Stakeholders
Individuals or groups who could be affected either directly or indirectly by a decision and thus have an interest, or stake, in the decision. In investment management: colleagues, clients, employers, communities, the profession, regulators, trade associations, and other financial market participants.

The ethical reasoning chain:

Beliefs → Values → Principles → Ethical conduct
  • Beliefs: assumptions or thoughts we hold to be true
  • Values: things we deem to have worth or merit (formed from beliefs)
  • Principles (ethical/moral): beliefs regarding what is good/acceptable vs. bad/unacceptable behavior
  • Ethical conduct: behavior aligned with principles, balancing self-interest vs. impact on others

Widely acknowledged ethical principles (know these for the exam):

  • Honesty — telling the truth about risks, costs, and recommendations
  • Transparency — open, clear disclosure; no hidden agendas
  • Fairness / Justice — treating all stakeholders equitably
  • Diligence — thorough, careful effort in carrying out responsibilities
  • Respect for the rights of others — acknowledging autonomy and dignity

Most societal groups share these fundamental principles and build on them, establishing shared rules for behavior within their communities.

💡 Why Trust Is Central to Investment Management

Businesses and financial markets thrive on trust — a strong belief in the reliability of a person or institution. A 2016 investor study found the top two attributes investors require from investment managers: (1) transparent and open business practices and (2) ethical business practices. Investment management is based almost entirely on trust — clients hand over their assets to professionals they cannot constantly monitor.

Key Definitions to Memorize

Term Definition
Trust A strong belief in the reliability of a person or institution. The foundation of financial markets.
Stakeholder Any individual or group that could be directly or indirectly affected by a decision — they have a "stake" in the outcome.
Ethics A set of moral principles and rules of conduct providing guidance for behavior. From Greek "ethos" (character).
Ethical Conduct Behavior following moral principles that balances self-interest with consequences for others.
Code of Ethics A written set of principles communicating a community's values and expected behavior of its members. Serves as a general guide.
Standards of Conduct Explicit rules that identify specific behaviors required of community members; benchmarks for minimally acceptable behavior. Expand on the code of ethics.
Profession An occupational community with specific education, expert knowledge, and a framework of practice and behavior that underpins community trust, respect, and recognition.
Fiduciary Duty The obligation to deliver a high standard of care when acting for the benefit of another party. Must always put client's interests ahead of own or employer's interests.
Behavioral Bias A tendency to behave in a way that is not strictly rational, often resulting from beliefs and emotions interfering with cognitive reasoning.
Situational Influences External factors (environmental or cultural elements) that shape our thinking, decision making, and behavior — often without our awareness.
Overconfidence Bias The tendency to overestimate the morality of our own behavior and our ability to make good ethical decisions — especially in unfamiliar situations.
Fiduciary Standard

Investment adviser must act in the client's best interest — always put client interests ahead of own or employer's. Must understand objectives, research multiple options, recommend the best overall option, and monitor ongoing suitability.

Suitability Standard

Investment professional must recommend investments suitable for the client. Legal to recommend a suitable investment even if a better, lower-fee alternative exists. Lower standard than fiduciary.

03

Code of Ethics — Role and Function

A code of ethics is a written set of principles communicating an organization's values and the expected behavior of its members. It serves as a general guide for how community members should act — not a detailed rulebook.

Standards of conduct expand on the code — they identify specific, explicit behaviors required and serve as benchmarks for minimally acceptable behavior.

What a Code of Ethics Does

  1. Communicates values — tells members and the public what the organization stands for
  2. Sets expectations — clarifies the behaviors required of members
  3. Fosters public confidence — assures clients that professionals will use specialized skills to serve them
  4. Provides decision guidance — a framework when no specific rule exists
  5. Creates accountability — violations can be investigated, damages repaired, and violators disciplined or expelled
💡 Code of Ethics vs. Standards of Conduct

Code of ethics = the general principles (the "what we stand for"). Standards of conduct = specific, explicit behavioral requirements (the "what exactly you must/must not do"). Standards are the operational tools that bring the code to life. Members can always choose behaviors that demonstrate even higher standards than the minimum benchmarks.

⚠ Violations of Code of Ethics — Consequences

Ethics violations can: (1) damage the community's external reputation (clients, public); (2) damage internal trust among community members; (3) cause the organization to fracture. The community can investigate, repair damage, discipline the violator, or in severe cases revoke membership. Even innocent members suffer career setbacks from colleagues' unethical behavior.

04

Professions — Definition & How They Establish Trust

Profession
An occupational community with specific education, expert knowledge, and a framework of practice and behavior that underpins community trust, respect, and recognition. Most professions emphasize an ethical approach, the importance of good service, and empathy with the client.

What distinguishes professions from trade bodies and craft guilds:

  • Professions require members to uphold high ethical standards — trade bodies generally do not
  • Professions have a mission to serve society — trade bodies do not
  • Professions set and enforce professional conduct rules — trade bodies generally do not

Driving forces behind the creation of new professions:

  • Governments and regulators — encourage ethical relationships between professionals and society
  • Individuals — see advantages in working as professionals (status, protection, career benefits)
  • Clients — desire to work with professionals who can be held accountable
💡 Why Professions Earn Trust — The Commercial Benefit

Professions have realized that earning community trust: (1) creates professional pride and acceptance, (2) delivers commercial benefits (more clients, better fees), and (3) can ultimately provide greater flexibility and independence from government regulators — allowing the profession to self-govern and develop its own service models. Trust is not just an ethical goal; it is also a business advantage.

05

Characteristics of Professions — How They Establish Trust

A primary goal of professions is to establish trust among clients and society in general. The following characteristics, when combined, greatly increase confidence and credibility in professionals.

1. Normalize Practitioner Behavior

Codes and standards developed by professional bodies; complementary to government regulations; often more detailed and sophisticated than regulatory high-level principles.

2. Provide a Service to Society

Beyond codes — professionals must advocate for higher educational and ethical standards. Professions widen access to services and support economic activity by building trust.

3. Client Focus

Integrity of profession and interests of clients placed above personal interests. At minimum: act in client's best interest with reasonable care, skill, and diligence. This is fiduciary duty.

4. High Entry Standards

Membership signals high-quality service beyond academic credentials. Curricula equip members with technical skills, knowledge, AND ethics.

5. Body of Expert Knowledge

Repository of knowledge from experienced practitioners made available to all members. Helps members work effectively and ethically based on best practice.

6. Continuing Education (CPD)

Entry doesn't guarantee ongoing competence. Continuing Professional Development (CPD) is typically required annually — specific time commitment by competency and learning type. Increases human capital value.

7. Monitor Professional Conduct

Members held accountable through self-regulation by professional bodies — monitoring and imposition of sanctions. Essential for maintaining integrity and reputation.

8. Collegiality

Professionals must be respectful to each other even when competing. Must respect the rights, dignity, and autonomy of others.

9. Recognized Oversight Bodies

Usually not-for-profit with mission of excellence, integrity, and public service. Monitors competence, provides educational resources, and imposes discipline framework.

10. Encourage Member Engagement

Volunteer participation is part of the essence of a profession. Members help develop values → more likely to adhere to them. Mentoring, volunteering, and peer education are expected.

💡 Memory Trick — 10 Profession Characteristics: "NSCHEMC-COE"

Normalize behavior · Society service · Client focus · High entry standards · Expert knowledge · Continuing education · Monitor conduct · Collegiality · Oversight bodies · Encourage engagement. These are all tested — particularly the distinction from trade bodies.

06

Professionalism in Investment Management

Investment management is a relatively young profession. The publication of Graham and Dodd's Security Analysis (1934) was an important early step in establishing a body of knowledge. Public understanding of its practice and codes is still developing.

The investment management profession meets most but not currently all expectations of a profession — it is "on a journey" to improve implementation.

What Makes It a Profession
  • Some certification or licensing required in most countries
  • Body of knowledge established (CFA Program, GBIK/CBOK)
  • Increasing global trend toward examined entry to practice
  • CFA Institute as the largest global professional body
  • Has become increasingly global — capital market opening, regulatory coordination, technology
Remaining Challenges
  • Not everyone engaged in investment management is a professional
  • Some practitioners have no specific investment training
  • No universal requirement to join a professional body in all countries
  • Regulatory recognition lags established professions (medicine, law)
  • Creates a trust challenge — not all practitioners bound by high ethical standards

CFA Institute — Role as Professional Body

CFA Institute mission: "To lead the investment profession globally, by promoting the highest standards of ethics, education, and professional excellence for the ultimate benefit of society."

  • Largest body for investment management professionals worldwide
  • Moved beyond North America in the 1980s as investment management globalized
  • Global Body of Investment Knowledge (GBIK) + Candidate Body of Knowledge (CBOK) updated via ongoing practice analysis with practitioners
  • Candidates and charterholders must meet the highest standard among those set by CFA Institute, regulators, or their employer
  • Where client interests and market interests conflict → duty to market integrity is the overriding obligation
  • Charterholders must sign an annual attestation of continued adherence to the Code and Standards
⚠ Key CFA Rule — Highest Standard Applies

CFA candidates and charterholders must meet the most stringent standard among CFA Institute, regulators, and their employer — NOT merely what their local employer or local law requires. If a local law permits something the CFA Standards forbid, the CFA Standard governs. This is tested repeatedly.

07

Challenges to Ethical Conduct

Even well-intentioned professionals face ethical failures. Understanding the reasons why people fail ethically is the first step to overcoming them.

Challenge 1 — Overconfidence in Own Morality

  • People tend to believe their ethical standards are higher than average — but everyone cannot be above average
  • This is the overconfidence bias — a common behavioral bias leading to faulty decision making
  • Results in overestimating the morality of your own behavior, particularly in unfamiliar situations
  • Leads to placing too much importance on internal traits ("I'm honest and wouldn't lie") — but studies show internal traits are generally NOT the main determinant of whether someone behaves ethically in a given situation
  • Can result in failure to consider important inputs and variables needed for the best ethical decision

Challenge 2 — Underestimating Situational Influences

  • Decision makers often fail to recognize or significantly underestimate how much external factors shape their behavior
  • Studies show even good people with honorable motives can and often will be influenced to do unethical things in difficult situations
  • Even strong, independent free thinkers will conform to social pressures in many situations
  • Bystander effect: people are less likely to intervene in an emergency when others are present
  • People tend to behave MORE ethically when they believe someone is watching or when a mirror is nearby

Challenge 3 — Short-Term vs. Long-Term Focus

  • Situational influences shift focus from long-term considerations (integrity, market integrity) to short/immediate term (bonus, promotion, prestige)
  • Our brains more easily identify short-term situational influences than long-term consequences
  • When decision making is too narrowly focused on short-term factors and/or self-interest → likelihood of ethical lapses increases
  • Long-term risks and consequences for both self and others are ignored or minimized
⚠ The "Compliance Check-Box" Trap

A well-intentioned strong compliance culture can itself become a situational influence that blinds employees to other considerations. Employees in a compliance-heavy firm may adopt a "check the box" mentality — asking "What can I do?" rather than "What should I do?". Compliance is a necessary starting point but NOT sufficient for ethical behavior. Ethics requires asking the harder question.

08

Situational Influences in the Investment Industry

Situational Influences
External factors, such as environmental or cultural elements, that shape our thinking, decision making, and behavior — often without our full awareness. The key insight: situational influences have a very powerful and often unrecognized effect on our behavior. Learning to recognize them is critical to making good decisions.
Situational Influence How It Creates Ethical Risk Real-World Investment Context
Money & Financial Rewards Simply mentioning money can reduce ethical behavior (e.g., "Wall Street Game" vs. "Community Game" experiment). Large rewards motivate self-interest over ethical conduct. Large bonuses, investment gains can drive analysts to recommend unsuitable products, misrepresent risks, or inflate valuations to benefit themselves or the firm.
Prestige & Status Desire to be seen as successful by peers, family, or supervisors can override ethical judgment. Issuing favorable research to attract investment banking fees; chasing "best analyst" rankings by making aggressive recommendations.
Loyalty (to employer/colleagues) An extremely powerful influence. Loyalty can tempt individuals to make compromises they would reject under different circumstances or judge harshly in others. Covering up a colleague's misconduct to protect the team; following the supervisor's instructions to violate standards out of loyalty; not reporting suspicious trades of a friend.
Social Conformity People conform to what those around them are doing — even strong independent thinkers are susceptible. An entire trading desk following what appears to be common practice — even if the practice violates regulations. "Everyone does it" mentality.
Compliance Culture (Check-Box) Overly rule-focused culture leads to "What can I do?" rather than "What should I do?" thinking. Rules are seen as the ceiling rather than the floor. A firm whose employees treat the Code and Standards as a checklist to satisfy rather than principles to genuinely internalize.
💡 Situational Influences Can Work Both Ways

Situational influences are not always negative — they can also induce MORE ethical behavior. People behave more ethically when they believe someone is watching or when there is a mirror nearby (increases self-awareness). Organizations can harness positive situational influences by creating cultures of accountability, visible ethical role models, and environments where ethical behavior is visibly rewarded.

Legal Standards vs. Ethical Standards

The Key Relationship
Legal ≠ Ethical (they frequently overlap but are not the same)

✅ Legal AND Ethical → Most common conduct (follow rules and do the right thing)
❌ Legal but UNETHICAL → Permitted by law but violates ethical principles
✅ Ethical but ILLEGAL → Civil disobedience, whistleblowing (varies by jurisdiction)
❌ Illegal AND Unethical → Both legally prohibited and morally wrong
CFA Standard: Ethical conduct GOES BEYOND what is legally required. Always ask "Is this ethical?" not just "Is this legal?"
When Legal = Ethical (Often)
  • Laws often codify widely shared ethical actions
  • Disclosure requirements, anti-fraud rules, fair dealing requirements
  • Complying with these creates better outcomes for clients, regulators, and the firm
  • Laws reflect moral beliefs of a society at a given time
When Legal ≠ Ethical (Important Cases)
  • Trading on material nonpublic information (MNPI): Some countries have no law against it — but CFA Institute and most investment professionals consider it unethical
  • Whistleblowing: May violate organizational policies or local laws yet be considered ethical by many
  • Civil disobedience: Peaceful protests against unjust laws may be illegal yet considered ethical
  • Suitability standard (legal) vs. fiduciary standard (more ethical)

Why Laws Alone Are Insufficient for Ethical Behavior

  1. Laws lag market practices — regulators react to crises; the problematic practice may continue for years before regulation catches up
  2. New laws can be vague, conflicting, or narrow — may eliminate one problematic activity while creating opportunities for similar ones
  3. Laws vary by jurisdiction — questionable practices migrate to places that lack relevant laws ("regulatory arbitrage")
  4. Interpretation and delay — market participants may interpret laws in the most advantageous way or delay compliance
  5. Laws cannot cover every situation — no definitive rule exists for many ethical dilemmas investment professionals face
💡 Ethics Requires Judgment — Not Just Rule Application

Ethical conduct requires the ability to make considered decisions and reach sensible conclusions even when no specific rule exists. There is often no simple algorithm. Good ethical judgment requires actively considering the interests of all stakeholders and trying to benefit multiple parties while minimizing risk — including reputational risk.

The Ethical Decision-Making Framework

An ethical decision-making framework helps professionals:

  • See a situation from multiple perspectives
  • Pay attention to aspects less evident with a short-term, self-focused view
  • Identify important issues they might otherwise miss
  • Explain and justify actions to a broader audience of stakeholders
  • Develop sound judgment and decision-making skills over time ("muscle memory")

The most important single factor in promoting ethical behavior: development, maintenance, and demonstration of a strong culture of integrity by senior management.

★ The 4-Phase Ethical Decision-Making Framework
Phase 1: IDENTIFY
Phase 2: CONSIDER
Phase 3: DECIDE AND ACT
Phase 4: REFLECT
The process is often ITERATIVE — you may move between phases in non-sequential order and repeat phases multiple times as new information emerges or consequences become apparent.
Phase 1 — Identify
  • Relevant facts available (and information you wish you had)
  • Distinguish facts from personal opinion, judgments, biases
  • Stakeholders and duties owed to each
  • Relevant ethical principles and/or legal requirements
  • Conflicts of interest — e.g., duty to client vs. duty to employer
Phase 2 — Consider
  • Situational influences and personal behavioral biases affecting thinking (bonus desire, desire for acceptance, peer pressure, loyalty)
  • Seek additional guidance from trusted sources — mentor, family member, compliance, outside legal counsel
  • Guidance seeker should be external to the firm or unaffected by same situational influences for fresh perspective
  • Consult firm's policies, CFA Institute Code and Standards
  • Evaluate alternative actions
  • Imagine how an ethical peer or role model would act
Phase 3 — Decide and Act
  • Make a decision based on thorough analysis from Phases 1 and 2
  • Act on the decision
  • Note: Using the framework is no guarantee of a positive outcome but helps avoid unethical decisions
Phase 4 — Reflect
  • Was the outcome what you anticipated? Why or why not?
  • Had you properly identified all facts, stakeholders, duties, conflicts, ethical principles?
  • Had you considered all situational influences and biases?
  • Had you sought sufficient guidance?
  • Had you properly evaluated all alternative actions?
  • Reflect multiple times as immediate and longer-term consequences become apparent
💡 The Framework Is Iterative, Not Linear

After Phase 1 (identify), you may realize you cannot decide without more information → seek guidance (Phase 2) → begin evaluating alternatives based on what new information might reveal → reflect → restart the process. In complex cases, multiple iterations may reveal that no totally acceptable solution exists — the framework still helps you make the best possible decision among imperfect options.

⚠ The Junior Analyst Case — Real-World Consequences

A junior analyst forwarded confidential IPO analysis to an outside party without approval during a restricted period — violating firm policy. Result: analyst was terminated and reported to regulators; supervisor lost his job for failure to supervise; firm fined millions of dollars; termination record posted permanently on the regulator's website visible to future employers. All of this from one email sent without seeking guidance. The framework's Phase 2 (seek guidance before acting) would have prevented all of it.
