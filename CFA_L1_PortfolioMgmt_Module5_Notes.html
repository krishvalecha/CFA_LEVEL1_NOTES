Cognitive Errors vs. Emotional Biases — Core Distinction

Traditional finance: individuals act rationally, considering all available information → leads to optimal outcomes → supports market efficiency.

Behavioral finance: individuals do NOT always act rationally — behavioral biases cause deviations from rational decision-making → suboptimal outcomes → can explain market anomalies.

Cognitive Errors

Source: faulty reasoning — statistical, information-processing, or memory errors

  • Result from faulty thinking or knowledge gaps
  • More susceptible to correction via better information, education, and advice
  • Two sub-types:
    • Belief perseverance biases (5): conservatism, confirmation, representativeness, illusion of control, hindsight
    • Processing error biases (4): anchoring, mental accounting, framing, availability
Emotional Biases

Source: impulse or intuition — feelings rather than conscious calculations

  • Arise spontaneously, not through conscious effort
  • May even be undesired by the person feeling them
  • Harder to correct — can typically only be adapted to (recognized and accommodated)
  • Six types: loss aversion, overconfidence, self-control, status quo, endowment, regret aversion
Cognitive Dissonance
The mental discomfort that occurs when new information conflicts with previously held beliefs or cognitions. To resolve this discomfort, people may ignore or modify conflicting information and seek only information that confirms existing beliefs. This is the psychological root of belief perseverance biases.
💡 Moderate vs. Adapt

Moderate a bias: recognize it and attempt to REDUCE or ELIMINATE it within the individual. Possible for cognitive errors (can be fixed with better information).
Adapt to a bias: recognize and ACCEPT the bias and ADJUST for it (rather than fixing it). Necessary for emotional biases (feelings are hard to change).

Cognitive errors → aim to moderate. Emotional biases → aim to adapt.

⚠️ Note — Overconfidence Is Both

Overconfidence has aspects of BOTH cognitive errors AND emotional biases, but is classified as emotional because the bias primarily results from emotion. A single bias can have elements of both — one type typically dominates.

02

Cognitive Errors — Belief Perseverance Biases (5)

Belief perseverance: tendency to cling to previously held beliefs by committing statistical, information-processing, or memory errors. Triggered by cognitive dissonance. Mnemonic: "CCRI H" — Conservatism, Confirmation, Representativeness, Illusion of control, Hindsight.

① Conservatism Bias
Definition
Maintaining prior views or forecasts by inadequately incorporating new, conflicting information. In Bayesian terms: overweights prior probability, underweights new information → beliefs underreact to new information.
Consequences
  • Slow or no updating of views even when presented with new information
  • Maintaining prior beliefs to avoid mental stress of processing complex/abstract data
Detection & Mitigation
Ask: "How does this new information change my forecast?" Use Bayes' Rule to systematically update beliefs. If information is difficult to interpret, seek expert guidance. Be especially alert with technical, abstract, or statistical information (higher cognitive cost to process).
② Confirmation Bias
Definition
Tendency to look for and notice what confirms prior beliefs while ignoring or undervaluing contradictory information. A predisposition to justify what we already want to believe.
Consequences
  • Consider only positive information about an existing investment; ignore negatives
  • Develop flawed screening criteria by ignoring contrary evidence
  • Under-diversify portfolios (convinced of one stock; ignore all negatives; build oversized position)
  • Hold disproportionate amount of employer's stock (believe in company; ignore unfavorable news)
Detection & Mitigation
Actively seek out information that CHALLENGES existing beliefs. Corroborate investment decisions from a different perspective or source (e.g., if convinced a stock is good, read the analyst with a sell rating).
③ Representativeness Bias
Definition
Classifying new information based on past experiences and classifications — judging how likely something is based on how well it matches an existing stereotype or pattern. Two sub-types:
  • Base-rate neglect: neglect general population rates (base rates) in favor of specific, individual information
  • Sample-size neglect: incorrectly assume small samples are representative of populations ("law of small numbers")
Consequences
  • Adopt a view based almost exclusively on individual/specific information or a small sample
  • Update beliefs using simple classifications instead of rigorous analysis
  • May lead to over-extrapolating recent past performance into expected future performance
Detection & Mitigation
Ask: "What is the probability that X belongs to Group A vs. Group B (which it's statistically more likely to belong to)?" Seek base-rate information; widen sample size of observations. Do more thorough research before classifying an investment.
④ Illusion of Control Bias
Definition
Tendency to believe one can control or influence outcomes when one cannot. People perceive more control than they actually have, infer causal connections where none exist, or display surprising certainty in predictions of chance events. Classic example: people prefer choosing their own lottery numbers.
Consequences
  • Inadequately diversify — prefer to invest in companies they believe they control (e.g., employer)
  • Trade more than is prudent — portfolio turnover negatively correlated with returns
  • Construct overly detailed models and forecasts, believing model complexity controls uncertainty
Detection & Mitigation
Recognize that investing is a PROBABILISTIC activity — even the largest firms have little control over outcomes. Actively seek contrary viewpoints. Ask: "What are the downside risks? What might go wrong?" Speak with someone who has an opposing view.
⑤ Hindsight Bias
Definition
Believing past events were predictable and reasonable to expect. Outcomes that occurred are more readily evident than outcomes that did not. People also remember their own predictions as more accurate than they were (biased by knowing what actually happened). Poorly reasoned decisions with positive results remembered as brilliant; poor results of well-reasoned decisions remembered as avoidable mistakes.
Consequences
  • Overestimate degree to which they correctly predicted an investment outcome
  • Unfairly assess portfolio manager performance by comparing against "what happened" rather than "what was expected at the time"
  • Closely related to overconfidence bias
Detection & Mitigation
Ask: "Am I re-writing history or being honest about mistakes I made?" Keep written records of investment decisions and key reasons at or around the time decisions are made. Consulting actual records (not memory) produces a far more accurate picture of past decisions.

Cognitive Errors — Processing Error Biases (4)

Processing errors: information is processed and used illogically or irrationally. Less related to memory or probability errors; more related to flaws in HOW information itself is processed. Mnemonic: "AMFA" — Anchoring, Mental accounting, Framing, Availability.

⑥ Anchoring and Adjustment Bias
Definition
Relying on an initial piece of information (the "anchor") to make subsequent estimates and adjustments. When required to estimate an unknown value, people start from an initial anchor and adjust up or down — but adjust insufficiently, producing biased estimates. Closely related to conservatism bias. Bayes' Rule again provides guidance for proper belief updating.
Classic Example
An analyst with last year's EPS of £1.00 as anchor only adjusts to £0.90 even when business conditions have severely deteriorated — the anchor is last year's EPS rather than expected future conditions.
Consequences
  • Stick too closely to original estimates when learning new information (underreact to changes)
  • Price anchors: hold a stock hoping to break even at purchase price rather than selling based on new information
  • Common anchors: purchase price, prior-year earnings, 52-week highs/lows
Detection & Mitigation
Ask: "Am I holding this stock to attain a price I'm anchored to (e.g., purchase price, high water mark)?" "Am I making this forecast based on past conditions or future expected conditions?" A company's revenues reflect the conditions of that period — not the prior period's conditions. Focus on fundamentals, not historical reference points.
⑦ Mental Accounting Bias
Definition
Mentally dividing money into separate "accounts" that influence decisions, even though money is fungible (one dollar is the same as any other dollar regardless of source). Investors mentally construct portfolios in layers, each addressing a specific financial goal — without considering interactions across layers.
Classic Example
Using windfall gains ("house money") to make higher-risk investments than would otherwise be appropriate — treating a gain differently because of its source, even though the money has the same value.
Consequences
  • Neglect opportunities to reduce risk by combining assets with low correlations across layers
  • Irrationally distinguish between income returns and capital appreciation returns
  • "Playing with house money" — believing greater risk is acceptable with returns than with principal
  • Suboptimal aggregate portfolio performance due to lack of holistic view
Detection & Mitigation
Combine ALL assets onto one spreadsheet WITHOUT account labels. View the holistic asset allocation. This often reveals surprising results (higher cash than expected, hidden concentration). Then create a strategy considering ALL assets together.
⑧ Framing Bias
Definition
Answering a question differently based on the way it is asked or framed. The same information presented with a gain context vs. a loss context leads to different decisions. Narrow framing: evaluating information based on a narrow frame of reference — losing sight of the big picture in favor of one or two specific points.
Classic Example
"One out of four start-ups succeed" (gain frame → positive outlook → invest in VC) vs. "Three out of four start-ups fail" (loss frame → negative outlook → don't invest). Same information; different decisions due to framing.
Consequences
  • Misidentify own risk tolerance based on how risk questions are framed:
    • Gain frame → more risk-averse
    • Loss frame → more risk-seeking
  • Focus on short-term price fluctuations; ignore long-run considerations
Detection & Mitigation
Ask: "Is this decision the result of focusing on a net gain or net loss position?" Try to eliminate any reference to gains and losses already incurred. Focus on the FUTURE prospects of an investment. Be neutral and open-minded when interpreting investment situations.
⑨ Availability Bias
Definition
Estimating the probability of an outcome or importance of a phenomenon based on how easily the information is recalled. Four sources applicable to FMPs:
  • Retrievability: ideas that come to mind first are judged as more likely/important
  • Categorization: difficulty categorizing a search set leads to biased estimates
  • Narrow range of experience: using a limited personal experience base (e.g., only considering tech stocks due to prior work experience)
  • Resonance: bias toward situations that closely parallel personal experience
Consequences
  • Limit investment opportunity set to familiar classifications (one country, one sector)
  • Choose investments based on advertising or media coverage quantity
  • Fail to diversify (narrow range of experience → overweight familiar industries)
  • Overweight importance of events receiving heavy media attention
Detection & Mitigation
Ask: "How did I decide which investments to consider? Did I choose based on familiarity, media coverage, or advertised products?" Develop a proper investment policy strategy. Research and analyze decisions carefully. Focus on long-term historical data. Consult colleagues/external resources to widen the search beyond familiar categories.
04

Emotional Biases (6)

Emotional biases: originate from impulse or intuition rather than conscious calculations. Harder to correct — typically can only be recognized and adapted to. Mnemonic: "LOSSER" — Loss aversion, Overconfidence, Self-control, Status quo, Endowment, Regret aversion.

① Loss-Aversion Bias
Definition
Tendency to strongly prefer avoiding losses to achieving equivalent gains. Losses feel more painful than equivalent gains feel pleasurable. Paradoxically: FMPs accept more risk to AVOID losses than to ACHIEVE gains (the opposite of rational behavior).
  • Utility function is S-shaped and asymmetric (Kahneman & Tversky 1979): steeper in the domain of losses than gains
  • Risk-SEEKING behavior below the reference point (domain of losses)
  • Risk-AVOIDING behavior above the reference point (domain of gains)
  • Disposition effect: hold losing investments too long; sell winning investments too quickly ("hold losers, sell winners")
Consequences
  • Hold investments in a LOSS position longer than justified (hope to break even)
  • Sell investments in a GAIN position sooner than justified (fear gains will erode)
  • Portfolio becomes riskier than optimal (holding losers adds risk; selling winners eliminates future gains)
Detection & Mitigation
Disciplined investment approach. Analyze investments and realistically consider probabilities of future gains and losses. It's impossible to make losses painless — but rational analysis can guide better decisions despite the emotion.
② Overconfidence Bias
Definition
Unwarranted faith in one's own abilities. Classified as EMOTIONAL (primarily from emotion) but also has cognitive aspects. Often intensified by self-attribution bias: take too much credit for successes (self-enhancing); assign failures to others or external factors (self-protecting). Two forms:
  • Prediction overconfidence: confidence intervals assigned to predictions are too NARROW (underestimate range of outcomes)
  • Certainty overconfidence: probabilities assigned to outcomes are too HIGH (overstate certainty of outcomes)
Consequences
  • Underestimate risks and overestimate expected returns
  • Hold poorly diversified portfolios → significant downside risk
  • Trade excessively (overconfident = active traders = lower returns, higher risk)
  • In bubbles: overtrading, rejection of contradictory information, failure to diversify
Detection & Mitigation
Review trading records — identify winners AND losers. Calculate portfolio performance over at least 2 years. Overconfident investors overcount winners and undercount losers. "Don't confuse brains with a bull market." Try to identify patterns in both profitable and unprofitable decisions. Distinguish between skill and luck.
③ Self-Control Bias
Definition
Failure to act in pursuit of long-term goals in favor of short-term satisfaction. Related to hyperbolic discounting: human tendency to prefer smaller payoffs now over larger payoffs in the future — present sacrifices require disproportionately large future rewards to be worthwhile.
Consequences
  • Save insufficiently for the future → may then accept TOO MUCH RISK in portfolios to try to generate required returns
  • Borrow excessively to finance present consumption
Detection & Mitigation
Ensure a proper investment plan is in place AND maintain a personal budget. Plans must be IN WRITING for regular review. Maintain a strategic asset allocation based on thorough evaluation.
④ Status Quo Bias
Definition
Choosing to do nothing ("maintain the status quo") instead of making a change, even when change is warranted. Driven by inertia rather than conscious choice. Distinguished from endowment and regret-aversion by the source: status quo = inertia; endowment/regret = conscious (but possibly incorrect) decisions.
Classic Example
Automatic 401(k) enrollment study: >65% of employees stuck with the default contribution rate (2-3%) and default investment option even when they had full freedom to change. Inertia, not conscious preference, kept them there.
Consequences
  • Unknowingly maintain portfolios with inappropriate risk characteristics
  • Fail to explore other (potentially superior) opportunities
Detection & Mitigation
Education is essential. Quantify the risk-reducing and return-enhancing advantages of diversification and proper asset allocation. Show concrete examples of what can happen if status quo is maintained (e.g., concentrated stock position collapse scenarios).
⑤ Endowment Bias
Definition
Valuing an asset MORE when you OWN it than when you do not. Inconsistent with standard theory: the price someone is willing to pay for a good should equal the price they'd accept to sell it. In reality: minimum selling price > maximum purchase price. Ownership "endows" the asset with added value. Can be caused by: loss aversion, anchoring, and overconfidence. Applies to purchased AND inherited securities.
Classic Test
Ask the FMP: "Would you BUY this security today at the current price?" If the answer is no (or "probably not"), but the FMP is also not willing to sell — endowment bias is present. Also: "Why are you not buying more of this security today?"
Consequences
  • Fail to sell certain assets and replace them with superior alternatives
  • Continue to hold familiar assets because familiarity adds perceived value
  • Maintain inappropriate asset allocation inconsistent with risk tolerance and goals
Detection & Mitigation
For inherited securities: "If you received an equivalent cash sum, how would you invest it?" (Often: very differently from the inherited portfolio). For purchased securities: ask the "buy today?" test. Turn focus from past cost to current and future value.
⑥ Regret-Aversion Bias
Definition
Avoiding making decisions out of fear that the decision will turn out poorly. "No action" becomes the default because regret is more intense when unfavorable outcomes result from an action TAKEN vs. an action NOT taken. Two dimensions: regret from actions taken and from actions not taken.
Consequences
  • Too conservative in investment choices after past losses → long-term underperformance
  • Herding behavior: follow crowd to limit potential future regret. Safer to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally (Keynes: "Worldly wisdom teaches that it is better for reputation to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally")
  • Prefer well-known company stocks over less-familiar ones (even with same risk/return) — choosing less-familiar involves more personal responsibility and potential for regret
Detection & Mitigation
Quantify risk-reducing and return-enhancing advantages of diversification. Recognize that losses happen to everyone — keep in mind long-term benefits of including risky assets. Recognize bubbles and keep long-term objectives in mind to prevent chasing popular investments.

💡 Three Biases That Lead to Under-Diversification

Multiple biases lead to under-diversification — a common exam question asks "which biases lead to this outcome?"
Confirmation: convinced one stock is great, ignore negatives → oversized position
Illusion of control: believe you control the company → concentrate in employer stock
Overconfidence: believe your ability is above-average → don't feel need to diversify
Availability: familiar with only one industry → overweight that industry
Endowment: love what you own → can't diversify away from inherited holdings

💡 "LOSSER" Emotional Biases — Key Portfolio Patterns

Loss aversion → disposition effect (hold losers, sell winners)
Overconfidence → overtrade, underdiversify
Self-control → undersave, then take too much risk
Status quo → inertia → wrong portfolio persists
Endowment → won't sell even inappropriate holdings
Regret aversion → herd behavior or excessive conservatism

⚠️ Status Quo vs. Endowment vs. Regret Aversion — Don't Confuse Them!

All three result in maintaining existing positions, but for different reasons:
Status quo: pure inertia — no conscious thought involved
Endowment: conscious belief that what you own is MORE VALUABLE than its market price
Regret aversion: conscious fear that making a change might turn out WORSE than the status quo
The OUTCOME looks similar; the MECHANISM differs. Exam tests the mechanism.

Behavioral Finance & Market Anomalies

Market Anomalies (from Behavioral Finance Perspective)
Apparent deviations from the EMH — persistent abnormal returns that differ from zero and are predictable in direction. Not every deviation is a true anomaly. Misclassifications stem from: (1) choice of asset pricing model, (2) statistical issues (small sample, survivorship bias, data mining), (3) temporary disequilibria that reverse once arbitraged.

Three reasons apparent anomalies may not be real:

  1. Model mismatch: "abnormal" return disappears when a better model is used (e.g., Fama-French 3-factor explains away many apparent anomalies)
  2. Statistical issues: small sample bias, survivorship bias, data mining (finding patterns by chance in historical data)
  3. Temporary disequilibria: anomaly exists briefly but is arbitraged away once publicized (e.g., January effect, weekend effect)

Momentum — Behavioral Explanation

  • Observation: future price behavior correlates with recent past (top performers continue outperforming for up to 2 years; then reversal in 2-5 year horizon)
  • London Business School study (2007): top 20% of stocks (prior 12 months) returned 18.3% annually vs. 6.8% for bottom 20% vs. 13.5% market average
  • Behavioral explanations:
    • Availability bias (recency effect): investors recall recent price rises more vividly → extrapolate trend to future
    • Hindsight bias: past moves seem predictable → investors feel they "missed out" → buy into trend (regret)
    • Loss aversion: fear of missing gains (FOMO) drives investors into trending stocks
  • Private investors tend to extrapolate trends (recency effect); professionals tend to expect mean reversion

Bubbles and Crashes — Behavioral Explanation

During Bubble Self-attribution, overconfidence Investors take personal credit for gains → faulty learning → reinforces overconfidence → more trading
Bubble Unwinding (Early) Anchoring, cognitive dissonance Investors don't sufficiently update beliefs as prices fall. Anchored to high prices. Ignore losses and rationalize flawed decisions.

💡 Career Risk and Bubble Participation

Rational investors may participate in bubbles KNOWINGLY — not just irrationally. Example: a fund manager who avoids the bubble may underperform peers → face redemptions → fund closure (even after 17 years of strong performance). Short-term performance accountability creates incentives to join bubbles even when you know they'll end badly. This is NOT necessarily irrational — it's a rational response to career incentives.

Value Effect — Behavioral Explanation

  • Observation: value stocks (low P/E, high book-to-market, high dividend yield) historically outperform growth stocks over long periods. Fama-French: value outperformed in 12 of 13 markets (1975–1995)
  • Risk-based explanation (Fama-French): value stocks carry more systematic risk → higher returns are compensation for risk, not mispricing. In the 3-factor model, the anomaly DISAPPEARS
  • Behavioral explanations:
    • Halo effect: favorable track record of growth stocks leads investors to expect high future returns → growth stocks become overvalued
    • Representativeness: extrapolate recent past performance → overvalue past winners (growth stocks)
    • Overconfidence: overpredicting growth rates → growth stocks overvalued relative to fundamentals
  • Home bias: related anomaly — portfolios exhibit strong bias toward domestic securities. Reasons: perceived informational advantage, comfort with proximity, psychological desire to invest locally
  • Emotional attraction: stocks of companies with well-known brands, personally used products, or nearby headquarters may be irrationally favored. Investors behave as if risk and return are negatively correlated (prefer "safe" popular stocks) — contrary to CAPM which assumes positive correlation
💡 Fortune 500 / Popular Stock Phenomenon

Companies rated "popular" (e.g., positive Fortune magazine ratings) subsequently showed LOW stock returns. A more positive emotional rating leads investors to perceive the company as LESS risky → they pay MORE for the stock → expected return falls. This inverts the CAPM relationship (risk and expected return become negatively correlated in practice for many investors).

Self-Control: short-term pleasure over long-term goals; hyperbolic discounting
Status Quo: inertia; maintain current state regardless of optimality
Endowment: value owned assets more than same assets not owned
Regret Aversion: avoid decisions → herd or be too conservative

═══ MARKET ANOMALIES — BEHAVIORAL EXPLANATIONS ═══
Momentum → Availability (recency), Hindsight, Loss aversion
Bubbles → Overconfidence, Confirmation, Regret aversion (formation)
→ Anchoring, Cognitive dissonance (unwinding)
→ Loss aversion (crash)
Value Anomaly → Halo effect, Representativeness, Overconfidence
Home Bias → Availability, Resonance, Informational comfort

═══ STATUS QUO vs. ENDOWMENT vs. REGRET AVERSION ═══
Status quo = INERTIA (no conscious choice to maintain)
Endowment = VALUE owned more than market price
Regret aversion = FEAR making a change will turn out worse
Correctable (moderate): Cognitive errors → better information, education, advice.
Adaptable (adapt to): Emotional biases → recognize, accept, adjust for.
Overconfidence: classified EMOTIONAL despite having cognitive aspects.
3 biases with similar outcome (maintain existing position): Status quo (inertia) + Endowment (overvalue owned) + Regret aversion (fear change).
Disposition effect: hold losers + sell winners → from LOSS AVERSION.
