01

What Financial Statement Analysis Is

Financial analysis is the process of interpreting and evaluating a company's performance and financial position in the context of its economic environment — for the purpose of making investment, credit, or other economic decisions.

It starts with financial reports (audited statements + required regulatory disclosures + unaudited management commentary), supplemented by industry and company research. The central focus: evaluating whether a company earns a return on capital at least equal to its cost of capital, can grow profitably, and generates enough cash to meet obligations.

Equity Analyst — What They Care About
  • Profitability — can the company grow earnings?
  • Per-share value — what is a fair price to buy?
  • Future earnings, free cash flow estimates
  • Buy / hold / sell recommendation
  • P/E ratio vs. peers; DCF-based fair value
Credit Analyst — What They Care About
  • Ability to pay interest (coverage ratios)
  • Ability to repay principal at maturity
  • Leverage: how much debt relative to assets/earnings?
  • Free cash flow forecasts + interest coverage
  • Credit rating assignment or loan terms
Examiner Mindset — Equity vs. Credit Analyst

The exam loves asking: "Which of the following would be of PRIMARY concern to a credit analyst vs. an equity analyst?" The instinct to build: credit analyst = can they pay their debts? (solvency, cash flow, leverage). Equity analyst = can they grow and create value? (profitability, EPS, free cash flow for growth). Both care about cash flow, but for different reasons — the credit analyst cares because it must cover debt service; the equity analyst cares because it supports dividends and buybacks. If the question mentions "extending a loan" or "assigning a credit rating" → think credit analyst. "Buy/sell recommendation" or "P/E comparison" → think equity analyst.

💡 The Central Question of FSA

All financial analysis ultimately asks: What is this company's past, current, and expected future performance and financial position — and what does that imply for the decision I need to make? The decision shapes which tools, ratios, and data sources matter. Never calculate ratios before knowing what question you're trying to answer.

02

The Financial Statement Analysis Framework — 6 Phases

The FSA framework is a structured process for conducting any financial analysis — whether for equity, credit, M&A, or private equity evaluation. The framework applies regardless of the specific task. Know all 6 phases, their sequence, their inputs (sources), and their outputs.

01
PURPOSE
Articulate the Purpose and Context of the Analysis

Define WHY you are doing the analysis before doing anything. Understand the decision it will support (equity investment? credit rating? M&A?). Define audience, deliverable format, timeline, and resource constraints. Compile the specific questions the analysis must answer.

Key warning from LES: Do NOT start calculating ratios without first understanding what question you are trying to answer. Less experienced analysts are tempted to compute everything — resist this. Ask: "If all calculations were instantly done, what decision would I be able to make?"

📥 Sources: Nature of analyst's function, client communication, institutional guidelines
📤 Outputs: Statement of purpose, list of specific questions, report format, timetable
02
COLLECT
Collect Data

Obtain information required to answer the specific questions. Key: understand the company's business model, financial performance, and financial position (trends over time, relative to peers). Financial statement data alone may suffice for simple screening; deeper questions (why did Company A outperform Company B?) require additional context.

Top-down approach: (1) Macroeconomic environment → (2) Industry prospects → (3) Company prospects. This sequencing helps frame expectations before diving into numbers.

📥 Sources: Financial statements, other financial data, questionnaires, industry/economic data, discussions with management/suppliers/customers/competitors, site visits
📤 Outputs: Financial statements in usable form (spreadsheet), completed questionnaires
03
PROCESS
Process Data

Apply analytical tools to the collected data. This is where the actual number-crunching happens — but always in service of the questions from Phase 1. Includes:

  • Computing ratios and growth rates
  • Preparing common-size financial statements (scale data as % of revenue or % of total assets)
  • Creating charts and running statistical analyses (regressions, Monte Carlo simulations)
  • Making forecasts and performing valuations
  • Adjusting financial statements to improve comparability (e.g., IFRS vs. US GAAP differences, lease adjustments)
  • Identifying factors that affect comparability: business models, operating decisions (leasing vs. buying), accounting policies (revenue recognition), tax jurisdictions
📥 Sources: Data from Phase 2
📤 Outputs: Adjusted statements, common-size statements, ratios and graphs, forecasts, valuations
04
ANALYZE
Analyze / Interpret the Data

The most critical phase. The answer to a specific analytical question is almost never a number alone — it requires the analyst's interpretation of the output and the use of that interpretation to support a conclusion or recommendation.

Equity analyst example: Forecasts of earnings + free cash flow + fair value range → buy/hold/sell recommendation. Credit analyst example: Free cash flow forecasts + interest coverage + leverage ratios → investment decision or credit rating.

📥 Sources: Input data + all processed data from Phase 3
📤 Outputs: Analytical results, forecasts, valuations
05
COMMUNICATE
Develop and Communicate Conclusions and Recommendations

Present findings in the appropriate format for the audience and purpose. For an equity analyst's external report, this typically includes: (1) Summary and investment conclusion; (2) Industry overview and competitive analysis; (3) Financial statement model (with scenarios); (4) Valuation; (5) Investment risks.

CFA Institute Standard V(B): Members must clearly distinguish between opinions and facts, disclose limitations and inherent risks, and include elements important to analysis so readers can evaluate the conclusions themselves.

📥 Sources: Analytical results + prior reports + institutional guidelines
📤 Outputs: Analytical report answering Phase 1 questions; buy/hold/sell or credit recommendation
06
FOLLOW-UP
Follow-Up

Analysis is not a one-time event. If an investment is made or a credit rating is assigned, periodic review is required to revise forecasts and recommendations as new information arrives. Even if an investment was rejected, subsequent analyses may be needed if price or business conditions change. Follow-up involves repeating all previous phases periodically.

📥 Sources: Periodically repeating all previous steps
📤 Outputs: Actual vs. expected comparison, revised forecasts, updated reports and recommendations
Examiner Mindset — The 6-Phase Framework

The exam tests this framework in two main ways: (1) Sequencing questions — "Which phase involves preparing common-size statements?" (Phase 3 — Process). "Which phase involves identifying specific questions to be answered?" (Phase 1 — Articulate). (2) What belongs in which phase — analysts must understand that interpretation (Phase 4) is separate from processing (Phase 3). Calculating a P/E ratio = Phase 3. Deciding it means the stock is overvalued = Phase 4. The most tested phases are Phase 1 (articulate — including the "resist calculating ratios without purpose" warning) and Phase 4 (interpretation is the critical step).

Common trap: The exam may describe an analyst doing something (e.g., "conducting site visits to production facilities") and ask which phase it belongs to. Site visits = data collection = Phase 2. Making adjustments for IFRS vs. GAAP differences = Phase 3 (Processing). A revised recommendation after a quarterly earnings release = Phase 6 (Follow-up).

03

Roles and Scope of Financial Statement Analysis

The role of FSA is to use financial reports — combined with other information — to evaluate the past, current, and potential performance and financial position of a company for the purpose of making investment, credit, and other economic decisions.

Key distinction: Managers inside a company also perform financial analysis for operating/investing/financing decisions, but they have access to non-public internal financial information — so they don't rely exclusively on published financial statements. External analysts only have public information.

Economic Decisions FSA Supports

Decision TypeWho Makes ItPrimary FSA Focus
Equity investment evaluation Portfolio managers, equity analysts Profitability, earnings growth, valuation (P/E, DCF)
Security valuation / recommendation Sell-side equity analysts Fair value estimate vs. current price → buy/hold/sell
Creditworthiness assessment / loan terms Credit analysts, bank loan officers Interest coverage, leverage, cash flow adequacy, collateral
Debt rating assignment Rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) Solvency, leverage ratios, EBITDA, free cash flow, business risk
Venture capital / private equity investment VC and PE analysts Business model viability, growth potential, exit multiples
M&A target evaluation Corporate development, investment bankers Synergies, combined financials, deal valuation
💡 Two Recurring Themes in FSA

Theme 1 — Past to forecast future: Analysts study past and current performance to form expectations about future performance and financial position. Financial statements are a historical record; FSA extrapolates forward.

Theme 2 — Risk assessment: Analysts are always concerned about factors that affect the risks to a company's future performance — not just the base case. Both profitability (earnings) and cash flow generation (receipts vs. disbursements) matter, but for different reasons depending on whether you're an equity or credit analyst.

Examiner Mindset — The Sea Limited Example (from LES)

The LES uses Sea Limited's Q2 2022 earnings as a real-world illustration. The key analyst insight: beating on EPS but missing on revenue AND suspending guidance = mixed result. The stock dropped 14.3%. This illustrates the analyst's role: it's not just reading numbers — it's interpreting revenue miss + guidance suspension in the context of macro uncertainty (rising inflation, rising rates, currency depreciation) to form a view on future performance. The exam may test whether you understand that analysts must read management commentary critically (management is biased to their own perspective) and that revenue guidance suspension signals uncertainty, not just caution.

04

Regulatory Bodies — IOSCO, SEC, and EU

IOSCO — The Global Framework

IOSCO (International Organization of Securities Commissions) — founded 1983. Technically NOT a regulatory authority itself, but its members (securities commissions and similar governmental regulatory authorities) regulate over 95% of the world's financial capital markets across more than 115 jurisdictions. 75% of its ordinary membership = securities regulators from emerging markets.

IOSCO's role: establish a global benchmark for securities regulation through its Objectives and Principles of Securities Regulation, facilitate cross-border cooperation, and assist in combating violations of securities and derivatives laws.

IOSCO's 3 Core Objectives of Securities Regulation
① Protecting investors
② Ensuring markets are fair, efficient, and transparent
③ Reducing systemic risk

Two IOSCO principles directly relevant to financial reporting:
→ Full, accurate, and timely disclosure of financial results, risk, and material information
→ Accounting standards used should be of high and internationally acceptable quality

US SEC — The Most Important Regulatory Authority to Know

The US Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has primary responsibility for securities and capital markets regulation in the United States. Created after the 1929 stock market crash that preceded the Great Depression. An ordinary IOSCO member. Any company issuing securities in the US (NYSE, NASDAQ) or involved in US capital markets is subject to SEC rules.

StatuteYearKey Provisions
Securities Act of 1933 1933 Specifies financial and significant information investors must receive when securities are sold. Prohibits misrepresentations. Requires initial registration of all public issuances of securities.
Securities Exchange Act of 1934 1934 Created the SEC. Gave SEC authority over all aspects of the securities industry. Empowered SEC to require periodic reporting by companies with publicly traded securities.
Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) 2002 Created PCAOB (Public Company Accounting Oversight Board) to oversee auditors. Required: auditor independence (auditors cannot provide certain non-audit services); executive certification of financial reports; management report on effectiveness of internal controls (with external auditor confirmation).
Examiner Mindset — SOX and the PCAOB

Sarbanes-Oxley (2002) is frequently tested because it introduced three major requirements: (1) Auditor independence — auditors cannot provide certain non-audit services to companies they audit (eliminates conflicts of interest). (2) CEO/CFO certification — executive management must personally certify that financial reports fairly represent the company's financial condition (personal liability). (3) Internal control reporting — management must report on the effectiveness of internal controls over financial reporting AND obtain external auditor confirmation of that effectiveness. The exam often asks: "What body oversees auditors of public companies in the US?" → Answer: PCAOB (created by SOX). "Who certifies that financial reports fairly present the company's condition?" → Executive management.

European Regulatory Structure

ESC — European Securities Committee

Consists of high-level representatives of EU member states. Advises the European Commission on securities policy issues. No direct regulatory authority — advisory role only.

ESMA — European Securities and Markets Authority

EU cross-border supervisor. Coordinates supervision of the EU market. One of three European Supervisory Authorities (the other two: banking and insurance). Regulation still rests with individual member states → reporting requirements vary by country.

💡 EU Financial Reporting Standard Adoption

From 2005, consolidated accounts of EU-listed companies must use IFRS. The endorsement process: IASB issues new standard → EFRAG (European Financial Reporting Advisory Group) advises European Commission → Standards Advice Review Group provides opinion → Commission prepares draft regulation → Accounting Regulatory Committee votes → if favorable → European Parliament and Council of EU approval. It's a multi-step process reflecting the balance between individual member state autonomy and EU-wide convergence.

Examiner Mindset — Which Form for Which Situation?

The exam frequently presents a scenario and asks which SEC form would contain the relevant information. Key distinctions to memorize: 10-K vs. Annual Report: 10-K is the SEC legal filing (required); Annual Report is the shareholder marketing document (not SEC-required). Both overlap, but only 10-K is mandatory. 10-Q vs. 8-K: 10-Q is periodic (quarterly); 8-K is event-driven (material events). A surprise acquisition announcement → 8-K. Quarterly financials → 10-Q. Forms 3/4/5: These track insider ownership changes. An analyst noticing heavy insider selling would check Form 4 (changes). Proxy (DEF-14A): Contains executive compensation — tested frequently. "Which filing would disclose the CEO's total compensation?" → Proxy statement (DEF-14A).

⚠️ Annual Report ≠ 10-K — Common Confusion

The annual report to shareholders is a voluntary, marketing-oriented document — it is NOT required by the SEC. The 10-K is the required legal filing. They overlap significantly, but the annual report may omit some of the legal detail in the 10-K, and it typically includes more promotional content (CEO letter, photos, narrative about strategy). Some companies publish only a Form 10-K or an abbreviated annual report + a copy of the 10-K. Never assume the annual report and the 10-K are the same document — the 10-K is always the authoritative source.

Financial Notes, Supplementary Schedules & Segment Reporting

Financial Notes — Why They Matter

Notes (footnotes) are required and often account for a large percentage of financial disclosures — Sea Ltd.'s 2021 financial statements included more than 60 pages of notes. They are essential to understanding the information in the financial statements themselves. An analyst who reads only the financial statements and ignores the notes is missing critical information.

What Financial Notes Disclose
① Basis of preparation: fiscal year, accounting standards used (IFRS or US GAAP),
currency/units, consolidation basis, rounding policy

② Accounting policies, methods, and estimates used to prepare the statements
(IFRS and US GAAP allow flexibility in policies → reduces comparability across companies)

③ Segment reporting (breakdown by business segment and geography)
④ Business acquisitions and disposals
⑤ Contractual obligations (on-balance-sheet AND off-balance-sheet debt)
⑥ Financial instruments and associated risks
⑦ Legal proceedings
⑧ Related-party transactions
⑨ Subsequent events (events occurring AFTER the balance sheet date)
The notes may include explanatory information about virtually every line item on the balance sheet and income statement. Flexibility in accounting choices is necessary (companies differ in their economic environments) but creates comparability challenges — analysts must understand policies to make appropriate adjustments when comparing companies.
Examiner Mindset — Notes and Accounting Flexibility

The exam tests whether you understand WHY accounting flexibility exists and what problems it creates. Why flexibility exists: different companies have different economic environments; a single set of rigid rules can't fairly reflect all businesses. The problem it creates: two companies with similar assets (e.g., equipment) can use different depreciation methods → different reported expenses → incomparable profitability. This is why analysts must read notes to understand accounting policies before comparing companies. The exam may ask: "An analyst finds that Company A uses straight-line depreciation and Company B uses accelerated depreciation. Where would the analyst find this information?" → Answer: Financial statement notes (footnotes).

Segment Reporting — The Rules

Both IFRS and US GAAP require companies to provide disaggregated information about operating segments in the notes. An operating segment is a component of a company that: (1) engages in activities generating revenue and expenses; (2) whose results are regularly reviewed by senior management; and (3) for which discrete financial information is available.

Segment Reporting — Quantitative Thresholds
A segment must be separately reported if it meets ANY of these (the "10% rule"):
→ Revenue ≥ 10% of combined segments' total revenue
→ Assets ≥ 10% of combined segments' total assets
→ Profit or loss ≥ 10% of the GREATER of:
(a) combined profits of all profitable segments, OR
(b) absolute combined losses of all loss-making segments

ADDITIONAL RULE — 75% test:
→ If reportable segments' combined external revenue < 75% of total company revenue,
additional segments must be identified until 75% is reached.

Small segments may be combined if they share substantial defining factors, or combined
with a similar significant segment. Non-reportable info → "All Other Segments" category.
For each reportable segment, disclose: external revenue + inter-segment revenue, profit/loss, assets and liabilities (if reviewed by chief decision-maker), interest revenue/expense, PP&E/intangibles acquired, D&A, other non-cash expenses, income tax, equity method investee share. Plus a reconciliation to consolidated financials.
💡 Why Segment Data Is Valuable — Sea Limited Example

Sea Ltd.'s 2021 segment data showed: E-commerce = 51% of revenue but generated a large operating loss. Digital Entertainment (Garena) = ~43% of revenue and was the ONLY profitable segment. Without segment data, an analyst looking only at consolidated results would see a $9.96bn revenue company — but would miss that the largest revenue segment is deeply unprofitable and the profitable segment is actually shrinking in relative importance. Segment data is essential for identifying which parts of a business drive value and which destroy it.

Examiner Mindset — Segment Reporting Rules

Two common exam traps: (1) The 10% threshold is OR, not AND — a segment only needs to exceed one of the three thresholds (revenue, assets, or profit/loss) to require separate reporting. (2) Single customer disclosure: If any single customer represents 10% or more of total revenues, the company must disclose THAT FACT but does NOT have to disclose the customer's identity. The exam may ask: "A company has a customer representing 12% of revenues. What must the company disclose?" → That a single customer exceeds 10% of revenue, but not the customer's name.

Management Commentary (MD&A) & Audit Reports

Management's Discussion and Analysis (MD&A)

The MD&A (also called management report, management commentary, or operating and financial review) is one of the most useful parts of an annual report besides the financial statements themselves. However — other than excerpts from financial statements — the MD&A is typically UNAUDITED. (Exception: Germany has required audited management reports since 1931.)

IASB Framework — 5 Content Elements of "Decision-Useful" MD&A
① The nature of the business
② Management's objectives and strategies
③ The company's significant resources, risks, and relationships
④ Results of operations
⑤ Critical performance measures
The IASB issued an IFRS Practice Statement "Management Commentary" — provides guidance (not a binding standard) for preparing and presenting management commentary.
SEC Requirements for MD&A (US-Listed Companies)
Management must highlight:
→ Favorable or unfavorable trends
→ Significant events and uncertainties affecting liquidity, capital resources, and operations
→ Effects of inflation, changing prices, or other material events that may cause future results
to materially depart from current reported financial information
→ Off-balance-sheet obligations
→ Contractual commitments (e.g., purchase obligations)
→ Critical accounting policies requiring subjective judgments with significant impact on results
The MD&A is a good starting point — especially its forward-looking disclosures (planned capex, new store openings, divestitures) for projecting future performance. But: management is biased. MD&A is only ONE input for an objective, independent analysis.
Examiner Mindset — MD&A: Useful but Biased

The exam knows that MD&A is simultaneously valuable and unreliable. The right answer to questions about MD&A: it is useful as a starting point (especially forward-looking disclosures), but: (1) it is generally unaudited (except Germany); (2) management is naturally biased toward presenting favorable information; (3) it is only ONE input — analysts must seek an objective, independent perspective. If the exam asks "Which section of the annual report contains unaudited forward-looking information about planned capital expenditures?" → MD&A. If it asks "Which limitation must analysts be aware of when using the MD&A?" → It is unaudited and management is biased to their own perspective.

Audit Reports — Types of Opinions

Financial statements in annual reports are generally required to be audited by an independent accounting firm in accordance with specified auditing standards. The auditor issues a written opinion — the audit report — on the financial statements.

What the audit provides: "Reasonable assurance" (not absolute assurance) that the financial statements are fairly presented and free from material misstatement. Auditors use sampling techniques; line items may be based on estimates. "Reasonable assurance" = high probability of no material error — NOT a guarantee. Analysts must maintain healthy skepticism.

Auditing Standards Bodies
International: IAASB (International Auditing and Assurance Standards Board) — issues ISAs
(International Standards on Auditing) — adopted by many countries

United States: PCAOB (Public Company Accounting Oversight Board) — created by SOX 2002
Sets auditing standards for US public companies

Audit objectives (under ISAs):
→ Obtain reasonable assurance that financials are free from material misstatement
(whether due to fraud or error)
→ Report findings in accordance with ISAs
Audit Opinion TypeWhat It MeansAnalyst Implication
Unqualified / Unmodified ("Clean") Opinion Financial statements give a "true and fair view" (international) or are "fairly presented" (international + US) in accordance with applicable accounting standards. No material exceptions. Desired outcome — analysts want to see this. Sea Ltd. received a clean opinion from Ernst & Young for FY2021.
Qualified Opinion Some scope limitation OR exception to accounting standards. Exceptions are described in the audit report with additional explanatory paragraphs. Caution — analyst must read the explanatory paragraphs to determine the importance and impact of the exception.
Adverse Opinion Auditor determines that the financial statements materially depart from accounting standards and are NOT fairly presented. Red flag — financial statements cannot be relied upon as presented. Serious issue requiring investigation.
Disclaimer of Opinion Auditor is UNABLE to issue an opinion (e.g., due to a scope limitation — insufficient access to evidence). Major concern — something prevented the auditor from completing the work. Often signals serious governance or information access issues.

Key Audit Matters (KAM) vs. Critical Audit Matters (CAM)

Key Audit Matters (KAM) — International

Issues the auditor considers most important — higher risk of misstatement, significant management judgment, or significant transactions. Sea Ltd. example KAMs: (1) DE revenue recognition (complex judgment about paying user lifespans); (2) Long-lived asset impairment in e-commerce (highly judgmental due to forecasted cash flows).

Critical Audit Matters (CAM) — US

Issues involving "especially challenging, subjective, or complex auditor judgment" — similar concept to KAM, applied under PCAOB standards. Both KAMs and CAMs highlight areas where management must make significant estimates or judgments — useful flags for analysts about where reported numbers are most uncertain.

⚠️ KAMs/CAMs ≠ Most Important for Investors

The LES specifically notes: "Key and Critical Audit Matters are not necessarily the most important factors for analysts and investors." They reflect what was most challenging for the auditor — not what is most decision-relevant for investment analysis. An analyst should not assume that just because something is a KAM/CAM it is the most important driver of the company's value. This is an exam trap — don't confuse "most difficult to audit" with "most important for investment decision."

Examiner Mindset — Audit Opinion Types

The exam loves testing audit opinion terminology. Key instincts: "Clean" = unqualified = unmodified = good. "Qualified" = exception exists, but not so severe as to make statements unreliable — read the explanatory paragraph. "Adverse" = statements materially wrong — don't trust them. "Disclaimer" = auditor couldn't form an opinion — often worse than adverse because you don't even know how bad it is. Common trap: "Which opinion means the financial statements are fairly presented WITH exceptions?" → Qualified (NOT adverse — adverse means NOT fairly presented at all). The exam may also test that the audit provides "reasonable assurance" not "absolute assurance" — auditing uses sampling, so it cannot guarantee zero errors.

💡 SOX Internal Control Requirements

Under SOX, US auditors must also express an opinion on the company's internal control systems (provided separately or incorporated in the audit report). Internal controls = the company's processes, personnel, and systems designed to ensure the financial reporting process is sound. Under SOX, management of US publicly traded companies must: (1) explicitly accept responsibility for effectiveness of internal control; (2) evaluate effectiveness using suitable criteria; (3) support evaluation with sufficient competent evidence; (4) provide a report on internal control. The external auditor must confirm management's assessment.

IFRS vs. US GAAP — Comparison & Monitoring Standards

IFRS — International Financial Reporting Standards
  • Set by: IASB (International Accounting Standards Board)
  • Approach: Principles-based — gives broader guidance, requires more professional judgment
  • Used by: Most countries outside the US (EU, most of Asia, Latin America, Australia, etc.)
  • Interest paid on cash flow statement: Can be classified as Operating OR Financing
  • Inventory methods: FIFO and Weighted Average only. LIFO is PROHIBITED
  • Development costs: Capitalized (if certain conditions met) → appears on balance sheet as asset
  • Inventory writedown reversal: Permitted (if conditions met) — reversed through income statement
US GAAP — Generally Accepted Accounting Principles
  • Set by: FASB (Financial Accounting Standards Board)
  • Approach: Rules-based — more specific rules for specific situations, less judgment required
  • Used by: United States companies; companies with US capital market access
  • Interest paid on cash flow statement: Always Operating Activities
  • Inventory methods: FIFO, LIFO, and Weighted Average. LIFO is PERMITTED
  • Development costs: Expensed immediately (treated as period expense, not capitalized)
  • Inventory writedown reversal: PROHIBITED — once written down, cannot be reversed

Examiner Mindset — IFRS vs. US GAAP Key Differences

The four most tested IFRS vs. US GAAP differences are: (1) LIFO: Permitted under US GAAP, PROHIBITED under IFRS. In inflationary environments, LIFO → higher COGS → lower taxable income → lower taxes. IFRS companies cannot use this. (2) Development costs: IFRS capitalizes (if criteria met) → higher assets, higher future amortization, smoother earnings. US GAAP expenses immediately → lower assets, higher current expenses. (3) Inventory writedown reversal: IFRS allows reversal (price recovers → asset marked back up → gain in income statement). US GAAP prohibits reversal — once written down, permanently stays at lower value. (4) Interest paid on cash flow: IFRS gives a choice (operating or financing); US GAAP requires operating. This affects operating cash flow comparability between IFRS and US GAAP companies.

Convergence note: IASB and FASB work to converge standards. Revenue recognition, leasing, and credit losses have largely converged. But differences remain (LIFO, development costs, writedown reversals) — analysts must know where convergence has NOT occurred.

💡 No Reconciliation Required — The Analyst's Challenge

Crucially: reconciliation disclosures between IFRS and US GAAP are NOT required. An analyst comparing two companies using different standards often does NOT have enough information to make the specific adjustments needed for full comparability. The implication: analysts must maintain caution when interpreting comparative financial measures across different accounting standard regimes. This is why monitoring developments in financial reporting standards matters — if FASB or IASB changes a rule, it affects comparability and can change valuations.

Monitoring Financial Reporting Developments

Analysts need to monitor developments from a user's perspective (how do changes affect financial reports and valuations?) — not from a preparer's perspective (how do I implement the change?). Three key areas to monitor:

  • New products or types of transactions — may lack explicit guidance (e.g., fintech products, cryptocurrencies). May appear in financial reports, business journals, or capital markets before standard-setters act.
  • Actions of standard setters (IASB, FASB) — changes in regulation can affect companies' financial reports and valuations. Example: requiring stock option expense on the income statement (previously only in footnotes) made dilution more visible and affected valuations.
  • Company disclosures on critical accounting policies — companies disclose where they exercise judgment. Changes in estimates or policies signal where future surprises may occur.
💡 CFA Institute's Role in Financial Reporting

CFA Institute actively advocates for improved financial reporting standards. Volunteer members serve on liaison committees meeting with IASB and FASB. CFA Institute's 2007 position paper "A Comprehensive Business Reporting Model" recommended: current fair value of assets and liabilities, neutrality in financial reporting, and the direct format for the cash flow statement. Analysts can contribute by submitting comment letters on proposed standards — IASB and FASB seek input from the financial analyst community (users of financial statements) when developing new standards.

Other Information Sources for Financial Analysis

Beyond annual and interim regulatory filings, analysts use four categories of additional sources. The quality and objectivity of information varies significantly across categories.

CategoryExamplesKey Characteristics
Issuer sources (non-regulatory) Earnings calls, investor days, press releases, speaking with management/IR/other personnel, company website visits, product/store visits Direct from the source → rich context. BUT: management is biased to their own perspective. Analysts must ask probing questions. Earnings calls: analysts get to probe management on results, strategy, and forward-looking targets. Platforms like Bloomberg, Wind, FactSet transcribe calls.
Public third-party sources Free industry whitepapers, consultancy reports, government economic/industry indicators (retail sales, price indexes), general news outlets, industry-specific news, social media Freely available, broad market view. Social media useful as a gauge of customer sentiment. Government statistics (monthly/quarterly releases) provide macro context. Least proprietary advantage from these sources.
Proprietary third-party sources Sell-side analyst reports, credit rating agency reports, Bloomberg/Wind/FactSet data, industry-specific consultancies (Rystad for energy, iQvia/Evaluate for biopharma, Gartner/IDC for IT) High quality, specialized data. Costs money. Sell-side reports have inherent conflicts of interest (banking relationships). Rating agency reports focus on creditworthiness. Industry-specific databases provide proprietary market data not in public filings.
Proprietary primary research Surveys, conversations, product comparisons, other studies commissioned or directly conducted by the analyst Highest proprietary advantage — generates unique insights competitors don't have. Examples: tasting the food, visiting stores, speaking with customers, conducting channel checks with suppliers. Labor-intensive and expensive but can provide edge.
Examiner Mindset — Analyst Sources and Their Limitations

The exam may present a source and ask what its limitation is, or ask which source provides the most independent/objective view. Key instincts: Earnings calls / investor days: useful but management is biased — always acknowledge this. Sell-side reports: potentially conflicted (investment banking relationships may bias recommendations). Company website visits / product testing: proprietary primary research — most independent, generates unique insight, but impractical for some industries (e.g., pharmaceuticals — you can't easily test drugs). Social media: useful for consumer sentiment — not for hard financial data. The most objective views come from independent primary research and non-issuer third-party sources.

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Financial analysis = interpreting/evaluating company performance and position in economic context, for investment/credit/economic decisions. Starts with financial reports + supplemented by industry/company research.
  • ✅ Equity analyst focus: profitability, per-share value, earnings growth, P/E vs. peers, DCF valuation → buy/hold/sell. Credit analyst focus: ability to pay interest and repay principal, leverage, interest coverage, free cash flow adequacy → loan terms or credit rating.
  • ✅ 6-Phase FSA Framework: (1) Articulate purpose/context; (2) Collect data; (3) Process data; (4) Analyze/interpret; (5) Communicate conclusions/recommendations; (6) Follow-up. Know which phase each activity belongs to.
  • ✅ Phase 1 warning: Resist calculating ratios before knowing what question you're answering. Define purpose, context, audience, deliverable, timetable first.
  • ✅ Phase 4 is critical: Interpretation of output — the answer is almost never the number alone. The analyst's judgment and interpretation turn data into a recommendation.
  • ✅ Top-down approach (Phase 2): Macroeconomic environment → Industry prospects → Company prospects. This sequencing provides proper context for financial data.
  • ✅ IOSCO: Not a direct regulator. Members regulate >95% of world's financial capital markets. Founded 1983. 3 core objectives: protect investors, ensure fair/efficient/transparent markets, reduce systemic risk.
  • ✅ SEC Acts: 1933 Act = initial registration + disclosure on new issuances. 1934 Act = created the SEC + periodic reporting requirement. SOX 2002 = created PCAOB, auditor independence, CEO/CFO certification, internal control reporting.
  • ✅ Key SEC forms: 10-K (US annual), 20-F (non-US annual), 10-Q (US quarterly), 6-K (non-US interim), 8-K (material events), DEF-14A/Proxy (shareholder meeting — contains exec compensation), Forms 3/4/5 (insider ownership). Annual Report to shareholders = NOT required by SEC, marketing-oriented.
  • ✅ MD&A: Generally UNAUDITED (exception: Germany). Forward-looking disclosures are useful. Management is biased. Must be read critically — only one input, not an independent view. IASB's 5 content elements: nature of business, objectives/strategies, resources/risks/relationships, results of operations, critical performance measures.
  • ✅ Audit opinions: Unqualified/clean = good, fairly presented. Qualified = exception exists, read explanatory paragraph. Adverse = materially wrong, not fairly presented. Disclaimer = auditor unable to form opinion. Audit provides reasonable assurance (NOT absolute assurance) — uses sampling.
  • ✅ KAM (international) / CAM (US): Most challenging audit areas (high misstatement risk, significant management judgment). NOT necessarily the most important factors for analysts/investors — don't confuse "hard to audit" with "most important for valuation."
  • ✅ Notes disclose: Basis of preparation, accounting policies/methods/estimates, segment data, acquisitions/disposals, off-balance-sheet obligations, financial instruments/risks, legal proceedings, related-party transactions, subsequent events. Often 60+ pages. Essential — don't skip them.
  • ✅ Segment reporting: 10% rule (revenue, assets, OR profit/loss); 75% test (combined reportable segment external revenue must = 75% of total). Single customer ≥10% of revenue must be disclosed, but identity does NOT have to be disclosed.
  • ✅ IFRS vs. US GAAP — 4 critical differences: LIFO (GAAP: permitted; IFRS: prohibited). Development costs (GAAP: expense; IFRS: capitalize if criteria met). Inventory writedown reversal (GAAP: prohibited; IFRS: permitted). Interest paid on cash flow (GAAP: always operating; IFRS: operating OR financing).
  • ✅ IFRS = principles-based (IASB). US GAAP = rules-based (FASB). Both converging on major new standards (revenue recognition, leases, credit losses). Reconciliation between IFRS and GAAP is NOT required — analysts cannot always make full comparability adjustments.
  • ✅ Monitoring standards: Analyst monitors from USER's perspective (how do changes affect reports/valuations?), not preparer's. Monitor: new products/transactions, IASB/FASB actions, company disclosures on critical accounting policies.
  • ✅ Information sources beyond filings: Issuer non-regulatory (earnings calls, investor days, press releases, site visits); Public third-party (govt data, industry news, social media); Proprietary third-party (sell-side reports, Bloomberg, industry databases); Proprietary primary research (surveys, channel checks, product testing — most independent, most proprietary advantage).

Top exam traps for Module 1:
(1) Annual Report ≠ 10-K — Annual Report is NOT required by SEC.
(2) MD&A is generally unaudited — management is biased.
(3) Audit = reasonable assurance, not absolute assurance.
(4) KAMs/CAMs = hardest to audit, NOT most important for investors.
(5) LIFO: PROHIBITED under IFRS, PERMITTED under US GAAP.
(6) Inventory writedown reversal: PROHIBITED under US GAAP, PERMITTED under IFRS.
(7) Segment 10% rule is OR (not AND) — only one threshold needs to be met.
(8) Single customer disclosure: must say ≥10% exists, but NOT who the customer is.
(9) Phase 4 (Interpret) is described as "the critical step" — not Phase 3 (Process).
(10) IOSCO is NOT a direct regulatory authority — it facilitates and coordinates.
