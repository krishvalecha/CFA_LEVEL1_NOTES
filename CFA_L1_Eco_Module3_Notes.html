01

Fiscal Policy vs. Monetary Policy

Fiscal Policy
  • Government's decisions on taxation and spending
  • Implemented by: Government / Legislature
  • Tools: spending, taxes, transfers
  • Can also redistribute income & wealth
  • Subject to political process → slower to implement
  • Action lag and impact lag are significant
Monetary Policy
  • Central bank activities influencing money and credit
  • Implemented by: Central Bank
  • Tools: interest rates, reserve requirements, open market operations
  • Can operate independently of government
  • Faster to implement than fiscal policy
  • Monetarists prefer this over fiscal tools
Shared Overarching Goal
Stable and positive economic growth + Stable and low inflation
Both policies aim to avoid booms (overheating) followed by extended periods of low/negative growth and high unemployment. The challenge: economies are frequently hit by unpredictable shocks.

Keynesian vs. Monetarist View

Keynesian View
  • Fiscal policy has powerful effects on aggregate demand, output, and employment
  • Especially effective when there is substantial spare capacity
  • Government spending can break deflationary spirals
  • Support active fiscal intervention (stimulus)
Monetarist View
  • Fiscal changes have only a temporary effect on aggregate demand
  • Monetary policy is more effective for controlling inflation
  • Do NOT advocate using fiscal policy for countercyclical adjustment
  • Concern: crowding out and long-run distortions
⚠ Exam Trap

Monetarists believe monetary policy is more effective — but they do NOT advocate using monetary policy for countercyclical adjustment either. Their preference is for rules-based policy (e.g., inflation targeting) rather than discretionary intervention. Know the nuance!

02

Roles & Objectives of Fiscal Policy

Three Core Objectives

  1. Influence overall aggregate demand and economic activity (real GDP)
  2. Redistribute income and wealth among different population segments
  3. Allocate resources between different sectors and economic agents

Expansionary vs. Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Policy TypeWhen UsedMechanismBudget Impact
Expansionary Recession / high unemployment ↑ Government spending OR ↓ Taxes → ↑ Aggregate Demand Budget deficit increases (or surplus decreases)
Contractionary Boom / overheating economy ↓ Government spending OR ↑ Taxes → ↓ Aggregate Demand Budget surplus increases (or deficit decreases)
💡 Quick Rule

Rising deficit = Expansionary policy signal. Rising surplus = Contractionary policy signal. BUT: deficit can also rise automatically during recession (automatic stabilizers) without any active policy change — this is why analysts use the structural budget deficit instead.

Automatic Stabilizers vs. Discretionary Policy

Automatic Stabilizers
  • Built-in mechanisms that work without active policy decisions
  • Recession: Tax revenues fall, unemployment benefits rise → automatic stimulus
  • Boom: Progressive income taxes rise → automatic dampening
  • Advantage: No recognition or action lag — respond immediately
  • Reduce output fluctuations by dampening the multiplier effect
  • Examples: income tax, VAT, unemployment benefits
Discretionary Fiscal Policy
  • Active, deliberate changes in spending and/or tax rates
  • Requires identification of shock and policy response
  • Subject to recognition, action, and impact lags
  • Risk: stimulus kicks in after economy already recovering
  • Examples: infrastructure stimulus packages, tax cuts, welfare program changes

Budget Terminology

Budget Balance
Budget Surplus/Deficit = Government Revenue – Government Expenditure
Revenue = tax revenues net of transfer payments | Expenditure = includes interest payments on government debt
Balanced budget: Revenue = Expenditure (rare in practice)
Structural (Cyclically Adjusted) Budget Deficit = deficit that would exist if economy were at full employment — the true fiscal stance indicator, removing cyclical noise.
⚠ Why Actual Deficit ≠ True Fiscal Stance

Two reasons the actual deficit misrepresents fiscal stance: (1) Automatic stabilizers change the deficit without any policy action — a recession widens the deficit even with no new spending. (2) The distinction between real vs. nominal interest rates — cash interest payments overstate the true burden when inflation is eroding the real value of outstanding debt. Analysts use the structural deficit to strip out both distortions.

03

National Debt — Does the Size Relative to GDP Matter?

Key Definitions

Government Deficit: Revenue minus expenditure in a single period (e.g., one year). Can be positive (surplus) or negative (deficit).

National/Government Debt: The accumulation over time of annual deficits. Financed by borrowing from private sector (bonds, pension funds, etc.).

Debt-to-GDP Ratio: The primary metric for assessing debt sustainability. If real GDP growth > real interest rate on debt → ratio improves. If real interest rate > real GDP growth → debt burden grows faster than economy.

Debt Sustainability Condition
Real GDP growth > Real interest rate on debt → Debt/GDP ratio improves
Real interest rate > Real GDP growth → Debt/GDP worsens even if economy grows
Inflation also reduces the real value of outstanding debt → nominal GDP rises even if real GDP is flat → debt/GDP ratio falls. Deflation has the opposite effect.

Arguments Against Worrying About National Debt

  • Internal debt: Debt owed to own citizens (e.g., Japan 93%, Canada 90%) — less dangerous than externally held debt (it's "we owe it to ourselves")
  • Productive investment: If borrowed money funds capital projects or education → raises future output and tax revenues
  • Reduces tax distortions: Large deficits can allow reducing existing tax inefficiencies
  • Ricardian Equivalence: Private sector may offset fiscal deficits by saving more in anticipation of future taxes → no net impact on aggregate demand
  • Unemployment context: If resources are idle, debt doesn't divert productive activity — it can actually create employment

Arguments For Worrying About National Debt

  • Higher future taxes: Large debt/GDP → higher marginal tax rates needed → disincentive to work, save, invest → lower long-run growth
  • Loss of market confidence: Central bank may be forced to print money to finance deficits → inflation (historical examples: Germany 1920s, Zimbabwe)
  • Crowding out: Government borrowing from a limited savings pool → higher interest rates → private sector investment falls
  • Solvency risk: Above an unknown threshold, solvency comes into question. Interest rate/GDP ratio is a key solvency metric.
🧠 Mnemonic — "RIPUR" for Against Worrying | "HCCS" for Worrying

Against: Ricardian equivalence, Internal debt, Productive investment, Unemployment context, Reduce distortions
For worrying: Higher taxes, Crowding out, Confidence loss (money printing), Solvency risk

💡 Ricardian Equivalence — Key Concept

If households are fully rational and forward-looking, a government deficit today means higher taxes tomorrow. Rational consumers will save today to pay for those future taxes. Result: government spending increase is fully offset by private savings increase → no net impact on aggregate demand. In practice, this is rarely observed perfectly, but it weakens the fiscal multiplier.

04

Tools of Fiscal Policy

Government Spending Tools

ToolDescriptionExamplesGDP Impact
Transfer Payments Welfare payments through social security. Redistribute income. NOT included in GDP (no factor production reward). Pensions, housing benefits, unemployment benefits, child benefits Indirect (boosts disposable income of recipients)
Current Government Spending Regular, recurring spending on goods and services. Impacts skill levels and labor productivity. Health, education, defense Direct (part of G in GDP)
Capital Expenditure (Capex) Investment spending on infrastructure. Adds to national capital stock and productive potential. Longest to implement. Roads, hospitals, schools, prisons Direct + multiplier effect; improves long-run supply
⚠ Exam Trap — Transfer Payments NOT in GDP

Transfer payments (welfare, pensions, unemployment benefits) are NOT included in GDP because they are not payment for current productive activity. They are a redistribution of existing income, not a reward to a factor of production. This is a frequently tested distinction.

Government Revenue Tools

Direct Taxes
  • Levied on income, wealth, and corporate profits
  • Income tax (personal and corporate)
  • Capital gains tax
  • National insurance / labor taxes
  • Property tax (local)
  • Inheritance tax (revenue + redistribution)
  • Harder and slower to change (payroll systems need updating — months of notice)
Indirect Taxes
  • Levied on spending on goods and services
  • VAT / sales tax
  • Excise duties (fuel, alcohol, tobacco)
  • Gambling taxes (social deterrence)
  • Fuel duties (environmental purpose)
  • Can be adjusted almost immediately after announcement
  • Generate revenue at little/no cost to government

Advantages & Disadvantages Summary

ToolSpeed of ImplementationKey AdvantageKey Disadvantage
Indirect Taxes Immediate Instant behavior change; low cost to implement Can be regressive; may not target spending effectively
Direct Taxes Months Powerful announcement effect on behavior Payroll systems require significant lead time
Transfer Payments Months Targeted redistribution; strong effect if directed at low-income households Administrative burden; subject to eligibility rules
Capital Expenditure Years Adds to productive capacity; permanent supply-side benefit Longest lag of all tools; economy may have recovered before impact
💡 Potency Ranking of Fiscal Tools

Direct government spending has the biggest impact on aggregate demand (1:1 direct addition to AD). Tax cuts and transfer increases have smaller multiplier effects because some income is saved. However, transfers directed at the poorest households (who spend nearly all income, MPC close to 1) can approach the effectiveness of direct spending.

05

Desirable Attributes of Tax Policy

Economists identify four desirable attributes of a tax system:

AttributeMeaningKey Nuance
Simplicity Easy for taxpayers to comply; easy for authorities to enforce. Final liability should be certain and not easily manipulated. Complex tax codes increase compliance costs and avoidance opportunities
Efficiency Should interfere as little as possible with individual marketplace choices. Should not discourage work and investment. Debate: should taxes deliberately favor "good" activities (savings) and discourage "bad" (tobacco)? Risk of unintended side effects.
Fairness Horizontal equity: Similar people pay similar taxes. Vertical equity: Richer people pay more. Progressive rates (income tax) are standard. Flat tax (same % for all) is an alternative advocated by some. Subjective concept.
Revenue Sufficiency Tax system must raise enough revenue for government spending needs. May conflict with efficiency — high rates to close deficits can reduce labor effort (Laffer curve logic)
🧠 Mnemonic — "SERF" for Tax Policy Attributes

Simplicity · Efficiency · Revenue sufficiency · Fairness

The Laffer Curve (Supply-Side Economics)

Associated with US economist Arthur Laffer. The idea: very high marginal tax rates reduce the incentive to work, save, and invest, and may actually reduce total tax revenue. At a certain tax rate, cutting taxes could increase revenues by expanding the tax base.

This is the theoretical basis for supply-side tax cuts (e.g., US tax reforms since 1981). Controversial in practice — evidence on labor effort, savings, and investment effects is mixed.

The Fiscal Multiplier

Key Variables

Core Definitions
MPC (c) = Marginal Propensity to Consume
MPS (s) = Marginal Propensity to Save
c + s = 1   →   s = 1 – c
MPC = fraction of each additional dollar of disposable income that is spent. MPS = fraction saved. These drive the multiplier chain.
Disposable Income
YD = Y – NT = (1 – t) × Y
t = net tax rate. Every $1 rise in national income → disposable income rises by $(1 – t). Net taxes reduce the proportion of income available to spend.

Simple Multiplier (No Taxes)

Simple Multiplier (ignoring taxes)
Multiplier = 1 / (1 – c) = 1 / s
If c = 0.9 → Multiplier = 1/(1–0.9) = 1/0.1 = 10. Every $1 of new spending generates $10 of total output (chain reaction of spending rounds). This is the maximum possible — taxes reduce it.

Fiscal Multiplier (With Taxes)

Fiscal Multiplier (with tax rate t)
Fiscal Multiplier = 1 / [1 – c(1 – t)]
The effective MPC in the presence of taxes is c(1–t), not c alone. Taxes "leak" out of the spending chain, reducing the multiplier. Higher tax rates → lower multiplier.
Worked Example — Fiscal Multiplier

Given: MPC (c) = 0.9, Tax rate (t) = 0.2, Government increases spending by $1 billion

Step 1: Effective MPC with tax = c(1–t) = 0.9 × (1–0.2) = 0.9 × 0.8 = 0.72

Step 2: Fiscal Multiplier = 1 / (1 – 0.72) = 1 / 0.28 = 3.57

→ $1 billion increase in G raises total income/spending by $3.57 billion

💡 What Reduces the Multiplier?

Any "leakage" from the spending chain reduces the multiplier: (1) Taxes — reduce disposable income at each round. (2) Saving — income saved doesn't circulate further. (3) Imports — spending on imports leaves the domestic economy. (4) Ricardian Equivalence — if households save in anticipation of future taxes, the multiplier approaches zero. Higher MPC = higher multiplier; higher tax rate = lower multiplier.

Balanced Budget Multiplier

Key result: If government increases spending (G) and taxes (T) by the same amount, aggregate output still rises (not stays the same).

Why? A $1 tax rise reduces spending by only $c (because MPC < 1 — some is saved). But the $1 of spending directly adds $1 to AD. Net impact on AD = $1 − $c = $(1 − c) > 0. This ripples through the multiplier.

Balanced Budget Multiplier = 1 (always takes the value of 1). A $1 balanced budget expansion raises output by $1.

Summary of Multiplier Values
Simple multiplier (no taxes):   1 / (1 – c)
Fiscal multiplier (with taxes):   1 / [1 – c(1 – t)]
Balanced budget multiplier:   always = 1
The fiscal multiplier is always smaller than the simple multiplier (taxes reduce it). The balanced budget multiplier is always 1 regardless of MPC or tax rate.
⚠ Exam Trap — Balanced Budget Multiplier

Students often assume a balanced increase in G and T has zero effect. It does NOT — output rises by the amount of the spending increase ($ΔG). The balanced budget multiplier equals exactly 1. This is a classic CFA question.

Fiscal Policy Implementation & Difficulties

The Three Policy Lags

1
Recognition Lag
Takes months to identify that the economy is slowing. Data released with delay and subject to revision. Like "driving looking in the rearview mirror."
→
2
Action Lag
Once identified, policy changes take many months to decide on and implement — especially capital spending (planning, legal permissions). Political debate adds further delay.
→
3
Impact Lag
After implementation, takes additional time for policy to affect real economic activity. By then, economy may have already turned on its own. Risk of pro-cyclical effect.
🧠 Mnemonic — "RAI" = Recognize → Act → Impact

Recognition → Action → Impact. These lags apply to both fiscal and discretionary monetary policy. However, fiscal policy typically has the longest action lag due to the political process and planning requirements for capital projects.

Why Fiscal Policy Cannot Fully Stabilize Aggregate Demand

  1. Incomplete information + Lags (RAI): By the time policy is designed and implemented, the economic situation may have already changed.
  2. Forecast uncertainty: Macroeconomic models have poor track records. Private sector behavior may change unpredictably — consumption or investment may surge or collapse independently.
  3. Unemployment-inflation trade-off: Raising AD toward full employment may also tighten the labor market and push up wages and inflation. Policy maker may hesitate to over-stimulate.
  4. Large existing deficit: If budget deficit is already large, financial markets may react negatively to further borrowing → higher sovereign bond yields → offsetting tightening.
  5. Unknown level of full employment: Productive capacity and willingness to work change over time → difficult to know whether the economy is actually below full employment.
  6. Supply vs. demand diagnosis: If unemployment reflects low supply of labor (not low demand), then demand stimulus will not help and may only cause inflation.
  7. Crowding out: Government borrowing from a limited savings pool raises interest rates → private investment falls → partial or full offset of fiscal expansion.

Crowding Out — Explained

When government borrows heavily, it competes with private firms for a limited pool of savings. This drives up interest rates, making borrowing more expensive for private businesses and potentially canceling profitable investment projects. The result: government spending partially or fully displaces private spending.

Short run: Crowding out may be limited (especially if spare savings exist, as in a recession). Long run: Persistent crowding out damages capital accumulation and long-run growth potential. Similarly, tax distortions compound over time even if small in the short run.

💡 Crowding Out is Weakest in a Recession

In a deep recession with high unemployment, the private sector is NOT competing aggressively for savings (investment is depressed). So crowding out is minimal — this is when expansionary fiscal policy is most effective. Crowding out is most damaging at or near full employment when private investment demand is high.
