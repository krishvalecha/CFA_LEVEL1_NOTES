What Is a Business Model?

A business model is a description of how a business works — its strategy, operations, target markets, key customers, suppliers, and ultimately its prospects, risks, and financial profile. It answers five core questions:

The 5W Business Model Framework
WHO? → Target customers and market segments
WHAT? → Product or service offerings (and WHY customers buy it)
WHERE? → Channels: how products reach customers (sales + distribution)
HOW MUCH? → Pricing strategy
HOW? → Key assets, partners, suppliers, capabilities (organization/execution)
A good business model provides enough detail for logic to be clear, without becoming a full business plan. It links value proposition → value chain → profitability.

Business model analysis answers:

  1. What is the firm selling?
  2. To whom?
  3. For how much?
  4. How is the company going to execute?
  5. How much does it cost, and how will costs behave as size and conditions change?
Value Proposition

The product/service attributes that lead customers to buy from this firm rather than competitors — at the given price.

Value Chain

Systems and processes within the firm that create value for customers. Includes primary and support activities.

Pricing Model

How much customers are billed for units of products or services. Many types exist depending on pricing strategy.

⚠ Analyst Warning — Don't Rely on Management's Description Alone

Analysts should not rely solely on management's description of its business model. Management may overstate differentiation, use overly broad terms, reference underdeveloped platforms, or suggest "everyone is a potential customer." Analysts must develop their own independent understanding and scrutinize management claims rigorously.

02

The Customers and Market ("Who")

A business model must identify target customers clearly. Key questions:

  • What types or segments of customers are served?
  • Are customers businesses (B2B) or consumers (B2C)?
  • What geographies are served?
B2B (Business-to-Business)
  • Customers are other businesses
  • Straightforward needs (e.g., ship goods)
  • Sophisticated buyers motivated to earn profit
  • Universe of potential customers is smaller → direct sales feasible
  • Examples: industrial equipment, enterprise software, raw materials
B2C (Business-to-Consumer)
  • Customers are individual consumers
  • Diverse and sometimes impossible-to-quantify needs
  • Emotional and aesthetic factors play a large role
  • Large universe → mass channels, advertising often required
  • Examples: retail, autos, apparel, streaming services
💡 Customer Segmentation — Analyst Implication

Clear customer segmentation helps analysts: (1) identify the addressable market for unit and price forecasts; (2) spot risks from shifting demographics or competition; (3) challenge management's assertions about market size. Narrow segmentation (e.g., "affluent EV early adopters with home charging") is more analytically rigorous than "everyone is a potential customer."

💡 Business Opportunities in Underserved Segments

Opportunities often arise because established firms fail to effectively serve — or even recognize — certain customer segments. This is a classic entry point for disruptive new businesses. The analyst's job is to evaluate whether the target segment is large enough, reachable, and defensible.

03

Product or Service Offering ("What" and Often "Why")

The business model must define what the firm offers and how it differs from competitors by referencing the needs of its target customers. This helps analysts:

  • Identify the addressable market
  • Understand key opportunities and risks (e.g., imitation risk, substitution risk)
  • Identify current and potential competitors
💡 Think About the "Job" the Product Is Hired to Do

Understanding why customers buy — the "job" they're hiring the product to fulfill — reveals non-obvious competitors. Trucks and railroads don't seem like competitors, but both fulfill the same job: transporting goods. A thoughtful analyst asks: in which scenarios would a customer choose truck vs. rail vs. air vs. marine? This mindset finds risks and opportunities management may overlook.

⚠ Red Flags in Management Product Descriptions

Watch for management that: (1) Uses overly broad terms for their product/market ("we serve everyone"); (2) Overstates differentiation — claims uniqueness that doesn't exist; (3) References weakly developed platforms or networks to inflate addressable market size. All are attempts to boost perceived value without substance.

04

Channels — "Where" (How Products Reach Customers)

A firm's channel strategy covers two main functions:

  • Sales & marketing — selling the firm's products and services
  • Distribution / logistics — delivering them to customers

Key analyst question: what can the firm do internally vs. what should be done by strategic partners/suppliers?

Traditional Channel

Product flows through a series of intermediaries (distributor → wholesaler → retailer) before reaching the final customer. Each stage purchases and re-sells the product.

Examples: grocery, consumer electronics

Direct Sales

Firm sells directly to the end customer — no intermediaries. Common for complex/high-margin products and B2B markets. Higher fixed costs (salespeople) but closer customer relationship.

Examples: Tesla (vs. franchised dealers), medical devices, industrial equipment, luxury goods

Omnichannel

Combination of digital and physical channels to complete a sale. Customer may order online and pick up in store, or select in-store and get delivery.

Examples: common for apparel, consumer packaged goods

Channel strategy affects profitability and risk:

  • Direct sales advantage: Close customer relationship; products not shown alongside competitors on a shelf
  • Direct sales disadvantage: Significant fixed costs (sales force salaries and benefits)
  • Channel differences affect comparability of financial results between competitors — e.g., Tesla (direct) vs. GM/Ford (dealer network) have fundamentally different revenue and cost structures
05

Pricing Strategy & Pricing Models ("How Much")

Pricing Power Spectrum

Commodity Producers — Price Takers

No product differentiation → receive market price set by supply and demand. Must pursue cost leadership strategy to remain competitive.

Examples: oil/gas, off-patent pharmaceuticals, many types of loans. Cost leaders: Costco, Lidl, Ryanair.

Differentiated Producers — Pricing Power

Few competitors or highly differentiated product → ability to maintain prices above costs or raise prices without losing customers.

Justified via: lower total cost of ownership, superior performance, brand prestige, proprietary network.

Price Discrimination
Charging different prices to different customers based on: quality/grade, units purchased, channel, customer size/type, unit cost. Objective: maximize profit by extracting value from customers with different willingness to pay. "Net prices" = published list prices adjusted for variable discounts, promotions, and bundles.

All Pricing Models — Complete Reference

Tiered Pricing

Different prices for different buyers based on volume or product features (e.g., base vs. premium trim levels).

Examples: SaaS plans (basic/pro/enterprise), vehicle trims

Dynamic Pricing

Different prices at different times and for different customer types based on supply, demand, or timing.

Examples: airline seats, hotel seasonal rates, Uber/Lyft surge pricing

Value-Based Pricing

Price set based on value received by customer — often estimates the customer's opportunity cost or cost savings.

Examples: pharmaceuticals (price reflects hospitalizations avoided); enterprise software (priced on productivity gains)

Auction / Reverse Auction

Price set through a bidding process. Digital tech has automated this, enabling it in new categories.

Examples: Google/Baidu (digital ad auctions), eBay (consumer goods)

Bundling

Multiple products/services sold together — effective when products are complementary, have high margins, and high marketing costs.

Examples: phone + cable + internet packages; Microsoft Office + OneDrive

Razor / Razorblade

Low price on initial equipment + high margin on repeat-purchase consumables. Equipment often designed to work only with proprietary consumables.

Examples: printers + ink, gaming consoles + games, diagnostic equipment + reagents. Risk: generic consumable competitors undercut the model.

Add-On Pricing

Core product sold at a base price; optional features/services sold additionally. Most profitable when customer is "captive" post-purchase.

Examples: in-game purchases, airline seat upgrades, insurance add-ons. Risk: excessive use damages customer goodwill.

Penetration Pricing

Deliberate discounting to build scale and market share quickly — effectively a marketing expense to acquire customers. Controversial if sustained (regulators may view as anti-competitive).

Examples: mobile phone providers subsidizing smartphones; Amazon discounting Alexa tablets/e-readers

Freemium

Basic functionality at no charge (or with ads); premium features require payment. Aims to scale user base, then convert free users to paying subscribers.

Examples: Spotify (free with ads / premium paid), LinkedIn, many mobile apps and games

Hidden Revenue Model

Service is "free" to users; revenue generated from a third party (e.g., advertisers or sellers). Users don't directly pay.

Examples: Google/Facebook (free to users; advertisers pay); online marketplaces (sellers pay; buyers don't); debit cards/checking accounts (appear free; banks earn interchange/float)

Subscription

Recurring fees for ongoing access to products/services. Provides predictable, recurring revenue streams. Can be fixed, tiered, or usage-based.

Examples: Netflix, SaaS (Salesforce, Microsoft 365), utilities, cloud computing (AWS), news publications

Leasing / Licensing / Franchising

Transfer of property or access rights rather than ownership. Leasing: physical assets (real estate, equipment). Licensing: intellectual property. Franchising: comprehensive licensing — franchisor provides right to sell under brand + support; earns royalty on franchisee sales.

Examples: commercial real estate leases; pharmaceutical patent licenses; McDonald's, Subway (franchises)

💡 Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) — A Common Value Justification

Companies charging premium prices often justify via lower total cost of ownership — the aggregate direct and indirect costs of owning an asset over its lifespan. Tesla example: premium sticker price BUT lower fuel costs + government subsidies → more competitive total cost. This is a common B2B value proposition in industrial equipment, software, and healthcare. Analysts should verify TCO claims independently.

06

Value Proposition (Who + What + Where + How Much Combined)

Value Proposition
The product or service attributes valued by customers that lead them to purchase the product and prefer one firm's offering over competitors', given relative pricing. It synthesizes all four business model dimensions (who, what, where, how much) into the reason customers choose this firm.

Value proposition dimensions:

  • Product/service itself: capability, performance, features, aesthetics, style
  • Service and support: "high-touch" vs. "low-touch" customer service; access to repairs, spare parts
  • Sales process: purchasing convenience, no-hassle returns, ease of transaction
  • Pricing relative to competitors: premium, parity, or discount
💡 Tesla Value Proposition — Case Study

Tesla's value proposition combines: high-performance electric vehicles + zero emissions + strong/silent acceleration + technological sophistication (self-driving, OTA software updates) + proprietary Supercharger network + customer loyalty from aesthetics and founder personality. The Supercharger network is key — it's a major capital investment that creates a competitive moat against new EV entrants who lack comparable charging infrastructure.

Constraints on value proposition:

  • Regulations limiting product features or pricing
  • Customers who prioritize price above all other factors
  • Commodity products with thin margins and no differentiation opportunity
07

Value Chain vs. Supply Chain — Business Organization

Value Chain (Porter, 1985)

The systems and processes within a single firm that create value for customers. Includes only the firm's own activities.

Primary Activities: Inbound Logistics → Operations → Outbound Logistics → Marketing & Sales → Service

Support Activities: Procurement · Human Resource Management · Technology Development · Firm Infrastructure

Supply Chain

The sequence of processes involved in creating and delivering a product to the end customer — both within AND external to a single firm.

Includes all steps from raw material extraction through final customer delivery, regardless of which firm performs each step.

A firm's supply chain includes its own value chain PLUS all supplier and distributor activities.

Value Chain Analysis Process (Porter Framework)
Step 1: IDENTIFY specific activities carried out by the firm in each value chain component
Step 2: ESTIMATE the value added and costs associated with each activity
Step 3: IDENTIFY opportunities for competitive advantage in each activity
Value chain analysis links the firm's value proposition (what customers value) to profitability (margin generated from activities). It reveals where a firm creates or destroys value.

Vertical integration vs. outsourcing decision:

  • Firms must decide which value chain functions to perform internally vs. outsource to strategic partners
  • Risk of outsourcing critical inputs: supplier relationships become strategic risks (e.g., COVID-19 semiconductor shortage caused major production disruptions for automakers relying on single-source suppliers)
  • Tesla's contrarian choice: heavy vertical integration (in-house batteries, software, electronics) requires substantial capital but creates proprietary technology moat and reduces supply chain dependency
  • Example of full outsourcing: some automakers outsource entire vehicle production (Porsche → Valmet and Magna Steyr)
💡 Unit Economics — Analyst Tool

Unit economics = expressing revenues and costs on a per-unit basis. Helps analysts understand profitability at the fundamental level. Example: Amazon Web Services cloud model has declining unit costs AND declining unit prices over time as volumes grow (most costs are fixed). Since unit costs fall faster than prices, margins improve — creating a virtuous cycle and a scale barrier to entry for competitors.

08

Conventional Business Models

Conventional business models are common and long-established. They make up most business models in practice. Success depends not on business model innovation but on superior execution, skill, proprietary technology, brand strength, scale, or scope economies.

Model Description Key Characteristic Examples
Manufacturer Converts raw materials or components into finished goods for sale Capital-intensive; owns production facilities; sells to distributors, retailers, or direct Automakers, semiconductor fabs, food producers
Distributor / Wholesaler Buys goods from manufacturers and sells to retailers or businesses; adds value via logistics and inventory management Asset-intensive (warehouses, trucks); thin margins; high volume Food/beverage distributors, pharmaceutical wholesalers
Retailer Sells goods directly to end consumers from physical or digital stores Large inventory; real estate exposure; brand and location critical; direct customer relationship Walmart, Amazon (retail), Zara, supermarkets
Financial Services Facilitates financial transactions, lending, investing, insurance, or asset management Regulated; balance-sheet intensive (banks); fee-based (asset managers, insurance); intermediary between savers and borrowers Banks, insurers, asset managers, payment processors
Real Estate Owns, develops, operates, or invests in real estate assets; generates rental income or capital gains Capital-intensive; leveraged; income from leases; valuation sensitive to interest rates REITs, real estate developers, property managers
Service (Professional) Delivers intangible services using human capital and expertise Low capital intensity; high labor costs; relationship-driven; limited scalability per person Law firms, consulting firms, accounting firms, hospitals
Technology (Software) Develops and licenses or sells software, platforms, or digital tools High R&D upfront; near-zero marginal reproduction cost; strong operating leverage once scale achieved Microsoft, Oracle, Salesforce, Adobe
Transportation / Infrastructure Provides physical movement of people or goods; operates critical infrastructure Capital-intensive (aircraft, ships, trains, pipelines); regulated; long asset lives; network effects possible Airlines, railways, toll roads, shipping companies, ports
09

Business Model Variations & Innovation

Common Variations on Conventional Models

  • Private label / Contract manufacturer: Produces goods to be marketed by others under their brand. Apple and NVIDIA design products but contract manufacturing to Asian firms (TSMC, Samsung). Firm specializes in R&D, product design, and sales while capital-intensive manufacturing is outsourced.
  • Value-added reseller (VAR): Distributes products but also handles complex installation, customization, service, or support. Common with complex, service-intensive products (construction machinery, IT hardware, enterprise software).
  • Licensing arrangements: Produces a product under someone else's brand in exchange for a royalty. Common in toys, apparel (rights to use film character names, sports teams, related brands).
  • Franchise model: Franchisees operate under a specific brand with proprietary products and processes. Franchisor earns royalty on franchisee sales PLUS an additional fee for product development and advertising support. Common in: restaurants, retail, auto dealerships.

Business Model Innovation — Key Principles

  • Business model innovation does NOT require product innovation — IKEA combined existing concepts (self-assembly furniture + modernist design + big-box retail) in a novel way
  • Google didn't invent search — it found a way to improve accuracy AND monetize via advertising based on user search data
  • Innovation is often pioneered by new market entrants rather than incumbents
  • "Innovation becomes convention over time" — today's disruption is tomorrow's standard model
💡 Digital Technology — 4 Key Business Model Impacts

1. Location matters less — e-commerce lets firms serve customers without local physical presence. 2. Outsourcing is easier — digital tools reduce coordination costs with external partners. 3. Digital marketing is more targeted and cost-effective — can reach specific customer segments globally. 4. Network effects more powerful and accessible — platforms can scale users exponentially.

Network Effects & Platform Business Models

Network Effects
The increase in value of a network to its users as more users join. More users → more valuable → attracts even more users → virtuous cycle. Network effects capitalize on both economies of scale and economies of scope. They create strong barriers to entry and are a form of competitive advantage.

Network effects exist in both old and new businesses:

  • Pre-internet: telephone service, credit card payment networks (Visa), stock exchanges
  • Internet: WeChat (messaging/payments), online classifieds, social media, ride-sharing, food delivery
  • Key metric for network businesses: number of users and the growth rate of users
One-Sided Network Effects

Only one type of user — each user adds value for all other users. Marginal cost of adding each user may be lower than marginal revenue from that user.

Examples: telephone service, peer-to-peer payment apps (Venmo/PayPal)

Two-Sided / Multi-Sided Networks

Two or more distinct types of users — each side creates value for the other. More users on one side → attracts more of the other side → exponential growth potential.

Examples: Visa (merchants + cardholders), Airbnb (hosts + guests), food delivery apps (restaurants + drivers + customers)

Crowdsourcing
Users contribute directly to a product or service. Common in network business models. A small amount of company moderation/oversight enables large-scale voluntary user collaboration.

Crowdsourcing examples:

  • Social media content: TikTok/Douyin, Reddit
  • Open-source software development
  • Knowledge aggregation: Wikipedia
  • Customer reviews and ratings: Yelp, Amazon, TripAdvisor
💡 Food Delivery — Multi-Sided Network Example

Food delivery platforms connect three groups: restaurants, independent drivers, and customers. As each group grows, it reinforces the other two: more restaurants → more customer choice → more customers → more demand → more driver income → more drivers → faster delivery → more customers. This is a multi-sided network effect creating exponential growth and a significant scale moat against new entrants.

11

Digital Business Models & Innovation

Digital technology has radically reduced the cost of communicating, exchanging information, and financially transacting, enabling significant business model innovation. New products (streaming, social media, digital ads) and new models (marketplaces, platforms) have emerged.

Key Digital-Native Business Models

Model How It Works Revenue Source Examples
Digital Marketplace Platform connecting buyers and sellers; does not own inventory. Earns commissions or fees from transactions. Transaction fees, commissions, listing fees eBay, Airbnb, Amazon Marketplace, Etsy
Freemium Basic service free (or ad-supported); premium features require subscription. Scales user base first, then converts to paid. Premium subscription, advertising Spotify, LinkedIn, Dropbox, mobile games
Hidden Revenue "Free" service to users; third parties (advertisers, merchants) pay for access to users. Advertising, merchant fees, data monetization Google, Facebook/Meta, legacy media (TV, newspapers)
SaaS / Cloud Subscription Software delivered over the internet via recurring subscription. Near-zero marginal cost per additional user. Monthly/annual subscription fees; often usage-based tiers Salesforce, Microsoft 365, AWS, Netflix
Platform / Ecosystem Provides infrastructure/tools for third parties to build on. Creates multi-sided network with switching costs. App store commissions, API fees, cloud services Apple App Store, Google Play, Amazon AWS, Shopify
💡 Penetration Pricing vs. Freemium — Key Distinction

Penetration pricing = discounting a product/service that will eventually be sold at full price — a time-limited strategy to build market share. Freemium = a permanent two-tier model where the basic version stays free forever, and the business model relies on converting a fraction of free users to premium paying users. Both are customer acquisition tools but with different long-term economics.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Business model = description of how a business works. Answers: Who (customers), What/Why (product), Where (channels), How Much (pricing), How (assets/capabilities/execution).
  • ✅ 3 key concepts: Value proposition (why customers choose this firm), Value chain (how the firm creates value internally), Pricing model (how customers are billed).
  • ✅ Analysts must NOT rely solely on management descriptions — management may overstate differentiation, market size, or platform development.
  • ✅ B2B: customers are businesses — straightforward, profit-motivated, smaller universe → direct sales feasible. B2C: consumers — diverse, emotional needs, large universe → advertising/mass channels needed.
  • ✅ Customer segmentation informs addressable market, unit and price forecasts, and competitive positioning.
  • ✅ Channels: Traditional (intermediary chain), Direct Sales (firm → end customer; high fixed cost but close relationship), Omnichannel (both digital + physical).
  • ✅ Commodity producers = price takers → must pursue cost leadership. Differentiated producers → have pricing power. Cost leaders: Costco, Lidl, Ryanair.
  • ✅ Price discrimination = charging different prices to different customers to maximize profit from varying willingness to pay. Net prices = list price adjusted for discounts/promotions.
  • ✅ 12 pricing models to know: Tiered · Dynamic (surge pricing) · Value-based · Auction · Bundling · Razor/razorblade · Add-on · Penetration · Freemium · Hidden revenue · Subscription · Leasing/licensing/franchising.
  • ✅ Razor/razorblade risk: generic consumable competitors can undercut the model by breaking the proprietary consumable lock-in.
  • ✅ Freemium goal: scale user base first; convert fraction of free users to premium paying users. Especially effective when marginal cost per user is near zero (digital services).
  • ✅ Hidden revenue model: users get service free; advertisers or third parties pay for access. Google, Facebook — users are the product, advertisers are the customers.
  • ✅ Total cost of ownership (TCO): aggregate direct + indirect costs of owning an asset over its lifespan — a common premium pricing justification (e.g., Tesla's lower fuel/maintenance costs).
  • ✅ Value chain (Porter, 1985): firm's own activities creating value. Primary: inbound logistics → operations → outbound logistics → marketing/sales → service. Support: procurement, HR, technology development, infrastructure.
  • ✅ Value chain ≠ supply chain. Supply chain = ALL steps across ALL firms to produce/deliver product. Value chain = only the one firm's activities.
  • ✅ Unit economics: revenues and costs expressed per unit. Important for evaluating scale effects and competitive barriers (AWS example: unit costs fall faster than prices → widening margins at scale).
  • ✅ Conventional models: Manufacturer, Distributor/Wholesaler, Retailer, Financial Services, Real Estate, Professional Services, Technology/Software, Transportation/Infrastructure.
  • ✅ Variations: Private label/contract manufacturer (Apple/NVIDIA model), Value-added reseller, Licensing, Franchise (royalty on franchisee sales).
  • ✅ Network effects: value of a network increases as more users join. Create barriers to entry and competitive advantage. Key metric: user count and growth rate.
  • ✅ One-sided: one user type (telephone, Venmo). Two-sided/multi-sided: multiple user types who create value for each other (Visa = merchants + cardholders; Airbnb = hosts + guests; food delivery = restaurants + drivers + customers).
  • ✅ Crowdsourcing: users contribute to the product (Wikipedia, TikTok, Amazon reviews) with minimal company oversight.
  • ✅ Digital technology impacts: location matters less, outsourcing easier, targeted digital marketing, more powerful network effects.
  • ✅ Penetration pricing = deliberate discounting to build scale/market share quickly — controversial if sustained (anti-competitive concerns).
Quick Reference — Pricing Model Cheat Sheet
TIERED → volume/feature tiers | DYNAMIC → time/demand varies (surge pricing)
VALUE-BASED → priced on customer benefit/savings | AUCTION → bidding (Google ads, eBay)
BUNDLING → complementary products together | RAZOR/BLADE → cheap equipment + costly consumables
ADD-ON → captive customer upsells | PENETRATION → deliberate discount to build scale
FREEMIUM → free basic + paid premium | HIDDEN REVENUE → free to user, third party pays
SUBSCRIPTION → recurring fees (SaaS, Netflix, utilities)
LEASE/LICENSE/FRANCHISE → property/IP/brand access rights (not ownership)
Know the definition, a real-world example, AND the key risk/limitation for each pricing model — all are testable.
