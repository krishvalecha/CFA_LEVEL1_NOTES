Why Derivatives? The Timing Gap Problem

Derivatives allow market participants to allocate, transfer, and/or manage risk without trading an underlying asset in the cash (spot) market. One of the most important reasons derivatives exist is to bridge a timing gap — the difference between when an economic decision must be made and when the actual cash market transaction can occur.

Issuer Timing Problems
  • Manufacturer: Must order commodity inputs NOW (before knowing finished-goods order volume) → needs to lock in input prices today for future delivery
  • Retailer: Awaiting a shipment priced in foreign currency → must decide when to sell domestic currency for payment BEFORE the shipment arrives
  • Debt issuer: Wants to lock in future debt costs BEFORE the existing debt matures → cannot wait for the cash market timing
Investor Timing Problems
  • Cash-constrained investor: Has a market view but lacks the immediate cash to execute in the spot market → derivatives require minimal upfront cash outlay
  • Reinvestment planning: Anticipates receiving future cash flows (dividends, coupon payments, principal repayments) and wants to decide how to reinvest them TODAY, before receiving the cash

A derivative instrument allows both issuers and investors to agree on a price today for a future transaction — effectively locking in the economics of the transaction now, even though the underlying cash exchange happens later. This is the fundamental economic rationale for derivatives: they allow the separation of economic decision-making from cash settlement timing.

Derivatives also enable the creation of exposure profiles unavailable in cash markets. For example, combining a long cash position with a sold call option creates a "covered call" profile — a strategy that would be difficult or expensive to replicate using only cash market instruments.

02

Four Key Benefits of Derivative Instruments

① Risk Allocation, Transfer, and Management
  • Allocate, trade, and/or manage underlying exposure without buying or selling the underlying in the cash market
  • Transfer risk across time and among counterparties willing to accept specific exposures
  • Create exposure profiles that are unavailable in cash markets (e.g., covered calls, collars, structured payoffs)
  • Bridge the timing gap between economic decisions and cash transactions
  • Isolate specific risks (e.g., FX risk from a foreign portfolio) while retaining others (equity market exposure)
  • Examples: FX forward to hedge commercial transaction, interest rate swap to fix floating debt cost, gold futures to speculate on commodity prices
② Information Discovery (Price Discovery)
  • Derivative prices deliver information about expected future prices and expected risk of the underlying
  • Futures prices provide a gauge of cash market direction (though NOT strictly an unbiased forecast of future spot prices)
  • Option prices (via implied volatility) reflect market's expectation of future price risk — a measure of uncertainty
  • Markets actively use derivatives for price discovery ahead of or between cash market trading hours
③ Operational Advantages
  • Lower transaction costs: Commodity derivatives eliminate transport, insurance, and storage costs of physical assets
  • Increased liquidity: Derivative markets are typically more liquid due to reduced capital requirements versus equivalent cash positions
  • Lower upfront cash requirements: Initial futures margin and option premiums are small fractions of full notional value → creates leverage
  • Easy short selling: Taking a short position via derivatives avoids the costly process of locating a cash owner willing to lend the underlying — short selling in cash markets requires borrowing the asset
④ Market Efficiency
  • When asset prices deviate from fundamental values, derivatives offer less costly ways to exploit mispricings
  • Lower capital requirements, lower transaction costs, easier short-selling → arbitrage is cheaper in derivative markets
  • Fundamental value is often restored in derivative markets before it is restored in the underlying cash market
  • Existence of derivative markets generally causes all financial markets to function more effectively (faster price discovery, tighter spreads)
★ Four Benefits — Quick Reference
① Risk Allocation/Transfer/Management → manage exposure without trading underlying
② Information Discovery → price discovery, implied volatility, futures as market indicators
③ Operational Advantages → lower costs, better liquidity, less cash needed, easy short sales
④ Market Efficiency → cheaper arbitrage → faster restoration of fundamental value
The exam often tests whether you can identify which benefit applies to a given scenario. The key distinction: ① is about risk management; ② is about information; ③ is about mechanics/costs; ④ is about market-level effects.
03

Price Discovery Function — Futures & Implied Volatility

Derivative markets serve an important price discovery function — they reveal information about expected future prices and expected risk that goes beyond what is available in spot markets alone.

Futures as Price Discovery Tools

MarketHow Futures Are Used for Price Discovery
Equity Markets Equity index futures prices (e.g., S&P 500 futures) are monitored BEFORE the stock market opens each day to gauge the expected direction of early cash market trading. A futures market that is open earlier or trades 24 hours provides real-time signals to equity investors.
Interest Rate Markets Interest rate futures (e.g., Fed funds futures, SOFR futures) are used by analysts to extract implied investor expectations about central bank benchmark rate changes at future policy meetings. Market-implied probability of a rate hike or cut is derived from futures pricing.
Commodity Markets Commodity futures prices across different maturities (the "forward curve") reveal supply and demand dynamics between producers, consumers, and investors. Contango (futures above spot) vs. backwardation (futures below spot) signals supply/demand balance.
⚠️ Futures Are NOT an Unbiased Forecast of Future Spot Prices

While market participants use futures prices to gauge the direction of cash markets, futures prices are NOT strictly an unbiased forecast of the future spot price. Futures prices incorporate: (1) the current spot price, (2) the cost of carry (financing, storage, insurance), (3) risk premiums (term premium, liquidity premium), and (4) supply/demand dynamics. These components mean a futures price systematically over- or under-estimates the expected future spot price depending on market conditions. The exam tests the distinction between "useful indicator" and "unbiased forecast."

Option Implied Volatility — Price Discovery for Risk

Implied Volatility
The market's expectation of future price risk (volatility) of the underlying, as derived from the current market price of an option and other known option features (strike, maturity, current underlying price, risk-free rate). Implied volatility represents the market's collective view of uncertainty in the underlying's future price. Higher implied volatility → higher option price (more uncertainty = more valuable right to exercise).
💡 Implied Volatility — The Market's Fear Gauge

The VIX index (CBOE Volatility Index) is the most famous measure of implied volatility — it measures the implied volatility of S&P 500 index options and is widely called "the fear gauge." A rising VIX means options are getting more expensive (market expects greater future uncertainty). Implied volatility is forward-looking — it tells you what the market EXPECTS going forward, whereas historical volatility tells you what happened in the past. The exam tests the concept and direction: higher option price → higher implied volatility (all else equal).

04

Operational Advantages — Detailed Analysis

AdvantageDetailExample
Lower Transaction Costs Commodity derivatives eliminate the physical costs of owning the underlying: no need to transport, insure, or store a physical asset. Bid-offer spreads in derivative markets are often tighter than in underlying markets. An investor who wants gold exposure buys gold futures rather than buying physical gold bars — avoids vault storage fees, insurance, transport costs, assay costs.
Increased Liquidity Derivative markets are typically MORE liquid than the underlying cash markets because less capital is needed to trade them. A larger number of market participants can participate, tightening bid-offer spreads and improving market depth. S&P 500 index futures are more liquid than buying all 500 component stocks. Interest rate futures markets often dwarf the underlying Treasury bond market in daily trading volume.
Low Upfront Cash Requirements Futures require only a small initial margin (fraction of full notional). Options require only the premium (often a small % of the underlying value). Both create exposure to the full notional value with far less cash upfront → implicit leverage. Procam deposits $4,950 initial margin to control a $179,213 notional gold futures contract — leverage ratio ≈ 36:1. An option buyer pays a $5 premium to control a $30 stock position — 6:1 leverage on a per-share basis.
Easy Short Selling In cash markets, short selling requires borrowing the asset from a willing owner and paying fees for the loan. If no owners are willing to lend, the short position is impossible. Derivatives make short selling trivial — simply sell a futures contract or buy a put option. Short-selling a thinly traded corporate bond is extremely difficult in the cash market. Buying CDS protection achieves the same short-credit exposure instantly, without needing to borrow the bond.
💡 Leverage is Both a Benefit AND a Risk

The low upfront cash requirement (benefit ③) is a double-edged sword. It creates implicit leverage — any percentage gain or loss on the small margin/premium is amplified relative to the full notional value. A 1% move in the underlying causes a much larger percentage move in the derivative position. The CFA exam tests this in both the "benefits" context (lower capital needed for same exposure) and the "risks" context (potential for amplified losses, margin calls, financial distress).

05

Six Key Risks of Derivative Instruments

① Greater Potential for Speculative Use (Implicit Leverage)
  • The low cash outlay of derivatives creates a high degree of implicit leverage versus an equivalent cash position
  • Small moves in the underlying produce proportionally large gains or losses on the derivative (relative to the cash invested)
  • Increases the likelihood of financial distress if the underlying moves adversely
  • Structured notes (debt + embedded derivative) may involve significant embedded leverage not immediately apparent to investors
  • Example: A futures position with 36:1 leverage means a 3% adverse move wipes out the entire initial margin
② Lack of Transparency
  • Derivatives can add significant portfolio complexity — the resulting exposure profile may not be well understood by stakeholders (investors, regulators, boards)
  • The risk increases with combinations of multiple derivatives or embedded derivatives (structured notes, structured products)
  • OTC derivatives are inherently less transparent than exchange-traded instruments
  • Structured notes: debt instruments with one or more embedded derivatives designed for specific issuer/investor objectives. May involve higher costs, lower liquidity, and less transparency than equivalent stand-alone derivatives
  • Complexity means risk exposures may be hidden or misunderstood until a crisis occurs
③ Basis Risk
  • The risk that a derivative's expected value diverges unexpectedly from that of the underlying or the hedged transaction
  • Arises when the derivative references a price or index that is similar but does not exactly match the underlying exposure
  • Examples of basis risk: hedging with a different-grade commodity than the one held; using a generic MRR when the loan references a different rate; CDS on an index instead of the specific issuer's bond
  • Affected by supply/demand dynamics in the derivative market itself (technical factors)
  • Hedger assumes the derivative will be highly effective in offsetting the underlying price risk — basis risk is the degree to which this assumption fails
④ Liquidity Risk
  • A divergence in cash flow timing between the derivative and the underlying/hedged transaction
  • The daily MTM settlement of futures creates liquidity risk: if a hedger cannot meet a margin call due to lack of funds, the position is forcibly closed and the investor must cover losses immediately
  • A hedger may have a correctly positioned hedge but lack immediate liquidity to meet margin calls during adverse price moves — forced to close at the worst time
  • The underlying hedged transaction may settle in the future while the derivative requires daily cash flows NOW
  • Less of a concern for OTC derivatives (negotiated credit terms) but can arise when collateral calls occur
⑤ Counterparty Credit Risk
  • Unlike loans/bonds (credit exposure = notional + accrued interest, predictable), derivative credit exposure is determined by daily swings in the underlying price — variable and harder to monitor
  • Credit exposure monitoring must be more frequent and dynamic than for fixed-income credit
  • Varies by derivative type: futures (low — daily MTM + clearinghouse guarantee), OTC (higher — bilateral, unless centrally cleared)
  • For contingent claims: one-sided credit risk (option buyer faces seller's credit risk; seller has no credit risk to buyer after premium is paid)
  • For firm commitments: bilateral credit risk — both counterparties can have mark-to-market exposure to each other
  • Mitigated by: daily MTM for ETDs, central clearing mandate for OTC swaps (post-2008), collateral agreements (ISDA credit support annex)
⑥ Destabilization and Systemic Risk
  • Excessive risk-taking and leverage through derivatives can contribute to market-wide stress and systemic risk
  • The 2008 global financial crisis highlighted how interconnected OTC derivative exposures (particularly CDS and mortgage-backed securities derivatives) could cascade across the financial system
  • Regulators continue to focus on financial innovation, leverage, and systemic risk monitoring
  • Post-2008 reforms: central clearing mandate for OTC swaps, margin requirements similar to futures, trade reporting requirements
  • Central clearing reduces bilateral counterparty risk but concentrates systemic risk in CCPs — which must have robust safeguards
★ Six Risks — Quick Reference
① Speculative/Leverage Risk → implicit leverage → amplified gains and losses
② Transparency Risk → complexity → stakeholders may misunderstand exposures
③ Basis Risk → derivative ≠ exactly match underlying → hedge effectiveness uncertainty
④ Liquidity Risk → cash flow timing mismatch → margin calls when funds aren't available
⑤ Counterparty Credit Risk → MTM exposure variable, monitoring more complex than bonds
⑥ Systemic Risk → excessive leverage/risk → market destabilization (2008 crisis)
Basis risk vs. liquidity risk: Basis risk = VALUE mismatch between derivative and underlying. Liquidity risk = TIMING mismatch between derivative cash flows and underlying cash flows. Both are "mismatch" risks but in different dimensions.
Counterparty credit risk (derivatives vs. bonds): Bond credit exposure = predictable (notional + accrued). Derivative credit exposure = variable (MTM swings daily with price changes).

Implicit Leverage — The Core Risk in Detail

The operational advantage of low upfront cash requirements directly translates to implicit leverage — the same dollar of capital controls a much larger notional exposure in derivatives than in cash markets. This leverage amplifies both gains AND losses proportionally.

Leverage Comparison — Futures vs. Cash Market (Gold Example)
Cash Market Purchase (No Borrowing):
Buy 100 oz gold at $1,770/oz → Outlay = $177,000
If gold rises to $1,780.50/oz → Gain = $1,050 → Return = $1,050/$177,000 = 0.59%

Futures Contract (Initial Margin = $4,950):
Same 100 oz exposure, f₀(T) = $1,792.13 → Cash outlay = $4,950 (initial margin only)
Net settlement = ($1,780.50 − $1,792.13) × 100 = −$1,163 loss
Return on margin = −$1,163/$4,950 = −23.5% (vs. −0.65% for cash purchase at same price)

Leverage ratio = Notional / Margin = $179,213 / $4,950 ≈ 36:1
→ A 1% move in gold price = ~36% move in the futures margin account value
💡 Leverage Works Both Ways — The Key Exam Concept

The futures leverage of ~36:1 means:
Positive scenario: Gold rises 1% → futures position gains ~36% on the margin deposited → dramatically outperforms the cash position.
Negative scenario: Gold falls 1% → futures position loses ~36% on the margin → far more severe than the cash position loss.
This is the essence of implicit leverage — it doesn't change the total dollar gain/loss (which is the same for both approaches on the same notional), but it dramatically changes the percentage return on capital deployed. The exam tests this amplification effect and the risk of margin calls forcing position closure at adverse prices.

⚠️ Liquidity Risk + Leverage = Forced Liquidation Risk

The combination of leverage (small margin) and daily MTM creates a dangerous scenario: an investor may be economically right about the long-term direction of the underlying but get forced out of the position in the short term due to an inability to meet margin calls. This forced liquidation — selling at the worst time — is a recurring theme in derivative market crises. Example: a hedger with a correctly positioned futures hedge may be forced to close out due to margin calls, leaving their underlying exposure unhedged at exactly the wrong moment.

Issuer Use of Derivatives

Non-financial corporate issuers typically face risks to their assets, liabilities, and earnings as a result of changes in the price of an underlying. These risks arise from their normal commercial operations and financing activities — not from a desire to speculate.

Primary motivation: To offset or hedge market-based exposures, reduce earnings volatility, and stabilize cash flows. Unlike investors, issuers are primarily focused on hedge accounting treatment — ensuring that derivative gains and losses are recognized at the same time as the underlying hedged transaction, minimizing income statement and cash flow volatility.

Why Issuers Hedge — The Core Problem
  • Commodity input price volatility → unpredictable earnings → harder for investors to forecast future EPS
  • FX volatility → translates foreign currency revenues/costs into unpredictable domestic currency amounts
  • Interest rate volatility → floating-rate debt creates variable cash outflows → harder to plan
  • Higher earnings volatility → perceived higher risk → potentially higher cost of capital/borrowing
  • Hedging with derivatives reduces all these effects → smooths earnings → reduces uncertainty → potentially lowers cost of capital

Derivatives accounting evolution: Derivatives historically had off-balance-sheet treatment — not shown as assets/liabilities. Post-reform, derivatives must be reported on the balance sheet at fair market value. This increases transparency and disclosure of derivative use, aligning with the principle of economic reality in financial reporting.

Default treatment (no hedge designation): Any derivative must be marked to market through the income statement via earnings — creating quarterly earnings volatility as the derivative's fair value fluctuates, even if it perfectly hedges the underlying.

Solution: Hedge accounting. If an issuer meets specific criteria, it can designate the derivative as a hedge and defer MTM changes to Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) — an equity account — releasing them into earnings only when the underlying hedged transaction is recognized. This eliminates the income statement volatility from timing mismatches.

💡 Why OTC Derivatives Are Preferred by Issuers (for Hedging)

To qualify for hedge accounting, the derivative's dates, notional amounts, and other contract features must closely match those of the underlying hedged transaction. Standardized exchange-traded contracts almost never match an issuer's specific exposure perfectly (e.g., a 75-day forward matches a specific commercial contract; a standardized 2-month futures contract does not). For this reason, issuers are FAR MORE likely to use OTC markets for customized hedges that can be tailored to exactly match the hedged transaction — enabling hedge accounting qualification.

08

Hedge Accounting — Three Designation Types

Hedge Accounting
An accounting treatment that allows an issuer to offset a hedging instrument (usually a derivative) against a hedged transaction or balance sheet item, reducing financial statement volatility. Derivative MTM changes are held in Other Comprehensive Income (OCI — an equity account) and released into earnings at the same time as the underlying hedged transaction — aligning the timing of recognition.
Hedge TypeDefinitionExamplesDerivative Types Used
Cash Flow Hedge Absorbs the variable cash flow of a floating-rate asset or liability (or a forecasted transaction). Offsets variability in cash flows, not fair value. 1. Interest rate swap converting floating-rate debt to fixed-rate → hedges variable interest payments
2. FX forward to hedge forecasted foreign currency sales (like Montau AG's KRW→EUR example)
3. Commodity forward to lock in input prices for future raw material purchases		 Forward commitments OR contingent claims (both qualify)
Fair Value Hedge Offsets the fluctuation in fair value of an existing asset or liability on the balance sheet. The hedged item's fair value change is also recognized in earnings (offsetting the derivative). 1. Interest rate swap receiving fixed rate and paying MRR → converts fixed-rate bond to floating (hedges fair value of the bond against rate changes)
2. Commodity futures contract to hedge the fair value of existing physical inventory against price declines
3. Fixed-to-floating rate swap on a fixed-rate debt issuance		 Mostly forward commitments (swaps, futures, forwards)
Net Investment Hedge Designated to offset the FX risk of the equity of a foreign operation. Protects the issuer's reported equity value from being eroded by exchange rate movements when translating a foreign subsidiary's equity into the reporting currency. 1. Currency swap to hedge the equity of a foreign subsidiary
2. Currency forward to offset FX risk on a foreign operation's equity
3. Foreign currency-denominated debt issued by the parent company (non-derivative hedge)		 Currency swaps, FX forwards, or foreign currency debt
★ Three Hedge Types — Memory Hook
CASH FLOW HEDGE: Hedges VARIABLE CASH FLOWS of floating-rate or forecasted transactions
→ "Lock in" a future rate/price | OCI treatment | Forward commitments OR options
Examples: floating→fixed swap, FX forward on forecasted sales

FAIR VALUE HEDGE: Hedges FAIR VALUE CHANGES of existing fixed-rate assets/liabilities
→ Converts existing fixed exposure to floating | Both derivative and hedged item marked to earnings
Examples: fixed→floating swap on a fixed-rate bond; inventory forward hedge

NET INVESTMENT HEDGE: Hedges FX RISK on equity of a foreign subsidiary
→ Protects equity translation from foreign currency movements
Examples: currency swap, FX forward, or FX-denominated debt issued by parent
Key confusion: Cash flow hedge (floating→fixed swap) vs. fair value hedge (fixed→floating swap) — opposite directions! The interest rate swap type depends on whether you want to stabilize cash flows (you have floating debt, go fixed = cash flow hedge) or stabilize fair value (you have fixed-rate debt, go floating = fair value hedge — because floating-rate bonds don't change in value when rates change, unlike fixed-rate bonds).
Why OCI? Hedge accounting stores derivative MTM changes in OCI (equity section) until the hedged transaction is recognized in earnings — preventing "phantom" earnings volatility from the derivative alone.
⚠️ Qualifying for Hedge Accounting — The Key Requirement

Not all derivatives automatically qualify for hedge accounting. To qualify, the issuer must document: (1) the hedging relationship, (2) the risk management objective, and (3) the strategy. The derivative must be highly effective in offsetting the designated risk of the hedged item — meaning the derivative's critical terms (notional, maturity, payment dates, underlying) must closely match the hedged transaction. This is why corporate issuers overwhelmingly prefer customized OTC derivatives for hedging — standardized ETD contracts rarely match the specific terms needed for hedge accounting qualification.

Investor Use of Derivatives

Investors use derivatives differently from issuers. While issuers primarily hedge commercial exposures, investors use derivatives to achieve investment objectives — often through strategies that would be impossible, too expensive, or too capital-intensive in cash markets alone.

Three Primary Investor Uses of Derivatives
  • Replicate cash market strategies more cheaply/efficiently: Use derivatives to get the same economic exposure as a cash market purchase but with less capital, lower costs, and faster execution. Example: Procam uses gold futures instead of buying physical gold — same exposure, far less cash outlay, no storage/insurance costs.
  • Hedge existing portfolio exposures: Isolate specific risks while retaining others. Example: Use FX forwards to hedge currency risk when investing in overseas equities — retain equity market exposure while eliminating (or reducing) currency exposure. Example: Buy CDS to hedge credit exposure without selling the bonds.
  • Modify or create exposures unavailable in cash markets: Use derivatives to create payoff profiles that cannot be replicated in spot markets alone. Example: Covered call (long stock + short call) → generates extra income in stable/slightly rising markets. Example: Use equity index swaps to rotate exposure from one sector to another instantly, without trading individual stocks.

💡 Why Investors Don't Need Hedge Accounting

An investment fund's derivative positions are typically marked to market daily and included in the fund's net asset value (NAV). Unlike corporate issuers who need to match derivative gains/losses to specific underlying transactions for accounting purposes, investors report everything at fair value on a daily basis anyway. There is no timing mismatch problem to solve through hedge accounting — the derivative's P&L hits NAV immediately. This is a major reason why investors use derivatives differently from issuers and are less focused on the OTC vs. ETD distinction — they can use more standardized, liquid ETD markets freely.

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Core rationale for derivatives: Bridge the timing gap between economic decisions and cash market transactions. Allow risk allocation, transfer, and management without trading the underlying asset.
  • ✅ Benefit ①: Risk Allocation/Transfer/Management. Allocate, trade, manage exposure without trading underlying. Transfer risk across time and counterparties. Create exposures unavailable in cash markets (e.g., covered calls, non-linear payoffs).
  • ✅ Benefit ②: Price Discovery (Information). Futures prices → direction of cash markets (equity index futures pre-open, rate futures for central bank expectations, commodity futures for supply/demand). Option implied volatility → market's expected future price risk. BUT: futures prices are NOT strictly unbiased forecasts of future spot prices.
  • ✅ Implied volatility = expected future price risk derived from current option prices. Higher implied vol = higher option price = more market uncertainty. Important price discovery function of options markets.
  • ✅ Benefit ③: Operational Advantages. Lower transaction costs (no storage/transport for commodity derivatives). More liquid markets. Low upfront cash (initial margin / premium = fraction of notional). Easy short selling (no need to borrow the underlying).
  • ✅ Benefit ④: Market Efficiency. Derivatives make exploiting mispricings cheaper → arbitrage corrects prices faster → derivative markets often reflect fundamental value before cash markets do.
  • ✅ Risk ①: Leverage/Speculation. Low cash outlay = implicit leverage. Small adverse moves wipe out the margin deposited. Amplifies losses proportionally. Structured notes may embed hidden leverage.
  • ✅ Risk ②: Transparency. Derivative complexity may create exposures not understood by stakeholders. OTC derivatives are less transparent. Structured notes (debt + embedded derivative) add layers of complexity.
  • ✅ Risk ③: Basis Risk. VALUE mismatch — derivative references an index/rate similar to, but not exactly matching, the hedged underlying. Hedge effectiveness is uncertain. Examples: different MRR, index CDS vs. specific issuer's bond.
  • ✅ Risk ④: Liquidity Risk. TIMING mismatch — derivative cash flows (daily MTM margin calls) don't match the hedged transaction's cash flows. A correctly positioned hedger may be forced to close due to inability to meet margin calls despite being economically right long-term.
  • ✅ Risk ⑤: Counterparty Credit Risk. Different from bond credit risk (predictable notional + accrued). Derivative credit exposure varies daily with MTM swings. Requires more frequent monitoring. ETDs mitigate via daily MTM + clearinghouse guarantee. OTC mitigated by central clearing post-2008.
  • ✅ Risk ⑥: Systemic Risk. Excessive leverage/risk in derivatives contributed to 2008 financial crisis. CCPs introduced to reduce bilateral risks — but concentrate systemic risk in CCPs (must have robust safeguards).
  • ✅ Issuers primarily hedge commercial and financing exposures — FX (commercial transactions), interest rates (debt costs), commodities (input costs). Goal: reduce earnings/cash flow volatility, stabilize financial statements.
  • ✅ Issuers seek hedge accounting treatment — allows MTM changes to flow through OCI (equity account) instead of P&L, releasing into earnings when the hedged transaction is recognized. Eliminates "phantom" earnings volatility.
  • ✅ Issuers prefer OTC derivatives because customized terms (exact maturity, notional, payment dates) are needed to match the hedged transaction and qualify for hedge accounting. Standardized ETD contracts rarely match precisely enough.
  • ✅ Three hedge accounting types: (1) Cash Flow Hedge: hedges variable cash flows of floating-rate/forecasted transactions (e.g., floating→fixed swap, FX forward on forecasted sales). (2) Fair Value Hedge: hedges fair value changes of existing fixed-rate assets/liabilities (e.g., fixed→floating swap on a fixed-rate bond). (3) Net Investment Hedge: hedges FX risk on equity of a foreign subsidiary (currency swap/forward).
  • ✅ Cash flow hedge vs. fair value hedge direction: If you have FLOATING debt and swap to FIXED → cash flow hedge (stabilizing cash flows). If you have FIXED-RATE debt and swap to FLOATING → fair value hedge (stabilizing fair value of the liability).
  • ✅ Investors use derivatives for: (1) Replicating cash market strategies cheaply (Procam: gold futures = gold exposure with minimal cash). (2) Hedging portfolio exposures (FX forwards to hedge overseas equity positions). (3) Creating/modifying exposures unavailable in cash markets (covered calls, interest rate duration changes via swaps).
  • ✅ Investors don't need hedge accounting — daily MTM in fund NAV already captures all derivative gains/losses. No timing mismatch to solve. This is why investors prefer ETD markets (more liquid, standardized, cheaper) over OTC.
  • ✅ Investment fund prospectus specifies which derivatives may be used and for what purpose — investors are constrained by their mandate.

① Risk Allocation/Transfer/Management: manage exposure without trading underlying
② Information/Price Discovery: futures = market direction; implied vol = price risk
③ Operational Advantages: lower costs, liquidity, low cash, easy short selling
④ Market Efficiency: cheaper arbitrage → faster price correction

═══ SIX DERIVATIVE RISKS ═══
① Leverage/Speculation: implicit leverage → amplified gains AND losses
② Transparency: complexity → stakeholders may misunderstand exposures
③ Basis Risk: VALUE mismatch (derivative ≠ exact underlying match)
④ Liquidity Risk: TIMING mismatch (derivative cash flows ≠ hedged transaction timing)
⑤ Counterparty Credit Risk: MTM-variable exposure, complex to monitor
⑥ Systemic Risk: excessive leverage → market destabilization (2008 crisis)

═══ ISSUERS ═══
Hedge commercial/financing exposures → reduce earnings/cash flow volatility
Seek hedge accounting (MTM to OCI, not P&L)
Prefer OTC (customized terms needed for hedge accounting qualification)
Three hedge types: Cash Flow | Fair Value | Net Investment

═══ INVESTORS ═══
Replicate cash market positions cheaply → hedge portfolio → create new exposures
No hedge accounting needed (daily NAV MTM handles it automatically)
Prefer ETD (more liquid, standardized, lower cost)
May be constrained by fund prospectus on permitted derivative use
Cash flow hedge (floating→fixed) vs. fair value hedge (fixed→floating): The swap direction determines the designation. Floating→fixed = stabilize cash flows (CF hedge). Fixed→floating = stabilize fair value (FV hedge).
Basis risk vs. liquidity risk: Both are "mismatch" risks. Basis = VALUE mismatch. Liquidity = TIMING mismatch in cash flows.
Futures price ≠ unbiased forecast of future spot price. Contains cost of carry, risk premiums, supply/demand — not just the "expected" future spot.
OCI (Other Comprehensive Income) = the equity account where hedge accounting MTM changes are stored until the hedged item is recognized in earnings.
