01

Types of Capital Investments

Capital Investment (Capital Project)
An investment with a life of one year or longer, typically appearing on the balance sheet as a long-term asset. Not limited to tangible assets (property, equipment) — increasingly includes digital capabilities and other intangible assets. All principles apply regardless of asset type because the focus is on cash flows.
Category Purpose Examples Risk Level Key Characteristic
Going Concern (Maintenance) Maintain existing operations / current size Replacing aging equipment, maintaining IT hardware/software, facility improvements Lower Involves replication of existing operations; easier to evaluate; match funding used
Regulatory / Compliance Meet required rules and standards Pollution reduction equipment, bank reporting systems for regulators Low–Moderate Non-discretionary; usually increases costs with no added revenue; can act as barrier to entry for incumbents
Expansion of Existing Business Increase scale / scope of existing operations New production facility, entering new region, adjacent product launch, pharma R&D, energy exploration Moderate Greater uncertainty, time, and capital than going concern; execution risks; early-stage firms use equity
New Lines of Business / Other Enter entirely new or unrelated business areas Startup-like R&D, acquisition in new industry, novel business model Highest Unfamiliar business; risk of overpaying; unforeseen challenges; special situation opportunities

Going Concern — Key Details

  • Match funding principle: Finance long-term assets with long-term financing (e.g., 30-year bond for 30-year equipment). Funding long-term assets with short-term debt → rollover risk (uncertain financing cost or availability before asset life ends).
  • Maintenance Capex estimation: Issuers do NOT have to disclose the breakdown between maintenance and expansion capex. Analysts typically estimate: Maintenance Capex ≈ Depreciation & Amortization expense. More accurate for shorter-lived assets.
  • Expansion Capex ≈ Total Capex − Depreciation & Amortization

Regulatory / Compliance — Key Details

  • Increases expenses but often adds no revenue — required to avoid fines or continue operations
  • Silver lining: Can act as a barrier to entry for new competitors → protects incumbent profitability
  • Firms working directly with regulators may tailor timing/impact to their advantage
  • Some costs can be passed on to end users as higher prices; otherwise, may make a business unit unviable → potential divestiture
  • Early adoption of new rules can reduce future uncertainty and create competitive advantage
💡 Analyst Use — Segmenting Capex

By subtracting D&A from total capex (disclosed on the cash flow statement), analysts can estimate expansion capex and assess management's growth priorities and investment trends. Examine the level AND trend of expansion capex by segment if disclosed — rising capex with falling returns on investment is a red flag for capital misallocation.

02

Accounting Treatment of Capital Investments

How Capital Investments Flow Through Financial Statements
At purchase → Balance Sheet: recorded at COST as Long-term Asset
Income Statement: NOT expensed immediately → instead, DEPRECIATION/AMORTIZATION recorded periodically over useful life
Cash Flow Statement: Full cash outflow recorded AS INCURRED (in investing activities)

Over time → Balance Sheet: shown at NET basis = Cost − Accumulated Depreciation
Net value declines to ZERO (or salvage value) over the asset's useful life
This "smoothing" effect means capital spending impacts the income statement gradually (via D&A) but hits cash flows immediately. This is why analysts use FCF and ROIC — not just net income — to assess capital efficiency.
⚠ Exam Trap — Capex Is NOT on the Income Statement

Capital expenditure is capitalized on the balance sheet and then depreciated over time — it is NOT expensed on the income statement when incurred. Only the periodic depreciation charge flows through the income statement. This is a fundamental accounting distinction tested early and often.

03

The Capital Allocation Process — 4 Steps

Capital allocation = the process used by management and the board to make capital investment and return decisions. The goal: deliver risk-adjusted returns greater than what investors could earn on similarly risky investments elsewhere (i.e., exceed the opportunity cost of capital).

When value-creative opportunities are exhausted → return remaining capital to shareholders so they can redeploy it at their required rate of return.

Step What Happens Key Points
1. Idea Generation Investment ideas originate (anywhere — managers, consultants, strategic review) Requires deep understanding of competitive environment, firm capabilities. Ideas often expand existing scale/scope. Capital spending is highly year-to-year correlated (0.92 correlation per McKinsey study) → inertia bias.
2. Investment Analysis Forecast amount, timing, duration, and volatility of expected cash flows; evaluate using NPV, IRR, ROIC Management uses non-public project-level data; analysts use aggregated financials (ROIC). NPV and IRR are key tools at this stage.
3. Planning and Prioritization Select and prioritize investments that are most value-enhancing on a risk-adjusted basis Only pursue investments with expected returns > investors' opportunity cost. Some projects attractive in isolation may be less desirable in context of existing operations or other projects.
4. Monitoring and Post-Investment Review Track actual performance vs. projections; make adjustments (real options) Reveals systematic forecasting errors; enforces operational discipline; generates future investment ideas. Scale down or divest underperforming assets. Most firms do this poorly — one of the biggest sources of long-term value destruction.
💡 Analyst Implication — Track Record Is Everything

Analysts cannot access project-level NPV/IRR data. Instead, they must judge whether an issuer will manage capital wisely over the long term by: (1) evaluating the capital allocation process itself and adherence to first principles, and (2) assessing the historical track record of capital allocation (ROIC vs. required return over multiple years).

04

Net Present Value (NPV)

Net Present Value (NPV)
The present value (in currency terms) of all expected future after-tax cash inflows from a project, less the investment's costs (cash outflows). It measures the amount by which investors' wealth increases from the investment.
★ NPV Formula
NPV = CF₀ + CF₁/(1+r)¹ + CF₂/(1+r)² + ... + CFₜ/(1+r)ᵀ

NPV = Σ [CFₜ / (1+r)ᵀ] for t = 0 to T
CFₜ = After-tax cash flow at time t (CF₀ is typically negative = initial outlay)
r = Required rate of return = rate investors could earn on a similarly risky investment
Positive cash flows in later periods are discounted back to present value
NPV is expressed in currency (e.g., USD, EUR) — it is an absolute, not relative, measure
INVEST if NPV ≥ 0
Positive NPV → increases shareholder wealth
NPV = 0 → meets required return (acceptable, but no margin for error)
DO NOT INVEST if NPV < 0
Negative NPV → destroys shareholder wealth

Key NPV properties and nuances:

  • NPV is a necessary but not sufficient condition — there may be competing projects, intangible factors, strategic considerations
  • Assumes interim cash flows reinvested at r (the required rate of return) — more economically realistic than IRR's assumption
  • Unconventional cash flows (multiple outflows at different points) → use XNPV function in Excel/Sheets (does not assume evenly spaced periods)
  • NPV directly measures wealth creation in absolute currency terms — theoretically the most sound metric
⚠ Exam Trap — NPV = 0 Is Acceptable

A zero-NPV project theoretically meets the required rate of return and can be accepted — but it leaves no margin for forecasting error. In practice, firms want positive NPV cushion. Do not confuse "NPV = 0 → reject" with "NPV < 0 → reject." Only strictly negative NPV projects should be rejected.

05

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
The discount rate that makes the NPV of an investment equal to zero. It is an estimate of the project's rate of return (subject to the reinvestment assumption). The required rate of return used as the comparison is called the hurdle rate.
★ IRR Formula (Solve for IRR where NPV = 0)
Σ [CFₜ / (1+IRR)ᵀ] = 0, for t = 0 to T
IRR cannot typically be solved algebraically — requires financial calculator, trial-and-error, or spreadsheet (IRR/XIRR functions). XIRR used when cash flows are not evenly spaced.
INVEST if IRR ≥ r
IRR exceeds hurdle rate → project earns more than required return
DO NOT INVEST if IRR < r
Project does not meet the required rate of return

Critical IRR Limitations

1. Reinvestment Rate Assumption

IRR assumes interim cash flows are reinvested at the IRR itself. If actual reinvestment rates are lower, the true return will be lower than IRR suggests. NPV's assumption (reinvest at r) is more economically realistic.

2. Multiple IRRs

If cash flow signs change more than once (e.g., negative → positive → negative), multiple valid IRRs exist. Financial calculators/spreadsheets default to lowest value — misleading. In such cases, use NPV instead.

Multiple IRR Example — Cash Flow Sign Changes Twice
t=0: −1,000 | t=1: +5,000 | t=2: −6,000

Two valid IRRs: 100% and 200% — both satisfy the IRR equation
→ Cannot use IRR reliably; must use NPV instead
Whenever you see cash flows going from negative to positive to negative again (or any pattern with multiple sign changes), default to NPV for the investment decision.
💡 Why Practitioners Like IRR

IRR is expressed as a percentage — easier to communicate. "The project returns 19.5% vs. our 10% hurdle rate" is more intuitive than "NPV = $42,000." But despite its communication advantage, NPV is theoretically superior. In practice: compute both, use NPV for the decision.

06

NPV vs. IRR — When They Conflict

NPV and IRR usually give the same accept/reject decision. BUT for mutually exclusive projects (can only choose one), they can give conflicting rankings.

Example: Project A has NPV = $500K, IRR = 12%. Project B has NPV = $300K, IRR = 18%. Hurdle rate = 10%.

IRR says: choose B (higher %). NPV says: choose A (adds more wealth). Choose A — NPV is correct.

Dimension NPV IRR
What it measures Absolute wealth increase (currency) Rate of return on the project (%)
Decision rule Invest if NPV ≥ 0 Invest if IRR ≥ hurdle rate (r)
Reinvestment assumption Interim flows reinvested at r (more realistic) Interim flows reinvested at IRR (often unrealistic)
Multiple sign changes Handles correctly Multiple IRRs possible → unreliable
Mutually exclusive projects Always choose higher NPV May conflict with NPV — do NOT rely on IRR
Ease of communication Less intuitive (currency amount) More intuitive (% rate)
Theoretical soundness Theoretically superior Useful but with key limitations
Analyst access Not calculable from public data (project-level) Not calculable from public data (project-level)
⚠ CRITICAL — When NPV and IRR Conflict, Always Use NPV

This is one of the most tested points in this module. For mutually exclusive projects, NPV is always the correct decision criterion. IRR ignores the scale of investment and the period over which returns are earned. A small project with a very high IRR can appear more attractive than a large project with a slightly lower IRR but far greater wealth creation.

07

Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)

ROIC (Return on Invested Capital / Return on Capital Employed / ROCE)
A company-wide profitability measure showing the rate of return the issuer earns across all its investments. Measured annually. Can be calculated using publicly available financial data — making it the key metric available to independent analysts (unlike project-level NPV/IRR).
★ ROIC Formula
ROIC = After-tax Operating Profit / Average Invested Capital

= [(1 − Tax rate) × Operating Profit] / Average Total LT Liabilities and Equity (t−1, t)

Decomposed:
ROIC = (After-tax Operating Profit / Sales) × (Sales / Average Invested Capital)
       = After-tax Operating Profit Margin × Capital Turnover
Invested Capital = Long-term liabilities + Equity (excludes short-term liabilities/working capital)
From balance sheet: Invested Capital = Short-term Assets + Long-term Assets (less Short-term Liabilities)
Use AVERAGE invested capital (average of beginning and ending balance)
💡 ROIC Decomposition — Two Paths to Value Creation

ROIC = Profit Margin × Capital Turnover. A firm can achieve high ROIC through high margins (e.g., luxury brands) OR high turnover (e.g., discount retailers like Walmart). A high-margin firm with low turnover may have low ROIC. A low-margin firm with very high turnover may have excellent ROIC. This decomposition is powerful for comparing business models.

ROIC Decision Rule

ROIC > Required Return
Issuer is creating value for investors — good capital steward
ROIC < Required Return
Investors would have been better off elsewhere → improve margins/turnover, divest, or return capital

Required return for ROIC comparison: ROIC measures returns to both debt AND equity investors. Compare to a blended required return (WACC) — not just equity's required return (which would overestimate the benchmark). Covered in the next module.

ROIC Advantages vs. Limitations

ROIC Advantages
  • Calculable from public financial data (unlike project NPV/IRR)
  • Accounts for the capital needed to generate returns (unlike operating profit margin alone)
  • Company-wide aggregate → reflects management's overall capital stewardship
  • Can be compared directly to required rate of return (WACC)
  • Decomposes into margin × turnover → reveals business model drivers
ROIC Limitations
  • Accounting-based, not cash-based — operating profit ≠ cash flows; D&A vs. capex timing differences distort
  • Backward-looking — can be volatile year-to-year; profitable investments may take years to earn competitive returns
  • Highly aggregated — may mask profitable OR unprofitable business segments
  • Less consensus on measurement (especially denominator — treatment of intangibles, excess cash, pension liabilities varies)
08

Capital Allocation Principles

Even with the right tools, capital allocation can fail if key principles are violated. Three fundamental principles must be followed when modeling investment cash flows:

1. After-Tax Cash Flows

Evaluate investments based on after-tax cash flows, not accounting profits. Must include tax benefits from non-cash deductions like depreciation and amortization (D&A creates a tax shield — reduces taxable income → preserves cash).

2. Incremental Cash Flows Only — But Broadly

Ignore sunk costs (already incurred — irrelevant to future decisions). Include only incremental cash flows. BUT examine broadly — capital investments affect the rest of the firm: include positive effects (cost savings) AND negative effects (cannibalization of existing product sales).

3. Timing of Cash Flows

The timing of cash flows matters significantly. NPV and IRR can change substantially when cash flows are moved from one period to another. Must carefully forecast the timing, duration, and volatility of all expected flows.

Key Principle Summary
✓ Use after-tax cash flows (not accounting income)
✓ Ignore sunk costs (already spent — cannot be recovered)
✓ Include ALL incremental effects: positive (savings) AND negative (cannibalization)
✓ Timing matters — when cash flows occur changes NPV and IRR significantly
✓ Nominal CF discounted at nominal rate; real CF discounted at real rate (must be consistent)
⚠ Sunk Cost Trap — The Most Common Mistake

Sunk costs are costs already incurred and unrecoverable. They must be completely ignored in investment decisions. The classic trap: "We've already spent $5M on this project, so we must continue." Wrong. The only question is: do the remaining incremental cash flows justify the remaining incremental investment? Past spending is irrelevant.

Capital Allocation Pitfalls — Cognitive Errors

Cognitive errors = calculation mistakes and analytical errors (as opposed to behavioral biases, which are emotional/psychological). Three key cognitive errors in capital allocation:

1. Internal Forecasting Errors

  • Errors in cost estimates, required return inputs, or other forecasts
  • Overhead costs (management time, IT support, financial systems) are especially difficult to estimate
  • Firms often fail to incorporate competitor responses into the analysis of planned investments
  • Hard for external analysts to identify — but will manifest as failed or underperforming investments over time

2. Ignoring the Cost of Internal Financing

  • Primary source of large corporate capex = internally generated cash flows (operating cash flows)
  • Many management teams treat internally generated funds as "free" → allocate them via budgets tied closely to prior-year amounts
  • External financing (debt/equity) treated as "expensive" → used only for larger investments
  • This is flawed logic. Internally generated cash is equity financing — it could be returned to shareholders as dividends. By retaining it, management incurs shareholders' opportunity cost. All capital has a cost.
  • Use appropriate risk-adjusted required returns regardless of financing source

3. Inconsistent Treatment of Inflation

  • Analysis can be done in nominal (includes inflation) or real (removes inflation) terms — but must be consistent
  • Nominal cash flows discounted at nominal rate; Real cash flows discounted at real rate
  • Mixing (e.g., real cash flows with nominal discount rate) produces incorrect NPV
  • Inflation may affect sales prices and input costs differently — if actual inflation differs from expected, after-tax cash flows will differ from projections
Inflation Consistency Rule
Nominal CF → discount at Nominal rate
Real CF → discount at Real rate

Approximate: Real rate ≈ Nominal rate − Inflation rate
Exact: (1 + Nominal rate) = (1 + Real rate) × (1 + Inflation rate)
10

Capital Allocation Pitfalls — Behavioral Biases

Behavioral biases = errors in judgment, blind spots, and emotional/psychological distortions (as opposed to calculation mistakes). Four key behavioral biases in capital allocation:

Bias Description How Analysts Detect It
Inertia Capital budgets anchored to prior-year amounts (0.92 correlation year-to-year per McKinsey study of 1,600+ US companies). Management continues investing in the same areas regardless of changing returns. Compare capex by segment/year to prior year AND returns on investment. Rising/static capex + falling ROI = red flag requiring management justification.
Accounting Measure Focus (EPS-Chasing) Managers optimize for short-term accounting metrics (EPS, net income, ROE) rather than long-term NPV. High-NPV investments can reduce near-term EPS (via D&A); cost cuts and buybacks can boost EPS immediately. Examine management compensation structure (incentives to boost EPS). Compare capex to historical/peer levels — unusually low capex may signal accounting-metric-driven decision-making.
Pet Project Bias Favored projects receive preferential treatment without rigorous analysis, or analysis is conducted with overly optimistic projections to make the project appear profitable. Examine governance: controlled companies, concentrated ownership, weak board oversight, compensation not aligned with shareholder interests → all increase pet project risk. Hard to detect from financials alone due to aggregation.
Failure to Consider Alternatives / Scenarios Many viable alternatives never considered; different scenarios (optimistic, base, pessimistic) ignored. No breakeven, scenario, or simulation analysis. Lack of failure experience → management may not be taking enough risk. Assess whether management conducts breakeven and scenario analysis. Check if company has made divestitures or acquisitions (breadth of experience). A track record with no failures may signal insufficient risk-taking.
💡 Inertia — The McKinsey Finding

A study of 1,600+ US listed companies found a 0.92 correlation between capital investment levels from one year to the next. This remarkably high correlation suggests that most firms don't fundamentally rethink their capital allocation annually — they simply anchor to last year's budget. This is one of the largest sources of long-term value destruction in corporate finance.

💡 No Failures ≠ Good Management

A management team with no failed investments may actually be taking too little risk — avoiding bold bets that could create substantial shareholder value. Moderate calculated risk-taking with a disciplined post-investment review is healthier than pure loss avoidance.

Real Options

Real Options
Rights (but NOT obligations) for management to alter aspects of a capital project in the future, contingent on future economic events or information. Similar to financial options — valuable because they provide flexibility. A company should only exercise a real option if it is value-enhancing.

Real options recognize that capital allocation is often a series of decisions, not a single all-or-nothing commitment. Some decisions are made at inception; others can be postponed and based on future information. Real options can significantly increase project NPV by adding flexibility value.

4 Types of Real Options

Real Option Type Description Exercise When... Example
Timing Option Delay the investment decision to obtain better information. Investing in a project can create the option to make future investments (sequential investment). Future information improves NPV confidence; defer when uncertain. Waiting to build a factory until demand trends are clearer. Phase 1 investment creates the right (not obligation) to proceed to Phase 2.
Sizing Option — Abandonment Abandon (exit) the investment if financial results disappoint. Exercise when cash flows from abandoning exceed the PV of continuing. PV(cash from abandonment) > PV(cash flows from continuing) Shut down a mine if commodity prices fall below break-even. Terminate a drug development program after failed Phase 2 trials.
Sizing Option — Growth/Expansion Make additional investments if financial results are strong (i.e., scale up). The initial investment creates the option to expand. Future results exceed expectations → exercise the growth option. Build a pilot plant, then expand to full-scale production if the pilot succeeds. A successful mobile app → invest in international markets.
Flexibility Option Operational flexibility post-investment: pricing options (raise prices when demand exceeds capacity) or production flexibility (add overtime, extra shifts, change input mix). Demand exceeds capacity (price-setting); demand varies from forecast (production). Raise hotel room prices during peak season. A multi-fuel power plant can switch between gas and oil depending on relative prices.
Fundamental Option The entire investment value depends on an external variable (often a commodity price) outside the firm's control. Commodity/underlying asset price makes the investment viable. Oil well value depends on oil prices — drill only if prices are high enough. Gold mine value depends on gold prices. R&D value depends on whether a discovery is made.

Valuing Projects with Real Options — 3 Approaches

  1. Ignore options, accept if NPV > 0: If the project has positive NPV even without considering options, AND it has real options that could further increase value → the NPV is a minimum return. Safe to accept.
  2. NPV with options formula:
    Project NPV = NPV (without options) − Option Cost + Option Value
    Add option value, subtract option cost. Accept if resulting project NPV > 0.
  3. Decision trees and option pricing models: Assign probabilities and expected timing to future outcomes → calculate probability-weighted NPV across all scenarios. More complex but more rigorous when future sequential decisions matter.
💡 Memory Framework — Real Options Add Value = "TSGFF"

Timing · Sizing (abandon) · Growth (expand) · Flexibility · Fundamental. Each option type adds flexibility in a different dimension: when to invest (timing), whether to exit (abandon), whether to scale (growth), how to operate (flexibility), or whether to proceed at all (fundamental).
