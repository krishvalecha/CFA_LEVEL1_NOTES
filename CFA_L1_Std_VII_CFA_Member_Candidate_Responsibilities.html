Standard VII: Responsibilities as a CFA Institute Member or CFA Candidate

VII(A) prohibits any conduct that compromises the reputation or integrity of CFA Institute or the CFA designation, or the integrity, validity, or security of CFA Institute programs — covering exam cheating, confidential information disclosure, testing rule violations, and misuse of CFA volunteer positions. VII(B) prohibits misrepresenting or exaggerating the meaning or implications of CFA membership, the CFA designation, or CFA candidacy. Both sources fully synthesised — the Handbook carries 5 new examples on computer-based testing not found in the LES.

VII(A)

Standard VII(A): Conduct as Participants in CFA Institute Programs

Standard VII(A) — Conduct as Participants in CFA Institute Programs

Members and Candidates must not engage in any conduct that compromises the reputation or integrity of CFA Institute or the CFA designation or the integrity, validity, or security of CFA Institute programs.

VII(A) applies across all CFA Institute programs — not just the CFA Program — including the CIPM Program, the Investment Foundations Program, and any other CFA Institute credentialing or educational program. The standard's purpose: protect the public's confidence that the CFA charter represents achievement based on merit and ethical conduct. The Handbook adds an important clarification: this standard does NOT require non-members or non-candidates who are participants in CFA Institute programs to comply with the Code and Standards, unless they are otherwise subject to it.

Prohibited conduct includes but is not limited to:

  • Giving or receiving assistance (cheating) on any CFA Institute examination
  • Violating rules, regulations, and testing policies of CFA Institute programs (calculator policy, personal belongings policy, etc.)
  • Providing confidential program or exam information to candidates or the public
  • Disregarding or attempting to circumvent security measures for any CFA Institute examination
  • Improperly using an association with CFA Institute to achieve personal or professional goals
  • Misrepresenting information to CFA Institute regarding any CFA Institute program (LES includes "misrepresenting on the Professional Conduct Statement or in the Continuing Education Program"; the Handbook adds fraudulent deferral requests and document falsification)
A2

Confidential Program Information

CFA Institute actively polices online platforms (blogs, forums, social networks) for confidential information. Candidates and members who are aware of potential violations should report them to CFA Institute.

Examples of information that CANNOT be disclosed include:

  • Specific details of questions appearing on the exam
  • Broad topical areas tested or not tested on the exam
  • Formulas tested or not tested on the exam

All aspects of an exam — questions, broad topical areas, and formulas, tested or NOT tested — are considered confidential until CFA Institute elects to release them publicly. This requirement maintains exam integrity and rigor for future candidates.

💡 What IS Permitted

VII(A) does NOT prohibit: discussing nonconfidential curriculum material with others or in study groups; expressing opinions about how difficult the exam was or how well you think you did; publicly disagreeing with CFA Institute's policies, procedures, or advocacy positions; factually stating your involvement as a volunteer. What is prohibited is disclosing content-specific information — the subjects covered, questions asked, formulas tested or not tested.

Examiner Focus — The Rossi Blog Post Example

Rossi writes: "Level II is complete! I think I did fairly well... It was really difficult, but fair... I think I did especially well on the derivatives questions. And there were tons of them! I think I counted 18! The ethics questions were really hard... I was surprised to see there were no questions at all about IPO allocations."

The critical distinction: Permitted — expressing how difficult the exam was, how well she thinks she did. Violation — revealing that derivatives questions were numerous ("tons of them", "counted 18"), that ethics was heavily covered, and specifically that there were NO questions on IPO allocations. Both the presence and the absence of topics on the exam are confidential. Rossi's disclosure could assist future candidates sitting the same exam administration (if posted before all administrations are complete) or give future exam administrations' candidates insights into what was or wasn't tested. The fact that the comments might seem casual and personal doesn't change their nature as confidential content disclosure.

A3

Volunteer Conduct — Special Obligations

Members who participate as volunteers — proctors, exam developers, graders, committee members — are exposed to confidential material and must maintain the same high level of integrity and confidentiality required of candidates. Volunteers must not disclose or solicit confidential material gained during the volunteer process to anyone outside the exam development process.

Information that CANNOT be shared by members involved in developing, administering, or grading exams:

  • Questions appearing on the exam or under consideration
  • Deliberation related to the exam process
  • Information related to the scoring of questions

Additionally, volunteer positions at CFA Institute must not be used to benefit the volunteer personally or commercially. Using a CFA Institute position (e.g., society program chair, committee membership) to steer business opportunities to oneself or one's clients, or to imply special informational advantages to clients, violates VII(A).

A4

Expressing Opinions — What Is Allowed

VII(A) explicitly does NOT cover expressing opinions regarding CFA Institute or its programs. Members and candidates are free to:

  • Disagree with CFA Institute on its policies, procedures, or advocacy positions
  • Express personal opinions about the exam's difficulty or their own performance
  • Discuss CFA Institute's approach to various issues
  • Factually state their involvement with CFA Institute volunteer roles

When expressing a personal opinion, a candidate is prohibited from disclosing content-specific information — any actual exam questions and information about subjects covered or not covered in the exam. The opinion protection applies to views about the programme; it doesn't extend to disclosure of what was on the exam.

VII(A) Worked Examples — All Examples From Both Sources

Example 1
Both (different scenarios)
Nero — Proctor/Candidate Sharing and Using Actual Exam Questions
VIOLATION
LES version: Nero is a proctor who reviews the Level II exam the night before administration and provides questions to two candidates. Handbook version: Nero is a candidate who finds what appears to be a copy of the Level II exam and posts questions in an online chatroom for other candidates; all three use the questions to prepare.
All parties violated VII(A). Both the givers and the receivers of exam content violate the standard. Providing an unfair advantage to other candidates undermines the integrity and validity of the Level II exam as an accurate measure of the knowledge and skills needed to earn the CFA designation. Receiving stolen exam content also undermines the ethical framework that is a key part of the designation.
🔑 Core Rule
VII(A) creates a two-sided obligation: both giving AND receiving confidential exam information constitutes a violation. This applies to proctors, volunteers, and candidates equally. The purpose of the exam — to serve as an objective measure of merit — is undermined by any participant who gives or uses exam information improperly, regardless of their role.

Handbook Only

Example 2 (LES) / Example 4 (Handbook)
Both (different framing)
Sullivan/Yu — Bringing Written Formulas into the Exam Room
VIOLATION
LES: Sullivan writes a formula on his palm before the exam; proctor notices him looking at his hand. Handbook: Yu writes several formulas on paper and hides it in her shirt sleeve before entering the CBT centre; a proctor notices her removing it during the exam. Both concealed their written notes differently, but the violation is identical.
Both violated VII(A). Taking written information from the Candidate Body of Knowledge (CBOK) into the exam room compromises the validity of exam performance and violates CFA Program testing rules. The Candidate Pledge explicitly prohibits bringing materials into the testing room.
🔑 Core Rule
All forms of unauthorised material in the testing environment = VII(A) violation. The method of concealment (palm vs. paper in sleeve) is irrelevant. The violation is compromising exam validity by using materials not available to all candidates. This also directly contradicts the Candidate Pledge.
Example 5 (Handbook-Only)
Handbook Only
Sharma — Looking at Other Candidates' CBT Screens During the Exam
VIOLATION
Sharma's mind wanders during the Level II CBT exam. He wonders if his test matches the one next to him, and looks at the neighbouring candidate's screen multiple times. The testing rules explicitly prohibit looking at other candidates' screens. His conduct is observed by a proctor and captured on video surveillance.
Violation of VII(A). Sharma violated testing rules by looking at other candidates' screens, compromising the validity, integrity, and security of the exam. The intent (idle curiosity) rather than deliberate cheating does not change the violation — the rules are absolute and Sharma was aware of them.
🔑 Core Rule (Handbook-Only)
Intent to gain an advantage is not required for a VII(A) violation — violating a clearly stated testing rule is sufficient. This is especially relevant in the CBT environment where visual separation between screens is a core security measure. Any breach of this rule, regardless of motivation, undermines exam security.
Example 3 (LES) / Example 6 (Handbook)
Both
Davis — Continuing to Fill in Answers After Time Is Called
VIOLATION
The proctors announce "Stop writing now." Davis continues to randomly fill in ovals on his answer sheet. A proctor approaches and reminds him to stop — Davis continues. Even after two additional requests to stop, Davis only finally puts down his pencil after the proctor's third intervention.
Violation of VII(A). By continuing to write after time was called, Davis took an unfair advantage over other candidates who complied with the rules and compromised the validity of his exam performance. Repeated defiance of the proctor's instructions further constitutes a violation of CFA Program rules and regulations.
🔑 Core Rule
Time limit compliance is a core component of exam validity. Writing after time is called — even randomly filling in answers — provides an advantage over candidates who stopped when required. The validity of the exam as a comparative measure of candidate knowledge depends on uniform adherence to time constraints.
Example 4 (LES) / Example 6 (Handbook)
Both
Rossi — Blog Post Revealing Exam Content (Topics Tested and Not Tested)
PARTIAL VIOLATION
After completing Level II, Rossi posts a blog entry: she expresses how difficult the exam was, mentions doing well on derivatives (implying many derivatives questions — counting 18), notes ethics was hard, and specifically states there were NO questions on IPO allocations.
Partial violation. NOT a violation: expressing that the exam was difficult, stating she thinks she did well. VIOLATION: revealing that derivatives was heavily tested (topics and number of questions), that ethics was significant, and that IPO allocations was NOT tested — all constitute disclosure of confidential exam content. Both the presence and absence of specific topics are confidential.
🔑 Core Rule
The personal/casual tone of a post doesn't change the confidentiality classification of the information disclosed. "Topics not covered" is just as confidential as "topics that were covered" — both provide meaningful guidance to future candidates. Even a seemingly innocent observation ("I was surprised to see no questions on X") is a disclosure of confidential information about exam content.

Both
Gagne — Online Forum Discussion Attempting to Reconstruct Exam Questions
VIOLATION
Gagne participates in an internet forum discussion the week after completing Level I. He and others discuss the most challenging questions and attempt to determine the correct answers — sharing question-specific details in the process.
Violation of VII(A). Providing and soliciting confidential exam information, including question-specific details in attempts to reconstruct exam content and determine correct answers, compromises the integrity of the exam process and violates the Candidate Pledge. Both providing and soliciting the information are violations.
🔑 Core Rule
Post-exam discussion threads that reconstruct specific questions = violation even when the intent is academic ("determining correct answers"). The reconstruction of exam content is itself a form of disclosure. Both those who share and those who solicit the information violate VII(A). The forum setting (online, seemingly private) doesn't change the classification.
Example 6 (LES) / Example 8 (Handbook)
Both
Pena / CFA4Sure — Third-Party Solicitation of Exam Content
VIOLATION — MULTIPLE PARTIES
CFA4Sure emails all customers the day after the exam, asking them to share the hardest questions so CFA4Sure can better prepare future customers. Pena responds by emailing a summary of the questions she found most difficult.
Pena violated VII(A) by disclosing exam questions. CFA4Sure employees who are members or candidates also violated VII(A) by soliciting confidential information. The fact that CFA4Sure is a commercial test-prep company (not a testing organisation) doesn't insulate its member/candidate employees from liability for soliciting confidential content.
🔑 Core Rule
Third-party solicitation of confidential exam content is still a violation by those who comply with the request. Commercial benefit to the requesting party doesn't change the confidentiality status of the information. Members/candidates working for test-prep companies have the same obligations as any other member/candidate — their employer's commercial interests don't override their duties under VII(A).

Both
Whitcomb — Grader Discloses Question Details and Guideline Answers to Candidate Colleagues
VIOLATION

Ramirez + Warrenski — Misusing CFA Institute Volunteer Positions for Personal Benefit

VII(B)

Standard VII(B): Reference to CFA Institute, the CFA Designation, and the CFA Program

Standard VII(B) — Reference to CFA Institute, the CFA Designation, and the CFA Program

When referring to CFA Institute, CFA Institute membership, the CFA designation, or candidacy in the CFA Program, Members and Candidates must not misrepresent or exaggerate the meaning or implications of membership in CFA Institute, holding the CFA designation, or candidacy in the CFA Program.

VII(B) is intended to ensure factual representations relating to CFA Institute and to prohibit unsupported statements promising competence or performance. Specifically prohibited: overstating the competency of an individual, or asserting or implying that superior investment performance can be expected from someone with the CFA designation.

Permitted statements include:

  • Highlighting the commitment of CFA members, charterholders, and candidates to ethical and professional conduct
  • Mentioning the thoroughness and rigor of the CFA exams
  • Making claims about the relative merits of CFA Institute, the CFA Program, or the Code and Standards — as long as they are explicitly or implicitly stated as the speaker's opinion
  • Factual statements supported by evidence

VII(B) applies to all forms of communication: firm letterhead, business cards, professional biographies, directory listings, printed advertising, LinkedIn pages, email signatures, websites, personal resumes, and oral statements to the public, clients, or prospects.

Handbook adds: LinkedIn pages and email signatures specifically listed as covered channels — reflecting the social media expansion of the Handbook's scope.

B2

Membership Status, Designation Rights, and Candidacy

Active CFA Institute Membership

Annual requirements to maintain active status:

  • Complete CFA Institute Membership Agreement (Handbook adds this vs. LES)
  • Submit completed Professional Conduct Statement
  • Pay applicable membership dues

If any requirement is missed: membership lapses. Must not present as an active member. Handbook adds: must remove all references from social media, business communications, reports. May state they were a member in the past.

CFA Charterholder Rights

Charterholders = individuals who earned the right to use the CFA designation from CFA Institute after completing the CFA Program and required years of acceptable work experience.

  • Must also satisfy annual membership requirements to maintain the right to use the designation
  • If membership lapses: forfeits the right to use the CFA designation
  • Handbook adds: must remove all references to the designation from social media, business communications, reports
  • May state they were a charterholder in the past
  • Retired status available at reduced dues through a formal CFA Institute process
  • Pseudonyms/anonymity: cannot tag the CFA designation to a fictitious identity or pseudonym
Candidacy Status

A person IS a candidate if:

  • CFA Institute has accepted their application (issued a notice of acceptance), AND
  • The person is enrolled to sit for a specified exam, OR
  • The person has sat for a specified exam but results have not yet been received

A person is NOT a candidate if they declined to sit or otherwise don't meet the CFA Institute Bylaws definition. Candidacy resumes when enrolled again.

Must never: imply a partial designation from passing one or more levels; cite an expected completion date for earning the charter.

⚠️ The Retired Status Trap — Both Sources

Both the Vasseur (travels for 2 years) and Simpson (retires after 25 years) examples illustrate the same point: stopping the Professional Conduct Statement and dues payments = membership lapses = loss of right to use the CFA designation. Simply being retired or not actively practicing does NOT automatically create "retired" status. CFA Institute has a specific formal process for obtaining "retired" member classification with reduced dues — this requires affirmative action by the charterholder. Without going through that process, a retired professional who stops paying dues loses their right to use the designation even if they never plan to practice again.

Proper and Improper References — Side-by-Side

✅ Proper References
❌ Improper References
"Becoming a charterholder has enhanced my portfolio management skills."
"CFA charterholders achieve better performance results."
"John Smith passed all three CFA exams in three consecutive sittings."
"John Smith is among the elite, having passed all three CFA exams in three consecutive attempts."
"The CFA designation is globally recognised and attests to a charterholder's success in a rigorous and comprehensive study program."
"As a CFA charterholder, I am the most qualified to manage client investments."
"The credibility that the CFA designation affords and the skills the CFA Program cultivates are key assets for my future career development."
"As a CFA charterholder, Jane White provides the best value in trade execution."
"I enrolled as a candidate for the CFA designation to obtain the highest set of credentials in the global investment management industry."
"Enrolling as a candidate for the CFA designation ensures one of becoming better at valuing debt securities."
"I passed the Level II CFA exam."
"CFA, Level II"
"I am a 20XX Level III candidate for the CFA designation."
"CFA, Expected 20XX"
"I passed all three levels of the CFA exam and will be eligible for the CFA charter upon completion of the required work experience."
"John Smith, Charter Pending"

Key patterns in the proper/improper distinctions:

  • Passing all three exams consecutively = factual and acceptable to state. Claiming this demonstrates "elite" status or superior ability = not acceptable.
  • Enhanced personal skills = acceptable personal outcome statement. "Achieve better performance" = overstated claim about outcomes.
  • "I passed Level II of the CFA exam" = acceptable factual statement. "CFA, Level II" = implies a partial designation, which doesn't exist.
  • "I am a Level III candidate" = acceptable factual statement. "CFA, Expected 20XX" = implies an expected charter before it's earned; "Charter Pending" = same problem.
  • The "ensures" problem: "Enrolling ensures one of becoming better at valuing debt securities" = guarantee of outcome = not acceptable. Factual benefits only.

VII(B) Worked Examples

Example 1
Both
AZ Investment Advisers — Linking First-Attempt Passes to Superior Fund Performance
VIOLATION
An advertisement for AZ Investment Advisers states that all principals are CFA charterholders who passed all three exams on their first attempt, and prominently links this fact to the notion that AZ's mutual funds have achieved superior performance.
Violation of VII(B). Stating that all principals passed on the first try is permissible IF true — it's a factual statement. However, linking that fact to claims of superior performance ability or investment results is prohibited. The advertisement implies: (1) CFA charterholders achieve better investment results and (2) first-attempt passers are more successful than those who required additional attempts — both are prohibited implications.
🔑 Core Rule
Factual statements about exam achievements are fine. The violation comes from the linkage to performance claims. CFA Institute makes no representation that charterholders outperform non-charterholders, and the standard prohibits members from making such implications. First-attempt passing is a factual achievement — implying it predicts superior investment performance converts a fact into an unsupported performance claim.
Example 2
Both
Vasseur — Using CFA Designation After Membership Lapsed
VIOLATION
Vasseur (CFA charterholder for 5 years) travels abroad for 2 years, doesn't file a Professional Conduct Statement or pay dues. Upon returning to work as a self-employed analyst, he prints business cards displaying "CFA" after his name without reinstating his membership.
Violation of VII(B). Vasseur's right to use the CFA designation was suspended when he failed to meet the annual membership requirements. Displaying "CFA" after his name implies active charterholder status he does not currently hold. He must reinstate membership through the appropriate CFA Institute process before using the designation.
🔑 Core Rule
The right to use the CFA designation is tied to active membership — not to having once earned the charter. Annual Professional Conduct Statement + dues = the ongoing price of using the designation. Lapsing membership = forfeiting the right to use the designation, even temporarily. There is no grace period during which the designation can be used while membership has lapsed.
Example 3
Both
Simpson — Retired Charterholder Using CFA Designation Without Retired Status
VIOLATION
Simpson retires after a 25-year career. He stops filing Professional Conduct Statements and paying dues, then designs a personal business card (no corporate logo) for friends with his new contact details — continuing to display "CFA" after his name.
Violation of VII(B). Same analysis as Vasseur — membership lapsed, forfeiting the right to use the designation. The personal/informal nature of the business card (no corporate logo, just for friends) doesn't change the classification. CFA Institute has a formal "retired" status process with reduced dues — Simpson must complete that process to regain the right to use the designation.
🔑 Core Rule
"Retired" status is NOT automatic. It is a specific formal classification with its own ongoing requirements (reduced dues + continued Professional Conduct Statement). Without actively obtaining retired status from CFA Institute, a retired charterholder who stops paying dues has lapsed membership — and cannot use the designation. The fact that the business card is "just for friends" doesn't matter — the designation reference misrepresents the member's current status.
Example 4
Both
Reese — Factual Statements About the CFA Program to a Friend
COMPLIANT
Reese (CFA charterholder since 2000) tells a friend considering the CFA Program: she learned a great deal from the programme, many firms require the CFA designation for their investment professionals, and she would recommend it to anyone pursuing investment management.
Compliant with VII(B). Reese's statements are factual: the CFA Program enhanced her knowledge; many firms do require it. These are supportable factual claims about the CFA Program's benefits — not unsupported guarantees of performance or superiority.
🔑 Core Rule
The key distinction: statements about the factual benefits of the CFA Program (knowledge enhancement, industry recognition) are permitted. Statements guaranteeing superior performance or competence claims that can't be factually supported are not. Reese's recommendations are based on her personal experience and factual industry patterns — both legitimate bases for positive statements about the programme.

Glass/"Expert CFA" — Using CFA Designation with a Fictitious Name Online

LES: An anonymous blogger runs a blog as "Expert CFA" — Cannon reads this blog and incorporates the factors into his proprietary model. The "Expert CFA" blogger is using the designation without being identified by full name. Example 5 (LES-Only)
LES Only
Prittima — Order of CFA Designation and PhD on Business Card
COMPLIANT
Prittima has earned both the CFA designation and a PhD in finance. She wants to cite both on her business card but is unsure of the proper ordering.
Compliant — no violation.} The order of designations cited on resumes and business cards is a matter of personal preference. Prittima is free to cite the CFA designation either before or after citing her PhD. Multiple designations must be separated by a comma.

Combined Exam Checklists — VII(A) and VII(B)

Standard VII(A): Conduct as Participants in CFA Institute Programs

  • ✅ Applies across all CFA Institute programs — CFA Program, CIPM, Investment Foundations, and any other CFA Institute credentialing/educational program.
  • ✅ Prohibited conduct: cheating (giving or receiving exam assistance) + testing rule violations + providing confidential information + circumventing security + improperly using CFA Institute association for personal goals + misrepresenting information to CFA Institute.
  • ✅ Confidential information includes: specific exam questions + broad topical areas tested + broad topical areas NOT tested + formulas tested + formulas NOT tested. All aspects are confidential until publicly released by CFA Institute.
  • ✅ Both providing AND receiving confidential exam information = violation. The obligation is bilateral. (Nero/candidates)
  • ✅ Topics NOT covered on the exam are as confidential as topics that ARE covered. (Rossi — IPO allocations disclosure)
  • ✅ Post-exam discussion threads reconstructing specific questions = violation — even when the intent is academic ("what was the right answer?"). (Gagne)
  • ✅ Third-party solicitation of exam content: members/candidates who comply with requests to share content violate VII(A). (Pena/CFA4Sure)
  • ✅ Volunteer obligations continue after the engagement ends. Whitcomb's disclosure weeks after grading = still a violation.
  • ✅ CFA Institute volunteer positions must not be used for personal/commercial benefit. (Ramirez — scheduling only personal clients; Warrenski — implying confidential committee advantage)
  • ✅ Warrenski rule: may state involvement with a CFA Institute committee factually; cannot infer or imply special advantages to clients from committee membership.
  • ✅ Testing rule violations (any rule): calculator policy, possession of unauthorised materials, continuing to write after time is called, looking at other screens, fraudulent deferral requests — all are VII(A) violations.
  • ✅ Permitted: discussing nonconfidential curriculum material + expressing personal opinions about exam difficulty/performance + disagreeing with CFA Institute policies + factually stating volunteer involvement.
  • ✅ Fraudulent documentation submitted to CFA Institute (altered death certificates for deferral) = VII(A) + I(C) Misrepresentation. (Chiu — Handbook only)

Standard VII(B): Reference to CFA Institute, the CFA Designation, and the CFA Program

  • ✅ Cannot misrepresent or exaggerate the meaning or implications of CFA membership, the CFA designation, or CFA candidacy.
  • ✅ Cannot imply superior performance will result from the CFA designation — investment results cannot be linked to charterholder status. (AZ Investment Advisers)
  • ✅ Facts about the programme are permitted — programme enhanced my skills, firms require it, rigorous and thorough — as long as they are factual or stated as personal opinion.
  • ✅ Annual membership requirements: Membership Agreement (Handbook) + Professional Conduct Statement + dues. Failing any = membership lapses = no right to use the designation.
  • ✅ Lapsed membership: must not present as active member; must remove ALL references to designation from social media, business communications, reports (Handbook). May state past membership/charterholder status.
  • ✅ "Retired" status is NOT automatic — must go through CFA Institute's formal retired status process with reduced dues. Simply retiring doesn't preserve the right to use the designation. (Simpson, Vasseur)
  • ✅ Pseudonyms and anonymous online profiles cannot be tagged with the CFA designation. Full name required with any use of the designation. (Glass/"Expert CFA")
  • ✅ Candidacy references: Must clearly state "candidate for the CFA designation" — cannot imply partial designation. Cannot cite an expected completion date.
  • ✅ "CFA, Level II" is improper — implies a partial designation that doesn't exist. "I passed the Level II CFA exam" is proper.
  • ✅ "CFA, Expected 20XX" and "Charter Pending" are both improper — imply a forthcoming designation before it is awarded. The charter is only awarded upon meeting all requirements and Board of Governors approval.
  • ✅ Passing all three exams consecutively is a fact that may be stated. Implying this demonstrates "elite" status or superior ability is a violation. (AZ Investment Advisers)
  • ✅ Order of CFA and academic credentials on business cards = personal preference; separate with a comma. No prescribed ordering. (Prittima — LES-only)
  • ✅ Applies to all communication forms: letterhead, business cards, biographies, directory listings, advertising, LinkedIn pages, email signatures, websites, resumes, oral statements.

Top VII(A) exam traps:
(1) "She only mentioned that derivatives was her strongest topic — not the actual questions" — revealing that derivatives was heavily tested (numbers of questions, being "tons") = confidential content disclosure
(2) "He posted that there were NO questions on IPO allocations — that's not actually a question, so it's fine" — what's NOT on the exam is equally confidential as what IS
(3) "She can say she's on the GIPS committee if a client asks" — stating involvement factually is fine; implying the position gives her or her clients special advantages is not
(4) "He used a pseudonym so it wasn't really CFA Institute's brand being misused" — pseudonyms tagged with the CFA designation violate both VII(A) (misuse of CFA Institute association) and VII(B) (misuse of the designation)

Top VII(B) exam traps:
(1) "He's retired and not working anymore — of course he can still put CFA on his card" — retired status is not automatic; must go through CFA Institute's formal process
(2) "She passed all three levels, so 'CFA, Level III' is just stating she's done" — "CFA, Level III" implies a partial designation; "I passed all three levels of the CFA exam" is the correct statement
(3) "Linking the fact that all principals passed on the first try to their fund's good performance — it's just marketing" — linking first-attempt exam passes to investment performance claims violates VII(B)
(4) "He can put 'Expert, CFA' online — it just means he's an expert who happens to be a CFA charterholder" — pseudonym + CFA designation = violation regardless of the pseudonym's meaning
