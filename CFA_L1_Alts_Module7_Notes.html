Overview — Digital Assets

Digital assets = assets that can be created, stored, and transmitted electronically and have associated ownership or use rights. Includes cryptocurrencies, tokens, and digital collectibles. Built on distributed ledger technology (DLT) / blockchain, which uses advanced encryption to assure authenticity.

Bitcoin (2009) was the first cryptocurrency. By early 2022, close to 10,000 different cryptocurrencies existed (up from ~70 in 2013). Bitcoin + Ether together make up >80% of total cryptocurrency market value (as of July 2022).

Investment Appeal
  • Higher expected returns than traditional assets
  • Potential diversification benefits (low correlation)
  • Growing institutional acceptance
  • 24/7 trading availability
Key Risks
  • Very high volatility
  • No underlying cash flows or fundamentals
  • Regulatory uncertainty / potential ban
  • Fraud, manipulation, theft
  • Lost wallet keys → irretrievable assets
Key Distinctions
  • Cryptocurrencies have their own blockchains
  • Crypto-tokens are built on an existing blockchain
  • No central bank or government backing
  • Decentralized ledger vs. private central ledger
02

Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) & Blockchain

What Is a Distributed Ledger?

A distributed ledger is a type of database that can be shared among potentially infinite numbers of entities in a network. Each participating entity has a matching copy of the digital database — making every copy a verified record of all current and past transactions.

Three basic elements of a DLT network:

  1. Digital ledger — the shared database itself
  2. Consensus mechanism — process by which nodes agree on a common state of the ledger
  3. Participant network — the nodes connected to and maintaining the ledger

Key DLT Features

Benefits of DLT
  • Greater accuracy in record keeping
  • Transparency — accessible to network participants near-real-time
  • Security via cryptography (data encrypted; unusable if intercepted)
  • Faster transfer of ownership
  • Immutability — records cannot be changed once added
  • P2P interactions — no central intermediary required
  • Supports smart contracts
Limitations of DLT
  • Not fully secure — breaches in privacy/data protection are possible
  • Massive energy consumption required for computational verification (especially PoW)
  • Slower and less cost-effective at scale (permissionless networks)
  • No regulatory oversight in most jurisdictions

Blockchain — How It Works

Blockchain = a type of DLT where information (e.g., ownership changes) is recorded sequentially within blocks that are then linked ("chained") together and secured using cryptographic methods.

  • Each block contains: a grouping of transactions + a secure hash link to the previous block
  • New transactions are inserted only after validation via the consensus mechanism
  • All authorized members must agree on the transaction AND the preceding order (history) of transactions
  • Once added → immutable (cannot be altered without controlling most of the network)
  • Result: a permanent, tamper-resistant, transparent transaction history

Smart Contracts

Smart contracts = computer programs that self-execute on the basis of pre-specified terms and conditions agreed to by the parties. Built into the blockchain code — execution is automatic, trackable, and irreversible.

Examples of smart contract use:

  • Automatic execution of contingent claims for derivatives
  • Instantaneous transfer of collateral in the event of default
  • DeFi applications (lending, trading, settlement, payment, transfer)
  • NFT authenticity verification
  • Governance voting on permissionless networks
💡 DLT vs. Blockchain

All blockchains are DLT, but not all DLT is blockchain. Blockchain = a specific type of distributed ledger where data is organized in sequentially linked, cryptographically secured blocks. DLT is the broader category — other structures (e.g., DAGs) also qualify as DLT without being blockchains.

03

Consensus Protocols — Proof of Work vs. Proof of Stake

A consensus protocol = a set of rules governing how blocks are cryptographically chained together to verify the complete and immutable history of transactions. It determines who gets to add the next block and how manipulation is resisted.

Consensus involves two steps: (1) transaction validation and (2) agreement on ledger update by network parties.

Both protocols reward successful validators with newly created digital assets (mining rewards). The rate of mining and validation speed control how much new digital asset can be created on a specific network.

⛏ PROOF OF WORK (PoW)
  • Determines which block to add via a computationally costly lottery
  • Miners use powerful computers to solve complex cryptographic puzzles
  • Significant amounts of energy consumed
  • Successfully solving the puzzle → miner earns cryptocurrency reward
  • Security: to manipulate history, attacker must control >51% of network's computational power (the "51% attack threshold")
  • Network agrees: the longest chain = truthful representation of all previous transactions
  • Very difficult to amass >51% computing power on major blockchains (Bitcoin, Ethereum)
  • Even if 51% is temporarily controlled, only limited history can be altered
  • Most widely used consensus protocol for digital assets historically
  • Used by: Bitcoin
🔐 PROOF OF STAKE (PoS)
  • Selected participants (validators) pledge / stake capital to vouch for a block's validity
  • Staking signals the validator is available to verify and propose a block
  • A majority of other validators (who also staked) must attest to proposed block's validity
  • Validators earn rewards for both proposing AND attesting to validity of blocks
  • Security: boundary built on group of stakers and their pledged stake controlling computational power
  • Less energy intensive than PoW
  • Used by: Ethereum (after "The Merge" in 2022)

🧠 Mnemonic — PoW vs. PoS

PoW = "Work it out" → computers do the hard computational work. PoS = "Put your money where your mouth is" → validators stake (risk) their own capital to vouch for validity. Both reward successful validators with new digital assets (mining). Both resist manipulation, but through different mechanisms.

Permissioned vs. Permissionless Networks

FeaturePermissioned BlockchainPermissionless Blockchain
AccessLimited — membership restrictedUnlimited — open to any user

DecentralizationPartially decentralized — limited membersFully decentralized — all members can access
GovernanceCentralized organization determines governanceDecentralized — maintained by all members
Trust requirementTrust between members needed (known participants)No trust between parties required

ExampleEnterprise/regulatory blockchainsBitcoin, Ethereum

Permissionless networks (like Bitcoin) do not depend on a centralized authority for validation — consensus mechanism handles it. No single point of failure. All transactions are permanent and immutable once recorded. Trust between transacting parties is NOT required.

Permissioned networks allow varying levels of access: adding transactions (participant), viewing only (regulator), or viewing selective details. Governance determined by centralized organization. More efficient but partially centralized.

💡 Financial Applications of DLT

DLT is being developed for: post-trade clearing and settlement (faster, eliminates reconciliation), compliance automation, smart contracts (auto-execute contingent claims, collateral transfers), identity verification, tokenization of ownership rights, and record keeping. Permissioned networks are favored by financial institutions because of speed, cost efficiency, and controlled access (e.g., regulators can view but not alter).

05

Types of Digital Assets

₿ BITCOIN (BTC / XBT)

Launched 2009. First & most widely traded cryptocurrency. Designed as medium of exchange + store of value. Supply hard-capped at 21 million BTC. Permissionless network. Uses PoW. Viewed as "digital gold." Influences all subsequent crypto development.

Ξ ALTCOINS (incl. Ether)

All cryptocurrencies other than Bitcoin. Most prominent = Ether (Ethereum, 2015). Ethereum has a programmable blockchain supporting smart contracts and dApps — not just a store of value. "Smart coins" = programmable altcoins with self-executing contracts.

💲 STABLECOINS

Designed to maintain stable value by linking to another asset. Collateralized by basket of assets (legal tender, precious metals, or other crypto). Protect holders from price volatility. Cannot be exchanged for fiat money. No legal or regulatory backing. Algorithmic stablecoins control supply via algorithms.

🐸 MEME COINS

Often inspired by a joke; launched for entertainment. Gain popularity rapidly in short periods. Allow early purchasers to sell at considerable profit. Highly speculative; no fundamental value basis.

🎨 NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens)

Link digital assets to certificates of authenticity using blockchain. Each NFT is unique (non-fungible) — unlike cryptocurrencies which are interchangeable (fungible). Represent unique digital or physical assets. Most common use: digital artwork trading.

📜 SECURITY TOKENS

Digitize ownership rights of publicly traded securities. Stored on blockchain → increases efficiency of post-trade processing, settlement, record keeping, custody. Eliminates reconciliation need. Example: ICOs (Initial Coin Offerings) — unregulated; investors receive tokens for future products/services. No voting rights typically.

⚙️ UTILITY TOKENS

Provide services within a network (pay for services, network fees). Unlike security tokens, utility tokens only compensate for activities on the network — they do NOT pay dividends.

🗳 GOVERNANCE TOKENS

Serve as votes to determine how permissionless networks are run. When technical problems arise, governance token holders vote on solutions or major network changes. Important for maintaining stability and integrity of permissionless blockchains.

🏦 CBDCs (Central Bank Digital Currencies)

Tokenized version of fiat currency issued by a central bank — essentially a digital bank note or coin. Government backed (unlike all other cryptocurrencies). Being explored by central banks worldwide as alternative to physical currency.

⚠️ Security Token vs. Utility Token vs. Governance Token — Exam Distinction

Security tokens: represent ownership rights (like equity); may pay dividends; regulated as securities. Utility tokens: access to network services only; no dividends; not securities. Governance tokens: voting rights on network decisions only. The key exam test: does the token give you an economic ownership claim? → Security token. Does it only give you network access/services? → Utility token. Does it only give you votes? → Governance token.

💡 ICO vs. IPO

ICO = unregulated, crypto-tokens sold for money or other cryptocurrency, used to buy future products/services, lower issuance costs, shorter time frame, typically NO voting rights, fraud risk is high. IPO = regulated, shares sold for cash, represent actual equity ownership with voting rights, full regulatory disclosure. Both raise capital — but ICO is the unregulated, riskier alternative.

⚠️ No Fundamental Value — Critical Exam Point

Digital assets derive their price from: (1) perceived scarcity (supply limitations via algorithms) and (2) potential to transfer value in the future (unique blockchain features). There are NO earnings, dividends, or cash flows to discount. This means standard fundamental valuation models do NOT apply. Price is entirely expectation-driven.

Digital Asset Investment Forms & Vehicles

Direct Investment

Direct ownership of Bitcoin/cryptocurrencies involves using a cryptocurrency wallet — stores public and private digital codes required to access the asset (on a website or mobile app). Available 24/7 on cryptocurrency exchanges.

Key risks of direct investment:

  • Fraud: Scam ICOs, pump-and-dump schemes, market manipulation, theft, credential hacking
  • Lost wallet access: If passkey is lost → holdings are irretrievable. Around 20% of all Bitcoins are reportedly in lost or deserted wallets
  • "Whales": Small number of large holders can manipulate price. Jargon for individuals/entities holding enough of a cryptocurrency to move its price

🧠 Crypto ETF Key Fact

Most crypto ETFs do NOT invest directly in cryptocurrencies — they gain exposure via cryptocurrency futures contracts and money market instruments (short-term government debt, repo agreements). This means the ETF may not track spot cryptocurrency prices perfectly due to roll costs and basis risk (similar to commodity ETFs in contango).

Cryptocurrency Exchanges — Centralized vs. Decentralized

Centralized Exchanges (CEX)
  • Most popular type of crypto exchange
  • Privately held; provide trading platforms
  • Offer volume, liquidity, price transparency
  • Electronic and direct — no broker/dealer intermediary
  • Hosted on private servers → security vulnerability
  • If servers compromised → entire system may be paralyzed; cryptographic keys exposed
  • Some are regulated (as financial exchanges or financial intermediaries, depending on jurisdiction)
  • Incompatible with Bitcoin's decentralized ideology — yet very popular
Decentralized Exchanges (DEX)
  • Emulate blockchain's decentralized protocol
  • No centralized control mechanism; operates on distributed platform
  • If one computer is attacked → exchange remains operational (other computers continue)
  • Attacking DEX is substantially more difficult — attacks almost certain to fail
  • Very difficult to regulate — no single controller
  • Users generally free to transact without regulatory scrutiny → enables potentially illegal activity
⚠️ Both Exchange Types Share Key Risk

Both centralized AND decentralized exchanges may face fraud, manipulation, and investor protection concerns because they are not subject to rigorous oversight. Unlike traditional equity/futures exchanges, cryptocurrency venues are largely unregulated. Individuals or groups may engage in fraud, market manipulation, and social media pump-and-dump schemes — and this is NOT necessarily illegal on crypto exchanges.

Asset-Backed Tokens, Tokenization & DeFi

Asset-Backed Tokens

Asset-backed tokens = digital claims on physical assets, financial assets, or financial instruments that are collateralized by the underlying asset and derive their value directly from it. A digital representation of ownership.

Examples of tokenized assets: gold, crude oil, real estate, equities, art, precious metals, precious stones.

Key benefits:

  • Fractional ownership: Allows multiple investors to possess a fractional interest in a high-priced asset (e.g., a house, artwork) → increases liquidity and accessibility
  • Immutable ownership record: Blockchain stores transparent, permanent record of ownership and all transfers
  • Reduces costs: Lowers transaction, intermediation, and record-keeping costs
  • Regulatory classification: Financial regulators typically classify asset-backed tokens as securities (entitle holder to ownership interest in underlying asset)

Tokenization Process

Tokenization = process of representing ownership rights to physical assets on a blockchain or distributed ledger. Creates a single digital record of ownership to verify title and authenticity, including complete historical activity. Eliminates need for repeated ownership verification and examination at each transfer.

Asset-backed tokens are typically issued on the Ethereum network or other smart contract platforms, enabling P2P interaction via interoperable, transparent smart contracts (dApps — decentralized applications).

Decentralized Finance (DeFi)

DeFi = movement to design, combine, and develop decentralized financial applications (dApps) as building blocks for sophisticated financial products and services. Built on open-source code and smart contracts.

What DeFi can do (theoretically):

  • Medium of exchange
  • Storage of value
  • Tokenization of underlying assets
  • Immutable record keeping of ownership and transfer
  • Lending, trading, investment, settlement, payment, and transfer — all decentralized, authenticated, and instantaneous

Current state: Nascent — not fully developed. Most dApps to date have centered around extending leverage to investment and speculation in digital assets. Some advocates claim DeFi has technical advantages over traditional finance (time saving, risk reduction in settlement). But the vision remains largely unrealized.

💡 Web3 Context

Web3 = the third iteration of the internet, built on blockchain technologies, decentralization, and token-based economies. Cryptocurrencies (especially Bitcoin) are used as direct substitutes for fiat currencies within the Web3 ecosystem — especially for online transactions. This is the primary "medium of exchange" use case for crypto today.

Digital Asset Price Drivers

  • No underlying cash flows — price is based solely on expected asset appreciation
  • Limited supply: Bitcoin hard-capped at 21 million → scarcity value (like gold)
  • Market demand for limited supply = significant price driver
  • Market adoption and network effects (more users → more useful → higher value)
  • Technological advancement (new use cases, upgrades)
  • Regulatory developments (bans reduce value; recognition increases value)
  • Speculation and general market risk appetite
  • Social media influence — pump-and-dump schemes are possible on unregulated exchanges

Unique Risks of Digital Asset Investments

  • Extreme price volatility: Bitcoin lost ~74% from peak to trough in a single year
  • No fundamental value floor: Value can theoretically decline to zero
  • Regulatory risk: Countries can and do ban outright (China 2021). Regulatory changes can instantly affect prices. Evolving rules create uncertainty.
  • Fraud and criminal activity: Scam ICOs, pump-and-dump, market manipulation, social media promotions, theft of wallet credentials — all rampant
  • Lost wallet passkey: ~20% of all Bitcoins are in lost/inaccessible wallets. Permanent, irrecoverable loss.
  • Whale manipulation: Small number of large holders can move prices
  • Exchange vulnerability: Centralized exchanges can be hacked; decentralized exchanges enable illegal activity
  • Brief track record: Bitcoin only since 2009; limited data for robust statistical inference
  • Rising correlations: As crypto becomes more mainstream, correlations with traditional assets may increase → diversification benefit erodes
⚠️ Diversification Caveat — Critical for Exam

Digital assets have shown historically low correlations with traditional assets, suggesting diversification potential. However, these correlations have been observed to increase during periods of high market uncertainty — exactly when diversification is most needed. The long-term diversification benefit of cryptocurrencies remains uncertain and should not be taken as guaranteed.
