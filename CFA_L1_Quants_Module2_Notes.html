PV / FV — Core TVM Equations

Fundamental TVM Relationships
Future Value (discrete): FV_t = PV × (1 + r)^t
Future Value (continuous): FV_t = PV × e^(r×t)

Present Value (discrete): PV = FV_t / (1 + r)^t = FV_t × (1 + r)^(−t)
Present Value (continuous): PV_t = FV × e^(−r×t)

where: r = discount rate per period, t = number of compounding periods
These equations are the foundation of all TVM applications in this module.
Continuous compounding is the limiting case as compounding frequency → ∞.
PV and FV factors are always reciprocals of each other.
💡 The Core Principle

Cash received today is worth more than cash received in the future because today's cash can be invested and earn returns. The discount rate r captures the opportunity cost, riskiness, and time preference between these two cash flows.

Fixed-Income Instruments — 3 Cash Flow Patterns

Fixed-Income Instruments
Debt instruments (bonds, loans) under which an issuer borrows money from an investor in exchange for a promise of future repayment. The discount rate is an interest rate, and the rate of return is the yield-to-maturity (YTM).
Pattern 1 — Discount (Zero-Coupon)

Investor pays PV today → receives a single principal cash flow (FV) at maturity. No intermediate payments.

  • Interest earned = FV − PV (the difference)
  • Also called: zero-coupon bond, discount instrument, T-bill
  • Sole source of return is the price discount from par
Pattern 2 — Periodic Interest (Coupon Bond)

Investor pays PV today → receives periodic interest payments (PMT = coupon) at pre-determined intervals → final coupon + principal (FV) at maturity.

  • Most common fixed-income structure
  • Coupons are often paid semiannually
  • YTM is the single discount rate used for ALL cash flows regardless of timing
Pattern 3 — Level Payments (Annuity / Mortgage)

Investor pays PV today → receives uniform cash flows (A) at pre-determined intervals through maturity, each payment containing both interest and principal repayment.

  • Fully amortizing loans: mortgages, auto loans
  • Payment A remains constant; but the interest/principal split within each payment changes over time
  • No separate principal repayment at maturity — all included in the level payments

Fixed-Income PV Formulas — Master Reference

Discount Bond (Zero-Coupon)
PV = FV_t / (1 + r)^t
r = periodic discount rate, t = periods to maturity
Example: FV = 1,000, r = 5%, t = 2 → PV = 1,000 / (1.05)² = 907.03
Coupon Bond (Periodic Interest + Principal at Maturity)
PV = PMT/(1+r)¹ + PMT/(1+r)² + ... + (PMT + FV)/(1+r)^N

Or using annuity + PV of par:
PV = PMT × [1 − (1+r)^(−N)] / r + FV / (1+r)^N
PMT = periodic coupon payment, FV = par/face value, N = total periods, r = YTM per period
For semiannual bonds: use r = YTM/2 and N = years × 2
YTM is a single implied discount rate for ALL cash flows — cannot be solved algebraically; use calculator or iteration.
Perpetual Bond / Preferred Stock (Constant PMT forever)
PV(Perpetuity) = PMT / r
Valid when r > 0. Derived by taking N → ∞ in the coupon bond formula.
Most perpetual bonds are issued by companies for equity-like financing and often have redemption features.
Annuity / Level Payment Instrument (Periodic Payment A)
A = r × PV / [1 − (1 + r)^(−t)]
PV = loan or bond principal, r = periodic interest rate, t = number of payment periods
Rearrange to find PV: PV = A × [1 − (1+r)^(−t)] / r
Example: Mortgage — same monthly payment over 30 years (360 periods)
Instrument Cash Flow Pattern PV Formula Key Feature
Zero-coupon / T-bill Single FV at maturity FV / (1+r)^t No intermediate payments
Coupon bond Periodic PMT + FV at maturity Σ PMT/(1+r)^t + FV/(1+r)^N YTM = single rate for all CFs
Perpetual bond PMT forever PMT / r No maturity date; N → ∞
Mortgage / annuity Level payment A (interest + principal) A = r×PV / [1−(1+r)^(−t)] No separate principal at end

Equity Instruments — 3 Dividend Valuation Models

Equity investments (preferred or common stock) represent ownership with no maturity date. Valued by discounting expected future dividends at the required return r. Unlike bonds, equity cash flows (dividends) are discretionary — not contractually promised.

Cash flows to value: periodic dividends + expected sale price at end of investment horizon. For perpetual holding: sum of all future dividends discounted to present.

Model 1 — Constant Dividends (Zero Growth)

Stock pays a fixed dividend D forever. Price = PV of a perpetuity.

PV_t = D / r
  • Same formula as perpetual bond — just replace PMT with D
  • Applicable to preferred stock with a fixed dividend
  • Also applicable to mature common stocks assumed to pay stable, non-growing dividends
Model 2 — Constant Dividend Growth (Gordon Growth Model)

Dividends grow at constant rate g forever. D(t+1) = D_t × (1+g).

PV_t = D_t(1+g) / (r − g) = D_(t+1) / (r − g)
  • Valid only when r > g (if g ≥ r, formula breaks down — price would be infinite)
  • D_(t+1) = next period's expected dividend (already grown by one period)
  • g = constant perpetual dividend growth rate
  • r = required rate of return (cost of equity)
  • Most commonly tested equity valuation formula on the CFA exam
Model 3 — Changing Growth (Two-Stage Model)

Company grows at short-term rate g_s for first n periods, then transitions to lower long-term rate g_l indefinitely. More realistic for high-growth companies.

PV_t = Σ[D_t(1+g_s)^i / (1+r)^i] + E(S_(t+n)) / (1+r)^n
(i = 1 to n, high-growth phase) (terminal value)
  • Terminal value E(S_(t+n)) = D_(t+n+1) / (r − g_l) = value of stock at end of high-growth phase using Gordon Growth Model
  • g_s > g_l (short-term growth exceeds long-term growth)
  • Step 1: PV each dividend in high-growth phase. Step 2: Compute terminal value at period n. Step 3: PV the terminal value back to today.
  • Sum of both components = total stock price today
⚠️ Gordon Growth Model — Critical Constraint

The Gordon Growth Model requires r > g. If the growth rate equals or exceeds the required return, the formula produces a negative or infinite price — which is meaningless. Also, D_(t+1) is next period's dividend (already one period of growth applied), NOT this period's D_t.

Implied Return — Fixed-Income (YTM)

Yield-to-Maturity (YTM)
The single implied market discount rate that equates the present value of all promised future cash flows to the current market price. It is the IRR of the bond's cash flows. Key assumption: all promised cash flows are received AND reinvested at the same YTM until maturity.
Implied Return Formulas
Discount Bond (solve for r): r = (FV_t / PV)^(1/t) − 1

Coupon Bond (YTM — solve iteratively):
PV = PMT/(1+r)¹ + PMT/(1+r)² + ... + (PMT+FV)/(1+r)^N
→ Cannot solve algebraically; use financial calculator or spreadsheet

Excel YIELD function syntax:
= YIELD(settlement, maturity, rate, pr, redemption, frequency, [basis])
where: pr = price per 100 face value; basis = 0 for US bonds (30/360)
YTM Assumptions: (1) Hold to maturity. (2) No default — all promised payments received. (3) Reinvest all coupons at the same YTM.
If reinvestment rates differ from YTM → actual realized return will differ from YTM.
💡 Bond Price vs. YTM Relationship

Price and YTM move in opposite directions. When market rates rise → bond price falls. When market rates fall → bond price rises. At YTM = coupon rate → price = par. At YTM < coupon → price > par (premium). At YTM > coupon → price < par (discount).

Equity: Implied Return & Implied Growth

Gordon Growth — Rearranged for r and g
Starting from: PV_t = D_(t+1) / (r − g)

Implied Required Return (r): r = D_(t+1) / PV_t + g
r = [Dividend yield based on next period's expected dividend] + [growth rate]

Implied Growth Rate (g): g = r − D_(t+1) / PV_t
g = r − [forward dividend yield]
D_(t+1) / PV_t = forward dividend yield (next period's dividend ÷ current stock price)
Interpretation: required return = dividend yield + capital gain (growth) component
Implied g tells you: "what growth rate does the market expect to justify this price?" Compare vs. analyst's forecast → overvalued or undervalued?
Using Implied r

Useful for understanding what return investors require at the current price. If r implied by price < your required return → stock appears overvalued (not enough compensation for risk).

Using Implied g

Compare implied growth against analyst's growth estimate. If implied g (from price) = 10% but analyst thinks company can only grow at 5% → shares appear overvalued. If implied g < analyst estimate → undervalued.

💡 Key Insight — Market Price Embeds Expectations

Solving for implied r and implied g reveals the market's embedded expectations. This is more useful than asking "what is the price?" — it lets analysts ask "what must be true about growth or required return for this price to be justified?"

P/E Ratio & Its Gordon Growth Connection

Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
A relative valuation metric: share price ÷ earnings per share. Controls for EPS and currency to improve comparability. A P/E of 20 means investors pay 20× EPS for each share — more expensive than a P/E of 10. Used for individual stocks AND stock indexes (S&P 500, FTSE 100, Nikkei 225).
P/E Ratio Derived from Gordon Growth Model
Trailing P/E: PV_t / E_t = (D_t/E_t)(1+g) / (r − g)

Forward P/E: PV_t / E_(t+1) = (D_(t+1)/E_(t+1)) / (r − g)

where D/E = dividend payout ratio (proportion of earnings paid as dividends)
Forward P/E is more commonly used in practice (based on next year's estimated EPS).

P/E is POSITIVELY related to: higher dividend payout ratio, higher expected growth rate (g)
P/E is NEGATIVELY related to: higher required return (r)

Application: Given P/E and payout ratio, solve for implied r OR implied g (assuming the other is known).
💡 P/E Intuition

Think of P/E as the price per unit of earnings — like price per square meter in real estate. Higher growth expectations → higher P/E (investors pay more for future earnings). Higher risk/required return → lower P/E (discount future earnings more heavily). Higher dividend payout → higher P/E (more earnings distributed now).

Cash Flow Additivity & No-Arbitrage

Cash Flow Additivity Principle
The present value of any future cash flow stream indexed at the same point in time equals the sum of the present values of the individual cash flows. This allows comparison of multiple cash flow streams as long as the cash flows occur at the same point in time.
No-Arbitrage Condition
No possibility exists to earn a riskless profit in the absence of transaction costs. Economically equivalent strategies must produce the same payoff and therefore must have the same price. If prices diverge, arbitrageurs exploit the difference until prices converge. Cash flow additivity ensures no-arbitrage pricing.

The key test for no-arbitrage: Two strategies that produce identical future cash flows in ALL scenarios must have the same present value today. If they don't, an arbitrageur can:

  1. Buy the cheaper strategy (invest)
  2. Sell the more expensive strategy (borrow)
  3. Earn a riskless profit with zero net investment

Three applications in this module: (1) Implied forward interest rates, (2) Forward exchange rates, (3) Option pricing via replicating portfolio.

Application 1 — Implied Forward Interest Rates

Setup — Two Equivalent Strategies

An investor has GBP100 to invest for 2 years. Two strategies:

  • Strategy 1: Invest for 2 years at known 2-year spot rate r₂
  • Strategy 2: Invest for 1 year at known 1-year spot rate r₁, then reinvest at the 1-year forward rate F_(1,1)

Both strategies must yield the same FV — otherwise arbitrage exists.

Implied Forward Rate Formula
No-arbitrage condition:
(1 + r₂)² = (1 + r₁)(1 + F_(1,1))

Solving for the implied 1-year forward rate 1 year from now:
F_(1,1) = (1 + r₂)² / (1 + r₁) − 1

General form — forward rate starting at t₁, ending at t₂:
(1 + r_(t₂))^(t₂) = (1 + r_(t₁))^(t₁) × (1 + F_(t₁,t₂−t₁))^(t₂−t₁)
Worked example: r₁ = 2.50%, r₂ = 3.50%
FV₂ = 100 × (1.035)² = GBP107.12
F_(1,1) = (1.035)² / (1.025) − 1 = 107.12/102.50 − 1 = 4.51%

Interpretation: F_(1,1) = 4.51% is the BREAKEVEN reinvestment rate for 1 year starting in year 1.
If actual available rate > 4.51% → profitable to invest short then roll (Strategy 2 wins).
If actual available rate < 4.51% → invest for 2 years directly (Strategy 1 wins).
⚠️ Arbitrage if Forward Rate Is Wrong

If the available 1-year forward rate (say 5%) ≠ implied forward rate (4.51%): (1) Borrow GBP100 for 2 years at 3.5% → owe GBP107.12. (2) Invest for 1 year at 2.5% → GBP102.50, then invest for 1 year at 5% → GBP107.63. (3) Riskless profit = GBP0.51 with zero net investment. Markets eliminate this via price pressure.

Application 2 — Forward Exchange Rates (Covered Interest Parity)

Setup — Two Equivalent Strategies (Continuous Compounding)

Investor has USD1,000, invests for 6 months. Current spot rate: JPY/USD = 134.40. r_USD (6-month) = 2.00%. r_JPY (6-month) = 0.05%. Uses continuous compounding.

  • Strategy 1: Invest USD1,000 in US T-bill at r_USD → receive USD1,010.05 in 6 months [= 1,000 × e^(0.02×0.5)]
  • Strategy 2: Convert USD to JPY at spot rate → invest in JPY T-bill → convert back to USD at forward rate F
    • t=0: USD1,000 → JPY134,400 (at spot 134.40). Invest at 0.05% for 6 months
    • t=T: Receive JPY134,433.60 [= 134,400 × e^(0.0005×0.5)]. Convert at forward F
Forward FX Rate — No-Arbitrage (Covered Interest Parity)
For continuous compounding:
F = S × e^((r_d − r_f) × T)

where: S = spot exchange rate (domestic per foreign)
r_d = domestic risk-free rate (continuously compounded)
r_f = foreign risk-free rate (continuously compounded)
T = time to forward contract maturity (in years)

Worked example: F = 134.40 × e^((0.02−0.0005)×0.5) = 134.40 × e^(0.00975) = 133.096 JPY/USD

For discrete compounding:
F = S × (1 + r_d)^T / (1 + r_f)^T
Both strategies yield USD1,010.05 in 6 months → they are economically equivalent → forward rate MUST be 133.096.
If F ≠ 133.096 → arbitrage opportunity exists (borrow in one currency, invest in the other, lock forward rate).
Note: JPY/USD declined from 134.40 → 133.096 because USD interest rate (2%) > JPY interest rate (0.05%) → USD at forward discount, JPY at forward premium.
💡 Forward FX Intuition — Interest Rate Parity

The currency with the higher interest rate will trade at a forward discount (weaker in the forward market). The currency with the lower interest rate will trade at a forward premium. This ensures no riskless profit from investing in the higher-rate currency — the FX move exactly offsets the interest rate differential.

Application 3 — Option Pricing via Replicating Portfolio

Setup — Binomial Tree (One Period)

Asset current price S₀ = CNY40. Asset can rise 40% to CNY56 OR fall 20% to CNY32. Call option: right to buy at CNY50 (strike price).

  • If price rises to CNY56: option value = 56 − 50 = CNY6 (exercise — buy at 50, sell at 56)
  • If price falls to CNY32: option value = CNY0 (do not exercise — why buy at 50 when market price is 32?)
  • Risk-free rate r = 5%
Replicating Portfolio Method — Step by Step
Step 1 — Find hedge ratio (h): h = (C_u − C_d) / (S_u − S_d) = (6 − 0) / (56 − 32) = 0.25
(Buy 0.25 units of the asset, sell 1 call option)

Step 2 — Verify portfolio is risk-free: V₁^up = 0.25 × 56 − 6 = 14 − 6 = CNY8
V₁^down = 0.25 × 32 − 0 = CNY8 ✓ (same in both scenarios → risk-free)

Step 3 — Set PV of risk-free payoff = current portfolio value: V₀ = V₁ / (1 + r) = 8 / 1.05 = CNY7.619

Step 4 — Solve for option price c₀: V₀ = 0.25 × S₀ − c₀
7.619 = 0.25 × 40 − c₀
7.619 = 10 − c₀
c₀ = CNY2.38
The hedge ratio (0.25) = proportion of underlying asset to hold per option sold to create a risk-free portfolio.
This is a simplified binomial option pricing model — the foundation of more complex option pricing (Black-Scholes).
The no-arbitrage condition: if c₀ ≠ 2.38, a riskless profit could be constructed → market forces c₀ to 2.38.
💡 Key Concepts in Option Pricing via Replicating Portfolio

Replicating portfolio: A combination of the underlying asset and the option (or risk-free borrowing) that produces the same payoff in ALL scenarios → must have the same value as the risk-free asset discounted at the risk-free rate.
Hedge ratio: (C_up − C_down) / (S_up − S_down) = the number of shares of the asset needed per option to make the portfolio risk-free.
Call option value: Always between 0 and the stock price (0 ≤ c ≤ S₀).
