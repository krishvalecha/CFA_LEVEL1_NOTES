01

Arbitrage — Two Key Conditions

Arbitrage
A riskless profit opportunity arising when the same asset — or assets with identical future cash flows — can be simultaneously bought and sold at different prices. Arbitrage exploits violations of the "Law of One Price": identical assets must trade at the same price at the same time. In efficient markets without transaction costs, arbitrage opportunities are quickly exploited and eliminated.

For derivatives, arbitrage arises under two distinct scenarios:

Arbitrage Condition 1 — Law of One Price

Identical assets (or assets with identical cash flows) traded at the same time must have the same price: S₀ᴬ = S₀ᴮ

Bond arbitrage example: Bond A = €99.00; Bond B = €99.15. Both: same issuer, same maturity, same cash flows (€100 at T).

  • At t=0: Sell Bond B short (receive €99.15) → Buy Bond A (pay €99.00) → Pocket €0.15 risk-free today
  • At t=T: Receive €100 from Bond A → Use €100 to buy Bond B to cover the short
  • Result: Zero cash flow at T, +€0.15 at t=0 → riskless profit = pure arbitrage
  • Market forces: Others see this → buy A (price rises) + sell B (price falls) → prices converge until S₀ᴬ = S₀ᴮ
Arbitrage Condition 2 — Present Value Condition

An asset with a known future price must have a spot price equal to the future price discounted at the risk-free rate: S₀ = S_T(1+r)⁻ᵀ

Gold example (Procam): Gold forward price = $1,792.13/oz in 3 months. Risk-free rate = 2%. No-arbitrage spot price = $1,792.13 × (1.02)⁻⁰·²⁵ = $1,783.28.

  • If spot = $1,770 (below $1,783.28): Borrow at 2% → Buy spot at $1,770 → Simultaneously sell forward at $1,792.13
  • At T: Deliver gold at $1,792.13, repay loan principal + interest ($1,770 × (1.02)⁰·²⁵ = $1,778.78)
  • Riskless profit = $1,792.13 − $1,778.78 = $13.35 per ounce
  • Market forces: Spot price rises (buying pressure), forward price falls (selling pressure) → until S₀ = S_T(1+r)⁻ᵀ
★ Two Arbitrage Conditions — Must Memorize
Condition 1: S₀ᴬ = S₀ᴮ
Identical assets (or assets with identical cash flows) → must have same price

Condition 2: S₀ = S_T × (1 + r)⁻ᵀ
Asset with known future price → spot price = present value of future price
Equivalently: S_T = S₀ × (1 + r)ᵀ

These two conditions establish the no-arbitrage relationship between:
Spot prices, Forward commitment prices, and the Risk-Free Rate
Relevant risk-free rate: For most market participants, the relevant risk-free rate is the repo rate (repurchase agreement rate) — the rate at which highly liquid securities (like Treasuries) are used as collateral for short-term borrowing. This is the practical "risk-free rate" in derivative pricing.

Forward price vs. expected future spot price: The forward price is NOT an unbiased forecast of the future spot price — it incorporates the cost of carry (risk-free rate, storage costs, income) but not risk premiums in the traditional sense.
💡 The Arbitrage Mechanism — Market Self-Correction

Arbitrage opportunities are self-eliminating: as traders exploit them, their buying/selling activity moves prices toward equilibrium. In Bond A/B example: buying A raises its price; selling B lowers its price → gap closes. In gold example: buying gold (spot) raises S₀; selling forward lowers F₀(T) → both converge to the no-arbitrage relationship. This is why derivatives are priced using no-arbitrage conditions — any deviation from these prices creates risk-free profits that traders instantly exploit, restoring equilibrium.

Discrete vs. Continuous Compounding

Derivatives can be priced using either discrete or continuous compounding. The CFA curriculum specifies which method to use for each asset class — it is important to use the correct one.

Equation 1 — Discrete Compounding (Future Value)
FV_N = PV × (1 + r)ᴺ
r = stated interest rate per period | N = number of compounding periods
Used for: individual underlying assets (individual stocks, individual bonds, individual commodities)
Example: $1,000 invested for 0.25 years (3 months) at 2% annual rate:
FV = $1,000 × (1.02)⁰·²⁵ = $1,004.98
Equation 2 — Continuous Compounding (Future Value)
FV_T = PV × e^(rT)
e = Euler's number ≈ 2.71828 | r = continuously compounded rate | T = time in years
Used for: portfolio underlyings (equity indexes, fixed-income indexes, commodity indexes), FX forwards
Example: $1,000 invested for 0.5 years at 2% continuously compounded:
FV = $1,000 × e^(0.02 × 0.5) = $1,000 × e^0.01 = $1,010.05
Discrete Compounding — Use For:
  • Individual equity forwards (single stocks)
  • Individual bond forwards
  • Individual commodity forwards (gold, oil)
  • Formula: F₀(T) = S₀(1+r)ᵀ
  • Benefits/costs as PV amounts: F₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(I) + PV₀(C)](1+r)ᵀ
Continuous Compounding — Use For:
  • Equity index forwards (e.g., NIFTY 50, S&P 500)
  • Fixed-income index forwards
  • Commodity index forwards
  • ALL FX forwards
  • Formula: F₀(T) = S₀ × e^(rT)
  • With carry: F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r+c−i)T)
⚠️ Exam Trap — Using Wrong Compounding Method

The exam will test whether you can identify which compounding method to use. The rule: Individual assets → discrete compounding (1+r)ᵀ. Portfolios/indexes and FX → continuous compounding e^(rT). Mixing these up produces different numerical answers. Read the question carefully to identify the type of underlying before applying a formula.

Replication — The Core Pricing Logic

Replication
A strategy in which a derivative's cash flow stream is recreated using a combination of long or short positions in the underlying asset and borrowing or lending at the risk-free rate. If the replicated position produces identical cash flows to the derivative, then by the law of one price (no-arbitrage), they must have the same price. Replication is used to derive the no-arbitrage forward price.

Key insight: A long forward commitment (agree to buy the underlying at F₀(T) in T periods) can be exactly replicated by: borrowing S₀ at the risk-free rate today, buying the underlying at S₀ today, and holding until T.

If both positions produce identical cash flows at time T, they must cost the same today (law of one price). Since the forward costs nothing today (no upfront payment), the cost of the replicating portfolio must also be zero — which means the forward price is exactly the future value of the spot price at the risk-free rate.

Long Forward = Borrow + Buy Underlying

Replication 1: Long Forward ↔ Borrow + Buy (Procam Gold Example)

Setup: S₀ = $1,783.28, r = 2%, T = 0.25 (3 months), F₀(T) = $1,792.13 per oz, 100 oz contract

STRATEGY 1 — Long Forward:
t=0: No cash flow (CF₀ = 0)
t=T: Settle at (S_T − F₀(T)) per ounce
→ S_T=$1,900: Profit = 100×($1,900−$1,792.13) = +$10,787
→ S_T=$1,700: Profit = 100×($1,700−$1,792.13) = −$9,213

STRATEGY 2 — Borrow + Buy:
t=0: Borrow $178,328 at 2% → Buy 100 oz at $1,783.28 (CF₀ = 0 net)
t=T: Sell 100 oz at S_T → Repay loan: $178,328×(1.02)⁰·²⁵ = $179,213
→ S_T=$1,900: Profit = 100×$1,900 − $179,213 = +$10,787 ✓
→ S_T=$1,700: Profit = 100×$1,700 − $179,213 = −$9,213 ✓

Conclusion: The payoffs under both strategies are identical for every possible value of S_T. By the law of one price, the forward price must be exactly F₀(T) = S₀ × (1+r)ᵀ — otherwise, arbitrage opportunities exist.

Long Asset + Short Forward = Risk-Free Return

Replication 2: Long Asset + Short Forward → Earns Risk-Free Rate
Procam buys 100 oz gold at S₀ = $1,783.28 AND simultaneously sells forward at F₀(T) = $1,792.13

t=0: Cash outflow = −$178,328
t=T: Cash inflow = F₀(T) = $179,213 (locked in regardless of S_T)

Rate of return: $179,213 = $178,328 × (1+r)⁰·²⁵
→ r = 2.00% = the risk-free rate ✓

The long asset + short forward position locks in the RISK-FREE RATE as the return,
regardless of what S_T turns out to be at maturity.
💡 The Replication Triangle — Three Equivalent Positions

The no-arbitrage condition creates a triangle of equivalent strategies (when there are no costs or benefits of ownership):
① Long forward at F₀(T) = Long asset + Borrow S₀ at risk-free rate
② Long asset + Short forward = Risk-free bond (earns risk-free rate)
③ Long forward + Risk-free bond (lend PV of F₀(T)) = Long asset

Any two of these positions can be combined to replicate the third. This is the fundamental principle that makes derivative pricing possible — derivatives are priced relative to combinations of the underlying and the risk-free rate.

No-Arbitrage Forward Price — Base Formulas (No Carry Costs)

For an underlying asset with no additional costs or benefits of ownership (no dividends, no storage costs, no income), the forward price is simply the future value of the spot price at the risk-free rate. This is the baseline formula from which all cost-of-carry adjustments are made.

Equation 3 — Forward Price, No Carry Costs (Discrete Compounding)
F₀(T) = S₀ × (1 + r)ᵀ
S₀ = current spot price of underlying | r = risk-free rate per period | T = time to maturity (in years or fraction of year)
This is the no-arbitrage forward price for an asset with no income or costs beyond the opportunity cost.
Examples: non-dividend-paying stocks, zero-coupon bonds, gold (when ignoring storage costs)

Key relationship: F₀(T) > S₀ when r > 0 (positive risk-free rate)
The forward price exceeds spot because of the opportunity cost of capital — the buyer doesn't have to pay now, so they're effectively lending money to the seller until T.
Equation 4 — Forward Price, No Carry Costs (Continuous Compounding)
F₀(T) = S₀ × e^(rT)
Used when the underlying is a portfolio or index (equity index, fixed-income index, commodity index) or for FX forwards.
Equivalent to Equation 3 but using continuous compounding.
For small values of rT, e^(rT) ≈ (1 + r)ᵀ — the two formulas give nearly identical results for short maturities and low rates.
💡 Why F₀(T) > S₀ When r > 0

Think of it this way: if you buy forward (pay F₀(T) at time T), you don't have to put up the money today — you're essentially getting a "free loan" from your counterparty from t=0 to t=T. The counterparty charges you for this by setting F₀(T) = S₀ × (1+r)ᵀ — the future value of the spot price at the risk-free rate. This is the opportunity cost of capital: the seller could have invested S₀ at the risk-free rate during the period, so they demand compensation for not doing so.

💡 Forward Price vs. Forward Value — Don't Confuse Them

Forward price F₀(T): the delivery price agreed at t=0, set so the contract has zero value at inception. Stays fixed for the life of the contract.
Forward value: the current market value of the forward contract AFTER inception, as the spot price and risk-free rate change. At t=0: value = 0. At t>0: value = PV(S_t − F₀(T)) = positive for buyer if S_t > F₀(T), negative if S_t < F₀(T). This distinction is tested in later derivatives modules.

Cost of Carry — The Complete Framework

Cost of Carry
The net of all costs and benefits related to owning an underlying asset for a specific period. It determines the relationship between the spot price and the forward price. When costs of carry are positive (costs exceed benefits), the forward price exceeds the spot price. When negative (benefits exceed costs), the forward price is below the spot price.
Opportunity Cost (Always Present)
  • The risk-free rate of return (r) foregone by tying up capital in the asset
  • Applies to ALL asset classes
  • Always adds to the forward price (since r > 0 typically)
  • Whether or not the investor borrows to buy
Benefits (Reduce Forward Price)
  • Cash flows received by the owner of the underlying
  • Dividends on individual stocks (I) or dividend yield (i) on indexes
  • Coupon income on bonds
  • Convenience yield for physical commodities
  • Reduce forward price because derivative buyer forgoes these benefits
Other Costs (Increase Forward Price)
  • Physical storage costs for commodities (C or c)
  • Insurance costs on physical holdings
  • Transportation costs
  • Spoilage costs for soft commodities (agricultural products)
  • Only apply to physical commodity underlyings
Equation 5 — Forward Price with Known Dollar Costs & Benefits (Discrete)
F₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(I) + PV₀(C)] × (1 + r)ᵀ
PV₀(I) = present value at t=0 of all income/benefits (dividends, coupons) received during [0, T]
PV₀(C) = present value at t=0 of all ownership costs (storage, insurance) paid during [0, T]
Logic: Subtract PV of income (buyer forgoes it), add PV of costs (buyer avoids paying them → seller demands compensation)

If no costs or income: Equation 5 reduces to Equation 3 → F₀(T) = S₀(1+r)ᵀ
If income only (I), no costs (C=0): F₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(I)](1+r)ᵀ → lower than no-income forward price
If costs only (C), no income (I=0): F₀(T) = [S₀ + PV₀(C)](1+r)ᵀ → higher than no-cost forward price
Equation 6 — Forward Price with Rate-Based Costs & Benefits (Continuous)
F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r + c − i) × T)
r = risk-free rate (opportunity cost) — always positive, always adds to forward price
c = cost rate (storage, insurance as annual % of asset value) — positive, adds to forward price
i = income/benefit rate (dividend yield, convenience yield as annual %) — positive, reduces forward price

Used when costs/benefits are expressed as rates rather than specific dollar amounts.
For equity indexes: c=0, i = dividend yield (i) → F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r − i)T)
For FX: c=0, i = foreign rate (rf), r = domestic rate (rd) → see Equation 7
Cost vs. Benefit RelationshipEffect on Forward PriceIntuition
Opportunity Cost + Other Costs > Benefits F₀(T) > S₀ (forward at premium) Holding the asset costs more than owning it benefits → buyer demands a lower entry price (equivalently, seller wants a higher forward price to compensate)
Opportunity Cost + Other Costs = Benefits F₀(T) = S₀ Costs and benefits exactly offset → forward equals spot
Opportunity Cost + Other Costs < Benefits F₀(T) < S₀ (forward at discount) Owning the asset provides net benefits (e.g., high convenience yield or dividend yield exceeds financing cost) → buyer must pay a premium over forward price to have those benefits NOW in the spot market

Equity Forwards — Individual Stocks & Indexes

Individual Stocks — With and Without Dividends

Example: Hightest Capital — UL Share Forward (6 months, r=5%)
CASE 1: No Dividend (I = 0)
F₀(T) = S₀ × (1 + r)ᵀ = €50 × (1.05)⁰·⁵ = €51.23
Opportunity cost = F₀(T) − S₀ = €51.23 − €50 = €1.23

CASE 2: Quarterly dividend of €0.30 (at T=0.25 and T=0.50)
Step 1: Calculate PV of dividends:
PV₀(I) = €0.30 × (1.05)⁻⁰·²⁵ + €0.30 × (1.05)⁻⁰·⁵
= €0.2964 + €0.2928 = €0.5892

Step 2: Apply Equation 5:
F₀(T) = (€50 − €0.5892) × (1.05)⁰·⁵
= €49.4108 × 1.02470 = €50.6310

With dividends: Forward price drops by ~€0.62 relative to no-dividend case.
For 1,000 shares: F₀(T) = €50,631.00
💡 Why Dividends LOWER the Forward Price

The buyer of the forward agrees to purchase shares in the future. During the waiting period, the OWNER of the shares (the seller) collects the dividends — not the buyer. The forward buyer forgoes these dividends. To compensate, the forward price is reduced by the present value of the dividends that the buyer will miss. Intuitively: the forward price is fair only if the buyer is compensated (via a lower entry price) for not receiving the dividends they would have gotten by buying the shares today.

Equity Indexes — Continuous Compounding with Dividend Yield

Example: VFO — NIFTY 50 Index Forward (3 months, r=4%, dividend yield i=2.2%)
Using Equation 6 with c = 0:
F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r − i) × T)
= 15,200 × e^((0.04 − 0.022) × 0.25)
= 15,200 × e^(0.018 × 0.25)
= 15,200 × e^(0.0045)
= 15,200 × 1.004510
= INR 15,268.55

FX Forwards — Covered Interest Rate Parity

Foreign exchange is a special case where both currencies have an opportunity cost — the foreign risk-free rate (r_f) for the foreign currency and the domestic risk-free rate (r_d) for the domestic currency. The FX forward is priced using the interest rate differential between the two currencies — not the absolute rate level.

FX rate notation: S₀,f/d = spot rate expressed as units of foreign currency (price currency, f) per unit of domestic currency (base currency, d). Example: AUD/USD = 1.3335 means 1.3335 AUD buys 1 USD (USD is the base/domestic currency).

Equation 7 — FX Forward Price (Continuous Compounding)
F₀,f/d(T) = S₀,f/d × e^((rf − rd) × T)
rf = foreign currency (price currency) risk-free rate
rd = domestic currency (base currency) risk-free rate
f/d = exchange rate quoted as units of foreign per unit of domestic

The cost of carry for FX is: rf (foreign rate, acts like a "benefit" — like a dividend on the foreign currency) vs. rd (domestic rate, acts like the opportunity cost)
Cost of carry net = (rf − rd)

This is Covered Interest Rate Parity (CIRP) — the no-arbitrage condition linking spot FX rates, forward FX rates, and the interest rate differential.

AUD/USD Forward — Worked Example (Covered IRP)

t=0
Given: AUD/USD spot = 1.3335 (1.3335 AUD = 1 USD)
USD domestic rate r_d = 0.20% p.a. (continuously compounded) | AUD foreign rate r_f = 0.05% p.a.

Borrow USD 1,000 at r_d = 0.20%
Convert to AUD at spot rate: AUD 1,333.50 (= USD 1,000 × 1.3335)
Lend AUD 1,333.50 at r_f = 0.05%
t=T
Receive AUD loan proceeds: AUD 1,333.50 × e^(0.0005 × 0.5) = AUD 1,333.83
Repay USD loan: USD 1,000 × e^(0.002 × 0.5) = USD 1,001

No-arbitrage forward rate: F₀,AUD/USD(T) = AUD 1,333.83 / USD 1,001 = 1.3325
Verify Using Equation 7
F₀,AUD/USD(T) = S₀,AUD/USD × e^((rf − rd) × T)
= 1.3335 × e^((0.0005 − 0.002) × 0.5)
= 1.3335 × e^(−0.00075)
= 1.3335 × 0.999250
= 1.3325 ✓

💡 Interest Rate Parity — The Core Intuition

Countries with higher interest rates see their currency trade at a forward discount. Why? If currency A has a 5% rate and currency B has a 1% rate, you'd borrow in B (cheap) and invest in A (high yield) — earning a 4% differential. But this arbitrage is eliminated by the forward market: A's currency depreciates in the forward market exactly enough to offset the interest rate advantage. Net result: no risk-free profit from currency carry trades in a world of covered interest rate parity.

= 1,300 × 1.002057
= 1,302.67

Commodity Forwards — Storage Costs & Convenience Yield

Physical commodities (gold, oil, grain, metals) are unique because they involve known costs of ownership — storage, insurance, transportation, and potential spoilage (soft commodities). These costs increase the forward price. Physical commodities may also have a convenience yield — a non-cash benefit of holding the physical commodity — which reduces the forward price.

Commodity Forward Price with Storage Costs (Discrete)
F₀(T) = [S₀ + PV₀(C)] × (1 + r)ᵀ
PV₀(C) = present value of storage + insurance costs per unit during [0, T]
Storage costs INCREASE the forward price — the seller of the forward contract would incur these costs if they owned the physical commodity; they demand compensation via a higher forward price.
Gold Storage Example (Procam) — Storage cost $2/oz at end, r=2%, S₀=$1,783.28, T=0.25
Without storage: F₀(T) = $1,783.28 × (1.02)⁰·²⁵ = $1,792.13

With $2/oz storage cost (payable at end):
Step 1: PV₀(C) = $2 × (1.02)⁻⁰·²⁵ = $1.99 per ounce

Step 2: F₀(T) = ($1,783.28 + $1.99) × (1.02)⁰·²⁵
= $1,785.27 × 1.00499
= $1,794.13 per ounce

Storage cost increases forward price by ~$2 (from $1,792.13 to $1,794.13)
Convenience Yield
A non-cash benefit of holding a physical commodity versus a derivative. Arises when physical market conditions make owning the commodity itself valuable beyond its price return. Example: if crude oil inventories are very low, refineries will bid up spot oil prices to ensure supply availability — the spot price rises above what storage costs and interest rates alone would predict. The convenience yield acts like a "dividend" for the physical commodity, reducing the forward price relative to the no-arbitrage price calculated without it.
Commodity Forward Price with Storage Costs and Convenience Yield (Continuous)
F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r + c − y) × T)
r = risk-free rate | c = storage cost rate | y = convenience yield rate
When convenience yield is HIGH relative to storage costs and the risk-free rate: F₀(T) < S₀ (forward price below spot)
This is called backwardation — the futures curve slopes downward.
When convenience yield is LOW: F₀(T) > S₀ — this is contango — futures curve slopes upward.
Contango — F₀(T) > S₀

The normal state for most commodities with storage costs and low convenience yields. Futures price is above spot. Occurs when: carrying costs (r + storage) exceed convenience yield. Futures curve slopes upward by maturity.

Backwardation — F₀(T) < S₀

Futures price is below spot. Occurs when: high convenience yield exceeds carrying costs (r + storage). Signals that physical supply is tight — holders of the physical commodity benefit disproportionately from possession NOW. Common during supply shortages.

💡 F₀ Significantly Below No-Arbitrage Price → Implies Convenience Yield

If you observe a commodity forward price that is significantly below the theoretical no-arbitrage price (calculated using just the risk-free rate and storage costs), this implies the presence of a convenience yield. Market participants are willing to pay a premium for the SPOT commodity (driving up S₀) relative to the forward — reflecting the value of immediate physical availability. The convenience yield bridges the gap between the observed forward price and the theoretical price.

Costs (storage, insurance, domestic rate) → ADD to forward price
Risk-free rate (r or rd) always adds to forward price (opportunity cost, always present)
Discrete compounding: individual stocks, individual bonds, individual commodities
Continuous compounding: equity indexes, FI indexes, commodity indexes, ALL FX
Contango vs. Backwardation: Contango = F₀ > S₀ (normal for commodities with carry). Backwardation = F₀ < S₀ (indicates high convenience yield / supply shortage).
Repo rate is the relevant risk-free rate for most derivative market participants.
★

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Two arbitrage conditions: (1) Identical assets must have the same price: S₀ᴬ = S₀ᴮ (Law of One Price). (2) Asset with known future price must have spot = PV of future price: S₀ = S_T × (1+r)⁻ᵀ.
  • ✅ Arbitrage is self-eliminating: buyers of the cheap asset and sellers of the expensive asset converge prices until the no-arbitrage condition holds. The relevant risk-free rate is the repo rate for most market participants.
  • ✅ Discrete compounding (Eq. 1): FV_N = PV × (1+r)ᴺ → used for individual assets. Continuous compounding (Eq. 2): FV_T = PV × e^(rT) → used for indexes (equity, FI, commodity) and all FX forwards.
  • ✅ Replication: A derivative's cash flow stream can be recreated using a long or short position in the underlying + borrowing/lending at the risk-free rate. Long forward ↔ Borrow S₀ + Buy underlying. Law of one price → identical payoffs → same price → forward price is determined.
  • ✅ Long asset + Short forward = Risk-free bond (earns the risk-free rate, regardless of S_T). This is the fundamental no-arbitrage replication relationship.
  • ✅ Base forward price formula (no carry): F₀(T) = S₀ × (1+r)ᵀ (discrete) or F₀(T) = S₀ × e^(rT) (continuous). Always: F₀(T) > S₀ when r > 0.
  • ✅ Cost of carry = net of all costs and benefits of owning the underlying for T periods. Costs (storage, insurance) increase F₀(T). Benefits (dividends, coupons, convenience yield) decrease F₀(T).
  • ✅ Formula with known dollar amounts (Eq. 5 — Discrete): F₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(I) + PV₀(C)] × (1+r)ᵀ. PV₀(I) = PV of income/benefits; PV₀(C) = PV of costs. Subtract PV(I), add PV(C), then compound.
  • ✅ Formula with rates (Eq. 6 — Continuous): F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r+c−i)T). r=risk-free rate; c=cost rate; i=income rate. All rates annualized.
  • ✅ Non-dividend stock: F₀(T) = S₀(1+r)ᵀ. No benefits to subtract.
  • ✅ Dividend-paying stock (discrete): F₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(I)](1+r)ᵀ. Calculate PV of each dividend individually: PV_i = D × (1+r)^(−t_i). Forward price is LOWER than for the non-dividend case.
  • ✅ Equity index (continuous): F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r−i)T) where i = continuous dividend yield. F₀ > S₀ if r > i; F₀ < S₀ if i > r.
  • ✅ FX forward (Eq. 7 — Covered Interest Rate Parity): F₀,f/d(T) = S₀,f/d × e^((rf−rd)T). rf = foreign (price) currency rate; rd = domestic (base) currency rate. The DIFFERENTIAL determines forward premium/discount — not the absolute rates.
  • ✅ FX forward rules: rf > rd → F₀ > S₀ → foreign currency at DISCOUNT in forward market. rf < rd → F₀ < S₀ → foreign currency at PREMIUM in forward market. Equal rates → forward = spot.
  • ✅ Commodity forward (with storage): F₀(T) = [S₀ + PV₀(C)](1+r)ᵀ or S₀ × e^((r+c)T). Storage costs add to forward price. Always F₀ > S₀ when storage costs are present (contango).
  • ✅ Convenience yield: Non-cash benefit of holding the physical commodity (not a derivative). Reduces the forward price. A forward price significantly below the theoretical no-arbitrage price signals the presence of a convenience yield.
  • ✅ Contango: F₀ > S₀ (normal carry). Costs (r + storage) exceed convenience yield. Futures curve slopes upward. Backwardation: F₀ < S₀. Convenience yield exceeds carrying costs (r + storage). Signals physical supply shortage.
  • ✅ F₀(T) vs. S₀ summary rule: Opportunity cost + other costs > benefits → F₀ > S₀. Equal → F₀ = S₀. Benefits > costs → F₀ < S₀.
  • ✅ All asset classes cost of carry: Non-dividend stock: only r. Dividend stock: r offset by dividends. Equity index: r offset by i (dividend yield). FX: (rf−rd) differential. Commodity: r + storage, offset by convenience yield if present. Bond: r offset by coupon income.
★ COMPLETE QUICK REFERENCE SUMMARY ★
═══ TWO ARBITRAGE CONDITIONS ═══
① Law of one price: identical assets must have same price (S₀ᴬ = S₀ᴮ)
② PV condition: S₀ = future price × (1+r)⁻ᵀ (or × e^(−rT))

═══ BASE FORWARD PRICE FORMULAS ═══
Discrete: F₀(T) = S₀(1+r)ᵀ ← individual assets
Continuous: F₀(T) = S₀ × e^(rT) ← indexes and FX

═══ COST OF CARRY ADJUSTMENTS ═══
Discrete: F₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(I) + PV₀(C)] × (1+r)ᵀ
Continuous: F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r + c − i) × T)
FX (Eq. 7): F₀,f/d(T) = S₀,f/d × e^((rf − rd) × T)

═══ ASSET-SPECIFIC FORWARD PRICES ═══
No-dividend stock: F₀ = S₀(1+r)ᵀ
Dividend stock: F₀ = [S₀ − PV₀(dividends)](1+r)ᵀ
Equity index: F₀ = S₀ × e^((r−i)T) [i = div yield]
FX: F₀,f/d = S₀,f/d × e^((rf−rd)T)
Commodity (carry): F₀ = [S₀ + PV₀(C)](1+r)ᵀ or S₀ × e^((r+c−y)T)

═══ KEY RELATIONSHIPS ═══
Benefits reduce F₀ | Costs increase F₀ | r always increases F₀
Contango: F₀ > S₀ | Backwardation: F₀ < S₀ (high convenience yield)
High foreign rate → foreign currency at forward DISCOUNT (F₀,f/d > S₀,f/d)
Replication: Long forward = Borrow + Buy spot
Long spot + Short forward = Risk-free bond (earns r)
Discrete vs. continuous compounding: Individual assets = (1+r)ᵀ. Portfolios/indexes/FX = e^(rT).
PV₀(I) to calculate dividends: Discount each dividend: PV_i = D_i × (1+r)^(−t_i), then sum all.
Convenience yield signal: F₀ significantly BELOW the cost-of-carry no-arbitrage price → convenience yield implied.
FX rule memory hook: Higher rate currency trades at forward DISCOUNT (it depreciates in forward market to offset its yield advantage — covered interest rate parity).
