01

Measures of Central Tendency — Mean, Median, Mode

Measure of Central Tendency
Specifies where data are centered — the "expected" return based on the observed sample. Addresses the reward dimension. Three main measures: arithmetic mean, median, and mode.
Arithmetic Mean (X̄)

Sum of all observations ÷ n. Most frequently used. Equal weight to every observation.

X̄ = ΣXᵢ / n
  • Sensitive to outliers — pulled by extremes
  • Most mathematically tractable
  • Uses ALL observations equally
Median

Middle value of sorted data. Odd n: position (n+1)/2. Even n: average of n/2 and (n+2)/2 positions.

  • NOT affected by outliers
  • Only ONE median per distribution
  • Preferred for skewed distributions
  • Less mathematically tractable than mean
Mode

Most frequently occurring value. Only measure usable with nominal/categorical data.

  • Can have 0, 1, or multiple modes
  • Unimodal = 1; bimodal = 2; trimodal = 3
  • If all values different → no mode
  • In histograms = tallest bar (modal interval)
  • Continuous distributions may have no mode
💡 Mean vs. Median

Use mean for symmetric distributions without extreme outliers — it uses all information. Use median when outliers are present or distribution is skewed — it is robust to extremes. A large mean−median gap signals skewness or outliers.

02

Dealing with Outliers — Three Options

Option 1 — Do Nothing

Use full data without adjustment. Use when: values are legitimate and the full distribution must be reflected. Outliers may contain meaningful information; excluding them may lose valuable data. Eliminates subjective judgment about what is "extreme."

Option 2 — Trimmed Mean

Delete the extreme observations at both ends. Arithmetic mean after removing a stated % of lowest and highest values.

Example: 5% trimmed mean = remove bottom 2.5% and top 2.5%, average remaining 95%. Used in sports scoring (discard extreme judges' scores).

Option 3 — Winsorized Mean

Replace extreme values with boundary values (nearest non-extreme). Same n, but extremes are capped.

Example: 95% winsorized mean = set bottom 2.5% = 2.5th percentile; top 2.5% = 97.5th percentile; then average all.

💡 Trimmed vs. Winsorized

Trimmed: removes extreme values → smaller effective n in the average. Winsorized: replaces extreme values → same total n but extremes are capped. Both reduce outlier influence. Comparing measures with/without outliers reveals important distributional insights.

Quantiles, IQR & Box and Whisker Plots

Quantile
A value at or below which a stated fraction of the data lies. General term covering quartiles, quintiles, deciles, and percentiles.
QuantileDivides IntoKey PointsNotes
Quartiles4 equal partsQ1 (25th pct), Q2 = median (50th), Q3 (75th)Most commonly used in box plots
Quintiles5 equal parts20th, 40th, 60th, 80th percentilesUsed in factor research
Deciles10 equal parts10th through 90th percentilesRanking companies by market cap
Percentiles100 equal parts1st through 99th percentilesPᵣ = value at/below which k% of data lies
IQR and Box Plot Fences
IQR = Q3 − Q1

Outlier detection (IQR-based fences):
Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR
Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5 × IQR
Observations beyond fences = outliers (plotted separately)
Box = Q1 to Q3 (IQR). Median or mean marked inside box. Whiskers extend to fences.
Simple variant: whiskers extend to max/min values. IQR variant: whiskers to 1.5×IQR, outliers as dots.

Investment applications of quantiles:

  • Performance ranking: Fund managers ranked by percentile within peer group (e.g., Morningstar star ratings based on performance percentiles)
  • Factor research: Companies sorted into deciles by market cap → compare small-cap (D1) vs. large-cap (D10) returns
  • Investment strategy: Sort companies by P/E, sales growth, or profitability into quantiles → study return differences across groups

Measures of Dispersion — Concepts

Dispersion
Variability of outcomes around the central tendency. If mean return addresses reward, dispersion addresses risk and uncertainty. Absolute dispersion = variability without reference to a benchmark. Relative dispersion = variability relative to a reference value.
Range

Max − Min. Simplest measure. Uses only 2 data points. Sensitive to outliers. Cannot describe distribution shape.

Mean Absolute Deviation (MAD)

Average of absolute deviations from the mean. Uses ALL observations. Solves cancellation problem via absolute values. Superior to range but less mathematically tractable than variance.

Sample Variance (s²) and Sample Standard Deviation (s)

Squaring deviations solves the cancellation problem. Variance = average squared deviation. Std dev = √variance (restores original units). Divisor = n−1 (degrees of freedom → unbiased estimate of population variance). Std dev: same units as data. Variance: squared units. Most widely used dispersion measures with known statistical properties.

Target Downside Deviation (Target Semideviation)

Only considers observations below a specified target B (e.g., minimum acceptable return of 6%). Focuses on downside risk — what loss-averse investors care about. Include only Xᵢ ≤ B in the numerator sum. Divisor = total n−1 (not count of sub-target obs).

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

CV = s / X̄. Scale-free (no units). Risk per unit of return. Allows comparison across datasets with different means or units. Lower CV = more efficient (less risk per unit of return). Meaningless if mean is negative.

⚠️ Exam Trap — Divisor n vs. n−1

Sample variance, std dev, target semideviation, and covariance all use n−1. MAD uses n. Target semideviation: sum only sub-target observations in numerator, but divide by total n−1 — not just the count of sub-target observations.

06

Skewness — Asymmetry of the Distribution

Skewness
Degree to which a distribution is asymmetric about its mean. Computed as average cubed deviation from the mean divided by s³. Scale-free. Cubing (unlike squaring) preserves the sign of deviations. Skewness ≈ (1/n) × Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)³ / s³ for large n (≥ 100).
Positive Skewness (Right-Skewed) — Long Right Tail

Mode < Median < Mean (mean pulled right by extreme gains)

  • Frequent small losses, few extreme gains
  • Long tail on the right side
  • Investors PREFER positive skew: limited downside + occasional large upside
  • Examples: venture capital, options, some commodity strategies
Negative Skewness (Left-Skewed) — Long Left Tail

Mean < Median < Mode (mean pulled left by extreme losses)

  • Frequent small gains, few extreme losses
  • Long tail on the left side
  • Investors DISLIKE negative skew: occasional catastrophic losses
  • Examples: selling options, credit portfolios, some hedge fund strategies
Zero Skewness — Symmetric

Mean = Median = Mode. Each side is a mirror image. Normal distribution has zero skewness.

💡 Mnemonic — Mean Chases the Tail

Positive skew: tail points RIGHT → mean pulled RIGHT → mean is LARGEST: Mode < Median < Mean.
Negative skew: tail points LEFT → mean pulled LEFT → mean is SMALLEST: Mean < Median < Mode.
Memory trick: "The mean chases the tail."

07

Kurtosis — Tail Weight of the Distribution

Kurtosis
Measures the combined weight of the tails relative to the rest of the distribution. Computed using 4th power of deviations divided by s⁴. Normal distribution kurtosis = 3.0. Excess kurtosis = kurtosis − 3 (normal = 0).
Kurtosis and Excess Kurtosis (large n ≥ 100)
Kurtosis ≈ (1/n) × Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)⁴ / s⁴

Excess Kurtosis (K_E) = Kurtosis − 3
K_E ≈ [(1/n) × Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)⁴ / s⁴] − 3
Normal distribution: kurtosis = 3.0, excess kurtosis = 0.
Most statistical software reports EXCESS kurtosis but labels it simply "kurtosis." If software says kurtosis = 1.5 → actual kurtosis = 4.5 → fat-tailed.
Leptokurtic — Fat Tails (Kurtosis > 3, Excess K > 0)
  • More weight in tails than normal → more extreme outcomes
  • Also more peaked near the mean
  • Fewer observations in intermediate regions (±1 to ±2.5 std devs)
  • Most equity return series are leptokurtic — fat tails are a stylized fact of financial returns
  • Ignoring fat tails → underestimate probability of extreme outcomes
Platykurtic — Thin Tails (Kurtosis < 3, Excess K < 0)
  • Less weight in tails than normal → fewer extreme outcomes. Flatter, more spread-out shape.
KurtosisExcess KurtosisDescriptionTerm
> 3.0> 0Fatter tails; more extreme outcomes than normalLeptokurtic (fat-tailed)

⚠️ Exam Trap — Software Reports Excess Kurtosis as "Kurtosis"

Many statistical packages report excess kurtosis but label it "kurtosis." If software output says kurtosis = 1.5, this means excess kurtosis = 1.5 (actual kurtosis = 4.5) → fat-tailed distribution. Always check which version is being reported.

08

Normal Distribution — Key Properties

The benchmark against which skewness and kurtosis are measured:

  • Symmetric and bell-shaped. Mean = Median = Mode
  • Completely described by two parameters only: mean (μ) and variance (σ²)
  • Skewness = 0. Kurtosis = 3.0. Excess kurtosis = 0
  • Central role in mean–variance portfolio selection model and financial risk management
Skewness Quick Reference
  • Positive skew: Mode < Median < Mean (right tail)
  • Zero skew (normal): Mode = Median = Mean
  • Negative skew: Mean < Median < Mode (left tail)
  • Investors prefer positive skew (upside surprises)
  • Investors dislike negative skew (downside surprises)
Kurtosis Quick Reference
  • Leptokurtic: K > 3, K_E > 0 (fat tails)
  • Mesokurtic: K = 3, K_E = 0 (normal)
  • Platykurtic: K < 3, K_E < 0 (thin tails)
  • Most equity returns: leptokurtic
  • Ignoring fat tails → underestimate extreme risk
09

Covariance & Correlation

Covariance (s_XY)
Measures how two random variables move together. Positive = move in same direction. Negative = move in opposite directions. Difficult to interpret alone because units are squared.
Covariance and Correlation Coefficient Formulas
Sample Covariance:
s_XY = Σ[(Xᵢ − X̄)(Yᵢ − Ȳ)] / (n−1)

Sample Correlation Coefficient:
r_XY = s_XY / (s_X × s_Y)

Range: −1 ≤ r_XY ≤ +1
Correlation = normalized covariance — divides out the scale/units. Correlation is unitless (−1 to +1).
Covariance uses squared units (e.g., %² if both vars are returns). Divisor = n−1 in both formulas.

Properties of Correlation

  1. Range: −1 ≤ r_XY ≤ +1 always
  2. r = 0: uncorrelated — no linear relationship
  3. r close to +1: strong positive linear. r = +1: perfect positive (all points on upward line)
  4. r close to −1: strong negative (inverse) linear. r = −1: perfect inverse (all points on downward line)
  5. Measures linear relationships ONLY. Strong non-linear relationship can still show near-zero correlation

Scatter Plot Interpretation

  • Tight clustering on upward line → strong positive correlation
  • Tight clustering on downward line → strong negative correlation
  • No pattern → near-zero correlation
  • Non-linear pattern (U-shape, parabola) → do NOT use correlation coefficient — it will mislead
  • Outliers are easily spotted — extreme points near axis ends
10

Limitations of Correlation Analysis

⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation

Even highly correlated variables do not necessarily cause each other. Correlation measures only association (co-movement). Confusing correlation with causation is a fundamental error in statistical reasoning and investment analysis.

Three Types of Spurious Correlation

Type 1 — Chance Relationship

High correlation in a dataset due to random chance — no true underlying relationship. Example: US retail beer/wine/liquor sales vs. atmospheric CO₂ levels (r = 0.824, 2000–2018) — clearly no causal link.

Type 2 — Induced by Third Variable

Two uncorrelated variables may appear correlated when both are divided by a common third variable. Example: dividends and total assets may show low correlation, but both divided by market cap may show artificially high correlation.

Type 3 — Common Driver

Two variables correlated not directly but because both relate to a third variable. Example: height and vocabulary are positively correlated — but age drives both independently.

Additional limitations:

  • Outliers: Correlation is sensitive to extreme values — a few outliers can dramatically change r_XY. May need to trim or winsorize before computing.
  • Non-linear relationships: Correlation only captures linear association. Variables may be perfectly associated non-linearly yet show r ≈ 0 (Anscombe's Quartet).
  • Anscombe's Quartet: Four very different data patterns can have identical means, standard deviations, and correlation coefficients. Summary statistics alone are insufficient — always visualize with scatter plots.
  • Investment strategies based on spurious correlations will appear profitable in backtests but fail in practice.
💡 Always Pair Statistics with Scatter Plots

A single correlation coefficient cannot tell the whole story. Always inspect the scatter plot alongside the number to identify: (1) whether the relationship is truly linear, (2) whether outliers are distorting the measure, and (3) whether there are sub-groups in the data.
