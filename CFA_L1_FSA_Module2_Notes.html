Revenue Recognition — General Principles

A fundamental principle of accrual accounting: revenue is recognized when it is earned — not when cash is received. The company's financial records reflect revenue when the risk and reward of ownership transfers to the customer (typically on delivery of goods or services).

Credit Sales (Delivery Before Cash)

Company delivers goods/services on credit → recognizes revenue immediately + records accounts receivable (an asset). Later, when cash arrives → cash replaces the receivable. Revenue was already recognized at delivery — no double-counting.

Example: Sell $100 of goods on credit → Dr Accounts Receivable $100, Cr Revenue $100. When cash received → Dr Cash $100, Cr Accounts Receivable $100.

Cash in Advance (Cash Before Delivery)

Company receives cash first → records a liability (deferred revenue / unearned revenue). Revenue is recognized over time as products/services are delivered. Example: annual subscription to cloud software paid upfront.

Example: Receive $1,200 subscription fee Jan 1 → Dr Cash $1,200, Cr Deferred Revenue $1,200. Each month recognize $100: Dr Deferred Revenue $100, Cr Revenue $100.

The Converged Revenue Recognition Standard (IFRS 15 / ASC 606)
  • Issued jointly by IASB and FASB in May 2014. Effective from beginning of 2018. Content is nearly identical under both IFRS (IFRS 15) and US GAAP (ASC 606). This was a major convergence — one of the most significant in recent accounting history.
  • Core principle: Revenue should be recognized to "depict the transfer of promised goods or services to customers in an amount that reflects the consideration to which the entity expects to be entitled in an exchange for those goods or services."
  • The standard provides a principles-based approach applicable to many types of revenue-generating activities.
  • One remaining IFRS vs. US GAAP difference: "Probable collectability" threshold. IFRS: probable = more likely than not (>50%). US GAAP: probable = likely to occur (a higher bar). Same contracts may be treated differently under each standard.
  • Revenue should be recognized only when it is highly probable it will not be subsequently reversed. If reversal is likely → record minimal revenue + refund liability + "right to returned goods" asset.
Examiner Mindset — Revenue Recognition Core Principle

The exam tests whether you understand the relationship between cash flows and revenue recognition. The key insight: revenue recognition timing ≠ cash collection timing. Cash collected in advance = liability (deferred revenue), not revenue. Revenue recognized before cash = asset (accounts receivable), not cash. The exam will present scenarios and ask: "When should revenue be recognized?" Always think: when is the performance obligation satisfied? When does control transfer to the customer? NOT when cash changes hands.

IFRS vs. GAAP trap: The "probable collectability" difference means an identical contract might be recognized as revenue under IFRS (more likely than not = just >50%) but not under US GAAP (likely = higher threshold). Economically similar contracts → different accounting treatment.

02

The 5-Step Revenue Recognition Model

①
Identify the Contract(s) with a Customer
A contract is an agreement with commercial substance, establishing each party's obligations and rights including payment terms. A contract exists only if collectability is probable. Contracts can be written, oral, or implied by customary practice. Multiple contracts may be combined if linked.
②
Identify the Separate or Distinct Performance Obligations
A performance obligation = a promise to transfer a distinct good or service. A good/service is distinct if: (a) the customer can benefit from it on its own or with readily available resources, AND (b) the promise to transfer it can be separated from other promises in the contract. Each distinct performance obligation is accounted for separately. This step is critical for contracts with multiple deliverables — software license + support contract = two separate obligations.
③
Determine the Transaction Price
The transaction price = what the seller estimates will be received in exchange for transferring the goods/services. Includes fixed amounts + variable considerations (e.g., discounts, rebates, performance bonuses). Excludes amounts collected on behalf of third parties (e.g., sales taxes). Reflects expectations about collectability. Step 3 addresses AMOUNT.
④
Allocate the Transaction Price to the Performance Obligations
If multiple performance obligations exist, allocate the transaction price to each based on standalone selling prices. If standalone price is not directly observable, estimate it. This allocation determines how much revenue is attributed to each obligation. Step 4 addresses AMOUNT per obligation.
⑤
Recognize Revenue When (or As) the Entity Satisfies a Performance Obligation
Revenue is recognized when control of the good or service transfers to the customer. Control transfers either at a point in time (e.g., delivery of physical goods) or over time (e.g., construction contract, subscription service). Step 5 addresses TIMING.

Indicators that control has transferred: (1) entity has right to payment; (2) customer has legal title; (3) customer has physical possession; (4) customer has significant risks and rewards of ownership; (5) customer has accepted the good/service.
Balance Sheet Items Created by Revenue Recognition
Contract Asset: Revenue recognized BUT payment conditional on future performance
(e.g., multi-stage contract; recognize revenue as stages complete → receivable
only when all obligations met)

Contract Liability: Consideration received BEFORE transferring goods/services
= Deferred Revenue / Unearned Revenue (a LIABILITY, not revenue)

Refund Liability: If revenue reversal is likely → record only minimal revenue +
refund liability + "right to returned goods" asset (at carrying value less
costs of recovery)
Steps 3 and 4 address AMOUNTS. Step 5 addresses TIMING. Simple one-deliverable contracts are straightforward. Complex contracts (multi-period, multiple components, changing terms) require more judgment in applying all five steps.
Examiner Mindset — The 5-Step Model

The exam frequently presents a scenario and asks which step is being applied, or asks when revenue should be recognized. Key instincts: Step 2 (identify performance obligations) is crucial for multi-deliverable contracts — the exam often gives a bundled deal (software + 1 year support) and asks: how many performance obligations? Each distinct deliverable = one obligation. Step 5 (timing) is the most practically important — revenue is recognized when control transfers, not when the invoice is sent or cash is received. "At a point in time" vs. "over time" is a frequently tested distinction — construction contract = over time; sale of goods at a store = at a point in time.

Revenue Recognition — Specific Applications

① Principal vs. Agent

Principal vs. Agent — Revenue Gross vs. Net
  • Principal: Controls the product before transfer to customer. Records revenue as the GROSS amount of consideration received.
  • Agent: Arranges for transfer of a product controlled by a third-party seller. Records revenue as the NET amount — only its fee or commission.
  • Net income is the same in both cases; but gross revenue and margins look very different.
Worked Example — Principal vs. Agent (MegaDigital, same product at $100)

Sales / Revenue$100100%$30 (commission only)100%
Cost of Sales$7070%$00%
Gross Profit$3030%$30100%
SG&A$1010%$1033%
Net Profit$2020%$2067%

Key insight: Same absolute profit ($20) but radically different margins. Agent: lower revenue, zero COGS, higher gross and net margins. Principal: higher revenue, higher COGS, lower margins. For companies like Amazon/Shopee that mix principal and agent sales — the mix matters enormously for margin analysis. If agent sales increase → revenue shrinks but margins expand → doesn't mean profitability changed.

Examiner Mindset — Principal vs. Agent Impact on Analysis

This is a high-frequency exam topic because it looks like the company's revenue and margins changed when only the mix of principal vs. agent sales shifted. The correct analyst instinct: evaluate the proportion of principal vs. agent sales separately. If an e-commerce company reports lower revenue growth but expanding margins, it may simply be shifting more sales to agent/marketplace arrangements (where it only reports commissions). Net income may be unchanged. The exam will often ask: "Which measure is LEAST affected by a shift from principal to agent sales?" → Net income (it's the same). "Which is MOST affected?" → Revenue and gross profit margin.

② Franchising / Licensing

Franchise royalties: Recognized as revenue when earned (typically as a % of franchisee sales, e.g., 5.5%). The franchisor recognizes royalty revenue, NOT the total sales of franchisees' restaurants.

Upfront franchise fees for opening new units: Initially recorded as deferred revenue (liability). Then amortized to revenue on a straight-line basis over the term of the franchise agreement (typically 10 years). This reflects the performance obligation being satisfied over time as the franchise operates.

Supply chain revenues (food, equipment, supplies sold to franchisees): Recognized upon delivery or shipment per shipping terms — a simple point-in-time recognition.

③ Software: License vs. SaaS

Software License (Perpetual / Term)

If the software is sold "as is" (no ongoing significant updates by the vendor that affect the customer) → revenue recognized at the point of transfer (when the license is made available to the customer). One-time revenue spike.

Support/update contracts: separate performance obligation → revenue recognized over the support term (ratably).

Software as a Service (SaaS / Cloud)

Customer accesses constantly updated software without taking possession → revenue recognized over the contract term. Subscription = series of performance obligations satisfied over time. More predictable, recurring revenue stream.

IFRS 15: license revenue reported over term IF: provider's activities significantly affect the software AND customer is exposed to those impacts AND activities don't result in a transfer of goods/services.

💡 Software Revenue Recognition — Why the Mix Matters

A company shifting from license software (point-in-time revenue recognition) to SaaS subscriptions (over-time recognition) will report LOWER revenue in the transition years — even if the business is fundamentally growing. This is because license revenue is recognized upfront; subscription revenue is spread over 12-36 months. Analysts must understand the composition of software revenue (license vs. SaaS) to properly interpret revenue growth trends during this transition.

④ Long-Term Contracts — Revenue Over Time

Long-Term Contracts — Performance Over Time (AVI Weapons System Example)
  • A performance obligation is satisfied over time if any ONE of these criteria is met: (a) customer simultaneously receives and consumes benefits as entity performs (routine services); (b) entity's performance creates/enhances an asset the customer controls as it's created; (c) entity's performance does NOT create an asset with alternative use to the entity AND entity has an enforceable right to payment for performance completed.
  • Progress toward completion measured by: output methods (appraisals, units completed) or input methods (costs incurred relative to estimated total costs — most common).
  • Revenue = Cost incurred × (Total contract price ÷ Total estimated costs). Equivalently: % complete × Total revenue.

⑤ Bill and Hold Arrangements

A "bill and hold" arrangement: the seller invoices (bills) the customer but retains physical custody of the goods (holds) because the customer cannot take immediate delivery (e.g., lack of storage space). Revenue can be recognized before physical delivery IF all of these criteria are met:

  • The reason for the bill and hold must be substantive (customer-requested, not seller-initiated)
  • The product must be separately identified as belonging to the customer
  • The product must be ready for physical transfer to the customer
  • The seller cannot use the product or direct it to another customer
Examiner Mindset — Bill and Hold Revenue Recognition

Bill and hold arrangements are a common revenue manipulation technique — sellers ship paper invoices but keep goods, inflating revenue. The exam tests whether you know the conditions that legitimize bill and hold recognition. All four criteria must be met. The most important: the reason must be substantive (customer needs it, not seller's convenience) and the seller must have NO ability to redirect the goods to another customer. If the seller can still sell those goods elsewhere, the risk/reward hasn't truly transferred → no revenue recognition. Red flag for analysts: sudden increase in bill and hold sales, especially near quarter end.

Expense Recognition — General Principles

A company recognizes expenses in the period it consumes the economic benefits of an expenditure, or loses a previously recognized benefit. Three models govern when expenses are recognized:

ModelPrincipleExamples
Matching Principle Expenses recognized in the same period as the associated revenues. Direct cause-and-effect linkage between revenues and expenses. Cost of goods sold (matched to the period the goods are sold), sales commissions (matched to the sales they generate)
Expensing as Incurred (Period Costs) Costs that less directly relate to revenues are expensed when incurred (either when cash is paid or liability is created). Systematic allocation not required. Administrative expenses, management salaries, IT expenses, R&D costs, maintenance and repair costs, most payroll
Capitalization + Depreciation/Amortization Long-lived asset costs capitalized on the balance sheet; expensed (depreciated/amortized) over the asset's useful life. A form of the matching principle — costs matched to the periods benefited. Equipment, buildings, vehicles, patents, software development costs (post-feasibility); interest on long-period construction projects
💡 IFRS Note: "Matching Concept" not "Matching Principle"

IFRS do not formally refer to a "matching principle" — they use the term "matching concept" or describe a "process resulting in matching of costs with revenues." The practical effect is the same, but the terminology differs from US GAAP. On the exam, if asked specifically about IFRS language, use "matching concept." If the question is about the principle itself (COGS matched to revenue), the mechanics are identical under both standards.

Implications of Expense Recognition Choices for Analysts

Key Areas Where Judgment Affects Reported Expenses
  • Uncollectible accounts (bad debt allowance): Estimated as a % of sales or receivables. Higher estimate → higher expense → lower income. Changes should be explained by business changes (e.g., riskier customers), not just by management's desire to manage earnings.
  • Warranty expenses: Estimated and accrued when sale occurs (matching). A company underestimating warranty provisions → lower expenses → higher current income → future earnings hit when claims come in.
  • Estimated useful lives of depreciable assets: Longer useful life → lower annual depreciation → higher income. Companies may extend useful life estimates to boost earnings. Compare depreciation/asset ratios across peers.
  • Salvage/residual value: Higher residual value → lower depreciable base → lower annual depreciation → higher income.
  • Significant year-to-year changes OR cross-company differences in these estimates should trigger analyst investigation: is it driven by genuine business changes or earnings management?
Examiner Mindset — Expense Recognition Estimates

The exam loves asking about the relative conservatism of expense recognition policies. More conservative = recognize expenses sooner/larger. Less conservative = recognize expenses later/smaller, boosting current income. Key pattern: if two companies in the same industry show dramatically different warranty expense rates or bad debt allowances, and there's no clear business reason (different customer base, different product quality), the analyst should question whether one company is managing earnings. The correct exam answer when comparing companies with different estimates: "characterize the policies as more or less conservative and qualitatively assess the impact on ratios." When monetary adjustment is possible, make it. When not, describe directional impact.

Capitalizing vs. Expensing — Impact on Financial Statements

Summary: Capitalizing vs. Expensing — Direction of Impact
Capitalizing (vs. expensing) in the CURRENT period results in:
→ Higher net income (lower current expenses — asset created instead)
→ Higher total assets (capitalized asset on balance sheet)
→ Higher shareholders' equity (higher retained earnings)
→ Higher cash flow from OPERATIONS (cost is investing outflow, not operating)
→ Lower cash flow from INVESTING (asset appears here instead)

This enhancing effect CONTINUES as long as capital expenditures > depreciation expense
(i.e., when the company keeps buying more assets than it depreciates)

Expensing (vs. capitalizing) in the CURRENT period:
→ Lower current net income (full cost hits P&L now)
→ Lower total assets (no asset created)
→ Lower CFO (cost flows through operations, not investing)
→ But: HIGHER future income + better profit trend (no future depreciation drag)
→ If tax same for both: expensing also gives earlier tax savings → more cash sooner
Cumulative total income over the full asset life is IDENTICAL whether capitalized or expensed. The difference is only TIMING. Capitalizing = front-loads income. Expensing = back-loads income (creates improving trend). Same total equity at end of asset life.
Examiner Mindset — Capitalize vs. Expense: The Most Tested Distinction

This is one of the highest-frequency calculation topics in FSA. Know the direction of impact for every financial statement line. The exam often asks: "Which of the following would be HIGHER if a company capitalized rather than expensed a cost?" or "Compared to a company that expenses, a company that capitalizes would report higher ____."

Critical cash flow trap: Capitalizing → cost appears as INVESTING outflow (capex). Expensing → cost appears as OPERATING outflow. So capitalizing artificially inflates operating cash flow! Analysts must be alert to companies capitalizing costs that should be expensed — it makes CFO look better than it really is. This is a red flag for earnings quality.

Profit trend trap: A company that expenses everything NOW will show IMPROVING trends later (no future depreciation drag). A company that capitalizes now will show DECLINING trends later as depreciation rolls through. An improving profit trend from a company that expenses everything heavily upfront is more genuine than one from a company that capitalizes everything.

Capitalization of Interest Costs

Companies must capitalize interest costs associated with acquiring or constructing an asset that requires a long period of time to get ready for its intended use. This interest is then depreciated as part of the asset's cost over its useful life — it appears as depreciation expense, not interest expense.

Interest Capitalization — Key Analyst Adjustments
Problem 1 — Interest Coverage Ratio:
Standard: EBIT / Interest Expense (only expensed interest)
Correct: EBIT / (Expensed interest + Capitalized interest) = EBIT / Gross interest
→ Rating agencies (e.g., S&P) use GROSS interest in coverage ratios
→ Capitalized interest appears in INVESTING cash flows; expensed interest in OPERATING (US GAAP)
or OPERATING/FINANCING (IFRS choice)

Problem 2 — Depreciation of Previously Capitalized Interest:
If income includes depreciation of prior-period capitalized interest → add back to income
to get a clean view of operating profitability without distortion from past interest capitalization

Rule: Use TOTAL interest (capitalized + expensed) in ALL solvency ratio calculations
"Including capitalized interest in coverage ratios provides a better assessment of solvency"
Capitalized interest flows through INVESTING cash outflows (as part of capex), NOT operating cash flows. Expensed interest reduces OPERATING cash flows (US GAAP) or OPERATING/FINANCING (IFRS). This affects comparisons between companies with different approaches to interest capitalization.

Capitalization of Internal Development Costs

IFRS Treatment

Research phase: Always expensed (too early to establish technical feasibility).
Development phase: Capitalized if specific criteria are met: technical feasibility established, intent and ability to complete, ability to use or sell, probable future economic benefits, adequate resources, ability to reliably measure the expenditure.

Result: IFRS companies may have capitalized development assets on their balance sheets. These are subsequently amortized over useful life.

US GAAP Treatment

Both R&D expensed: Research and development costs are generally expensed as incurred. Exception: software development costs after technological feasibility is established may be capitalized.

Microsoft example: determines technological feasibility only shortly before product release → effectively expenses all development costs. This is more conservative than IFRS companies that capitalize development.

💡 Analyst Adjustment for Capitalized Development Costs

To compare a company that capitalizes development costs (IFRS) with one that expenses them (US GAAP), adjust the capitalizing company: (1) Income statement: add current-period development costs as an expense; remove amortization of prior-period capitalized costs. (2) Balance sheet: remove capitalized software (reduce assets and equity). (3) Cash flow: decrease CFO and decrease capex by the current-period development costs. This makes the two companies comparable. If current-period development spending exceeds prior-period amortization → expensing would lower income vs. capitalizing.

Non-Recurring Items — Separating Persistent from Transitory Earnings

To forecast a company's future earnings, an analyst must separate: (a) items likely to continue in future periods (persistent earnings), and (b) items not likely to continue (transitory/non-recurring earnings). Some items are clearly non-recurring and are separately disclosed on the income statement.

Item TypeIFRS TreatmentUS GAAP TreatmentAnalyst Action
Unusual or Infrequent Items Material items relevant to understanding performance → disclosed separately. May include restructuring charges, asset disposal gains/losses, settlement receipts, acquisition integration costs, asset impairments. Since Dec 15, 2015: material unusual OR infrequent items (not both as before) → shown as part of continuing operations but presented separately on the income statement. Do NOT automatically ignore all unusual items. Assess likelihood of recurrence. Some "unusual" items (e.g., restructuring charges at companies that restructure every few years) are semi-recurring. Adjust forecasts accordingly.
Discontinued Operations Results reported separately, net of tax, at the bottom of the income statement. Assets/liabilities aggregated as "held for sale" on balance sheet. Same treatment: separately reported, net of tax. Shown on per-share basis too. Must be operationally and physically separable. Exclude from future earnings forecasts after disposal date. Continuing operations (remaining business) are what the analyst forecasts. Discontinued operations = zero contribution post-sale.
Changes in Accounting Policy Retrospective application required (unless impractical) — restate all prior periods shown as if new policy was always used. Notes disclose change and justification. Same: retrospective application. Companies may also use "modified retrospective" approach (adjust opening retained earnings, don't restate prior periods) — permitted for certain standards like the new revenue recognition standard. Financial statements within the report ARE comparable (retrospective restatement). But compare with companies that adopted differently. For modified retrospective adoption: pre-adoption years are not comparable.
Changes in Accounting Estimates Prospective treatment — affects current and future periods only. No restatement. Significant changes disclosed in notes. Same: prospective only. Not shown on the face of the income statement — disclosed in notes. Prior periods are NOT restated → trend analysis is distorted. Must identify estimate changes in notes and adjust manually for meaningful trend analysis. Red flag if estimate changes coincide with earnings targets.
Correction of Prior Period Error Restate all prior period financial statements (balance sheet, equity statement, cash flows). Disclosures required. Same: full restatement of prior period statements presented. Disclosures required. Restatements reveal weaknesses in accounting systems and financial controls. Examine carefully. Multiple restatements = serious red flag for earnings quality and management integrity.
Changes in Scope (Acquisitions) Consolidate from closing date. May materially affect comparability with prior periods. Not required to be separately disclosed. Same. Companies may voluntarily disclose organic growth rates excluding acquisition effects. Compare constant-currency, organic revenue growth rates disclosed in MD&A. Acquisition effects can make revenue growth look much higher than underlying business performance.
Examiner Mindset — Non-Recurring Items

Discontinued operations: The exam will often say "net income includes a gain from discontinued operations of $X." The correct action: exclude this from earnings forecasts going forward. Continuing operations EPS is the relevant figure for valuation. Accounting policy change: Retrospective = prior periods restated → statements in the report ARE comparable. Modified retrospective = prior periods NOT restated → prior vs. current year not fully comparable. Accounting estimate change: Prospective only → prior periods NOT restated → creates artificial trend if estimate changed mid-analysis. Common trap: "Which items would be presented net of taxes on the income statement?" → Discontinued operations are ALWAYS presented net of taxes. Unusual/infrequent items are presented separately BEFORE tax within continuing operations (not net of tax).

Basic Earnings Per Share (EPS)

Simple vs. Complex Capital Structure
  • Simple capital structure: No potentially dilutive financial instruments. Basic EPS = Diluted EPS.
  • Complex capital structure: Has instruments potentially convertible into common stock — convertible bonds, convertible preferred stock, employee stock options, warrants. These could dilute (decrease) EPS if converted/exercised.
  • Dilution = when conversion/exercise would DECREASE EPS. Antidilution = when it would INCREASE EPS (excluded from diluted EPS calculation).
  • Both basic AND diluted EPS must be reported (for continuing operations and in total). Diluted EPS ≤ Basic EPS always.
  • IFRS: ordinary shares. US GAAP: common stock / common shares. Same concept — the residual equity class subordinate to all others.
Basic EPS Formula
Basic EPS = (Net Income − Preferred Dividends) ÷ Weighted Average Shares Outstanding

Numerator = Net income MINUS preferred dividends paid (common shareholders get what's left)
Denominator = Weighted average common shares outstanding during the period

Weighting shares: each block of shares × fraction of year outstanding
Example: 2,000,000 shares for full year, repurchased 100,000 on July 1:
WASO = 2,000,000 × (6/12) + 1,900,000 × (6/12) = 1,000,000 + 950,000 = 1,950,000

IMPORTANT — Stock splits & stock dividends: reflected RETROACTIVELY to beginning of period
(as if the split/dividend always existed — no time weighting needed for splits/dividends)
Preferred dividends are subtracted because EPS relates only to COMMON shareholders. The preferred shareholders get their dividends first; what remains belongs to common shareholders. If there are cumulative preferred dividends — subtract them regardless of whether declared. If non-cumulative — only subtract if declared.
Worked Example — Basic EPS with Weighted Average Shares
EventDateSharesFraction of YearWeighted Shares
Beginning of yearJan 1500,0006/12250,000
Issue new sharesJul 1+100,000 = 600,0006/12300,000

If Net Income = $1,100,000 and Preferred Dividends = $100,000:
Basic EPS = ($1,100,000 − $100,000) ÷ 550,000 = $1,000,000 ÷ 550,000 = $1.82

Examiner Mindset — Basic EPS Calculation Traps

Trap 1 — Stock splits: If a 2-for-1 stock split occurs mid-year, you do NOT time-weight it. You retroactively apply it to the beginning of the year (and to prior year comparatives). So if there were 500,000 shares from Jan–Jun, then a 2-for-1 split, treat as if 1,000,000 shares were outstanding the whole year. Trap 2 — Preferred dividends: Always subtract preferred dividends from net income in the numerator, even if the company had net income. Preferred shareholders get paid first. Trap 3 — Share repurchase: Repurchased shares REDUCE the weighted average. A repurchase on July 1 means shares only exist for 6/12 of the year. Trap 4 — EPS from continuing operations: Calculated using ONLY income from continuing operations (not total net income). Discontinued operations EPS shown separately.

Diluted EPS — The If-Converted Method

Used for convertible preferred stock and convertible debt. The if-converted method asks: "What would EPS have been if the convertible securities had converted at the BEGINNING of the period?" Two adjustments are made — one to the numerator (income available) and one to the denominator (shares outstanding).

If-Converted Method — Convertible Preferred Stock
Diluted EPS = Net Income ÷ (WASO + New shares from preferred conversion)

Numerator adjustment: ADD BACK preferred dividends (as if they were not paid)
→ Net income (not net income minus preferred dividends)
Denominator adjustment: ADD new common shares that would be issued on conversion
(assumed converted at BEGINNING of period)

Logic: If preferred converted → no preferred dividends → more income for common shareholders
BUT more shares outstanding → net effect may be dilutive or antidilutive
If-Converted Method — Convertible Debt (Bonds)
Diluted EPS = (Net Income + After-tax interest on convertible debt − Preferred dividends)
÷ (WASO + New common shares issued on conversion)

Numerator adjustment: ADD BACK after-tax interest on the convertible debt
= Interest expense × (1 − tax rate)
(if bonds converted → no more interest payments → income increases)
Denominator adjustment: ADD new common shares from bond conversion
(assumed converted at BEGINNING of period)
The key difference between preferred and debt conversion: for preferred, you add back preferred dividends (after-tax, since dividends are not tax-deductible). For convertible debt, you add back AFTER-TAX interest (interest IS tax-deductible, so the tax savings are lost if bonds convert — hence after-tax, not pre-tax).
Worked Example — If-Converted: Convertible Debt

Examiner Mindset — If-Converted Method

The single most important trap: For convertible DEBT — add back AFTER-TAX interest (interest is tax-deductible → the tax shield disappears if debt is converted → income increases by less than the gross interest). For convertible PREFERRED — add back the preferred dividends as-is (no tax adjustment needed because dividends are NOT tax-deductible — there's no tax shield to lose). Always assume conversion at the BEGINNING of the period — regardless of when the securities were actually issued. This is what the "if-converted" method means. Quick check: If diluted EPS from the formula > basic EPS → security is antidilutive → do NOT include it → reported diluted EPS stays at basic EPS level.

Diluted EPS — The Treasury Stock Method (Options & Warrants)

Used for stock options, warrants, and equivalents. The treasury stock method assumes: (1) the options/warrants are exercised and the company receives the exercise proceeds; (2) the company uses those proceeds to repurchase shares at the average market price during the period. The net effect = incremental shares issued (shares issued on exercise MINUS shares repurchased with proceeds).

Key difference from if-converted: No change to the NUMERATOR (net income). Only the denominator changes — by the NET incremental shares.

Treasury Stock Method — Step by Step
Step 1: Shares issued on exercise = number of options/warrants exercised (assumed all exercised)
Step 2: Proceeds received = Exercise price × Number of options
Step 3: Shares repurchased = Proceeds ÷ Average market price during the period
Step 4: Net incremental shares = Shares issued (Step 1) − Shares repurchased (Step 3)

Diluted EPS = (Net income − Preferred dividends) ÷ (WASO + Net incremental shares)

Numerator: NO change (exercise of options doesn't affect net income directly)
Denominator: WASO + net incremental shares from option exercise
Under IFRS, the method is identical but not called "treasury stock method." IFRS refers to "inferred shares" — shares the company would have issued at average market price if it received the exercise proceeds from issuing new shares at market. The result (net incremental shares) is mathematically identical to the US GAAP treasury stock method.

💡 When Are Options Dilutive? The Exercise Price vs. Market Price Rule

Options/warrants are dilutive only when the exercise price is BELOW the average market price (i.e., they are "in-the-money"). When exercise price > market price → exercising options would be irrational (you'd buy shares at a higher price than market) → they are antidilutive → excluded from diluted EPS. When exercise price < market price → company raises less money from exercise than it costs to buy shares back → net increase in shares → dilutive. This makes intuitive sense: in-the-money options create shareholder dilution; out-of-the-money options do not.

Examiner Mindset — Treasury Stock Method

The exam almost always provides a full numerical question for this. Memorize the four steps cold: (1) shares issued on exercise; (2) proceeds = shares × exercise price; (3) shares repurchased = proceeds ÷ average market price; (4) net incremental shares = issued − repurchased. No numerator change — options exercise doesn't directly affect net income. Key trap: If the question says options are "out of the money" (exercise price > market price) → antidilutive → don't include → diluted EPS = basic EPS. IFRS naming trap: IFRS doesn't call it "treasury stock method" — but the math is identical. The exam may ask which method IFRS uses for options — answer: the same method as treasury stock (just unnamed under IFRS), or "inferred shares method."

Antidilutive Securities

A security is antidilutive if including it in the diluted EPS calculation would result in an EPS higher than basic EPS. Since diluted EPS should show the maximum potential dilution, antidilutive securities are EXCLUDED from the diluted EPS calculation. Diluted EPS is always ≤ Basic EPS.

Worked Example — Antidilutive Convertible Preferred (Dim-Cool Utility Company, from LES)
ItemBasic EPSIf-Converted (Test)
Net income$1,750,000$1,750,000
Preferred dividends−$200,000 (20,000 shares × $10)$0 (added back — as if converted)
Numerator$1,550,000$1,750,000
WASO500,000500,000
New shares (20,000 pref × 3)—+60,000
Denominator500,000560,000
EPS$3.10$3.13 ← EXCEEDS basic EPS → ANTIDILUTIVE

Result: Antidilutive. Reported diluted EPS = $3.10 (same as basic EPS). The preferred conversion is excluded.

💡 Why Are These Preferred Securities Antidilutive?

The preferred dividend ($200,000) is large relative to the new shares issued (60,000). Adding back $200,000 to the numerator and only 60,000 to the denominator boosts EPS. Intuitively: the preferred shareholders are receiving expensive dividends. If they converted, the company would save those dividends — benefiting common shareholders more than the dilution from new shares hurts them. This "beneficial conversion" situation is antidilutive — it increases EPS, so it's excluded. The key test: always calculate the if-converted EPS and compare it to basic EPS. If higher → antidilutive → exclude.

Common-Size Analysis & Income Statement Ratios

Common-size income statement: Each line item expressed as a percentage of revenue (net sales). Eliminates size differences between companies, enabling meaningful cross-company (cross-sectional) and over-time (time-series) comparisons. Also called vertical analysis.

Key Income Statement Ratios
Gross Profit Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue
= (Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue
Measures: % of each revenue dollar retained after direct production costs

Operating Profit Margin = Operating Profit (EBIT) ÷ Revenue
Measures: % retained after all operating costs (including D&A, SG&A, R&D)

Pretax Margin = Profit before Tax ÷ Revenue

Net Profit Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue
(also: profit margin, return on sales)
Measures: % of each revenue dollar that becomes profit after ALL costs

Effective Tax Rate = Income Tax Expense ÷ Pretax Income
(compare to revenue is less meaningful; compare to pretax income)
All margins: higher = more profitable (generally). BUT differences in gross margin across companies reflect strategic differences, not just efficiency. A company with high R&D and advertising spend may have high gross margins (premium products) and lower net margins (heavy investment in brand/tech). Don't judge on gross margin alone.

Key insights from common-size analysis: (1) Company C is as profitable as Company A (identical margins) despite being 1/5 the size. (2) Company B has the worst margins — cheap products, no brand investment. (3) Companies A and C spend heavily on R&D and advertising → premium/differentiated products → high gross margins. (4) Absolute operating profit dollars are misleading — Company B has more dollars ($1.5m) but lower % (15%) vs. Company C ($400k at 20%).

Examiner Mindset — Common-Size Analysis

Common-size analysis is tested through interpretation questions: "Which of these two companies has better profitability?" The correct answer uses PERCENTAGE terms, not absolute dollars. A small company with 20% margins is more profitable per dollar of sales than a large company with 15% margins, even if the large company has more absolute profit. The exam also tests understanding of what DRIVES margin differences: high gross margins + high R&D/advertising → premium differentiated strategy. Low gross margins + low operating expenses → commodity/low-cost strategy. Both can be valid competitive positions. Tax rate exception: Taxes should be expressed as % of pretax income (effective tax rate), NOT as % of revenue — the effective tax rate is what's meaningful for comparison and forecasting.

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Accrual principle: Revenue recognized when EARNED (performance obligation satisfied), not when cash received. Cash before delivery → deferred revenue (liability). Delivery before cash → accounts receivable (asset).
  • ✅ 5-Step revenue model (IFRS 15 / ASC 606, converged 2014, effective 2018): ① Identify contract(s) ② Identify performance obligations ③ Determine transaction price ④ Allocate price to obligations ⑤ Recognize revenue when obligation is satisfied. Steps 3–4 = amounts; Step 5 = timing.
  • ✅ Probable collectability: IFRS = more likely than not (>50%). US GAAP = likely (higher threshold). Same contract may be recognized differently under each standard.
  • ✅ Control transfer indicators: Right to payment, legal title, physical possession, risks and rewards of ownership, customer acceptance.
  • ✅ Principal vs. agent: Principal → gross revenue. Agent → net revenue (commission only). Same absolute profit; radically different margins. Shift from principal to agent → lower revenue, higher margins, same net income.
  • ✅ Long-term contracts: Revenue recognized over time (if criteria met). Input method: % complete = costs incurred ÷ total estimated costs. Revenue = % complete × contract price. Cost of sales = costs incurred.
  • ✅ Bill and hold: Revenue recognized before physical delivery IF: substantive reason (customer-requested), product separately identified, ready for transfer, seller cannot redirect to another customer. All four criteria required.
  • ✅ Three expense recognition models: Matching (COGS with revenues), expensing as incurred (R&D, admin), capitalization + depreciation/amortization (long-lived assets).
  • ✅ Capitalize vs. expense: Capitalize → higher current income, higher assets, higher equity, higher CFO (cost goes to investing, not operating). Expensing → lower current income, better future profit trend, lower CFO. Total income over asset life = IDENTICAL. Capitalizing inflates CFO — red flag for earnings quality.
  • ✅ Interest capitalization: Must capitalize interest on long-period construction projects. Use GROSS interest (expensed + capitalized) in coverage ratio calculations. Capitalized interest = investing cash outflow; expensed interest = operating (US GAAP) or operating/financing (IFRS).
  • ✅ Development costs: IFRS = research expensed; development capitalized if criteria met. US GAAP = both generally expensed (except post-feasibility software development).
  • ✅ Non-recurring items: Unusual/infrequent = in continuing operations, separately presented. Discontinued ops = net of tax, bottom of income statement, "held for sale" on B/S. Exclude from future earnings forecasts.
  • ✅ Accounting policy change: RETROSPECTIVE application (restate prior periods). Statements within report are comparable. Modified retrospective = adjust opening retained earnings, no restatement of prior periods.
  • ✅ Accounting estimate change: PROSPECTIVE only — current and future periods. No restatement. Not on face of income statement — in notes. Creates artificial trends if changed mid-analysis.
  • ✅ Error correction: Full restatement of prior periods presented. Reveals internal control weaknesses — scrutinize carefully.
  • ✅ Basic EPS = (Net income − Preferred dividends) ÷ WASO. Time-weight share changes. Stock splits/dividends: retroactively applied to beginning of period (no time-weighting).
  • ✅ Simple capital structure: No dilutive securities. Basic EPS = Diluted EPS. Complex: has convertible bonds, convertible preferred, options, warrants.
  • ✅ If-converted method (convertible preferred): Numerator = Net income (add back preferred dividends). Denominator = WASO + shares from conversion. Assume conversion at BEGINNING of period.
  • ✅ If-converted method (convertible debt): Numerator = Net income + AFTER-TAX interest on convertible debt − Preferred dividends. Denominator = WASO + shares from bond conversion. After-tax because interest IS tax-deductible.
  • ✅ Treasury stock method (options/warrants): Proceeds = options × exercise price. Shares repurchased = proceeds ÷ average market price. Net incremental shares = shares issued − shares repurchased. NO numerator change. Dilutive only when exercise price < market price (in-the-money).
  • ✅ Antidilutive securities: If-converted EPS > basic EPS → antidilutive → EXCLUDE from diluted EPS. Diluted EPS ≤ Basic EPS always. Both basic and diluted EPS must be reported.
  • ✅ Common-size income statement: Each line item as % of revenue. Enables size-independent comparison across companies and time periods. Tax: express as % of pretax income (effective tax rate), not % of revenue.
  • ✅ Key margins: Gross profit margin = Gross profit ÷ Revenue. Operating margin = EBIT ÷ Revenue. Net profit margin = Net income ÷ Revenue. Pretax margin = Pretax income ÷ Revenue. All higher = better, but interpret in context of business strategy.

Top exam traps for Module 2:
(1) Revenue ≠ cash received. Cash before delivery = deferred revenue (liability), not revenue.
(2) Principal vs. agent: same net profit, very different revenue and margins. Analyst must evaluate mix.
(3) Capitalize vs. expense: capitalizing inflates CFO (cost shifts to investing). Red flag for earnings quality.
(4) Interest coverage: use GROSS interest (capitalized + expensed), not just expensed interest.
(5) If-converted for convertible DEBT: add back AFTER-TAX interest (not pre-tax).
(6) If-converted for convertible PREFERRED: add back preferred dividends as-is (no tax adjustment).
(7) Treasury stock method: NO numerator change. Net incremental shares only to denominator.

(9) Discontinued ops: reported NET of taxes. Unusual/infrequent items: BEFORE tax in continuing ops.
(10) Estimate changes: PROSPECTIVE (no restatement). Policy changes: RETROSPECTIVE (restatement).
(11) Stock splits/dividends: retroactively applied to BEGINNING of period — no time weighting.
(12) Diluted EPS ALWAYS ≤ Basic EPS. If the calculation gives higher — it's antidilutive.
