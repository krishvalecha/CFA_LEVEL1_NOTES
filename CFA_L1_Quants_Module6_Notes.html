Key Definitions — At a Glance

Lognormal Distribution
A random variable Y is lognormally distributed if its natural logarithm ln Y is normally distributed. Bounded below by 0. Right-skewed (long right tail). Used to model asset prices.
Continuously Compounded Return
The return expressed as r = ln(P_T / P_0). The sum of continuously compounded sub-period returns equals the total holding period continuously compounded return. Used in asset pricing models and risk management (volatility).
Volatility
Standard deviation of continuously compounded returns on the underlying asset. Stated as an annualized measure. Estimated from historical daily continuously compounded returns.
Monte Carlo Simulation
Generates a large number of random samples from a specified probability distribution to estimate the likelihood of a range of results. Provides statistical estimates (not exact results). Used to value complex securities and estimate portfolio risk/return.
Bootstrap Resampling
Mimics random sampling from a population by repeatedly resampling from an observed data sample (treating the sample as if it were the population). Sampling is with replacement. Does not require assuming a distribution — uses the empirical distribution.
i.i.d. Returns
Independently and identically distributed returns. Independence: future returns are not predictable from past returns. Identical distribution: mean and variance do not change from period to period (stationarity).
02

The Lognormal Distribution

Core definition: A random variable Y is lognormally distributed if and only if ln Y is normally distributed. Memory trick: think "lognormal" = "the log is normal."

The reverse also holds: if ln Y is normally distributed, then Y is lognormal.

Two Key Properties of the Lognormal Distribution

① Bounded Below by Zero

Asset prices can never go below zero (a stock can't have a negative price). The lognormal distribution naturally enforces this constraint — it has no probability mass below 0.

This is the primary reason lognormal is used for asset prices (not returns).

② Right-Skewed (Long Right Tail)

The lognormal distribution is skewed to the right. The distribution can spread upward without limit, but cannot spread downward past 0.

This causes the mean to be pushed to the right of the median — larger σ² → more rightward skew.

★ Lognormal Mean and Variance (Reference — Do Not Memorize)
If Y is lognormal, and ln Y ~ Normal(μ, σ²):

Mean of Y: E(Y) = exp(μ + 0.5σ²)
Variance of Y: σ²(Y) = exp(2μ + σ²) × [exp(σ²) − 1]

Where μ and σ² are the mean and variance of the ASSOCIATED NORMAL (ln Y),
NOT the mean and variance of Y itself.
Key insight: E(Y) = exp(μ + 0.5σ²), NOT exp(μ). The expected value is LARGER than exp(μ) by a factor of exp(0.5σ²) > 1.
Why? As σ² increases, the distribution spreads — it can go up without limit, but not below 0. So the mean is pushed rightward.
Two sets of parameters to track: (1) μ and σ of the associated normal (these are the parameters); (2) mean and std dev of Y itself (computed from the formulas above).
⚠️ Exam Trap — Two Distinct Sets of Parameters

The lognormal distribution is described by μ and σ² of its associated normal distribution (i.e., of ln Y). These are NOT the same as the mean and standard deviation of Y itself. Always clarify which set of parameters a question is referring to. The mean of Y = exp(μ + 0.5σ²), which is greater than exp(μ).

💡 Memory Trick — Which Distribution for What?

Returns → Normal (returns can be negative; e.g., −30% is a valid return)
Prices → Lognormal (prices cannot be negative; a stock price cannot be −$5)

If continuously compounded return r ~ Normal → future price P_T = P_0 · exp(r) → P_T ~ Lognormal.
The exponential transformation of a normal variable produces a lognormal variable. The log transformation of a lognormal variable produces a normal variable.

Black-Scholes-Merton (BSM) Option Pricing Model — most famous application of the lognormal distribution. BSM assumes that the price of the asset underlying the option is lognormally distributed. This is one of the most important uses of the lognormal distribution in finance.

04

Continuously Compounded Returns & Asset Price Modeling

★ Core Relationships — Price and Return
Future price in terms of continuously compounded return:
P_T = P_0 × exp(r₀,_T)

Continuously compounded return over T periods:
r₀,_T = r_{T-1,T} + r_{T-2,T-1} + ... + r_{0,1}
       (= sum of one-period continuously compounded returns)

Volatility (annualized from simulation):
Price(1 year later)ᵢ = USD 1 × exp(rᵢ)
P_0 = current price | r₀,_T = continuously compounded return from time 0 to T | exp() = e^()
The total holding period return is the SUM (not product) of sub-period continuously compounded returns.
This additivity property is why continuously compounded returns are used in many models.
★ Statistical Properties of Continuously Compounded Returns (i.i.d. assumption)
If one-period returns r are i.i.d. with mean μ and variance σ²:

E(r₀,_T) = μ × T          [mean scales linearly with T]
σ²(r₀,_T) = σ² × T        [variance scales linearly with T]
σ(r₀,_T) = σ × √T         [std dev scales with √T]
Consequence of i.i.d. assumption and independence: variance of sum = sum of variances → σ²(r₀,_T) = T × σ².
If one-period returns are normal → r₀,_T is also normal (linear combination of normal variables is normal).
Even if not normal → r₀,_T is approximately normal by the Central Limit Theorem (CLT).
Either way, r₀,_T ≈ normal → P_T = P_0 · exp(r₀,_T) → P_T is lognormal.

Why this matters: This chain of logic provides the theoretical justification for using the lognormal distribution to model asset prices:

  1. Assume one-period continuously compounded returns are i.i.d.
  2. The sum of T such returns (= r₀,_T) is normally distributed (exactly if normal, approximately via CLT otherwise)
  3. P_T = P_0 · exp(r₀,_T) → since r₀,_T is normal, P_T is lognormal
  4. Lognormal is bounded below by 0 → consistent with asset price non-negativity

This is the theoretical foundation for the lognormal model of asset prices.

💡 i.i.d. Returns — What Each Part Means

Independence: Investors cannot predict future returns using past returns (efficient market implication — no serial correlation in returns).
Identical distribution: Mean and variance of return are constant across periods (stationarity — no structural changes in the return process).

These are key assumptions underlying many financial models. Violations (e.g., volatility clustering, trending) are why more complex models exist.

05

Volatility Estimation & Annualization

★ Volatility Annualization
Annual volatility = Daily volatility × √250

Steps:
1. Collect daily holding period returns
2. Convert to daily continuously compounded returns: r_daily = ln(P_t / P_{t-1})
3. Compute std dev of daily continuously compounded returns: σ_daily
4. Annualize: σ_annual = σ_daily × √250

Example: σ_daily = 0.01 → σ_annual = 0.01 × √250 ≈ 0.158 = 15.8%
250 trading days per year (approximate number of business days financial markets are open).
Annualization uses √250 because σ scales with √T (from the i.i.d. assumption: σ(r₀,_T) = σ × √T).
Volatility is defined as the standard deviation of continuously compounded returns, annualized by convention.
Volatility is a key input in the Black-Scholes-Merton option pricing model and in VaR calculations.
💡 Why 250 Trading Days (Not 365)?

Financial markets are open approximately 250 business days per year (365 calendar days minus weekends and holidays). Volatility is measured when markets are open — overnight/weekend price moves are typically small and treated separately. Always annualize with √250 for return volatility in finance (not √365).

Monte Carlo Simulation

Monte Carlo simulation generates a very large number of random samples from a specified probability distribution (or distributions) to obtain the likelihood of a range of results. Each random draw produces one simulated outcome; repeating thousands of times builds a simulated frequency distribution.

Key characteristic: You specify and control the distributional assumptions. Results are statistical estimates (NOT exact values).

Investment Applications of Monte Carlo

Risk & Return Estimation
  • Simulate portfolio profit/loss for a specified time horizon
  • Asset-by-asset or aggregate portfolio basis
  • Repeated trials → simulated frequency distribution of portfolio returns
  • Derive performance and risk measures (VaR, expected return, etc.)
Complex Security Valuation
  • Value securities with no analytic pricing formula available
  • Examples: mortgage-backed securities with embedded options, Asian options, path-dependent derivatives
  • Examine sensitivity to changes in key model assumptions
  • Complement to analytical methods — not a replacement

Monte Carlo Simulation — 6-Step Process

Specify the simulation (Steps 1–3):

1
Specify the quantity of interest and underlying variables. Define what you are trying to value or estimate (e.g., value of a contingent claim, C_iT). Identify the underlying variable(s) driving it (e.g., stock price). Specify starting value(s).
2
Specify a time grid. Take the horizon in calendar time and split into K subperiods. Time increment Δt = Calendar time / K. Example: 1 year horizon, K = 12 months → Δt = 1 month.
3
Specify the method for generating random data. State distributional assumptions for the key risk factors. Specify the model for the underlying variable's dynamics. Example stock price model:

ΔStock price = (μ × Prior price × Δt) + (σ × Prior price × Z_k)

where Z_k = standard normal random variable (the key risk factor). Choose μ (mean) and σ (std dev) to control the distribution. Draw K random values of each risk factor using a computer/spreadsheet.

Run the simulation (Steps 4–6):

4
Convert random numbers into stock price paths. Use the random values from Step 3 and the model from Step 3 to compute K stock price changes (ΔStock price). Convert changes into a sequence of K stock prices using the initial price as the starting point. This creates one complete simulated price path.
5
Calculate the security value for this trial. Example: compute average stock price over the K periods. Calculate the contingent claim's payoff at maturity (C_iT). Discount to present value (C_i0). One simulation trial is now complete.
6
Repeat Steps 4–5 for the required number of trials (I). After all I trials are complete, produce summary statistics. The Monte Carlo estimate = mean value of C_i0 across all I trials. Example: I = 1,000 trials → estimate = average of 1,000 present values.
Asian Option Example — Key Numbers
Contingent claim: max(Final price − Average price, 0)
Example: Final = $34, Average = $31 → Payoff = $3

Simulation: I = 1,000 trials, K = 12 monthly steps
Results from 1,000 trials:
— 654 trials: final price ≤ average price → payoff = $0 (65.4%)
— 346 trials: final price > average price → positive payoff (34.6%)
— Maximum payoff observed: $11

MC estimate = mean present value of all 1,000 payoffs
Note: Simulated final price distribution is WIDER than simulated average price distribution — averaging smooths out volatility.
The contingent claim's value depends on the DIFFERENCE between final and average — cannot be read directly from either histogram alone.
💡 Monte Carlo — Advantages and Limitations

Advantages: Handles complex securities with no closed-form formula; flexible distributional assumptions; can incorporate multiple correlated risk factors; produces a full distribution of outcomes (not just a point estimate); useful for sensitivity analysis.

Limitations: Provides only statistical estimates (NOT exact results); results depend critically on the distributional assumptions made — "garbage in, garbage out"; computationally intensive; analytical methods, where available, provide more insight into cause-and-effect relationships.

Bootstrap Resampling

Core idea: Bootstrap mimics sampling from a population by treating the observed sample as if it were the population. Repeatedly draw samples of the same size as the original sample — with replacement — from the observed data.

Why "with replacement"? Each drawn item is put back before the next draw, so the same observation can appear multiple times in a single resample, and some observations may not appear at all. This is what makes each bootstrap sample distinct.

Bootstrap — The Core Mechanism
True world (left side of diagram):
True Population → [draw many samples] → True Sampling Distribution

Bootstrap world (right side of diagram):
Observed Sample (treated as "Estimated Population")
→ [draw B bootstrap samples with replacement]
→ Bootstrap Sampling Distribution

Key: Each bootstrap resample is the SAME SIZE as the original sample (n).
Sampling is WITH REPLACEMENT — same item can be drawn multiple times.
Bootstrap infers population parameters (mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis) from the bootstrap sampling distribution.
Does NOT require assuming any parametric distribution — uses the empirical distribution of the observed data.
Works for statistical inference without relying on analytical formulas (no z-test, no t-test needed).

Bootstrapping — 6-Step Process (for Contingent Claim Valuation)

1
Specify the quantity of interest and underlying variables. [Same as Monte Carlo Step 1] Define the contingent claim value (C_iT) and underlying variable (stock price). Specify starting values.
2
Specify a time grid consistent with the sample periodicity. [Same as Monte Carlo Step 2] Split the horizon into K subperiods with Δt increment. Must match the frequency of the observed data (e.g., monthly sample → monthly time steps).
3
Specify the data generation method. [KEY DIFFERENCE from Monte Carlo] Instead of assuming a parametric distribution (normal), use observed historical stock price changes/returns as the empirical distribution. Draw from this observed data, not from a theoretical distribution.
4
Draw K random values from the empirical distribution using bootstrap. [Replaces Monte Carlo's drawing from a normal distribution] Randomly sample K stock price changes from the historical data with replacement. Convert these changes into a sequence of K stock prices.
5
Calculate the average stock price and contingent claim value. [Same as Monte Carlo Step 5] Compute average price, payoff at maturity (C_iT), and present value (C_i0). One bootstrap trial is complete.
6
Repeat Steps 4–5 for the required number of trials (I). [Same as Monte Carlo Step 6] Bootstrap estimate = mean C_i0 across all I trials. This estimate is based on the empirical distribution created from observed historical prices.
💡 Bootstrap — Advantages and Limitations

Advantages: No distributional assumption required — uses the actual observed data; works when analytical formulas (z-stat, t-stat) are not applicable; easy to compute with modern computers; robust to violations of parametric assumptions.

Limitations: Provides only statistical estimates based on the empirical distribution — not exact results; quality depends on the representativeness of the observed sample; if the sample is unrepresentative or small, bootstrap estimates will be poor; analytical methods, where available, provide more insight into cause-and-effect relationships.

⚠️ Key Exam Distinction — The One Critical Difference

The only step that differs between Monte Carlo and Bootstrap is Step 3 (source of random data):

Monte Carlo: draws random numbers from a specified theoretical distribution (e.g., standard normal Z_k). You must specify μ and σ.

Bootstrap: draws random values from the observed historical data (empirical distribution). No distributional parameters needed.

Steps 1, 2, 4, 5, and 6 are effectively the same for both methods.

When Monte Carlo is Preferred
  • No historical data available for the specific risk factor
  • Need to stress test under hypothetical scenarios not in the data
  • Want to examine sensitivity to distributional assumptions
  • Modeling forward-looking scenarios with specified parameters
When Bootstrap is Preferred
  • Sufficient historical data available
  • Distribution of data is unknown or non-parametric
  • Standard analytical formulas (z-test, t-test) not applicable
  • Want to avoid making distributional assumptions
