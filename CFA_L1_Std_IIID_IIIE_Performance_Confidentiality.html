CFA LEVEL 1 · STANDARD III · MODULE 3

Standards III(D): Performance Presentation & III(E): Preservation of Confidentiality

III(D) requires that investment performance information communicated to clients and prospective clients be fair, accurate, and complete — covering composites, simulated results, attribution methodology, and GIPS compliance. III(E) requires maintaining the confidentiality of client information — covering former/prospective clients, illegal activities, vulnerable investors, and electronic information security. All examples from both LES and Handbook synthesised.

III(D)

Standard III(D): Performance Presentation — The Standard

Standard III(D) — Performance Presentation

When communicating investment performance information, Members and Candidates must make reasonable efforts to ensure that it is fair, accurate, and complete.

III(D) requires three characteristics in all performance communications: fair (no misleading emphasis), accurate (no errors, no misrepresentation), and complete (all relevant context). The standard applies to both performance presentation (how numbers are displayed) and performance measurement (how numbers are calculated). Both past performance and reasonably expected future performance are covered.

D2

Scope of III(D)

III(D) covers any practice that would lead to misrepresentation of a member's or candidate's performance record. Key scope points:

  • Covers individual accounts, composites, and groups of accounts
  • Covers pooled funds — any performance history the member claims responsibility for
  • Covers research analysts' recommendations — claims about the success or accuracy of recommendations must be fair, accurate, and complete
  • If a presentation is brief, must make detailed supporting information available on request; best practice = note the limited nature of the information provided
  • The LES adds: members must not state or imply that clients will obtain or benefit from a rate of return generated in the past

Common Performance Misrepresentation Scenarios

Single-Period / Single-Fund Extrapolation

Presenting one year's exceptional performance (e.g., 25%) as representative of expected returns, when the fund never achieved that rate before and the firm average is 5% annually. (Taylor example)

False GIPS Compliance Claim

Claiming compliance with GIPS standards while not meeting all requirements — e.g., not asset-weighting composite returns. (Judd example). GIPS claim = all requirements must be met.

Prior Employer Performance

Including performance from a prior employer without disclosing that it was earned at a different firm in a different role. (McCoy example). Permitted IF properly disclosed with source, role, and context.

Simulated / Back-Tested Results

Presenting back-tested model results as actual performance without disclosing they are simulated. (Davis example). Must clearly identify simulated results and disclose the retrospective application of the model.

Composite Manipulation

Including only favourable accounts in a composite; excluding underperformers; including accounts outside the stated mandate to boost results; changing composite composition over time. (Kilmer example)

Attribution Methodology Changes

Changing attribution methodology solely to improve the appearance of performance without disclosing the change and providing a comparison. (Purell example). LES: must report both methodologies and explain why the new one is preferred.

Examiner Focus — LES-Only: Clark Example and Performance Calculation Consistency

The LES adds the Clark (Example 8 LES) scenario: Richmond Equity Investors manages a long-short equity fund. A daily NAV is calculated but the fund only trades on Fridays. The monthly fact sheets report performance based on the higher of the last trading day OR the last business day of the month — whichever gives a better number — without specifying the exact date. Clark questions whether these changing metrics are appropriate. Clark's actions comply with III(D) — she correctly identified that the inconsistent performance metric does not present an accurate and complete picture. The key rule: performance metrics must be consistent and clearly disclosed. Selecting the higher of two calculation dates solely to report the best possible number = violation of the "fair" and "complete" requirements.

💡 LES Adds: Purell Must Show Both Methodologies

The LES version of the Purell (Example 6) attribution methodology example goes further than the Handbook. The LES states: "Had Purell believed the new methodology offered improvements to the original model, then he would have needed to report the results of both calculations to the client. The report should also include the reasons why the new methodology is preferred, which would allow the client to make a meaningful comparison to prior results and provide a basis for comparing future attributions." This gives the correct path when a genuine methodological improvement exists — dual reporting with explanation, not silent substitution.

D3

GIPS Compliance — Best Method for Meeting III(D)

Compliance with the GIPS standards is the best method to meet obligations under III(D) for members showing the performance history of assets they manage. Members should encourage their firms to comply.

For members whose firms do NOT comply with GIPS, they can meet III(D) obligations by:

  • Considering the knowledge and sophistication of the audience when tailoring performance presentations
  • Presenting a weighted composite of similar portfolios rather than a single representative account
  • Including terminated accounts as part of composite performance history (with clear indication of when accounts terminated)
  • Including disclosures explaining the performance results — simulated vs. actual, prior entity vs. current, gross of fees vs. net of fees vs. after-tax
  • Maintaining the data and records used to calculate the performance
⚠️ Claiming GIPS Compliance = Meeting ALL Requirements

When a firm claims compliance with the GIPS standards, it must meet ALL requirements, make all mandatory disclosures, and meet any other requirements applicable to its specific situation. The Judd example: the violation is not misuse of data but from publishing a performance report with a false claim of GIPS compliance. Partial compliance + compliance claim = violation. The Judd example in the LES covers a different historical period (1988-2004) but the same principle applies.

III(D) Worked Examples — All (Including LES-Only)

Example 1
Taylor — "25% Annual Growth" from Single Year, Single Fund
VIOLATION
Taylor's brochure states "steady 25% annual compound growth" — based on one fund's one-year return (which mirrored the overall market). The fund has never averaged 25% and the firm's five-year average is 5%.
Violation of III(D) and I(C) (LES adds: also violates I(C) Misrepresentation — using past performance figures to imply future returns, which also functions as a guarantee). Must disclose that the 25% occurred only in one year and only for the common trust fund. A general claim of firm performance must reflect all account categories.
🔑 Core Rules
(1) One year's exceptional performance cannot be presented as representative of expected returns. (2) Firm-wide claims must reflect all categories of accounts, not just the best-performing one. (3) The LES cross-reference to I(C): stating or implying clients will obtain a past rate of return = misrepresentation.
Example 3
McCoy — Prior Employer Performance Without Disclosure
VIOLATION
McCoy uses his six-year track record from G&P Financial in advertising for his new firm Mastermind — without identifying the performance as earned at G&P.
Violation of III(D). The advertisement misrepresents Mastermind's historical performance. Permitted use: showing prior-firm performance records IS allowed — with appropriate disclosure of where the performance took place and the person's specific role in achieving it. McCoy must make full disclosure of the source of the historical performance.
🔑 Core Rule
Prior firm performance may be portable with proper disclosure — source firm + individual's specific role + context. Without those disclosures, it is presented as the new firm's performance, which is false.
Example 5
Kilmer — Manipulated "Composite" for Prospective Clients
VIOLATION
Kilmer's "balanced composite" includes only a few of the qualifying accounts, excludes those under a certain asset size without disclosing this, includes accounts outside the balanced mandate (because they boost results), and changes the composite composition over time to achieve better results.
Violation of III(D). Multiple violations: (1) exclusion of qualifying accounts without disclosure, (2) inclusion of non-qualifying accounts that happen to boost results, (3) dynamic manipulation of composite membership over time. All misrepresent the firm's track record and fail to include disclosures that would clarify the presentation.
🔑 Core Rule
A composite must include all accounts that meet the definition — not just the good ones. Excluded accounts must be disclosed. Accounts that don't fit the mandate cannot be included because they improve the numbers. Dynamic manipulation of composite membership = cherry-picking = violation.
Example 6
Purell — Changing Attribution Methodology to Look Better
VIOLATION
Purell discovers a different attribution methodology that makes the firm's stock selection look better. He recommends switching to this methodology in client reports — because "the firm defines the attribution terms and the results better reflect the stated strategy."
Violation of III(D). Modifying attribution methodology without proper disclosure and solely to improve the firm's appearance fails to meet the requirements. The LES version adds: if the new methodology is genuinely better, must report both calculations with an explanation of why the new one is preferred — allowing clients to compare.
🔑 Core Rule
Attribution methodology changes must be disclosed and, per the LES, if the new method is believed to be superior, must present both methods with explanation. Silent substitution of a more flattering methodology = misrepresentation.
Example 7
Singh — Showing Highest Return Type Without Labelling
VIOLATION
Singh discovers the new reporting system calculates both time-weighted and money-weighted returns. She is told not to label the returns and to show whichever calculation gives the highest return for the period.
Following these instructions would violate III(D). Reporting inconsistent return types without labelling does not provide complete information. Clients need sufficient information to judge the performance generated by the firm — including understanding what return type is being reported.
🔑 Core Rule
Performance reporting must be consistent and clearly labelled. Selectively showing whichever of two valid calculation methods happens to be higher in a given period — without identifying which method is being shown — is misleading by omission. "Complete" means sufficient information for clients to make independent judgments.
Example 8 (LES-Only)
Clark — Questioning Inconsistent Performance Date Selection
COMPLIANT
Richmond Equity Investors reports monthly performance based on the higher of the last trading day (typically not the last business day) or the last business day of the month — without specifying the exact date. Clark questions whether the frequently changing approach is appropriate.
Clark's questioning complies with III(D). She correctly identifies that selecting the calculation date based on which gives the higher return — without clear disclosure — does not present an accurate and complete picture of performance. The inconsistent and undisclosed selection methodology is the violation.
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only)
Performance measurement methodology must be consistent and clearly disclosed. Selecting among valid calculation dates based on which yields a higher return — without transparency about the selection criteria — fails the "fair, accurate, and complete" standard even if each individual calculation is technically accurate.
III(E)

Standard III(E): Preservation of Confidentiality — The Standard

Standard III(E) — Preservation of Confidentiality

Members and Candidates must keep information about current, former, and prospective clients confidential unless: 1. The information concerns illegal activities on the part of the client, 2. Disclosure is required by law, or 3. The client or prospective client permits disclosure of the information.

III(E) protects the trust relationship between investment professionals and clients. It applies when: (1) the member receives information because of their special ability to conduct a portion of the client's business or personal affairs, AND (2) the information arises from or is relevant to the portion of the client's business that is the subject of the confidential relationship.

Three permitted exceptions to confidentiality: (1) illegal activity by the client, (2) required by law, (3) client authorises disclosure. Even when disclosure is permitted by these exceptions, applicable law may further restrict or require specific procedures.

E2

When Disclosure Is Permitted — The Three Exceptions

Exception 1: Client's Illegal Activities

If client information concerns illegal activities, members may have an obligation to report to appropriate authorities. But applicable law may still restrict disclosure — must consult compliance personnel or legal counsel. Not automatic disclosure — must assess applicable law first.

Exception 2: Required by Law

Must comply with applicable law when it requires disclosure. Also must comply when applicable law mandates confidentiality — even if the information involves illegal activities. Law can both compel and restrict disclosure. When in doubt: consult compliance or legal counsel.

Exception 3: Client Permits

If a client or former client expressly authorises disclosure, may disclose under the terms of the authorisation. Must follow the specific terms of the authorisation — not disclose more than permitted.

Key Principles

  • Former clients: Confidentiality obligation continues after the client relationship ends. Must maintain confidentiality of former clients' records.
  • Prospective clients: The standard explicitly covers prospective clients — information shared during a prospective client relationship is also confidential.
  • CFA Institute investigations: III(E) does not prevent cooperation with CFA Institute Professional Conduct investigations. Members must cooperate when permissible under applicable law. The LES states: "Members and candidates shall consider the PCP an extension of themselves when requested to provide information about a client in support of a PCP investigation into their own conduct."
Examiner Focus — Illegal Activity Disclosure: Process Matters

When members suspect a client is engaged in illegal activities (Samuel/Garcia pension fund fraud; Bradford/CFO embezzlement), the correct process is NOT to immediately disclose. The correct process: (1) consult compliance personnel or legal counsel, (2) determine whether applicable law requires or permits disclosure, (3) act accordingly. Samuel's obligation: work with employer to remedy violations with Garcia; if unsuccessful, seek legal advice; may have a duty to provide records to government investigators who formally request them. Bradford's obligation: check with compliance/legal to determine whether securities regulations require reporting. Neither automatically discloses — both first seek legal guidance.

E3

Vulnerable Investors — A Key Handbook Addition

The Handbook (but not the LES) adds a dedicated section on vulnerable investors — clients with potentially diminishing mental capacity who may be subject to financial exploitation. Key principles:

  • Best practice: Establish a secondary contact at the beginning of the client relationship — a trusted family member, legal adviser, or third-party intermediary whom the client permits to be contacted if concerns arise about their decision-making capacity
  • Without prior authorisation: Confidentiality requirements may prevent discussing concerns with anyone other than the direct account holder — making it difficult to protect vulnerable clients
  • With prior authorisation: May contact the designated secondary contact when concerns arise — does NOT violate III(E)
  • Documentation: All conversations with the client and any outside parties regarding disclosure of sensitive information must be fully documented and retained in client files
  • Regulatory resources: Often exist for investigating vulnerable investor situations — members following applicable law on permitted disclosures do not violate III(E)
Gonzales (Compliant — Prior Authorisation)

Brennan showed signs of declining mental capacity over several months and directed Gonzales to liquidate 50% of her portfolio for a speculative venture with her physical therapist. Gonzales had previously obtained Brennan's consent to contact her son in such circumstances. Gonzales contacts the son, fully documents the situation.

No violation. Prior authorisation obtained at account inception + documented decision = compliant action to protect a vulnerable client.

Smith-Pelley (Compliant — No Prior Authorisation)

Carlson suddenly divorced, remarried a much younger man from another country, directed large withdrawals, and added the new husband to accounts. Carlson's children demand Smith-Pelley freeze the accounts. Smith-Pelley does nothing and maintains confidentiality.

No violation. Carlson's decisions may raise red flags, but they don't necessarily indicate a loss of decision-making ability. Without a prior authorisation to contact family members, Smith-Pelley correctly acts for the benefit of his client (Carlson), follows her direction, and maintains confidentiality against the children's demands.

Electronic Information and Security

III(E) does not require members to become information security experts — but does require thorough understanding of their employer's policies for ensuring confidential information security. The LES adds that regulatory authorities have imposed stricter data security laws applying to mobile/remote digital communication and social media. Key practices:

  • Follow firm policies for electronic communication of sensitive client information
  • Encourage development of procedures if firm lacks them
  • Ensure firm-supported communication methods are designed to prevent accidental disclosure
  • Regular review of privacy protection measures given rapid technology changes
  • Train ALL firm personnel — including portfolio associates, receptionists, and other non-investment staff with client contact

III(E) Worked Examples — All 7

Example 1
Connor — Client's Building Plans: Cannot Disclose to Third Party
CONFIDENTIALITY MAINTAINED
Connor provides investment advice to City Medical Center's trustees and has received confidential internal reports about building plans. A businessman (Kasey) asks to find out the building plans before deciding where to donate — doesn't want to speak to the trustees directly.
Connor must refuse to disclose. The trustees gave Connor the reports so she could advise on managing endowment funds — not so she could share building plans with potential donors. The information is both confidential and within the scope of the confidential relationship.
🔑 Core Rule
Information received in the course of a confidential professional relationship must be protected even when the purpose of disclosure (helping a potential donor) seems benign. The two conditions for III(E) to apply are met: Connor received the info because of her advisory relationship, and the info arises from that relationship.
Example 2
Moody — Using Client's Charitable Giving Plans to Identify Donor for HIW
VIOLATION
Moody's client mentioned plans to give ~$50,000 to charity. Moody (also treasurer of HIW) recommends that HIW's VP call on the client to solicit a donation in that amount.
Violation of III(E). Even though the intent was to help a charitable organisation, Moody revealed confidential information about her client's financial plans and charitable intentions to a third party without the client's authorisation.
🔑 Core Rule
Good intentions do not cure a confidentiality violation. The client's information about their tax planning and charitable giving intentions was shared in the context of the advisory relationship — it cannot be used by the member to benefit a third party (even a charity) without the client's authorisation.
Examples 3 + 4
Samuel + Bradford — Suspecting Illegal Activity: Consult First
PROCESS REQUIRED
Samuel: learns that Garcia's CEO is making excessive/improper charges to the pension fund. Government officials then contact her for records. Bradford: believes the CFO is embezzling money from the corporation and putting it into his personal investment account.
Both must consult compliance/legal counsel first. Samuel: should work with employer to remedy the violations; if unsuccessful, seek legal counsel; likely has a duty to provide records to government investigators when formally requested. The LES version adds that Samuel should inform her supervisor of the activities and may need to resign if the approach fails. Bradford: check with compliance or legal counsel to determine whether securities regulations require reporting.
🔑 Core Rule
Suspecting client illegal activity does NOT automatically mean the member must disclose. The correct process: (1) consult compliance/legal counsel, (2) determine what applicable law requires, (3) act accordingly. When government officials formally request records (Samuel), the member likely has a legal duty to cooperate — but even this is confirmed through legal counsel, not assumed.
Example 5
Moody — Client Discloses Confidential Info on Group Social Media Page
NO VIOLATION BY MOODY
Moody establishes a clients-only social media group for market outlook communications. She instructs clients this is not an appropriate channel for personal/confidential information. One client responds to a market outlook post with highly personal financial details — visible to all group members until Moody has the comment removed.
Moody has NOT violated III(E). She took reasonable steps to protect confidentiality: established the group under firm direction, warned clients against sharing personal information, and promptly removed the comment once aware. The accidental disclosure by a client is not within Moody's control. The LES adds: this event highlights a need for further training and Moody might advocate for additional warnings or controls for two-way social media communications.
🔑 Core Rule
Reasonable steps to protect confidentiality + prompt remedial action when disclosure occurs = compliance with III(E). The member cannot control what clients choose to post — but must have systems to detect and respond quickly. The LES addition: use the incident to advocate for better controls and additional client training.

Combined Exam Checklists — III(D) and III(E)

Standard III(D): Performance Presentation

  • ✅ Three requirements: Fair (no misleading emphasis) + Accurate (no errors or misrepresentation) + Complete (all relevant context). All three must be met.
  • ✅ Covers: Individual accounts, composites, pooled funds, and research analyst recommendations. Both performance presentation and performance measurement.
  • ✅ Cannot state or imply clients will obtain a rate of return generated in the past. (LES — also I(C) Misrepresentation)
  • ✅ Single-period performance: Cannot be presented as representative of expected returns or firm-wide performance. (Taylor)
  • ✅ Firm-wide performance claims: Must reflect all categories of accounts, not just best-performing ones.
  • ✅ GIPS compliance claim = meeting ALL requirements — partial compliance + GIPS claim = violation. (Judd)
  • ✅ Prior firm performance: May be shown with full disclosure of source firm and member's specific role. Without disclosure = misrepresentation. (McCoy)
  • ✅ Simulated/back-tested results: Must clearly identify as simulated and disclose the retrospective application period. (Davis)
  • ✅ Composite construction: Must include all accounts meeting the definition; excluded accounts must be disclosed; cannot include out-of-mandate accounts to boost results; cannot dynamically manipulate composition. (Kilmer)
  • ✅ Attribution methodology changes: Must disclose changes; if genuinely improved, show both methodologies with explanation (LES). Cannot change silently to improve appearance. (Purell)
  • ✅ Performance calculation consistency: Must use consistent methodology and clearly disclose it. Cannot select among valid methods based on which gives the highest number. (Singh, Clark)
  • ✅ Brief presentations: Must make detailed supporting information available on request.

Standard III(E): Preservation of Confidentiality

  • ✅ Three exceptions to confidentiality: (1) client's illegal activities, (2) disclosure required by law, (3) client/prospective client permits disclosure.
  • ✅ Continues after relationship ends: Must maintain confidentiality of former clients' records even after the advisory relationship terminates.
  • ✅ Covers prospective clients: Information shared during a prospective client relationship is also confidential.
  • ✅ Good intentions don't cure violations: Charitable intent, beneficial third parties, helpful purposes — none overcome the confidentiality obligation without authorisation. (Moody)
  • ✅ Illegal activity by client: Does NOT automatically require or permit disclosure. Must consult compliance/legal counsel first to determine what applicable law requires. (Samuel, Bradford)
  • ✅ Law can restrict disclosure even for illegal activities: Must follow applicable law even when it mandates confidentiality about illegal client activities.
  • ✅ CFA Institute investigations: III(E) does not prevent cooperation. Must cooperate when permissible under applicable law.
  • ✅ Vulnerable investors: Best practice = establish secondary contact at inception. With prior authorisation: may contact secondary contact when concerns arise. Without prior authorisation: confidentiality may prevent discussing concerns with anyone but the client. (Gonzales vs. Smith-Pelley)
  • ✅ Document all disclosure decisions — conversations with clients and outside parties about sensitive disclosures must be retained in client files.
  • ✅ Electronic information: Must understand firm's security policies. Don't need to be security experts — but must follow and advocate for adequate procedures. Train ALL staff including non-investment personnel.
  • ✅ Accidental disclosure by clients (not the member) does not violate III(E) if the member took reasonable preventive steps and acts promptly to remedy. (Moody social media example)

Top III(D) exam traps:
(1) "The numbers are accurate, so the presentation is fine" — accurate numbers with misleading context (e.g., single-year cherry-pick) = still a violation
(2) "The firm claims GIPS compliance but doesn't asset-weight composites" — partial GIPS compliance + GIPS claim = violation
(3) "The new attribution methodology better reflects the strategy" — must disclose the change AND (per LES) show both methodologies

Top III(E) exam traps:
(1) "The client is breaking the law, so the member must report immediately" — must first consult compliance/legal counsel
(2) "Good intent justifies sharing client information" — good intentions don't cure a III(E) violation
(3) "Smith-Pelley should have protected Carlson from her new husband" — without a prior authorisation from Carlson to contact others, Smith-Pelley correctly maintains confidentiality and follows Carlson's instructions
