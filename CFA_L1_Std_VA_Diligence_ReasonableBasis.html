CFA LEVEL 1 · STANDARD V · MODULE 3

Standard V(A): Diligence and Reasonable Basis

Requires diligence, independence, and thoroughness in investment analysis, and a reasonable and adequate basis — supported by appropriate research — for any recommendation or action. Covers primary research, secondary/third-party research, quantitative models, external adviser selection, and group research. Both sources synthesised with all 17 LES examples and 12 Handbook examples cross-referenced.

01

The Standard — Verbatim Text

Standard V(A) — Diligence and Reasonable Basis

Members and Candidates must: 1. Exercise diligence, independence, and thoroughness in analyzing investments, making investment recommendations, and taking investment actions. 2. Have a reasonable and adequate basis, supported by appropriate research and investigation, for any investment analysis, recommendation, or action.

Two interlocking requirements: (1) the process must be diligent, independent, and thorough; and (2) the output (recommendation or action) must be supported by a reasonable and adequate basis from that research. The Handbook adds a definitional note: diligence requires careful, consistent, and thorough work; a reasonable basis requires sound judgment, understanding, care, and caution — following a rational, well-considered process.

The standard scales with: the investment philosophy being followed, the member's role in the decision-making process, and the support and resources available from the employer. These factors determine the required level of diligence and investigation.

02

Defining Diligence and Reasonable Basis

At a basic level, clients want assurance that members are putting forth the necessary effort. The basis for a recommendation is formed through a balance of resources appropriate for the security or decision — no single type of source is sufficient alone. Members must make reasonable efforts to cover all pertinent issues when arriving at a recommendation.

Factors to consider when forming a basis for a recommendation (non-exhaustive):

  • Global, regional, and country macroeconomic conditions
  • A company's operating and financial history
  • The industry's and sector's current conditions and the stage of the business cycle
  • A pooled fund's fee structure and management history
  • The output and potential limitations of quantitative models
  • The quality of the assets included in a securitization
  • The appropriateness of selected peer-group comparisons
💡 Downside Risk ≠ Failure to Meet the Standard

A well-supported recommendation can still result in a loss. Members base decisions on information available at the time. Even thorough research cannot eliminate downside risk — but diligent analysis minimises unexpected negative outcomes. A failed investment, by itself, does not establish a violation of V(A) (Newbury example). What matters is whether the process was sound at the time the decision was made.

03

Secondary and Third-Party Research

Secondary research = conducted by someone else within the member's firm. Third-party research = conducted by entities outside the firm (brokerages, banks, research firms). Members may rely on both, but must make reasonable and diligent efforts to determine whether the research is sound.

If there is reason to suspect that research lacks a sound basis or may be biased, unreliable, or otherwise deficient, members must NOT rely on it. Criteria for evaluating soundness:

  • Assumptions used
  • Rigor of the analysis performed
  • Date and timeliness of the research
  • Evaluation of the objectivity and independence of the recommendations

Members may rely on others in their firm who conducted the due diligence on information sources (e.g., vendor selection by senior management) and use that information in good faith — unless there is specific reason to question its validity.

⚠️ Internet Sources Require Greater Review

Information from personal blogs, independent research aggregation websites, or social media likely requires a greater level of review than information from established research organisations. The Cannon example (Example 9/13) illustrates: incorporating factors from an online blog ("Expert CFA") into a proprietary model without independently testing the effect = V(A) violation. The blog can be a source of ideas — but the member is responsible for independently validating the effect on their own models before acting on it.

04

Quantitative Models — Use and Development

Using Quantitative Models
  • Must understand the parameters used in models incorporated into recommendations
  • Not required to be experts in every technical aspect — but must understand assumptions, limitations, and how results are used in the decision-making process
  • Must test model output before incorporating into methods, models, or analyses
  • Must consider source and time horizon of data inputs — databases may not capture full positive and negative market cycles
  • Must test scenarios beyond historical data when historical data doesn't include relevant negative scenarios (Thompson example)
  • Must evaluate components of a strategy as an integrated whole, not just individually (Liakos example)
Developing Quantitative Models
  • Developers must exhibit a HIGHER level of diligence than those who use the output
  • Must understand the full technical aspects of what they create
  • Must complete thorough testing of the model before product distribution
  • Must ensure analyses incorporate a broad range of assumptions sufficient to capture underlying investment characteristics
  • Must include scenarios representing extreme positive and negative outcomes
  • Responsibility for model quality remains with the developer — even if the model user (portfolio manager) doesn't understand it fully
Examiner Focus — The Dupont/Smith (Reichardt) Example

This is a two-party violation found in both sources (with different names for the fund manager: "Smith" in LES, "Reichardt" in Handbook). Dupont (model developer) violated V(A) even though he doesn't trade or interact with clients — because his model gives investment recommendations and he is accountable for their quality. He neglected model updates, introduced a quick "fix" without proper testing, and failed to correct the error promptly. Smith/Reichardt (fund manager who uses the model) violated V(A) because he has "only very superficial knowledge of the model" — members must understand the parameters and limitations of the models they rely on for investment decisions. Crucially, V(B) is also implicated: when Smith discovers the error, he quietly rebalances the portfolio without informing clients — a V(B) violation for failure to disclose a material error.

05

Selecting External Advisers and Subadvisers

V(A) applies to the level of review necessary in selecting external advisers or subadvisers with the same rigour as reviewing individual investments. Members must review advisers as diligently as they review individual investment opportunities.

Required due diligence criteria include (but are not limited to):

  • Reviewing the adviser's established code of ethics
  • Understanding the adviser's compliance and internal control procedures
  • Assessing the quality of the published return information
  • Reviewing the adviser's investment process and adherence to its stated strategy

CFA Institute resources that can be used as criteria: CFA Institute Asset Manager Code, Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS), and the Model Request for Proposal (for equity, credit, or real estate managers).

⚠️ Fee Alone Is Not a Sufficient Basis for Adviser Selection

Both Ostrowski (Examples 5/7) and Jackson (Examples 11/15) illustrate that selecting a subadviser or service provider based solely on fees or performance respectively violates V(A). Ostrowski selects based on lowest fees; Jackson selects based on highest total returns; both fail to conduct full review of the advisers' services, investment processes, compliance, and suitability for the client's needs. A full and complete review of services, performance history, AND cost structure is required. Also note the V(A) + III(C) cross-standard interaction — subadviser selection must also consider whether the adviser's strategy is suitable for clients.

06

Group Research and Decision Making

In group research settings, the report represents the consensus of the group — not necessarily the individual member's personal view. Key rules:

  • If the member believes the consensus opinion has a reasonable and adequate basis and is independent and objective, they do NOT need to dissociate from the report even if it doesn't reflect their personal opinion
  • Members may ask to have their name removed from a report — but they are not required to dissociate if satisfied with the process quality
  • The firm can issue a report different from an analyst's original research as long as there is a reasonable and adequate basis for the changed conclusion
  • Key test: confidence in the process, not agreement with the conclusion

Worked Examples — LES Full Set (17 examples) vs Handbook (12 examples)

Example 1
Both
Hawke/Sarkozi — Pricing IPOs by Size Without Research (Time Pressure)
VIOLATION
Sarkozi Securities lacks resources to conduct adequate research on all IPO candidates but proceeds anyway. Hawke prices them based on relative company size, planning to justify the pricing later when staff have more time.
Violation of V(A). The firm should have taken on only the work it could adequately handle. Pricing new issues requires research on all relevant aspects — not just size classification. The plan to justify pricing retroactively is not acceptable. Bypassing research under time pressure = insufficient due diligence.
🔑 Core Rule
Time pressure does not lower the standard of due diligence. If a firm lacks resources to research all aspects of an investment, it should decline or reduce the workload — not proceed without adequate basis and promise to justify it later. Investors can purchase shares at prices with no actual analytical basis — and they are harmed by this.
Example 2 (LES-Only)
LES Only
Dhaliwal — Using Maximum Production Scenarios as Base-Case
VIOLATION
Dhaliwal presses a company to project maximum (optimal scenario) production and then presents these maximum figures as base-case production levels in a sales pitch to brokers for a secondary equity offering.
Violation of V(A). Should have presented a range of scenarios and probabilities of achieving each level. Presenting the maximum/optimal scenario as the likely base case misrepresents the probability of achieving that production level and seriously misleads the brokers.
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only)
A reasonable basis requires a realistic range of scenarios with appropriate probabilities — not a selection of the most optimistic scenario presented as likely. Converting a maximum/stress-test scenario into a "base case" is a misrepresentation of the investment's prospects, regardless of how it was derived.
Example 3 (LES) / Not in Handbook
LES Only
Witt — News Articles and Conventional Wisdom as Research Basis
VIOLATION
Witt (part-time analyst for an internet research firm) creates a stock recommendation report based on "a few news articles and what the conventional wisdom of the markets has deemed the 'hot' security of the day" because he lacks time to do more.
Violation of V(A). General market enthusiasm ("hot security") and news article summaries — without further analysis — do not constitute a reasonable and adequate basis for a stock recommendation. Also see IV(C) for the firm's inadequate review procedures.
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only)
The quality of the underlying research, not the accessibility of information, determines whether the basis is reasonable and adequate. Widely circulated popular sentiment ("hot stock") is not a substitute for independent analysis. Also note: this is the same Witt from IV(C) — the supervisor's failure to review the report is a separate IV(C) violation.
Example 2 (Handbook) / Example 4 (LES)
Both
Chandler — Not Updating Report After Material Personnel Changes
VIOLATION
Chandler prepares a manager recommendation report and delivers it to a client. Between the database search and delivery, she learns that one recommended firm lost its CIO, head of US equity research, and majority of portfolio managers — all left to start their own firm. She does not update the report.
Violation of V(A). Material new information about a recommended manager must be incorporated before the recommendation is finalised and delivered. The personnel exodus fundamentally changes the quality assessment of that manager.
🔑 Core Rule
A reasonable basis must be current. When material information becomes available between the time of analysis and the time of delivery, the member must update the analysis to reflect the new information. Delivering a stale recommendation with known material errors = V(A) violation.
Example 3 (Handbook) / Example 5 (LES)
Both
Mastakis — Group Research Changed by Committee
NO REQUIRED DISSOCIATION
Mastakis submits her interest rate forecast report. The committee significantly changes her conclusion to predict a larger rate increase. She asks whether she should have her name removed. Handbook version: she asks; LES version: she considers it.
No required dissociation if Mastakis is confident the process produced a conclusion with a reasonable and adequate basis. Different people can reach different conclusions from the same evidence. If she is satisfied with the process quality, she does not need to remove her name — even if the report doesn't reflect her personal opinion.
🔑 Core Rule
The test for dissociation is confidence in the process, not agreement with the conclusion. If the group process was rigorous, independent, and had an adequate basis, the member need not dissociate. Members may always ask to have their name removed — but are not required to if the process was sound.
Example 4 (Handbook) / Example 6 (LES)
Both
McDermott — Relying on Third-Party Research
CONDITIONAL COMPLIANCE
McDermott's two-person firm makes recommendations based entirely on subscribed third-party research reports.
Compliant IF McDermott regularly reviews the third-party research for soundness and objectivity. Members may rely on third-party research — but must make reasonable and diligent efforts to determine it is sound on an ongoing basis.
🔑 Core Rule
Third-party research reliance is permitted but not automatic. The "in good faith" safe harbour applies only when: (1) due diligence was done on the research provider's quality, (2) there is no reason to question validity, and (3) the review is ongoing (regular). A one-time initial check that then never recurs is insufficient.
Example 5 (Handbook) / Example 7 (LES)
Both (different details)
Ostrowski — Selecting Subadviser Based Solely on Lowest Fees
VIOLATION
Ostrowski reviews seven subadviser questionnaires but selects the one with the lowest fees because it minimises the impact on his firm's bottom line. The Handbook version specifies he "feels unqualified" — making the fee-based selection even more clearly inadequate.
Violation of V(A). Selection must be based on a full and complete review of services, performance history, and cost structure. Fees alone are insufficient. Also implicates III(C) — the subadviser's suitability for clients must also be assessed.
🔑 Core Rule
External adviser selection must be as rigorous as individual investment selection. The member's interest in minimising their firm's bottom line impact does not constitute a reasonable basis for the selection. A personalised conflict (protecting the firm's margins) further undermines the objectivity of the decision.
Example 6 (Handbook) / Example 10 (LES)
Both
Thompson — Scenario Testing Beyond Historical Database Ranges
COMPLIANT
Thompson insists on including a negative housing price appreciation scenario (–10%/–5%/0%) even though the firm's database has never shown negative HPA. The manager says the assumptions are "too dire." Thompson runs the analysis anyway and recommends against the subprime mortgage investment — which proves prescient when the housing market collapses.
Thompson's actions comply with V(A). Testing scenarios beyond historical database ranges — when the member identifies good reasons to do so — is exactly what V(A) requires. The fact that "it's never happened before" in the database doesn't make the scenario unreasonable when there are good analytical reasons to include it.
🔑 Core Rule
Historical data limitations do not define the boundaries of responsible analysis. When observable data doesn't include relevant extreme scenarios, members must test those scenarios independently. The manager's resistance to "too dire" assumptions does not relieve the analyst of their V(A) obligations. Notably, Thompson's analysis was prescient — vindicating the stress-testing approach.
Example 7 (Handbook) / Example 11 (LES)
Both
Liakos — Strategy Components Validated Individually, Not as Integrated Whole
VIOLATION
Liakos implements a derivative strategy assembled from components built by multiple third parties under time pressure. Each component was individually validated, but they were never tested as an integrated whole. In extreme market conditions, components work at cross-purposes with each other, causing the strategy to fail dramatically despite the client being correct on the underlying commodity view.
Violation of V(A). Liakos did not ensure a thorough review of the whole integrated model. Because the client clearly intended to implement the strategy as a whole, Liakos should have tested how the components interacted — not just how they performed independently.
🔑 Core Rule
Validation at the component level is insufficient when clients will implement components as an integrated whole. The member must understand and test the system-level behaviour under relevant scenarios. Time pressure and trusted third-party validators do not remove the obligation to test integration.
Example 8 (Handbook) / Example 12 (LES)
Both
Newbury — Good Process, Failed Investment
COMPLIANT
Newbury recommends the Top Shelf hedge fund after thorough analysis of: track record, fund principals, fees, and risk profile. The fund subsequently loses 60% of its value and suspends operations. The LES version adds context (Calgary-based fund, 3-year 20% returns) and notes the next step: "discuss the analysis process with the client while reminding them that past performance does not guarantee future gains."
Compliant with V(A). A bad outcome does not establish a V(A) violation if the process was sound at the time. V(A) requires a sound process based on information available at the time — not prescience.
🔑 Core Rule
Investment losses are not sufficient evidence of a V(A) violation. The analysis is judged on the quality of the process at the time decisions were made — ex ante, not ex post. This is critical for exam scenarios: a well-analysed investment that subsequently fails ≠ V(A) violation.
Example 9 (Handbook) / Example 13 (LES)
Both
Cannon — Incorporating Unvalidated Blog Research into Proprietary Model
VIOLATION
Cannon (lead quant analyst) reads blog research from "Expert CFA" and incorporates the factors into a proprietary hedge fund model without independently testing their effect. The updated model output recommends new investments to portfolio managers.
Violation of V(A). Cannon may use the blog for ideas — but is responsible for independently determining the effect on the firm's proprietary models before acting on them. Incorporating untested factors under executive pressure = insufficient diligence. Also: the blog's "Expert CFA" designation may violate VII(B).
🔑 Core Rule
Ideas from any source (including unverified online sources) may inspire analysis — but the member must independently test and validate the effect before incorporating into production models. The source's credentials (or claimed credentials) do not substitute for independent validation. Pressure from firm executives does not lower the due diligence standard.
Example 10 (Handbook) / Example 14 (LES)
Both
Smith/Stefansson — Recommending Attribution System Without Reviewing Others
VIOLATION
Smith/Stefansson (performance analyst) is asked to review five attribution systems. She recommends the system she remembered from a conference exhibitor booth without reviewing the other four and without determining whether it aligns with the firm's investment practices (different countries and currencies).
Violation of V(A). Did not review all the alternatives she was asked to evaluate. Did not determine whether the selected system aligns with the firm's practices. A conference booth conversation — however impressive — is not a sufficient basis for selecting a firm-wide analytical system.
🔑 Core Rule
Due diligence in service provider selection requires reviewing all relevant alternatives and assessing suitability for the firm's specific needs. Favouring a familiar name without comparative review = insufficient basis. The "black box" nature of the system (LES describes it as a "black box") makes independent review even more important.
Example 11 (Handbook) / Example 15 (LES)
Both
Jackson — Selecting Subadviser Based Only on Performance and GIPS Compliance
VIOLATION
Jackson selects a subadviser from 20+ candidates based solely on the highest total returns and GIPS compliance, due to a short turnaround deadline.
Violation of V(A). Performance and GIPS compliance alone are insufficient — must thoroughly investigate firms and operations to ensure the subadviser would increase diversification of clients' portfolios and is suitable for the fund-of-funds product.
🔑 Core Rule
GIPS compliance + strong returns is a starting point, not a complete due diligence basis. The same rigour applied to individual investment selection must be applied to manager selection. Time pressure does not reduce the V(A) standard.
Example 12 (Handbook) / Example 17 (LES)
Both
Dupont + Smith/Reichardt — Model Developer and Model User Both Violate V(A)
BOTH VIOLATE
Dupont (model developer) neglects model updates, applies a quick fix without proper testing, and creates an error that affects fund trades. Smith/Reichardt (fund manager using the model) has "only very superficial knowledge of the model" and doesn't consult Dupont before finalising client investment strategies. When the error is discovered, Smith/Reichardt rebalances without informing clients.
Both violate V(A): Dupont — accountable for model quality regardless of not trading directly; failed to test and maintain the model adequately. Smith/Reichardt — must understand the parameters, assumptions, and limitations of models relied upon for investment decisions. Not required to be an expert in every technical aspect, but must understand the basic how and why. Smith also violates V(B) by not disclosing the error to clients.
🔑 Core Rules
(1) V(A) applies to model developers even if they don't trade — the model's investment recommendations make them responsible for the quality of those recommendations. (2) V(A) applies to model users — must understand the model's assumptions and limitations at a conceptual level. "I don't understand it" is not a defence for using a model in client management. (3) The V(B) dimension: discovering and correcting errors without informing clients = separate violation.
Examples 8+9 (LES-Only)
LES Only
Papis + Shrub — Arbitrary Adviser Change / Personal Relationship as Research Basis
BOTH VIOLATIONS
Papis: CIO of a state retirement fund changes the real estate allocation to his business school classmate's new firm with apparent arbitrariness and minimal disclosure. Shrub: Creates a fund-of-funds product invested in his good friend Reed's hedge funds, relying on personal friendship and prior knowledge of performance without conducting a thorough analysis of the funds' operating procedures, reporting practices, and transparency.
Both violate V(A). Papis: a proper solicitation process was required; the decision appeared arbitrary and was motivated by personal relationships rather than diligent analysis. Shrub: personal friendship and prior performance knowledge are insufficient justification — a full review of operating procedures, reporting practices, and transparency is necessary.
🔑 Core Rules (LES-Only)
Personal relationships with managers do not substitute for due diligence. Prior knowledge of performance (even firsthand) does not substitute for a current review of the manager's operating quality. The obligation is to conduct a rigorous, objective process regardless of personal connections. Both also implicate VI(A) Disclosure of Conflicts for the personal relationships involved.
Examples 16 (LES-Only)
LES Only
Green — Relying Solely on Manager RFP Questionnaire Responses
LIKELY VIOLATION
Green creates proprietary models to screen RFP questionnaire responses from managers. Because of the large number of respondents, he uses only the data submitted by managers — no outside verification — to make recommendations for adding managers to the fund-of-funds platform.
Likely violation of V(A). Relying exclusively on self-reported data from manager questionnaires without outside verification is probably insufficient. Additional steps required: interviews with interested managers, review of regulatory filings, discussions with the managers' custodian or auditor.
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only)
Self-reported information from parties with a strong incentive to present favourably requires independent verification. The due diligence obligation goes beyond accepting questionnaire responses at face value — the member must go outside the questionnaire to verify key claims before recommending a manager for inclusion.

Exam Checklist — Standard V(A): Diligence and Reasonable Basis

  • ✅ Two requirements: (1) Diligent, independent, thorough process AND (2) Reasonable and adequate basis supported by appropriate research and investigation.
  • ✅ Standard scales with: investment philosophy, member's role, and employer resources. These factors determine the required level of diligence.
  • ✅ Time pressure ≠ reduced diligence standard. If resources are insufficient, take on less work — do not proceed without adequate basis. (Hawke)
  • ✅ Third-party research reliance: Permitted with ongoing due diligence to verify soundness. Cannot rely if there is reason to suspect bias, deficiency, or unreliability. (McDermott)
  • ✅ Internet/blog sources require more scrutiny than established research organisations. May use for ideas, but must independently validate before incorporating into analysis. (Cannon)
  • ✅ Quantitative models: Must understand assumptions and limitations — not required to be technical experts, but must know how results are used in decision-making. (Smith/Reichardt)
  • ✅ Model validation must occur BEFORE incorporating into methods — cannot test retrospectively after recommending investments. (Dupont)
  • ✅ Model developers face a HIGHER diligence standard than model users. Responsible for model quality even if they don't trade directly. (Dupont)
  • ✅ Scenario testing must go beyond historical data when good analytical reasons exist to consider scenarios not present in available data. (Thompson)
  • ✅ Integrated systems must be tested as a whole — component validation is insufficient when clients implement as an integrated strategy. (Liakos)
  • ✅ External adviser selection = same rigour as individual investment selection. Must review: code of ethics, compliance procedures, quality of returns, investment process and strategy adherence.
  • ✅ Fee alone is NOT sufficient basis for selecting an external adviser or service provider. (Ostrowski, Jackson)
  • ✅ Personal relationships ≠ due diligence. Cannot rely on personal friendship with a manager as a basis for allocation decisions. (Papis, Shrub)
  • ✅ Manager questionnaire responses alone are insufficient — must independently verify through interviews, regulatory filings, and custodian discussions. (Green)
  • ✅ Failed investment ≠ V(A) violation if the process was sound at the time. Judged ex ante, not ex post. (Newbury)
  • ✅ Group research: If satisfied the process is rigorous and has a reasonable basis, need not dissociate even if the conclusion differs from personal view. Test is confidence in the process. (Mastakis)
  • ✅ Material new information must be incorporated before delivery — cannot deliver stale analysis when material changes are known. (Chandler)
  • ✅ Presenting optimal/maximum scenarios as base cases = misrepresentation of likely outcomes = V(A) violation. (Dhaliwal)
★ V(A) QUICK REFERENCE
TWO REQUIREMENTS: (1) Diligent + independent + thorough PROCESS
(2) Reasonable + adequate BASIS supported by appropriate research
SCALES WITH: Investment philosophy + member's role + employer resources

TIME PRESSURE: Never reduces diligence standard; take on less work if resources insufficient
3RD PARTY RESEARCH: Permitted with ongoing due diligence; not if reason to suspect bias/deficiency
INTERNET SOURCES: Higher review level required; use for ideas only; validate independently

QUANT MODELS (USER): Must understand assumptions + limitations + how results are used
QUANT MODELS (DEV): Higher diligence standard; validate BEFORE distribution; not required to trade to be accountable
SCENARIO TESTING: Must go beyond historical data when analytical reasons exist
INTEGRATED SYSTEMS: Test as a whole, not just component by component

EXT ADVISER REVIEW: Same rigour as individual investment; code of ethics + compliance + returns quality + process
FEE ALONE: NEVER sufficient basis for adviser selection
PERSONAL RELATIONSHIP: NEVER substitutes for due diligence
RFP RESPONSES: Self-reported data requires independent verification

FAILED INVESTMENT: ≠ V(A) violation if process was sound at the time (judged ex ante)
GROUP RESEARCH: Need not dissociate if confident in process quality — even if disagree with conclusion
MATERIAL NEW INFO: Must update analysis before delivery
Top V(A) exam traps:
(1) "The investment failed, so V(A) must have been violated" — a bad outcome with a sound process = no violation (Newbury)
(2) "Each component of the model was individually validated" — must also test components as an integrated whole (Liakos)
(3) "He selected the adviser using the CFA Institute Model RFP questionnaire" — using the RFP tool is good practice, but relying solely on self-reported responses without verification may still violate V(A) (Green)
(4) "The model developer doesn't trade, so V(A) doesn't apply" — V(A) applies to model developers because their models give investment recommendations (Dupont)
(5) "She disagreed with the committee's conclusion so she must dissociate" — only dissociation required if she is not confident the process had a reasonable and adequate basis
