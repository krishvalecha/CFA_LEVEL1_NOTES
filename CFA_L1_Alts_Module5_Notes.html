01

Overview — Natural Resources

Natural resources = commodities + raw land used for agricultural purposes (farming and timber). They are basic production inputs fundamental to the economy.

Three main sub-categories:

  • Plants and animals → soft commodities (grown over time: livestock, grains, coffee, cash crops)
  • Energy and minerals → hard commodities (mined: copper, iron; or extracted: oil, gas)
  • Land → farmland, timberland, raw land (with associated mineral/drilling rights)
Hard vs. Soft Commodities
  • Hard: Mined (copper, iron, gold, silver, lithium, cobalt, nickel) or extracted (oil, gas, coal)
  • Soft: Grown over time (livestock, grains, coffee, soybeans, wheat)
  • Both trade on commodity exchanges and via derivatives
Key Investment Vehicles
  • ETFs, ETNs (exchange-traded products)
  • Commodity futures & forwards
  • Limited partnerships
  • REITs (farmland/timberland)
  • TIMOs (timberland investment mgmt orgs)
  • CTAs (commodity trading advisers)
  • Swaps, options on futures
🧠 Classification Anchor

Natural Resources vs. Real Estate vs. Infrastructure: Raw land / farmland / timberland = Natural Resources. Land + buildings for commercial/residential use = Real Estate. Public-use long-lived essential-service assets = Infrastructure. The key dividing line for natural resources: it's about the quality of the soil, geology, or biology — NOT the physical improvements or buildings.

💡 Raw Land Risk — "Best Alternative Use"

Raw land's main risk is the best alternative use of the land. If zoning changes, better uses emerge, or land remains undeveloped — the investor may lose out. Unlike farmland/timberland, raw land has NO biological output to generate ongoing income, so all return comes from price appreciation alone. This makes raw land the riskiest of the three land types.

Geographic Context

  • Timberland: Nearly half of global private investable timberland is in the United States. Next largest: Central/Eastern Europe, Latin America, Australia, New Zealand. Institutional investors hold ~25% of the global investable timberland base (~USD 285 billion).
  • Farmland: Global investable farmland market ≈ USD 1 trillion. Less than 5% held by institutional investors. 98% of US farmland is family owned. Globally, farmland is a primary source of family wealth.
  • Middle Eastern sovereign wealth funds have invested in farmland in Africa and Southeast Asia.
🧠 TIMO — What It Is

Timberland Investment Management Organization (TIMO): Specialist entities that support institutional investors by managing their timberland investments — analyzing, acquiring, and managing suitable timberland holdings. Used because direct timberland investing requires deep forest investment expertise to manage a forest over its full life cycle. TIMOs are often used alongside LPs, LLCs, and private REITs.

03

Farmland vs. Timberland — Investment Characteristics

The Factory & Warehouse Analogy — Timberland's Key Feature

Timberland = Factory + Warehouse

  • Factory: Trees are continuously grown (biological growth = value creation)
  • Warehouse: Trees can be stored by simply not harvesting them — harvest timing is flexible
  • This gives timberland investors the ability to harvest when prices are high and delay harvest when prices are low

Farmland contrast: Crops must be harvested when ripe — no flexibility in production timing. Farm products have a fixed harvest cycle driven by biology, not market prices.

Farmland Characteristics
  • Row crops: planted & harvested (can be multiple rounds/year depending on crop & climate)
  • Permanent crops: grow on trees or vines (orchards, vineyards)
  • Also used as pastureland for livestock
  • Must harvest when ripe — no price-timing flexibility
  • Found in smaller sizes (tens to hundreds of acres)
  • 98% of US farmland = family owned
  • Fixed cash flows (owner-renter model) OR variable (owner-operator of permanent crops)
Timberland Characteristics
  • Trees grown continuously (biological growth adds value)
  • Harvest timing is flexible — can wait for better prices
  • Long market cycle — new-growth forests take decades
  • Typically trades in large units (thousands of acres)
  • Mostly institutional owners (pension funds, endowments)
  • Managed via TIMOs for institutional investors
  • Carbon offset value in addition to timber income

Sources of Return — Both Asset Classes

  1. Output price changes: Market prices for agricultural products and timber fluctuate — this directly affects revenue
  2. Harvest quantities: Volume harvested × price = revenue (quantities affected by weather, disease, growing cycles)
  3. Land value appreciation: The underlying land itself may increase in price over time
  4. Lease income: If land is leased to operators rather than owner-operated
  5. Biological growth (timberland only): Trees grow in volume/mass each year → intrinsic value increase without any market movement

Farmland Hedging with Futures

A farm has an inherent long position in its crop (it will produce and sell the crop). To hedge price risk, farmers sell futures contracts for delivery at harvest time. This locks in a price and reduces revenue uncertainty from commodity price swings.

Agricultural commodity futures can be combined with farmland holdings to generate an overall hedged return.

ESG & Other Value Sources

  • Carbon offsets: Farmland and timberland consume carbon as part of the plant life cycle → carbon sequestration has growing commercial value
  • Water rights: Part of the direct and implied value of these properties
  • Conservation easements: May create value by supporting nature conservation traditions
  • Growing ESG investor demand for sustainable farming and timberland used for carbon offsets
  • Arable land demand may rise as ESG considerations grow in importance
⚠️ Exam Trap — Farmland Return Data vs. Reality

Despite farmland showing attractive historical returns (10.95% annualized), it has definite risks: very low liquidity, high risk of negative cash flow (fixed costs are relatively high — fertilizer, seed, land care), and highly variable revenue from weather. Farmland and timberland trade infrequently in non-public markets → they appear less volatile than they truly are (smoothed appraisal-based pricing).

⚠️ Global Risk Factor for Farmland & Timberland

Unlike local real estate, farmland and timberland generate globally traded commodities. They face global risks: interruptions in world trade, growing agricultural competition, declining grain prices. The international context is a major risk factor that doesn't affect domestic real estate in the same way.

Commodities may be further classified by physical location and grade/quality. For example, there are many grades and delivery locations for crude oil and wheat. Commodity derivative contracts specify: quantity, quality, maturity date, and delivery location.

Commodities do NOT generate cash flows — they incur costs (cost of carry: storage, transportation, insurance). Investors seek to benefit from price appreciation in excess of carry costs.

Why Derivatives (Not Physical) for Most Commodity Investing

Problems with Physical Commodity Ownership
  • Unwelcome tax obligations
  • Storage costs (warehousing)
  • Insurance costs
  • Transportation costs
  • Brokerage costs
  • Lack of price transparency in physical markets
Benefits of Derivatives
  • Traded on organized exchanges → highly liquid
  • Excellent price discovery
  • No storage/transportation costs
  • Counterparty risk managed via clearinghouse/exchange
  • Marked to market daily (futures)
  • Most commonly used: futures and forwards

Ways to Gain Commodity Exposure

  • Commodity futures/forwards: Primary method. Exchange-traded futures = marked to market daily, managed via clearinghouse. Delivery obligation becomes critical during stressed markets (e.g., oil going negative in 2020 COVID).
  • Exchange-traded products (ETPs — ETFs and ETNs): Suitable for investors restricted to equity shares or seeking simplified access. May hold physical commodity (e.g., SPDR Gold Shares ETF holds gold bullion) or futures. Can use leverage; inverse/bearish versions exist. Charge fees (expense ratios).
  • Commodity Trading Advisers (CTAs): Managed futures funds using directional strategies (technical + fundamental) primarily in futures markets. May focus on specific commodity (e.g., grains) or be broadly diversified. Individual investors can use Separately Managed Accounts (SMAs).
  • Specialized funds (e.g., private energy partnerships): Similar to PE funds. Management fees: 1%–3% of committed capital. Typical life: 10 years + 1–2 year extensions. Focus on upstream (drilling), midstream (refineries), or downstream (chemicals).
  • Public energy mutual funds: Focus on oil/gas sector. Management fees: 0.4%–1% (comparable to public equity managers).
⚠️ Delivery Obligation Risk in Futures

The physical delivery obligation in commodity futures becomes dramatically important during stressed markets. Example: During COVID-19 (April 2020), WTI crude oil futures went negative because demand collapsed, storage filled, and holders of futures contracts could not find buyers — they had to pay to have the oil taken away. Even commodity ETFs were affected and some were forced to close, imposing large losses on investors.

Government Role in Commodity Markets

  • Governments provide food price subsidies to consumers and price support to farmers
  • Governments often control extractable natural rights (energy, mining) — especially in emerging markets
  • Example: SOCAR (State Oil Company of Azerbaijan) = fully state-owned; controls oil/gas extraction, refining, and export pipelines
  • In many countries, landowners can only cultivate soil — subsurface rights (oil, gas, coal, gold, silver) owned and managed by government
  • Global climate change policies shifting demand: reducing fossil fuels → increasing renewables → higher demand for lithium, cobalt, nickel (EV batteries)

Commodity Pricing — The Core Formula

Commodity Forward Pricing Formula (Continuous Compounding)
F₀(T) = S₀ · e^(r + c − i)T
Where:
F₀(T) = Forward price (price to be paid at time T)
S₀ = Current spot price
r = Risk-free rate
c = Cost of carry (storage + transportation + insurance)
i = Convenience yield (benefit of holding the physical commodity)
T = Time to expiration of the forward contract

Key insight: Forward price is HIGHER than spot when (r + c) > i (carry costs exceed convenience yield).
Forward price is LOWER than spot when i > (r + c) (convenience yield exceeds carry costs) → backwardation.

Cost of Carry

The cost of carry = the opportunity cost of holding a physical asset. It mirrors:

  • The risk-free rate (opportunity cost of capital tied up)
  • Storage costs (warehousing)
  • Transportation costs
  • Insurance costs

The holder of the physical commodity should expect to be compensated via a higher forward price to justify bearing these costs.

Convenience Yield

A non-cash benefit from holding the physical commodity (rather than a derivative). Arises when market participants prefer physical ownership — e.g., to ensure continuous access to an input they need for operations.

  • Convenience yield is inversely related to inventory levels — when inventories are low, the benefit of having physical access is high → convenience yield rises
  • Because it is a benefit to the holder, it reduces the forward price
  • When i is very high (low inventories, tight supply), spot > forward → backwardation
🧠 No-Arbitrage Logic

The relationship F₀(T) = S₀ · e^(r+c−i)T is a no-arbitrage condition. If forward price deviated from this relationship, arbitrageurs could profit risk-free by buying spot and selling forward (or vice versa), until prices realign. The difference between spot and forward prices = cost of carry net of convenience yield.

06

Contango & Backwardation

📈 CONTANGO
  • Forward price > Spot price
  • Upward-sloping forward curve
  • Occurs when: cost of carry > convenience yield
  • (r + c) > i in the formula
  • Typical in: abundant inventory; low immediate demand
  • BAD for long-only investor — as futures expire, you roll into more expensive contracts → negative roll yield
  • Generally lowers the return of the long-only investor
📉 BACKWARDATION
  • Spot price > Forward price
  • Downward-sloping (inverted) forward curve
  • Occurs when: convenience yield > cost of carry
  • i > (r + c) in the formula
  • Typical in: low inventories, tight supply, high immediate demand
  • GOOD for long-only investor — roll into cheaper contracts → positive roll yield
  • Generally enhances the return of the long-only investor
Quick Decision Rule — Contango or Backwardation?
If (r + c) > i → F₀(T) > S₀ → CONTANGO → Long investor LOSES on roll
If i > (r + c) → F₀(T) < S₀ → BACKWARDATION → Long investor GAINS on roll

Mnemonic: "Back is BACK for the investor" (backwardation = good for long-only)
Roll yield is the gain or loss from rolling a futures contract as it approaches expiration. In contango, you sell a low-priced expiring contract and buy a higher-priced new contract → negative roll yield. In backwardation, you sell a high-priced expiring contract and buy a cheaper new contract → positive roll yield.
⚠️ Exam Trap — Forward vs. Spot Direction

The forward price is greater than spot ONLY IF carrying costs exceed the convenience yield. Do not assume forward price is always greater than spot. Oil is frequently in backwardation during supply squeezes; gold (high storage, low convenience yield) is typically in contango.

The low observed volatility is partly a measurement artifact of infrequent appraisal-based pricing, NOT evidence that these assets are truly low-risk.

Critical observations:

  • Commodities = strongest inflation hedge. Huge performance difference: +22.87% in high inflation vs. −9.26% in low inflation. Commodities (especially energy and food) are components of inflation calculations → direct link.
  • Farmland and timberland = some outperformance in higher inflation (12.78% vs. 9.85% for farmland), but the difference is much smaller than for commodities. Their performance does NOT suffer dramatically during low inflation → less "pure" inflation hedges.
  • Commodities correlation with inflation = 0.54 — much higher than any other asset class → best inflation hedge.
  • Farmland correlation with inflation = −0.17; Timberland = 0.02 → NOT effective inflation hedges by correlation measure.
🧠 Why Commodities Hedge Inflation

Energy and food commodity prices are direct inputs into CPI calculations. When inflation rises, it's often because oil prices or food prices are rising — those same rising prices benefit commodity investors directly. Stocks and bonds have no such direct linkage; they're driven by earnings growth and interest rates, which respond to inflation more indirectly.

⚠️ Exam Trap — Farmland/Timberland as Inflation Hedges

The exam may try to make you say farmland and timberland are good inflation hedges. The data shows they are not — their correlation with inflation is near zero or negative. The reason their returns don't collapse in low inflation (unlike commodities) is not because they hedge inflation, but because their return drivers (soil quality, biological growth, land appreciation) are independent of the inflation cycle.

🧠 Why Commodities and Stocks Are Correlated (0.41)

Both commodities and stocks have exposure to the global business cycle. When the economy booms, demand for commodities rises AND corporate earnings rise → both move up. When recession hits, both can fall simultaneously (as in 2008). This reduces the diversification benefit of commodities vs. stocks compared to farmland/timberland.

Supply Dynamics — Why Commodity Prices Are Volatile

  • Producers cannot alter supply quickly — extended lead times needed to change production levels
  • Agricultural output: changing crops or farming techniques requires at least one full growing cycle before results appear
  • Oil/mining infrastructure: may take many years to develop (the mine + transportation + smelting components)
  • Result: supply too low during economic booms → price spikes; supply too high when economy slows → price collapses
  • Weather = unpredictable exogenous shock for agricultural commodities (outside producer's control)
  • Despite technology advances, the cost of new supply may grow over time
  • Geopolitical factors and natural phenomena (weather) also create commodity price volatility
