Futures Pricing at Inception — Same Formula as Forwards

At initiation, futures contracts are priced using the same no-arbitrage formula as forward contracts. The futures price f₀(T) equals the spot price compounded at the risk-free rate over the contract's life — incorporating any cost of carry (storage costs, income benefits). The initial value of a futures contract is zero, just like a forward.

Futures Pricing Formulas — At Inception (Equations 2, 3, 4)
Equation 2 (Discrete — individual assets):
f₀(T) = S₀ × (1 + r)ᵀ

Equation 3 (Continuous — indexes, FX):
f₀(T) = S₀ × e^(rT)

Equation 4 (With Cost of Carry — Discrete):
f₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(I) + PV₀(C)] × (1 + r)ᵀ

Initial value: V₀(T) = 0
Same rules as forwards: Discrete compounding for individual assets. Continuous for indexes and FX.
PV₀(I) = PV of income/benefits (subtract). PV₀(C) = PV of storage costs (add).
A futures price f₀(T) significantly below the no-arbitrage price → implies a convenience yield (as for forwards).

Gold Example (Procam):
S₀ = $1,770/oz, r = 2%, T = 91/365 = 0.24932
f₀(T) = $1,770 × (1.02)⁰·²⁴⁹³² = $1,778.76/oz
Contract price = $1,778.76 × 100 oz = $177,876

With $2/oz storage cost: PV₀(C) = $2 × (1.02)⁻⁰·²⁴⁹³² = $1.99
f₀(T) = ($1,770 + $1.99) × (1.02)⁰·²⁴⁹³² = $1,780.78/oz
💡 Futures Price = Forward Price at Inception (Under Simplifying Assumptions)

At inception, f₀(T) = F₀(T) using the same no-arbitrage formula. The two prices diverge over time due to different cash flow patterns (futures = daily settlement; forward = single settlement at maturity). The conditions under which they remain equal are covered in Section 7. For the exam: use the same pricing formula for both at t=0.

02

Forward vs. Futures MTM — The Critical Difference

Both forward and futures contracts start with a price of f₀(T) = F₀(T) and zero initial value. But as time passes, the MTM value behaves completely differently due to one feature: futures contracts are marked-to-market daily; forward contracts are not.

Forward Contract — MTM Behavior
  • Forward price F₀(T) stays FIXED for the entire life
  • MTM value accumulates: Vt(T) = S_t − F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾
  • No daily cash settlement — MTM builds up
  • Can accumulate a large MTM value over time (e.g., −$5,683 in 71 days)
  • All settled in a SINGLE payment at maturity
  • Creates counterparty credit risk (no cash exchanged until T)
  • No margin account required (unless credit support agreed)
Futures Contract — MTM Behavior
  • Futures price ft(T) changes DAILY based on market
  • Daily MTM settled via margin account → contract value RESETS to zero each day
  • The "accumulated" MTM at any time = only today's daily gain/loss (small)
  • Cannot reach the large MTM values of a comparable forward
  • Cumulative realized MTM ≈ comparable forward contract settlement
  • Eliminates buildup of credit risk (daily cash settlement)
  • Requires initial margin + maintenance margin throughout
★ The Key Difference — Where the MTM Goes
FORWARD: MTM accumulates in the contract value → settled at maturity
Vt(T) ≠ 0 during the life; grows with time and spot price movement

FUTURES: MTM settled in cash DAILY → contract resets to zero each day
After each day's settlement: Vt(T) = 0 (reset to current futures price)
The "current MTM" of a futures is only today's gain/loss (before the next reset)

NET RESULT: Both approaches yield approximately the same cumulative gain/loss
(timing of cash flows differs, but final economic outcome is the same)
Why "approximately" the same cumulative payoff?
The time value of money means that receiving (or paying) cash daily is not identical to a single lump sum at maturity. For short maturities and low interest rates, the difference is trivial. For longer maturities or high-rate environments, the timing can matter — this gives rise to the forward/futures price difference (Section 7).

Daily Settlement Mechanics — Step-by-Step Gold Example

Setup: Procam buys 100-oz gold futures at f₀(T) = $1,778.76/oz. Initial margin = $4,950. Maintenance margin = $4,500. Forward comparison: F₀(T) = $1,778.76 (same price at inception).

Day 1 — Futures Price Falls $5

Day 1: f₀(T) = $1,778.76 → f₁(T) = $1,773.76 (−$5/oz)
FUTURES CONTRACT:
MTM loss = $5/oz × 100 oz = $500
Margin account: $4,950 − $500 = $4,450 ← Below maintenance ($4,500)
Margin call: Must deposit $500 to restore to $4,950
Futures contract MTM RESETS to zero at new price f₁(T) = $1,773.76

FORWARD CONTRACT (same underlying, same initial price):

Vt(T) = $1,765.12 − $1,778.76 × (1.02)⁻⁰·²⁴⁶⁵⁷ = −$4.98/oz
Total forward MTM loss = −$4.98 × 100 = −$498
NO cash settlement — loss accumulates in contract value

💡 Cumulative Effect — Forward and Futures Give Approximately Same Total Loss

After multiple days: Forward total accumulated MTM loss ≈ Futures cumulative daily MTM losses. The economic result is similar — if the gold price falls $9/oz cumulatively (from $1,778.76 to $1,769.76), both the forward and futures lose approximately $900. But: the forward collects it in one lump sum at maturity; the futures collects/pays it day by day via the margin account. The timing difference causes a small but theoretically important pricing difference between the two (covered in Section 7).

Interest Rate Futures — The (100 − Yield) Convention

Interest rate futures offer a highly liquid, standardized alternative to FRAs. The underlying is the MRR (market reference rate) on a hypothetical deposit for a specific future period. But unlike FRAs (which are quoted as rates), interest rate futures trade on a price basis using a special convention: price = 100 − yield.

Equation 5 — Interest Rate Futures Price Convention
f_A,B-A = 100 − (100 × MRR_A,B-A)

OR: MRR_A,B-A = (100 − f_A,B-A) / 100
f_A,B-A = futures price for MRR starting in A periods with tenor B−A
MRR_A,B-A = the implied market reference rate for period B−A starting at A

Example: Interest rate futures price = 98.25
MRR = (100 − 98.25) / 100 = 1.75%

Example: MRR₃ₘ,₃ₘ = 1.75% → futures price = 100 − 1.75 = 98.25

Important: This (100−yield) convention creates an INVERSE price/yield relationship — when MRR rises, futures price falls. But it is NOT the same as the price of a zero-coupon bond at the contract rate.

Long vs. Short Interest Rate Futures — Exposure Direction

Long Futures (= Lender Position)
  • Earns or receives MRR in the future
  • Gains when futures PRICE RISES (= MRR falls)
  • Benefits from FALLING interest rates
  • Equivalent to: FRA short position (floating-rate payer = FRA seller = fixed-rate receiver)
  • Example: A bank with floating-rate assets (lending at MRR) benefits when MRR falls
Short Futures (= Borrower Position)
  • Pays MRR in the future
  • Gains when futures PRICE FALLS (= MRR rises)
  • Benefits from RISING interest rates
  • Equivalent to: FRA long position (fixed-rate payer = FRA buyer = floating-rate receiver)
  • Example: Baywhite borrows at MRR; sells futures to hedge against rising MRR
PositionGains from Rising MRRGains from Falling MRRFRA Equivalent
Short futures contractYES (price falls → gain)NoLong FRA (fixed-rate payer)
Long futures contractNoYES (price rises → gain)Short FRA (floating-rate payer)
⚠️ Direction Trap — Interest Rate Futures vs. Other Futures

For most futures (equity, commodity): Long = benefits from price rising. For interest rate futures priced at (100−yield): when MRR rises, the futures PRICE FALLS → the LONG position LOSES. So for interest rate futures: Short futures benefits from rising MRR (price falls → short gains). This is counterintuitive and frequently tested. Always remember: short interest rate futures = hedging a liability (like Baywhite — borrower hedges rising MRR by selling futures).

💡 Remembering Long/Short Interest Rate Futures Direction

Think of it from the price perspective first: Long futures → profits when PRICE rises. For interest rate futures: price rises when MRR FALLS. So: Long futures → profits when MRR falls → "lender" position.
Conversely: Short futures → profits when MRR rises → "borrower" position.
The mnemonic: "Lenders love low rates (Long long-futures). Borrowers hate high rates (Sell short-futures to hedge)."

06

Basis Point Value (BPV) — Interest Rate Futures

Basis Point Value (BPV)
The dollar change in a futures contract's value for a one basis point (0.01%) change in the MRR. Interest rate futures have a fixed, linear relationship between price and yield changes — a constant BPV for any given notional and period. This linearity is a key feature distinguishing futures from FRAs (which have non-linear, convex settlement).
Equation 6 — Futures Contract BPV
Futures Contract BPV = Notional Principal × 0.01% × Period
Period = tenor of the underlying MRR expressed as a fraction of a year (e.g., 3 months = 1/4, 1 month = 1/12)
0.01% = one basis point = 0.0001

Example — 3-month MRR, $1,000,000 notional:
BPV = $1,000,000 × 0.01% × (90/360) = $1,000,000 × 0.0001 × 0.25 = $25
Every 1 bp change in the 3-month MRR changes the contract value by $25 — regardless of direction.

Baywhite Example — 1-month MRR, $50,000,000 notional:
BPV = $50,000,000 × 0.01% × (1/12) = $50,000,000 × 0.0001 × 0.0833 = $416.67
If MRR moves 100 bps: Gain/Loss = $416.67 × 100 = $41,667

BPV in Practice — Baywhite Financial Hedge

Baywhite: Sells f₁,₁ at 98.75 (MRR₁,₁ = 1.25%), settles at 97.75 (MRR₁,₁ = 2.25%)
MRR rose by 100 bps (1.25% → 2.25%)
Futures price fell from 98.75 to 97.75 (by 1.00 point = 100 bps)

Gain on short futures = BPV × bps change = $416.67 × 100 = $41,667

This gain offsets the higher MRR cost on Baywhite's floating-rate borrowing.
Without the hedge: borrowing cost rises by ~$41,667 per month for $50M
With the hedge: the futures gain compensates for the higher MRR payment
💡 BPV Is Linear — Constant for All Rate Changes

Unlike FRA settlement (which involves discounting and is therefore non-linear/convex), the futures contract BPV is CONSTANT — a fixed $25 per basis point regardless of whether MRR moves from 2% to 2.01% or from 5% to 5.01%. Every single basis point change always causes the same dollar move in the futures margin account. This linearity makes futures easy to use for hedging: number of contracts needed = target BPV / futures contract BPV.

When Forward = Futures Price — and When They Differ

Despite their similar payoff profiles at maturity, the different cash flow timing (daily settlement for futures vs. single settlement at maturity for forwards) can create a pricing difference between the two contracts. Understanding when prices are equal — and when they differ — is a key exam topic.

★ Conditions for Forward Price = Futures Price
CONDITION 1: Interest rates are CONSTANT
→ No reinvestment advantage/disadvantage to receiving daily vs. lump-sum cash flows
→ Daily margin gains/losses reinvested at the same rate as lump-sum settlement
→ f₀(T) = F₀(T)

CONDITION 2: Futures prices and interest rates are UNCORRELATED
→ The reinvestment rate for daily futures gains/losses is independent of whether prices rose or fell
→ No systematic advantage to one settlement pattern vs. the other
→ f₀(T) = F₀(T) (on average)

WHEN THESE CONDITIONS ARE VIOLATED:
Positive correlation (futures prices ↑ when rates ↑):
→ Long futures more attractive (gains reinvested at HIGH rates; losses occur at LOW rates)
→ Futures price HIGHER than forward price: f₀(T) > F₀(T)

Negative correlation (futures prices ↑ when rates ↓):
→ Long forward more attractive (avoid the disadvantage of gains at low rates)
→ Futures price LOWER than forward price: f₀(T) < F₀(T)
General rule: The more desirable (attractive) contract tends to trade at a HIGHER price. If long futures is more attractive than long forward (positive correlation case), futures price will be bid up above forward price.

The price differential also depends on interest rate VOLATILITY: Higher rate volatility → larger potential reinvestment advantage/disadvantage → larger futures/forward price difference.

In practice: For most short-maturity contracts (under 1 year) and typical market conditions, the futures/forward price difference is very small. The main exception: the convexity bias in interest rate futures (Section 8).
Positive Correlation (Prices ↑ when Rates ↑)

Example: Equity futures — when stock markets rally (prices rise), the economy is strong and rates tend to rise. Daily MTM gains are reinvested at higher rates. Daily losses occur when rates are falling.

Result: Long futures preferred → f₀(T) > F₀(T)

Negative Correlation (Prices ↑ when Rates ↓)

Example: Treasury bond futures — when bond prices rise, rates are falling (flight to safety). Daily gains occur when rates are low (poor reinvestment). Daily losses occur when rates are high.

Result: Long forward preferred → f₀(T) < F₀(T)

⚠️ Interest Rate Futures — Always: f₀(T) < F₀(T) (Convexity Bias)

Interest rate futures have a systematic NEGATIVE correlation between futures prices and interest rates — when rates rise, bond/interest rate futures prices fall. This makes long forward positions more attractive than long futures (the negative correlation disadvantages the long futures holder). Therefore: interest rate futures prices are BELOW comparable FRA prices. The magnitude of this convexity bias grows with the tenor and maturity of the instrument. This is covered in detail in Section 8.

08

Convexity Bias — Interest Rate Futures vs. FRAs

Even when the underlying rate and forward period are identical, interest rate futures and FRA contracts have different payoffs due to a feature unique to FRAs: the settlement is discounted at the prevailing MRR. Futures contracts have NO discounting — their settlement is a fixed linear function of the rate change. This creates a non-linear (convex) relationship for FRAs vs. a linear one for futures — known as convexity bias.

The Difference in Settlement — FRA vs. Futures
FUTURES settlement (for 1 bp change, fixed linear):
ΔValue = BPV = Notional × 0.01% × Period = CONSTANT
Example: Always $25 per bp change (for $1M notional, 3-month period)

FRA settlement (discounted, non-linear):
Net Payment (end) = (MRR − IFR) × Notional × Period
PV = Net Payment / (1 + MRR × Period/Year)
→ PV changes non-linearly with MRR because the discount rate ALSO changes
The discounting feature of FRAs creates the convexity property:
When MRR FALLS: FRA gains MORE (both numerator rises AND denominator shrinks → double benefit)
When MRR RISES: FRA loses LESS (both numerator falls AND denominator grows → partial offset)
This asymmetry (larger gains when MRR falls, smaller losses when MRR rises) is CONVEXITY.

For futures: symmetric. Every 1 bp move = exactly the same dollar amount regardless of direction.

Key observation: The FRA settlement values are smaller in absolute terms than the futures settlement values — both when MRR rises AND falls. This asymmetry is the convexity property: the FRA benefits from larger gains on rate declines and smaller losses on rate increases RELATIVE to futures. The futures has a perfectly linear (symmetric) payoff — each 1 bp always = exactly $25.

Implication for pricing: The FRA's convexity is a valuable feature — the FRA is worth slightly MORE than the futures due to this non-linear advantage. Therefore, to be equivalent in no-arbitrage terms, the futures PRICE must be set LOWER than the FRA rate → futures price < FRA rate (or equivalently: futures implied rate > FRA implied forward rate). This is the convexity bias adjustment.

★ Convexity Bias — Key Direction Rule
FRA has CONVEXITY (non-linear, curved price/yield relationship)
→ FRA gains more when rates fall, loses less when rates rise
→ FRA is more VALUABLE than futures for the same rate

To compensate: Interest rate futures price is SET LOWER than FRA
Equivalently: Interest rate futures IMPLIED RATE > FRA fixed rate

f₀(T) < FRA price → futures implied MRR > FRA implied forward rate

The convexity bias is LARGER for:
→ Longer forward periods (more time for MRR to change)
→ Longer underlying tenor (larger BPV per bp change)
→ Higher interest rate volatility
Interest rate futures vs. FRA — price/yield relationships:
Interest rate futures: LINEAR price/yield (fixed BPV per bp, symmetric) — like a straight line
FRA (and bonds): NON-LINEAR, CONVEX price/yield — like the convex curve from Fixed Income Module 6

This is the SAME convexity concept from fixed income applied to derivatives! The FRA is essentially a short-duration bond equivalent, and bonds have convexity.
💡 Convexity Bias — The Fixed Income Connection

Convexity in derivatives = exact same concept as convexity in fixed income (Module 6):
Fixed income convexity: Price rises more than duration predicts when yields fall; falls less than duration predicts when yields rise. Convexity is valuable → convex bonds have higher prices.
FRA convexity: FRA gains more when MRR falls; loses less when MRR rises. Convexity is valuable → FRAs have higher value than linear-payoff futures for the same rate.
The FRA's discounting mechanism creates this convexity, just as the discounting mechanism creates convexity in bond pricing.

09

Central Clearing — Effect on Forward/Futures Price Difference

The post-2008 central clearing mandate (covered in Module 1) has created futures-like margining requirements for OTC derivatives — including forward contracts. This has narrowed the practical difference between forwards and futures in terms of cash flow patterns.

Central Clearing's Effect on OTC vs. ETD
  • OTC dealers (who sell forwards to end users) are now required to post margin to the CCP — cash or highly liquid securities
  • Dealers pass this requirement on to their derivatives end users — requiring end users to also post collateral similar to futures margin
  • Result: OTC forwards now have SIMILAR cash flow patterns to exchange-traded futures (both require daily-like collateral management)
  • This reduces the cash flow difference between ETDs and OTC derivatives that previously gave rise to the forward/futures price difference
  • In practice: the forward/futures price difference has become even smaller after central clearing mandates
  • Market stress scenario: During periods of large price movements + counterparty stress, futures may be FORCED CLOSED (clearinghouse can't accept failed margin call), while OTC forwards with more flexible credit terms may remain outstanding

Practical implication for portfolio managers: Investors using both ETD futures AND OTC forwards must maintain sufficient cash or eligible collateral to fulfill margin/collateral requirements on both. Additional costs to consider:

  • Financing costs: Cost of borrowing cash to post as margin/collateral
  • Transaction costs: Exchange fees, bid-offer spreads for ETD; dealer bid-offer for OTC
  • Administrative costs: Compliance reporting, margin management infrastructure
💡 Why Central Clearing Reduces (but Doesn't Eliminate) Forward/Futures Price Difference

Before central clearing: forwards had NO margin requirements → pure credit risk → very different cash flow patterns from futures → larger forward/futures price differences.
After central clearing: forwards require margin-like collateral → similar cash flow patterns to futures → smaller price differences.
However, the difference is NOT completely eliminated because: (1) collateral terms for OTC may still differ from futures (eligible collateral types, haircuts); (2) the convexity bias in interest rates remains a structural feature; (3) in stress periods, the practical behavior still differs (OTC may remain open while futures are forcibly closed).

═══ FORWARD vs. FUTURES MTM ═══
Forward: F₀(T) fixed; Vt(T) = S_t − F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾; settled at maturity only
Futures: ft(T) changes daily; Vt(T) = 0 after each daily settlement; margin mechanism
Cumulative P&L: approximately equal for both

═══ INTEREST RATE FUTURES ═══
Price = 100 − (100 × MRR) ← inverse price/yield
Long futures → lender → gains when MRR falls (price rises)
Short futures → borrower → gains when MRR rises (price falls)
Short futures ≡ Long FRA (fixed payer) | Long futures ≡ Short FRA (floating payer)
BPV = Notional × 0.01% × Period ← fixed, linear, symmetric

═══ FORWARD vs. FUTURES PRICE DIFFERENCE ═══
Equal when: rates constant OR prices uncorrelated with rates
f₀ > F₀ when: positive correlation (prices ↑ with rates ↑)
f₀ < F₀ when: negative correlation (prices ↑ with rates ↓)
Interest rate futures: always f₀ < FRA price (negative correlation → convexity bias)

═══ CONVEXITY BIAS ═══
FRA: non-linear payoff (discounted settlement) → convex → more valuable
Futures: linear payoff (fixed BPV) → no convexity
Result: Futures implied MRR > FRA implied forward rate
Bias grows with: longer period, longer tenor, higher rate volatility
Memory shortcut — interest rate futures direction: "Short = Sell = Borrower = Hedges rising MRR." Long position benefits from falling MRR. Same as owning a floating-rate asset (lender).
Convexity bias = same concept as bond convexity from Fixed Income: FRA is like a bond with convexity; futures is like a linear (duration-only) instrument. Convex instruments are worth more → futures must be priced lower.
Central clearing reduced (but didn't eliminate) the forward/futures cash flow difference — hence also reduced (but didn't eliminate) the price difference.
