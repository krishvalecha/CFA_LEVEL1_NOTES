01

FX Market Basics & Quoting Conventions

Key Facts About the FX Market

  • Largest market in the world — daily turnover ~USD 6.6 trillion (2019); ~10–15× global fixed-income; ~50× global equities
  • Operates 24 hours a day every business day — truly global via electronic networks
  • Facilitates international trade AND cross-border capital flows
  • Regulated by the Bank for International Settlements (BIS) which conducts a Triennial Survey. In 2017, the Global Foreign Exchange Committee (GFXC) established the voluntary FX Global Code for best practices.
  • FX market composition: Spot transactions + Forward transactions + FX Swaps (largest daily volume) + Exchange-traded derivatives

Exchange Rate Quoting — A/B Convention

Exchange Rate Convention
A/B = number of units of currency A per ONE unit of currency B
A = Price currency (quote currency)  |  B = Base currency (always = 1 unit)
Example: USD/EUR = 1.1700 → 1 euro buys 1.1700 US dollars
EUR is the base currency; USD is the price currency
In a/b notation, the BASE currency is second (denominator), PRICE currency is first (numerator)

Direct vs. Indirect Quotes

Direct Quote
  • Domestic currency = Price currency (numerator)
  • Foreign currency = Base currency
  • Example for Paris-based trader: EUR/GBP (euros per pound)
  • EUR/GBP = 1.1211 → GBP1 costs EUR1.1211
Indirect Quote
  • Domestic currency = Base currency (denominator)
  • Foreign currency = Price currency
  • Example for Paris-based trader: GBP/EUR (pounds per euro)
  • GBP/EUR = 0.8920 → EUR1 costs GBP0.8920
  • Direct and indirect are simply reciprocals of each other

Market Hierarchy for Base Currency (Professional FX)

  1. EUR is base currency in any pair involving it (e.g., GBP/EUR, USD/EUR)
  2. GBP is base currency if EUR is not in the pair (e.g., USD/GBP, CAD/GBP)
  3. USD is base currency if neither EUR nor GBP is in the pair (e.g., CAD/USD, JPY/USD)
  4. Exception: AUD and NZD serve as base against USD (USD/AUD, USD/NZD)

Bid/Offer Spread

Banks quote a two-sided price: Bid (bank buys base currency at lower price) / Offer or Ask (bank sells base currency at higher price).

Example: CHF/EUR = 1.1583 – 1.1585. Client sells EUR1 and receives CHF1.1583; client buys EUR1 and pays CHF1.1585. Bank buys low, sells high — the spread is the bank's profit margin.

Electronic dealing systems have compressed bid/offer spreads to very tight levels through global competition.

⚠ Exam Trap — Base Currency Identification

In the notation A/B, B is the BASE currency (set at 1 unit) and A is the PRICE currency. When the quote says "GBPJPY," GBP is the BASE and JPY is the price — so the actual number is JPY per GBP (JPY/GBP). The base currency is listed FIRST in the name/code but is SECOND in the ratio. This reversal trips up many candidates.

Nominal vs. Real Exchange Rates

Nominal Exchange Rate

The nominal exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another — the rate that is actually quoted and traded in the FX market (e.g., USD/EUR = 1.1700).

Real Exchange Rate

The real exchange rate adjusts the nominal rate for differences in price levels (inflation) between two countries. It measures relative purchasing power — how many real foreign goods you can buy with domestic currency.

Real exchange rates are NOT quoted or traded in FX markets — they are analytical indexes created by economists and analysts to understand international competitiveness.

Real Exchange Rate Formula
Real ER(d/f) = Nominal Spot(d/f) × (Pf / Pd)
d = domestic currency (price currency in d/f notation)
f = foreign currency (base currency)
Pf = foreign price level  |  Pd = domestic price level (CPI or similar)

Intuition: If foreign prices rise OR nominal exchange rate rises (domestic currency weakens) → Real ER rises → you can buy FEWER foreign goods → lower real purchasing power
Approximate % Change in Real Exchange Rate
%ΔReal ER ≈ %ΔNominal ER + %ΔPf − %ΔPd
(Exact formula multiplies the three factors: (1 + %ΔS) × (1 + %ΔPf)/(1 + %ΔPd) − 1)
For small changes, the approximation works well.
Worked Example — Real Exchange Rate Change (UK/Eurozone)

Given: GBP/EUR nominal spot ↑ 10%, Eurozone CPI ↑ 5%, UK CPI ↑ 2%

%ΔReal ER ≈ 10% + 5% − 2% = 13%

→ Real ER rose 13% → UK purchasing power for Eurozone goods declined ~13% → Eurozone goods are more expensive in real terms

Worked Example — India/US Real Exchange Rate (2018)

Given: INR/USD nominal spot ↑ 6.7% (USD appreciated), US inflation 2.5%, India inflation 4.7%

%ΔReal ER ≈ 6.7% + 2.5% − 4.7% ≈ 4.5%

→ Real ER rose only ~4.5% despite nominal ↑ of 6.7% because India's higher inflation partially offset the nominal weakness → India's real purchasing power in USD terms declined.

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

PPP theory: Nominal exchange rates adjust in the long run so that identical goods (or standardized baskets) have the same price in different markets (law of one price extended to currencies).

In practice: PPP is a poor predictor of nominal exchange rate movements because: goods are not identical across countries, many services are non-traded, trade barriers and transaction costs exist, capital flows dominate trade flows in determining rates. Nominal rates can deviate from PPP for years.

The Big Mac Index (The Economist) is a simple, widely known PPP comparison — shows wide variation in purchasing power across countries for a standardized product.

💡 Key Intuition — Real Exchange Rate Direction

Real ER (domestic/foreign) is the "real price" you pay to buy foreign goods. Higher real ER → more expensive foreign goods → lower your real purchasing power. If your domestic currency weakens nominally (S rises) OR foreign prices rise, the real ER rises. If your domestic inflation rises, the real ER falls (your goods become relatively cheaper). Even if the nominal rate doesn't move, inflation differentials change the real ER and affect trade competitiveness.

03

Percentage Change in Exchange Rates

Core Formula

% Change in Exchange Rate
% Change = (New Rate − Old Rate) / Old Rate
A positive result means the BASE currency appreciated (more price currency needed per unit of base). A negative result means the base currency depreciated. Always identify which is the base currency first.
Worked Example — Euro Appreciation

Given: USD/EUR rate moves from 1.1500 to 1.2000

% Change = (1.2000 − 1.1500) / 1.1500 = 0.0500 / 1.1500 = +4.35%

→ Euro (base) APPRECIATED 4.35% against the USD

This is euro appreciation (not USD appreciation) because USD/EUR has USD as the price currency. When USD/EUR rises, each euro buys more USD → euro is worth more → euro appreciated.

USD Depreciation — Inverting the Quote

Same move expressed from USD perspective:

EUR/USD old = 1/1.1500 = 0.8696  |  EUR/USD new = 1/1.2000 = 0.8333

% Change = (0.8333 − 0.8696) / 0.8696 = −4.17%

→ USD DEPRECATED 4.17% against euro — NOT the same percentage as euro appreciation (4.35%)

⚠ Critical Exam Point — Appreciation ≠ Depreciation % (in magnitude)

If currency A appreciates by X% against currency B, then currency B does NOT depreciate by the same X%. The percentages are mathematically different because the bases are different. In the example: euro ↑ 4.35% vs. dollar ↓ 4.17%. This asymmetry is always tested.

Appreciation vs. Depreciation — Quote Direction

If A/B exchange rate...Base currency (B)...Price currency (A)...
Rises (↑)Appreciates (more A needed per B)Depreciates
Falls (↓)Depreciates (less A needed per B)Appreciates
💡 Memory Trick — USD/EUR Rises → Euro Stronger

If USD/EUR rises from 1.15 to 1.20, each euro now buys MORE dollars → euro is more valuable → euro appreciated. Think: "the price currency (USD) is what you pay — if you have to pay MORE USD per euro, the euro got more expensive (appreciated)." The base currency appreciating = its price tag going up.

04

FX Market Participants

Buy Side vs. Sell Side

Sell Side (FX Dealers)
  • Large FX trading banks that make markets and provide quotes
  • Tier-1: Deutsche Bank, Citigroup, UBS, HSBC — huge fixed-cost investment in technology; global client base; often cross trades internally
  • Tier 2/3: Regional/local banks — often outsource FX to tier-1 banks
Buy Side (FX Users)
  • All entities that transact in FX to meet financial, business, or investment needs
  • 6 main categories: Corporate, Real Money, Leveraged, Retail, Government, Central Banks (+ SWFs)

Buy-Side Categories

Corporate Accounts

FX for cross-border goods/services purchases and sales. Also M&A, foreign asset investment, foreign currency borrowing.

Real Money Accounts

Insurance companies, mutual funds, pension funds, endowments, ETFs. Restricted in use of leverage/derivatives — invest for long-term return.

Leveraged Accounts

Hedge funds, proprietary trading shops, commodity trading advisers (CTAs), high-frequency algorithmic traders. Actively manage FX risk for profit. Fastest-growing share of daily turnover. Wide range of styles (macro, HFT, technical).

Retail Accounts

Individuals (including tourists), smaller hedge funds. Electronic trading has surged retail participation. Estimated ~10% of spot transactions in some pairs. Includes Japanese households moving savings into foreign currencies.

Governments

FX for military purchases, consulates, overseas bases. Also debt issuance in foreign currencies. Public pension plans (e.g., Caisse de dépôt et placement du Québec — CAD 420bn AUM). Mix of transactional and public policy motives.

Central Banks

Intervene to manage exchange rate levels — to prevent excessive weakness/strength or when market dysfunctional. Also manage FX reserves (~USD 13 trillion total globally; ~60% in USD, ~20% in EUR). Bank of Japan intervened 2004 and 2011; Swiss National Bank intervened 2010–2015.

Sovereign Wealth Funds (SWFs): Government entities with investment mandates (not public policy focus). Countries with large current account surpluses (Singapore, Kuwait, oil exporters) channel surplus into SWFs. Can be enormous and materially affect exchange rates. Akin to real money accounts but may use derivatives or aggressive strategies.

Exchange Rate Regimes

The Impossible Trinity (Trilemma)

The ideal currency regime would have three properties: (1) credibly fixed exchange rates, (2) fully convertible currencies, and (3) independent monetary policy. But all three cannot coexist simultaneously:

If rates are fixed AND currencies fully convertible → any attempt at independent monetary policy (e.g., cut rates) → unlimited capital outflow seeking higher returns elsewhere → CB forced to buy domestic currency to maintain peg → money supply falls → rates rise back to equality → policy negated.

The more freely the exchange rate floats and the more capital convertibility is restricted, the more effective is independent monetary policy.

🧠 Impossible Trinity — "FCI" — Pick Any Two

Fixed rate + Convertibility + Independent monetary policy — pick any two. Most countries choose: floating rate + convertibility + independence (developed markets) OR fixed rate + convertibility but sacrifice independence (dollarization, currency board).

Historical Timeline

PeriodSystemKey Feature
19th C – 1914Classical Gold StandardCurrencies fixed to gold. Price-specie-flow mechanism auto-adjusted trade imbalances via gold flows → money supply changes → price changes → trade rebalances.
1930sCollapse / ChaosProtectionism, deflation, hyperinflation → gold standard abandoned. World trade fell >50%.
1944–1973Bretton WoodsFixed parities with periodic realignments. US dollar linked to gold; others pegged to USD. Collapsed 1973 due to chronic inflation and speculative attacks.
1973–presentFlexible Rates (Smithsonian)Most industrial countries float. Much higher exchange rate volatility than expected — investment flows dominate trade flows in setting rates.
1979–1992European ERM"Snake" — limited flexibility bands. UK forced out by speculative attack in Sep 1992 (currency crisis + German high rates).
1999–presentEuro / EMUCommon currency for most of Western Europe. Eliminates intra-Eurozone FX risk but removes individual country monetary policy.

IMF Classification — 8 Regime Types (Fixed → Float)

MOST FIXED
1. No Separate Legal Tender
Country uses another nation's currency (dollarization) OR joins a monetary union (EMU/euro). Zero monetary independence. Inherits credibility but NOT creditworthiness. Seigniorage goes to foreign CB (dollarization) or is shared (monetary union).
Examples: Panama, Ecuador, El Salvador (dollarized); Eurozone (EMU)
↓
2. Currency Board System (CBS)
Legislative commitment to exchange domestic currency at a fixed rate for a specified foreign currency. Domestic currency issued ONLY against foreign exchange — fully backed by foreign assets. CB cannot function as lender of last resort. Earns seigniorage (unlike dollarization).
Example: Hong Kong SAR — HKD/USD fixed at 7.80 since 1983
↓
3. Fixed (Conventional) Parity
Fixed to single currency or basket. Band of ±1% around parity. No legislative commitment (can abandon). CB can serve as lender of last resort. Credibility depends on FX reserve levels. Vulnerable to speculative attack.
Examples: Many emerging markets pegged to USD
↓
4. Target Zone (Wider Band)
Fixed parity with ±2% horizontal intervention bands. More discretionary policy space than simple fixed parity.
Example: Similar to old European ERM mechanism
↓
5. Active Crawling Peg
Exchange rate pre-announced for coming weeks/months in small steps. Goal: anchor inflation expectations. Rate changes signal future inflation changes for import-priced goods.
Examples: Argentina, Chile, Uruguay (1980s)
↓
6. Passive Crawling Peg
Exchange rate adjusted frequently (weekly/daily) to keep pace with current inflation. Prevents reserve depletion. Reactive rather than forward-looking.
Examples: Brazil and Latin America (1980s high inflation periods)
↓
7. Managed Float (Dirty Float)
CB intervenes based on internal or external policy targets (trade balance, price stability, employment) without pre-announced targets. Risk of inviting retaliation from trading partners. Exchange rate target implicit, not explicit.
Examples: China (historically), India, many emerging markets
MOST FLOAT
8. Independently Floating
Exchange rate determined by market forces. CB has full monetary independence — can target inflation, employment, etc. Can serve as lender of last resort. Even here, occasional intervention occurs (Plaza Accord 1985; ECB/Fed/BoJ coordinated euro support 2000).
Examples: USD, EUR, GBP, JPY, CHF, CAD, AUD — the major currencies
💡 CBS vs. Dollarization — The Key Difference

Both give up monetary independence. BUT under a Currency Board System (CBS), the country retains its own currency (just fixed to another at a set rate) and earns seigniorage (profit from issuing money). Under dollarization, the country literally uses another country's currency — seigniorage goes to the foreign CB (e.g., the US Fed). Also, CBS can have a convertibility zone with minor FX fluctuations; dollarization has zero FX risk between the two currencies.

Trade Balance, Capital Flows & Exchange Rates

The Fundamental Identity

Trade Balance Identity
X − M = (S − I) + (T − G)
X = Exports  |  M = Imports  |  S = Private Savings  |  I = Investment (plant & equipment)
T = Taxes (net of transfers)  |  G = Government Expenditure

Trade surplus (X > M) → Country saves MORE than needed for domestic investment → excess saving flows abroad (buys foreign assets)
Trade deficit (X < M) → Country saves LESS than domestic investment needs → must borrow from or sell assets to foreigners

Trade Balance and Capital Account — Mirror Image

A trade deficit (surplus) is exactly offset by a capital account surplus (deficit). They are two sides of the same coin. Any factor affecting the trade balance has an equal and opposite impact on capital flows — they cannot affect one without affecting the other.

  • Trade surplus: Country exports more → earns more foreign currency → uses it to buy foreign assets (capital outflow) → capital account deficit
  • Trade deficit: Country imports more → pays foreign currency → foreigners invest those funds back (capital inflow) → capital account surplus

How Exchange Rates Affect Trade and Capital Flows

Capital flows dominate in the short to medium term — financial investment decisions and asset prices adjust much faster than expenditure/saving decisions and goods prices.

In a floating exchange rate regime: If investors expect a currency to depreciate, they sell it → currency depreciates → reduces further depreciation expectations → caps capital flows → exchange rate stabilizes. The exchange rate itself does the adjustment work.

In a fixed exchange rate regime: CB intervenes (buys domestic currency with FX reserves) to offset private capital outflows and maintain the peg → adjustment happens via interest rates, not exchange rates.

Long-term exchange rate movements are increasingly driven by trade flows and expenditure/saving decisions, while short-term movements are primarily driven by capital flow expectations.

💡 Key Takeaway — Short vs. Long Run FX Drivers

Short/medium term: Capital flows dominate exchange rate determination. Investor expectations about returns and risk drive most FX movements.
Long term: Trade flows and relative prices (including PPP relationships) become increasingly important as goods prices and saving/investment decisions adjust.

Exchange Rate Impact on Competitiveness

Currency MovementImpact on ExportsImpact on ImportsImpact on Trade Balance
Domestic currency depreciates ↑ Rise (cheaper for foreigners to buy) ↓ Fall (more expensive for domestic consumers) Improves (X↑, M↓)
Domestic currency appreciates ↓ Fall (more expensive for foreigners) ↑ Rise (cheaper for domestic consumers) Worsens (X↓, M↑)

Capital Restrictions

Definition & Economic Case for Free Capital Flows

Capital restrictions: Controls on foreigners' ability to own domestic assets OR domestic residents' ability to own foreign assets (limit openness of financial markets — distinct from trade restrictions which limit goods markets).

Economists favor free capital flows because: (1) capital flows to highest-return use; (2) inflows fund investment beyond domestic savings; (3) FDI brings technology, skills, management practices; (4) creates local supplier networks; (5) forces domestic firms to be more efficient through competition.

Why Governments Restrict Capital Flows

Inflow Restrictions
  • Prevent "hot money" inflows that cause currency appreciation → hurts export competitiveness
  • Protect strategic industries from foreign ownership (defense, telecom)
  • Employment and regional development objectives
  • Prevent speculative bubbles in domestic asset markets
Outflow Restrictions
  • Prevent capital flight during macroeconomic crises
  • Restrict repatriation of profits, dividends, royalties
  • Generate revenue for government (keeps capital in domestic economy for taxation)
  • Maintain low interest rates (reduce govt borrowing costs)
  • Complement fixed exchange rate targets

Forms of Capital Restrictions

  • Price controls (taxes): Special taxes on returns to international investment; taxes on certain transaction types
  • Mandatory reserve requirements: Foreign depositors must deposit a % of inflow with CB at zero interest for a minimum period
  • Quantity restrictions: Ceilings on new foreign borrowing; special authorization requirements
  • Administrative controls: Government agency must approve certain cross-border asset transactions
  • Outright prohibitions: Complete bans on certain types of international capital transactions

Effectiveness and Costs of Capital Restrictions

IMF findings: Restrictions on capital inflows require comprehensive coverage and forceful implementation to avoid circumvention. Controls on capital outflows during crisis provide mixed results — temporary relief in some cases; Malaysia's use during 1997–98 Asian crisis was a notable success giving it time to restructure.

Costs of capital restrictions:

  • Non-trivial administration costs; loopholes must be continuously closed
  • Postpones necessary policy adjustments
  • Impedes private-sector adaptation to changing international circumstances
  • Negative market perceptions → makes foreign funding more costly and difficult to access

Capital Controls and Fixed Exchange Rates — Complementarity

Capital restrictions and fixed exchange rate targets are complementary instruments. Under perfect capital mobility, governments cannot achieve both domestic and external policy objectives simultaneously using standard monetary and fiscal tools (impossible trinity). By limiting capital flows, capital controls allow CB to maintain the exchange rate peg while using other tools for domestic objectives.

⚠ Exam Point — Capital Restrictions vs. Trade Restrictions

Trade restrictions limit openness of goods markets (tariffs, quotas, subsidies). Capital restrictions limit openness of financial markets (controls on buying/selling assets across borders). Both are government interventions but operate in different markets with different effects.
