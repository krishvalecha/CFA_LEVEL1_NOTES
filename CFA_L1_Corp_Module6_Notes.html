Weighted-Average Cost of Capital (WACC)

Cost of Capital / WACC
An issuer's required rate of return on its capital investments, derived from its investors' required rates of return. Used as r in NPV analysis and as the hurdle rate for IRR analysis. The objective: minimize WACC to maximize firm value (lower WACC → higher NPV for any given set of cash flows).
★ WACC Formula
WACC = (r_d × W_d) + (r_e × W_e)

After-tax version (most common):
WACC = [r_d × (1 − t) × W_d] + (r_e × W_e)

With preferred stock or other financing:
WACC = [r_d × (1−t) × W_d] + (r_p × W_p) + (r_e × W_e)
r_d = before-tax cost of debt | r_e = cost of equity | r_p = cost of preferred stock
W_d, W_e, W_p = weights (proportions) of debt, equity, preferred stock
t = marginal corporate tax rate
Weights must sum to 1.0. Use market value weights (or management's disclosed target weights).
Interest is tax-deductible → multiply cost of debt by (1 − t) → interest tax shield lowers effective debt cost.

Two management objectives in capital structure:

  1. Minimize WACC — every 1% reduction in WACC increases the NPV of every project and creates value
  2. Match duration of assets and financing — fund long-term assets with long-term capital (match funding principle)

Important: The cost of debt and equity are NOT chosen by management — they are determined by investors in financial markets. Management influences but does not set these rates.

Weights: Market Value vs. Book Value

Market Value Weights (Preferred for WACC)
  • Reflect current prices of debt and equity
  • Based on investors' opportunity costs at current prices
  • Used in WACC calculations
  • Can fluctuate significantly — stock price changes shift weights
Book Value Weights (For Target Capital Structure)
  • Reflect historical costs of capital
  • Less volatile than market values
  • Used for management's target capital structure
  • Aligned with how lenders, rating agencies, and debt covenants measure leverage
Converting D/E Ratio to a Weight
W_d = (D/E) / (1 + D/E) = D / (D + E)
Example: D/E = 0.5 → W_d = 0.5/1.5 = 33.3%; W_e = 1/1.5 = 66.7%
02

WACC — Component Costs

Component Definition How Estimated Tax Treatment Relative Level
Cost of Debt (r_d) Debt investors' required return on debt financing Interest rate on existing unsecured debt (historical starting point) OR recent borrowing rates of comparable companies (forward-looking) Interest is tax-deductible → multiply by (1−t) → LOWER effective cost Lower (priority, fixed claim → less risk)
Cost of Equity (r_e) Equity investors' required return on equity financing No observable rate. Start with historical equity market returns (e.g., ~10% CAGR US long-run); adjust for company-specific factors. More formal methods (CAPM, DDM) covered in later modules. Dividends are NOT tax-deductible → no tax benefit Higher (residual claim → more risk; no tax deductibility)
Cost of Preferred Stock (r_p) Preferred stockholders' required return Preferred dividend / preferred stock price Preferred dividends typically NOT tax-deductible Between debt and equity
After-Tax Cost of Debt
After-tax cost of debt = Before-tax cost of debt × (1 − Marginal tax rate)
Example: Pre-tax cost of debt = 6%, Tax rate = 25%
After-tax cost of debt = 6% × (1 − 0.25) = 4.5%

Key insight: The tax deductibility of interest is one of the most important reasons firms use debt — it reduces the effective cost of borrowing.
💡 Why Cost of Debt < Cost of Equity

Three reasons: (1) Debt has priority over equity in cash flows and liquidation → less risk → lower required return. (2) Debt may be secured by specific assets (collateral) → further reduces risk. (3) Interest is tax-deductible → lowers effective cost. All three make debt cheaper for the issuer than equity.

💡 Estimating Target Weights When Undisclosed

If management hasn't disclosed target capital structure, analysts can: (1) Use current capital structure at market value weights; (2) Examine capital structure trends or management statements; (3) Use averages of comparable companies' capital structures. All three are valid approaches — the exam may test which is most appropriate in context.

03

Factors Affecting Capital Structure — Business Model

The total amount and type of financing needed — and therefore the weights in WACC — are primarily determined by the issuer's business model and life cycle stage. The costs of debt and equity are determined by top-down market factors and issuer-specific factors.

Capital-Intensive vs. Capital-Light Businesses

Capital-Intensive Businesses

Require large amounts of physical assets

  • Industries: utilities, transportation, real estate, semiconductor manufacturing, natural resources
  • Signals: low asset turnover, high capex/sales, high NWC/sales
  • Need external financing for assets
  • Often regulated (e.g., banks must maintain minimum equity ratios → raises WACC)
  • Trend: many have separated capital-intensive operations from brand/service business (e.g., Hilton franchises; NVIDIA outsources manufacturing to TSMC/Samsung)
"Capital-Light" / Asset-Light Businesses

Low capital needs; mainly intangibles and cash

  • Industries: technology platforms, service businesses
  • Signals: high asset turnover, low capex/sales
  • Network model: operate platform but don't own underlying assets (Uber, Airbnb)
  • May receive cash upfront / negative CCC → no external WC financing needed
  • May compensate employees with stock → reduces cash financing needs
  • If early-stage profitable and capital-light → may not need significant external financing
⚠ Regulatory Capital Constraints

Governments regulate some firms' capital structures. Banks must maintain minimum equity ratios (Basel standards). Utilities are regulated on pricing — constraining leverage. These regulatory requirements typically increase WACC because they force higher equity financing (equity costs more than debt).

04

Corporate Life Cycle and Capital Structure

Capital structure is dynamic, not static — it evolves as the firm matures. Business risk declines over time and cash flows stabilize, allowing progressively greater use of lower-cost debt.

Life Cycle Stage Revenue Growth Free Cash Flow Business Risk Debt Availability Typical Debt Type Dominant Financing
Startup / Early Stage Minimal / Zero Negative High Little or none Convertible debt, leases Equity — founders, employees, VC (not IPO; exchanges have min. requirements)
Growth Rising, accelerating Improving (still negative) Medium Rising Secured debt Equity dominant; conservative debt use to preserve flexibility
Mature Slowing but predictable Stable & positive Low High Unsecured debt Significant debt; aim for investment-grade rating; distribute to shareholders (dividends/buybacks)
Convertible Debt
Debt that gives investors the right to convert to equity in the future at a predetermined price, while usually deferring interest payments until maturity. Available to startups when traditional debt is not — investors accept lower/deferred interest for the equity upside.
💡 Life Cycle ≠ Industry Stage

A startup or growth company can exist in a mature industry. Examples: new restaurant concepts, new fashion brands in established sectors; TikTok (advertising), Shopify (retail), Tesla/BYD (automobiles) — all disrupted mature industries at their growth stage. Don't assume industry maturity = firm maturity.

05

Determinants of Costs of Debt & Equity

Top-Down Factors

  • Economic conditions (macro): Debt investors start with risk-free sovereign rate → demand a credit spread above it. Macroeconomic factors (real growth, inflation, monetary policy, exchange rates) affect both sovereign rates and credit spreads. Recession → higher spreads (especially cyclicals: mining, materials, industrials). Companies want to borrow when rates are low and issue equity when stock prices are high.
  • Industry conditions: The products/services an issuer sells determine its macro exposure. Example: Higher oil prices → tighter credit spreads for oil producers (revenue up) but wider spreads for airlines (input cost up).

Issuer-Specific Factors

Factor Definition / Formula Impact on Cost of Capital
Sales Risk Stability, predictability, and growth of revenues. Influenced by firm size, customer diversification, product type (subscription vs. one-time), and economic sensitivity. Stable, predictable revenues → lower risk premium → lower cost of debt and equity. Highly cyclical revenues → higher required return (e.g., auto manufacturing vs. SaaS).
Operating Leverage Operating leverage = Fixed costs / Total costs. High operating leverage = higher proportion of fixed costs. High operating leverage amplifies swings in profitability for a given revenue change → higher business risk → higher required returns from both debt and equity investors.
Financial Leverage & Interest Coverage Financial leverage: D/E or Debt/EBITDA. Interest coverage = Profit before interest and taxes / Interest expense. High existing debt → less room for incremental debt → higher cost of debt (more default risk). For equity investors: high debt means large priority claims ahead of them → higher equity required return.
Collateral / Asset Type Assets that can be pledged or sold in default: real estate, aircraft, automobiles, receivables (fungible, liquid, generate cash). Intangible/knowledge assets cannot easily serve as collateral. Tangible, liquid collateral → lower cost of debt (secured lending). Tech/pharma/service firms with mostly intangibles → higher financial distress costs → higher cost of debt.
Key Ratio Formulas — Issuer Factors
Operating Leverage = Fixed Costs / Total Costs
(Higher = profits more volatile for given revenue change → higher risk)

Interest Coverage = Profit Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT) / Interest Expense
(Higher = more able to service debt → lower default risk → lower cost of debt)
06

Modigliani–Miller — Assumptions

In their landmark 1958 paper, Nobel laureates Franco Modigliani and Merton Miller (MM) argued that under certain assumptions, a company's capital structure is irrelevant to firm value. Firm value equals the present value of expected future cash flows, discounted by WACC — which does NOT change with capital structure under their assumptions.

Assumption 1
Homogeneous Expectations

All investors agree on a given investment's expected cash flows.

Assumption 2
Perfect Capital Markets

No transaction costs, no taxes, no bankruptcy costs. Everyone has the same information (no asymmetry).

Assumption 3
Risk-Free Borrowing Rate

Investors can borrow and lend at the risk-free rate.

Assumption 4
No Agency Costs

Managers always act to maximize shareholder wealth — no principal-agent conflicts.

Assumption 5
Independent Decisions

Financing and investment decisions are independent of each other.

💡 Key Insight — What MM Tell Us

None of these assumptions hold perfectly in reality — but that's the point. MM showed what changes when you relax each assumption. By identifying where and why capital structure matters (taxes, financial distress costs), they gave practitioners a rigorous framework. The fundamental takeaway remains: it's the firm's future cash flows, not capital structure, that primarily drive value.

07

MM Propositions I & II — Without Taxes

The "Homemade Leverage" Argument

MM's core insight: investors can replicate any capital structure themselves by borrowing and lending alongside their share ownership. If a firm has 50% debt and an investor wants 70% debt, she can simply borrow personally to lever up her position. This "homemade leverage" means the firm cannot create value by changing capital structure — investors can already do it themselves.

MM used arbitrage to formalize this: if V_levered ≠ V_unlevered, investors would sell the overvalued and buy the undervalued until values equalized. Therefore, capital structure cannot affect value.

MM Proposition I — Without Taxes
  1. V_L = V_U (levered firm = unlevered firm in value)
  2. Firm value is determined solely by expected future cash flows — NOT by capital structure
  3. In the absence of taxes, WACC is unaffected by capital structure
MM Proposition II — Without Taxes (Equity Cost Rises with Leverage)
  1. Higher leverage raises the cost of equity exactly offsetting the benefit of lower-cost debt → WACC unchanged
  2. The increase in cost of equity perfectly offsets the greater use of lower-cost debt
  3. Cost of equity is a linear function of D/E:
    r_e = r_0 + (r_0 − r_d) × (D/E)
    r_0 = cost of all-equity firm; r_d = cost of debt; D/E = leverage ratio
⚠ Critical Insight — Without Taxes: WACC Doesn't Change With Leverage

This is the foundational result. As you add debt (cheaper financing), the cost of equity rises to exactly offset the benefit. The WACC remains flat regardless of how much debt you add. Note: MM did NOT assume away bankruptcy — they assumed it occurs at zero cost.

08

MM Propositions I & II — With Corporate Taxes

When MM relaxed the no-tax assumption, the analysis changes dramatically. Interest is tax-deductible → debt creates a tax shield that enhances firm value. The tax savings are real and add to firm value — not cancelled out by rising equity costs.

★ MM Prop I with Taxes — Firm Value Formula
V_L = V_U + tD
V_L = value of levered firm
V_U = value of all-equity (unlevered) firm
t = marginal corporate tax rate
D = market value of debt
tD = present value of the debt tax shield

As D increases → tD increases → V_L increases. Taken to the extreme: optimal capital structure = 100% debt (unrealistic).
MM Proposition I — With Corporate Taxes
  1. In the presence of taxes, a profitable company can increase its value by using debt
  2. The higher the tax rate, the greater the benefit of debt in the capital structure
  3. V_L = V_U + tD (levered firm is worth more by the amount of the tax shield)
★ MM Prop II with Taxes — Cost of Equity Formula
r_e = r_0 + (r_0 − r_d)(1 − t) × (D/E)
Compare to no-tax version: r_e = r_0 + (r_0 − r_d) × (D/E)
With taxes: (1−t) factor reduces the slope → cost of equity rises more slowly with leverage than in the no-tax case
This means the tax benefit of debt is NOT fully offset by rising equity costs → WACC actually FALLS as debt increases
MM Proposition II — With Corporate Taxes
  1. Cost of equity still rises with more debt, but at a slower rate than in the no-tax case (because of the (1−t) dampening factor)
  2. As debt increases, WACC decreases and firm value increases (the tax shield benefit now outweighs the rising equity cost)
  3. In the presence of taxes but NO financial distress costs, the use of debt is value-enhancing — extreme result: 100% debt = optimal
Scenario V_L vs. V_U WACC as Debt ↑ Cost of Equity as D/E ↑
No Taxes, No Distress Costs V_L = V_U (equal) Unchanged (flat) Rises linearly — exactly offsets lower debt cost
With Corporate Taxes, No Distress Costs V_L = V_U + tD (V_L > V_U) Decreases (tax shield benefit) Rises, but more slowly than no-tax case (slope dampened by (1−t))
With Taxes AND Financial Distress Costs V_L = V_U + tD − PV(distress costs) Falls then rises (U-shaped) → optimal at D* Rises; beyond D* distress costs dominate

Costs of Financial Distress

Financial Distress
Heightened uncertainty regarding a company's ability to meet its obligations because of diminished earnings power or actual losses. Even before formal bankruptcy, distressed companies face significant operational and financial damage.
Direct Costs of Financial Distress
  • Legal fees (bankruptcy proceedings)
  • Administrative fees
  • Accounting and advisory costs
  • Other cash expenses associated with bankruptcy
Indirect Costs of Financial Distress
  • Lost business — customers stop buying (fear of no warranty, support)
  • Creditors tighten terms or withdraw credit
  • Suppliers demand cash payment instead of trade credit
  • Key employees leave (fear of job security)
  • Reputational damage
  • Foregone investment opportunities
  • Agency costs of debt: manager-debtholder conflicts intensify near bankruptcy

Which Firms Face Higher/Lower Financial Distress Costs?

Lower Distress Costs — Better Debt Capacity
  • Airlines, shipping companies, some manufacturers
  • Have tangible, liquid assets with ready secondary markets
  • Assets can be sold in bankruptcy without major value loss
  • E.g., aircraft, ships, real estate, receivables
Higher Distress Costs — Less Debt Appropriate
  • Technology, pharmaceutical, IT, service firms
  • Assets are primarily intangible (IP, brand, human capital)
  • Value evaporates quickly in distress — customers and employees leave
  • Difficult to use as collateral or sell

Probability of financial distress rises with:

  • More debt in the capital structure
  • Higher sales risk and operating leverage
  • Lower liquidity

Optimal Capital Structure — Static Trade-Off Theory

★ Static Trade-Off Theory — Firm Value with Taxes AND Distress Costs
V_L = V_U + tD − PV(Costs of Financial Distress)
At LOW debt: tax benefit (tD) > distress costs → V_L rises with debt → ADD more debt
At HIGH debt: distress costs > tax benefit → V_L falls with more debt → REDUCE debt
OPTIMAL POINT D*: marginal benefit of tax shield = marginal cost of financial distress → V_L is maximized
Beyond D*: more debt reduces firm value

Key implications of the static trade-off theory:

  • Optimal capital structure exists at D* where MB(tax shield) = MC(financial distress)
  • Firms should target a range around D*, not a precise number (transaction costs, market fluctuations)
  • Example: "30–50% debt" range rather than "exactly 40% debt"
  • Actual capital structure may deviate from target: management exploits favorable market conditions; market value changes in equity/debt shift weights; transaction costs prevent continuous rebalancing

Target Capital Structure — Book vs. Market Values

Why target is expressed in book values:

  1. Market values fluctuate substantially and seldom impact the appropriate borrowing level. A rising stock price is an opportunity to raise equity, not a signal to reduce debt.
  2. Management focuses on capital invested by the company, not in the company — how projects and WC will be funded.
  3. Aligned with third-party measures — lenders, debt investors, and credit rating agencies use book values; management must align to these for covenants and ratings.

Despite using book values for targets, management watches market prices and interest rates closely to gauge timing and optimal type of new financing.

💡 Target Capital Structure — Common Expressions

Management typically expresses targets indirectly via: Maximum Debt/EBITDA ratio (e.g., "we target net debt/EBITDA ≤ 2.5×") or Minimum credit rating (e.g., "we aim to maintain BBB/Baa investment-grade rating"). These are more practical than a precise debt/equity percentage and align directly with lender and rating agency metrics.

Pecking Order Theory & Agency Cost Implications

Pecking Order Theory (Myers & Majluf, 1984)
Due to information asymmetry between managers and investors, firms prefer financing methods with the least potential information content (internal funds) over those with the most (public equity). Managers choose financing in a hierarchy based on what signals to outsiders.
Pecking Order — Financing Hierarchy (Most Preferred → Least Preferred)
1st: Internal financing (retained earnings / operating cash flows) — least information content
2nd: Private debt (bank loans, private placements) — moderate information content
3rd: Public debt (bonds) — higher information content
4th: Public equity issuance — MOST information content; used last
Key insight: Investors are suspicious that managers issue equity when stock is overvalued → existing shareholders wouldn't want to share ownership if prospects are strong. Public equity issuance = negative signal. Debt issuance = commitment to pay → positive signal of confidence.

Signaling Effects of Capital Structure Decisions

Action Signal to Market Typical Investor Reaction
Equity issuance (public) Negative — management believes stock is overvalued; willing to dilute existing owners; OR needs capital beyond what internal sources can fund Stock price often falls on announcement of new equity issuance
Debt issuance / Dividends initiation Positive — management confident in future cash flows to service committed payments (too costly for poorly performing firms to afford) Often interpreted as management confidence signal
Share repurchase Positive — management believes stock is undervalued; returning excess capital Stock price often rises on buyback announcement

Agency Costs and Jensen's Free Cash Flow Hypothesis

  • High debt disciplines managers: committed interest and principal payments force efficient cash management → leaves less free cash flow for discretionary or wasteful spending
  • Michael Jensen (1986) — Free Cash Flow Hypothesis: Higher debt levels force managers to manage efficiently and use cash wisely to meet debt service → reduces agency costs of equity (empire building, wasteful investment)
  • Savings in agency costs of equity can arise with more debt → a partial benefit of leverage beyond the tax shield
💡 Static Trade-Off vs. Pecking Order — Key Difference

Static trade-off: optimal capital structure exists — firms have a target D* balancing tax benefit and distress cost → firms will adjust toward target over time. Pecking order: no single optimal structure — firms just take the cheapest available financing; observed leverage is a byproduct of historical financing choices, not a target. Both theories coexist in practice — real firms use both lenses.
