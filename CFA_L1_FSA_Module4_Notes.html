01

Cash Flow Statement — Overview & Structure

The statement of cash flows reports the change in an entity's cash, cash equivalents, and restricted cash between balance sheet dates. Unlike the income statement (accrual basis), the cash flow statement shows actual cash received and paid — critical for valuation (company payments to investors are made in cash), liquidity assessment, and detecting earnings quality issues.

It is a flow statement (like the income statement) — it covers a period of time, not a single point.

The Three Sections — What Each Covers

① CFO — Operating Activities
  • Cash effects of transactions that enter into the determination of net income
  • Cash from customers, to suppliers, to employees, for taxes, for interest (US GAAP)
  • The "engine" of the business — should be the primary source of cash for a healthy company
  • Can be presented: Direct (gross receipts/payments) or Indirect (start from net income, adjust)
  • Indirect method is most common in practice
② CFI — Investing Activities
  • Cash from purchase/sale of long-term assets (PP&E, investments, businesses)
  • Purchase of equipment → cash outflow (investing)
  • Sale of equipment → cash inflow (investing, regardless of gain/loss on IS)
  • Capital expenditures (capex) are here — key for understanding growth investment
  • Always presented using direct method regardless of CFO presentation
③ CFF — Financing Activities
  • Cash flows related to the company's capital structure
  • Issuance/repurchase of debt and equity
  • Dividends paid to shareholders (US GAAP: always CFF)
  • Repayment of borrowings (principal only — interest is CFO under US GAAP)
  • Always presented using direct method
Cash Flow Statement — The Bottom Line Reconciliation
Beginning Cash Balance
+ Net Cash from Operating Activities (CFO)
+ Net Cash from Investing Activities (CFI)
+ Net Cash from Financing Activities (CFF)
─────────────────────────────────────────
= Ending Cash Balance (must equal Cash on ending Balance Sheet)
This is the fundamental integrity check of the cash flow statement. If the beginning cash + net changes does not equal the ending cash per the balance sheet, there is an error. The cash flow statement is the bridge that explains how the cash balance moved from one balance sheet date to the next.
💡 Direct vs. Indirect — Who Uses What?

Both IFRS and US GAAP encourage the direct method but allow either. In practice, the indirect method dominates — most companies use it because it's easier to prepare (just start with net income and adjust). Under US GAAP, even if a company uses the direct method, it must also provide a reconciliation of net income to CFO (i.e., an indirect method reconciliation as a supplement). Key insight for the exam: CFI and CFF are always presented the same way regardless of which method is used for CFO.

02

Linkages Between the Financial Statements

The four financial statements are deeply interconnected. Understanding these linkages is not just conceptual — it allows analysts to detect accounting irregularities by identifying inconsistencies across statements. For example: revenue growing rapidly while cash collections stagnate (receivables building) could signal premature revenue recognition.

The Big Picture — How Statements Connect

Statement Linkages — The Flow
  • Balance Sheet → Cash Flow Statement: The beginning and ending cash balances on the balance sheet are reconciled by the cash flow statement. BS (yr1) cash + net CFS flows = BS (yr2) cash.
  • Income Statement → Balance Sheet (via retained earnings): Net income flows into retained earnings (equity). Statement of shareholders' equity shows this linkage explicitly.
  • Income Statement → Cash Flow Statement: Under the indirect method, net income from the IS is the starting point for CFO. Adjustments bridge accrual income to cash flows.
  • Balance Sheet changes → Cash Flow Statement: Changes in working capital accounts (∆ receivables, ∆ inventory, ∆ payables) directly appear as adjustments in CFO. Changes in long-term assets explain CFI. Changes in debt/equity explain CFF.
  • Statement of Shareholders' Equity → Balance Sheet & IS: The equity statement reconciles opening to closing equity using net income (from IS) and dividends (from CFS).

The Key Accrual-to-Cash Linkage Rule

Differences between accrual accounting (IS) and cash accounting (CFS) always manifest as changes in current assets or current liabilities on the balance sheet. This is the core mechanical logic behind all CFO adjustments:

Revenue > Cash Collected

→ Accounts Receivable increases (company earned revenue but hasn't received cash yet)

Cash from customers = Revenue − ∆ A/R (subtract the increase)

Cash Received > Revenue

→ Deferred Revenue increases (company received cash before earning it)

Cash from customers = Revenue + ∆ Deferred Revenue (add the increase)

Expense > Cash Paid

→ Accrued Liability increases (expense accrued but not yet paid in cash)

Cash paid = Expense − ∆ Accrued Liability (subtract the increase)

Cash Paid > Expense

→ Prepaid Asset increases (cash paid but benefit not yet recognized as expense)

Cash paid = Expense + ∆ Prepaid (add the increase)

The Universal Accrual → Cash Conversion Formula
Cash collected = Revenue − ∆ Accounts Receivable + ∆ Deferred Revenue
Cash paid = Expense + ∆ Prepaid − ∆ Accrued Liability

For Working Capital adjustments (Indirect Method rule):
∆ Current Operating ASSET ↑ → SUBTRACT from net income (used more cash than IS shows)
∆ Current Operating ASSET ↓ → ADD to net income (used less cash than IS shows)
∆ Current Operating LIAB ↑ → ADD to net income (paid less cash than IS shows)
∆ Current Operating LIAB ↓ → SUBTRACT from net income (paid more cash than IS shows)
Memory trick: "Asset up = cash down (subtract). Liability up = cash up (add)." Think of it as: if receivables go up, you earned revenue but didn't collect it — so real cash received is LESS than the IS revenue → subtract. If payables go up, you expensed items but didn't pay for them → real cash paid is LESS than the IS expense → add back.

Direct Method — Cash Flows from Operating Activities

The direct method shows operating cash flows by their major categories: gross cash receipts and gross cash payments. It answers the question: "where did the cash actually come from and go to?" It is more informative than the indirect method for trend analysis but harder to prepare — which is why most companies use indirect. However, the mechanics of computing each direct method line item are highly testable.

The approach: start with an accrual-basis income statement amount → adjust for changes in the related balance sheet accounts → arrive at the cash-basis figure.

① Cash Received from Customers

Formula: Cash from Customers
Cash from Customers = Revenue
    − Increase in Accounts Receivable (or + Decrease in A/R)
    − Increase in Deferred Revenue collected but not yet earned... wait:
    + Increase in Deferred Revenue (more cash collected than revenue recognized)
    − Decrease in Deferred Revenue (less cash collected; earning prior period deposits)

② Cash Paid to Suppliers

💡 Two-Step Process for Suppliers

Step 1: Convert COGS to Purchases: Purchases = COGS + ∆ Inventory (if inventory rises, you bought more than you sold). Step 2: Convert Purchases to Cash Paid: Cash paid = Purchases − ∆ Accounts Payable (if A/P rises, you bought more on credit than you paid for in cash → subtract). Both steps are combined in the final formula below.

Cash Paid to Suppliers — Full Formula
Step 1: Purchases = COGS + Increase in Inventory (or − Decrease in Inventory)
Step 2: Cash Paid = Purchases − Increase in A/P (or + Decrease in A/P)

Combined: Cash Paid = COGS + ∆ Inventory − ∆ Accounts Payable

Indirect Method — Cash Flows from Operating Activities

The indirect method reconciles net income (accrual basis) to net cash from operating activities (cash basis). It makes three categories of adjustments to net income: (1) remove non-operating items, (2) add back non-cash items, and (3) adjust for working capital changes. The result is the same CFO figure as the direct method — just arrived at differently.

The Three Adjustment Categories

①
Remove Non-Operating Items
Items already included in net income that belong in CFI or CFF, not CFO — their cash effects are shown elsewhere.
  • Subtract gains on sale of assets (cash from sale belongs in CFI; the gain inflated net income → remove it)
  • Add back losses on sale of assets (loss reduced net income → add back; cash received still in CFI)
  • Gains/losses on debt retirement: same logic — remove from CFO
  • Income from equity method investments (if cash dividend was less than income recognized): complex — remove income, add back actual dividends received
②
Add Back Non-Cash Charges (Subtract Non-Cash Credits)
Items that reduced (or increased) net income but involved no cash movement.
  • Add back: Depreciation, amortization, depletion (non-cash expense)
  • Add back: Amortization of bond discount (increases interest expense above cash paid)
  • Add back: Impairment losses on assets (non-cash)
  • Add back: Increase in deferred tax liability (tax accrued but not yet paid in cash)
  • Subtract: Amortization of bond premium (reduces interest expense below cash paid)
③
Working Capital Changes
Adjustments for the difference between accrual-basis income items and their cash equivalents.
  • Current Operating Asset ↑ → Subtract (e.g., A/R ↑ → collected less cash than revenue earned)
  • Current Operating Asset ↓ → Add (e.g., inventory ↓ → sold more than purchased → cash positive)
  • Current Operating Liability ↑ → Add (e.g., A/P ↑ → paid less cash than purchased)
  • Current Operating Liability ↓ → Subtract (e.g., wages payable ↓ → paid more cash than expensed)

Examiner Focus — Indirect Method Adjustments

This is one of the highest-frequency calculation areas in FSA. The exam often asks which adjustments to net income are required under the indirect method. The two most commonly tested traps: (1) Depreciation is added back — it's non-cash, it reduced net income, so we add it back to get to cash. But depreciation is NOT a source of cash; it simply means "this expense didn't require cash." (2) Gain on sale is subtracted — the gain inflated net income, but the entire cash proceeds from the sale appear in CFI (investing activities). To avoid double-counting, the gain is removed from CFO. The cash itself is not "lost" — it just shows up in CFI. Similarly, a loss on sale is added back because the cash in CFI represents the actual proceeds (which already reflect the loss).

Converting Indirect to Direct Method

Most companies publish only the indirect method. An analyst who wants to analyze trends in specific cash flows (e.g., "are cash collections growing faster than revenue?") can convert to an approximate direct format using a 3-step process. The conversion is an approximation — accurate enough for most analytical purposes.

STEP 1
Disaggregate Net Income into Revenues and Expenses
Break net income back into its components. Total revenues and total expenses from the income statement. They should sum to net income: Revenues − Expenses = Net Income.
  • Acme: Total revenues = $23,803; Total expenses = $21,593; Net income = $2,210 ✓
STEP 2
Remove All Non-Cash and Non-Operating Items
Strip out items that don't belong in the direct method operating section.
  • Remove gain on sale of equipment ($205) from revenues → adjusts revenues to $23,598
  • Remove depreciation expense ($1,052) from expenses → adjusts expenses to $20,541
  • This leaves only the accrual-basis operating revenues and expenses
STEP 3
Convert Accrual Amounts to Cash Flows Using Working Capital Changes
Apply the same balance sheet adjustments used in the direct method to each revenue/expense line.
  • Revenue → Cash from customers: Revenue − ∆A/R
  • COGS → Cash to suppliers: COGS + ∆Inventory − ∆A/P
  • Salary expense → Cash to employees: Expense − ∆Wages Payable
  • Other expenses → Cash for expenses: Expense − ∆Prepaid − ∆Accrued Liab
  • Interest expense → Cash for interest: Expense ± ∆Interest Payable
  • Tax expense → Cash for taxes: Expense ± ∆Tax Payable ± ∆Deferred Tax

06

Cash Flows from Investing Activities (CFI)

CFI captures all cash flows from the purchase and sale of long-term assets (PP&E, investments in securities, acquisitions of businesses). It is always presented using the direct method regardless of how CFO is presented. A consistently negative CFI is generally healthy for a growing company — it means they're investing in future productive capacity.

Common CFI Items

INFLOWS (positive CFI)

  • Cash proceeds from sale of PP&E (regardless of gain or loss)
  • Cash proceeds from sale of investments/securities
  • Cash proceeds from sale of businesses
  • Repayment of loans made to others

OUTFLOWS (negative CFI)

  • Capital expenditures (purchase of PP&E)
  • Purchase of investments/securities
  • Purchase of businesses/acquisitions
  • Loans made to other entities
  • Capitalized interest (under some IFRS treatments)

Computing Cash from Sale of Equipment — The Key Calculation

💡 The Equipment Sale Calculation — Three Steps

When a company sells equipment mid-year, you need to figure out the cash received. The approach: (1) Find the original cost of equipment sold using the equipment account T-account; (2) Find accumulated depreciation on the sold equipment using the accum. depreciation account T-account; (3) Cash received = Book value of sold asset + Gain (or − Loss) on sale.

Acme Corp — Cash from Equipment Sale (New equipment purchased: $1,300; Gain on sale: $205)
StepCalculationAmount
① Find cost of equipment sold Beg equip $8,555 + Purchased $1,300 − End equip $8,798 = $1,057 (cost of disposed equipment)
② Find accum. depreciation on sold equipment Beg accum. dep $2,891 + Dep expense $1,052 − End accum. dep $3,443 = $500 (dep on disposed equipment)
③ Book value of sold equipment Cost $1,057 − Accum. dep $500 = $557 (net book value)
④ Cash proceeds from sale Book value $557 + Gain $205 = $762 ← CFI inflow

Why this matters: The $205 gain appeared in net income (IS) and was REMOVED from CFO in the indirect method. The actual $762 cash from the sale appears here in CFI. Total cash is fully captured — it's just in CFI, not CFO.

Cash Flows from Financing Activities (CFF)

CFF reflects cash flows from capital structure changes — issuing or repaying debt, issuing or repurchasing equity, and paying dividends. Like CFI, it's always presented using the direct method. Understanding CFF is key for assessing how a company funds itself and returns capital to investors.

Common CFF Items

INFLOWS (positive CFF)

  • Proceeds from issuing debt (bonds, notes, loans)
  • Proceeds from issuing equity (common stock, preferred stock)

OUTFLOWS (negative CFF)

  • Repayment of debt principal (principal only; interest → CFO under US GAAP)
  • Repurchase of common stock (buybacks)
  • Payment of dividends (US GAAP: always CFF; IFRS: CFF or CFO)

Computing Dividends Paid — The Retained Earnings T-Account

Dividends Paid Calculation — Always From Retained Earnings
Retained Earnings: Beg + Net Income − Dividends = End
→ Dividends = Beg Retained Earnings + Net Income − End Retained Earnings

Acme: $2,876 + $2,210 − $3,966 = $1,120 in dividends paid → CFF outflow
This is the standard way to "back into" dividends paid when the CFS is not directly provided but the balance sheet and income statement are available. Watch out: if there are other items that affect retained earnings (e.g., correction of error restated through retained earnings), those need to be included in the formula.

IFRS vs. US GAAP — Cash Flow Classification Differences

This is a high-frequency exam topic. The key differences between IFRS and US GAAP cash flow statements concern the classification of certain items as CFO, CFI, or CFF. Under IFRS, companies have more flexibility. Under US GAAP, the rules are more rigid.

ItemUS GAAPIFRSExam Significance
Interest Received CFO (only) CFO or CFI Under IFRS, can be CFI if viewed as a return on investment. US GAAP: always CFO.
Interest Paid CFO (only) CFO or CFF Under IFRS, some companies put interest paid in CFF (it relates to financing debt). US GAAP: always CFO — even though principal is CFF.
Dividends Received CFO (only) CFO or CFI Under IFRS, can be CFI (return on investment). US GAAP: always CFO.
Dividends Paid CFF (only) CFF or CFO Under IFRS, some companies show dividends paid as CFO (it's a cost of obtaining equity capital). US GAAP: always CFF.
Income Taxes CFO (only) Generally CFO IFRS: If taxes can be specifically identified with investing or financing (e.g., tax on gain from discontinued operation sale), can classify there. US GAAP: ALL taxes → CFO.
Bank Overdrafts CFF (not part of cash) Part of Cash & equivalents Under IFRS, bank overdrafts that are repayable on demand and form an integral part of cash management are treated as a component of cash, not financing.
⚠️ Why IFRS Flexibility Matters for Analysts

When IFRS companies make different elections (e.g., Company A puts interest paid in CFO; Company B puts it in CFF), the CFO figures are NOT directly comparable even for companies in the same industry and country. Analysts must adjust for these classification differences before comparing CFO across IFRS companies. For example: a company that classifies dividends paid as CFO will show lower CFO than one that classifies them as CFF — even with identical underlying cash flows. Always check the cash flow statement notes for classification policies.

Examiner Focus — IFRS vs. US GAAP Cash Flow Classification

This table is frequently tested with questions like: "Under IFRS, interest paid can be classified as ___." Or: "Which of the following is a difference between IFRS and US GAAP cash flow statements?" The most tested items: interest paid (CFO under US GAAP; CFO or CFF under IFRS) and dividends paid (CFF under US GAAP; CFF or CFO under IFRS). Also commonly tested: dividends received (CFO under US GAAP; CFO or CFI under IFRS). Remember: under US GAAP, all of these are fixed. Under IFRS, they're all flexible (management choice).

Exam Checklist — Module 4: Cash Flows I

  • ✅ CFS structure: CFO + CFI + CFF = Net change in cash. Ending cash = Beginning cash + net changes. Must match cash on ending balance sheet.
  • ✅ CFO = operating cash flows (customer collections, supplier payments, employee payments, interest/taxes under US GAAP). CFI = long-term asset purchases/sales. CFF = debt issuance/repayment, equity issuance/buybacks, dividends paid (US GAAP).
  • ✅ Direct method: Shows gross operating cash receipts and payments. Adjusts accrual IS items using balance sheet changes. More informative but less common in practice.
  • ✅ Indirect method: Starts with net income; adjusts for (1) non-operating items, (2) non-cash items, (3) working capital changes. Most common in practice.
  • ✅ CFI and CFF are always presented using the direct method regardless of whether direct or indirect is used for CFO.
  • ✅ Cash from customers = Revenue − ∆A/R + ∆Deferred Revenue. A/R ↑ → subtract (earned but not collected).
  • ✅ Cash paid to suppliers = COGS + ∆Inventory − ∆A/P. Inventory ↑ → add (bought more than sold). A/P ↑ → subtract (bought on credit, haven't paid).
  • ✅ Cash paid to employees = Salary expense − ∆Wages Payable. Wages payable ↑ → subtract (accrued more than paid).
  • ✅ Cash for interest = Interest expense ± ∆Interest Payable. Payable ↑ → subtract; payable ↓ → add.
  • ✅ Cash for taxes = Tax expense ± ∆Tax Payable ± ∆Deferred Tax Liab. DTL ↑ → subtract (taxed less than expense).
  • ✅ Indirect method: Depreciation/amortization/impairment → ADD BACK (non-cash charge, reduced net income).
  • ✅ Indirect method: Gain on sale of assets → SUBTRACT (non-operating; cash from sale is in CFI).
  • ✅ Indirect method: Loss on sale of assets → ADD BACK (non-operating; loss removed; cash in CFI).
  • ✅ Working capital indirect method rule: Current operating asset ↑ → subtract. Current operating liability ↑ → add. (Opposites for decreases.)
  • ✅ Equipment sale — cash proceeds = Book value + Gain (or − Loss). Book value = Cost − Accum. depreciation on sold asset. Use T-accounts on equipment and accum. dep accounts to derive cost and dep on disposed items.
  • ✅ Dividends paid = Beg R/E + Net Income − End R/E. Always back into dividends from retained earnings when not directly given.
  • ✅ Indirect → Direct conversion (3 steps): (1) Disaggregate net income to revenues/expenses; (2) Remove non-cash/non-operating items; (3) Adjust each line for working capital changes.
  • ✅ IFRS flexibility: Interest received (CFO or CFI), interest paid (CFO or CFF), dividends received (CFO or CFI), dividends paid (CFF or CFO), taxes (generally CFO, but portion can be CFI/CFF if identifiable). All fixed under US GAAP.
  • ✅ US GAAP fixed rules: Interest received = CFO. Interest paid = CFO. Dividends received = CFO. Dividends paid = CFF. All taxes = CFO. Bank overdrafts = CFF (not cash).
  • ✅ Bank overdrafts: IFRS = part of cash and cash equivalents. US GAAP = financing activity (not part of cash).
  • ✅ IFRS requires disclosure of direct method equivalent when indirect is used. US GAAP also encourages direct but requires reconciliation even if direct is used.
★ FSA MODULE 4 — COMPLETE QUICK REFERENCE ★
═══ CFS STRUCTURE ═══
Beg Cash + CFO + CFI + CFF = End Cash (must = balance sheet cash)

═══ DIRECT METHOD — KEY FORMULAS ═══
Cash from customers = Revenue − ∆A/R + ∆Deferred Revenue
Cash paid suppliers = COGS + ∆Inventory − ∆A/P
Cash paid employees = Salary exp − ∆Wages payable
Cash paid int (US GAAP CFO) = Int exp ± ∆Int payable (payable ↑ = subtract)
Cash paid taxes = Tax exp ± ∆Tax payable ± ∆DTL (DTL ↑ = subtract)
Cash paid other opex = Other exp + ∆Prepaid − ∆Accrued liab

═══ INDIRECT METHOD ADJUSTMENTS ═══
Start: Net income
+ Depreciation / Amortization / Impairment (non-cash)
− Gain on asset sale (non-operating → appears in CFI)
+ Loss on asset sale (non-operating → add back)
+ Amortization of bond discount (non-cash interest add-on)
− Amortization of bond premium (non-cash interest reduction)
+ DTL increase / − DTL decrease
− Current operating asset ↑ / + Current operating asset ↓
+ Current operating liability ↑ / − Current operating liability ↓
= Net CFO

═══ EQUIPMENT SALE PROCEEDS ═══
Cost of sold asset: Beg equip + Purchases − End equip
Dep on sold asset: Beg accum dep + Dep exp − End accum dep
Book value: Cost − Accum dep on sold asset
Cash proceeds: Book value + Gain (or − Loss)

═══ DIVIDENDS PAID ═══
Beg R/E + Net Income − Dividends = End R/E
→ Dividends = Beg R/E + NI − End R/E

═══ IFRS vs. US GAAP CLASSIFICATION ═══
Item US GAAP IFRS
Interest received CFO CFO or CFI
Interest paid CFO CFO or CFF
Dividends received CFO CFO or CFI
Dividends paid CFF CFF or CFO
Taxes paid CFO Generally CFO (portion can be CFI/CFF)
Bank overdrafts CFF Part of cash & equivalents
Top exam traps for Module 4:
(1) Depreciation is added back in indirect method — but it is NOT a source of cash. It merely means "this expense required no cash."
(2) Gain on sale is SUBTRACTED from CFO (indirect) — the actual cash proceeds appear in CFI. Don't double-count.
(3) Interest paid is ALWAYS CFO under US GAAP — even though the principal repaid is CFF. IFRS allows CFO or CFF.
(4) Dividends paid are ALWAYS CFF under US GAAP. IFRS allows CFF or CFO.
(5) Cash paid to suppliers: two-step process — COGS → Purchases (adjust for ∆Inventory), then Purchases → Cash (adjust for ∆A/P).
(6) A/P ↑ = ADD in indirect method (paid less cash than purchased). Inventory ↑ = SUBTRACT (used more cash to build stock).
(7) Ending cash on CFS must ALWAYS equal cash on ending balance sheet — if not, there's an error somewhere.
(8) Bank overdrafts: IFRS = cash equivalent; US GAAP = financing activity.
(9) DTL ↑ = ADD back in indirect method (taxes accrued but not yet paid — like a non-cash tax expense).
(10) Dividends paid backed into via retained earnings: Beg R/E + NI − End R/E = Dividends.
(11) Equipment sale proceeds = Book value + Gain. The gain was removed from CFO (indirect); the gross cash proceeds land in CFI.
(12) CFI and CFF always use the direct method — only CFO can use direct OR indirect.
