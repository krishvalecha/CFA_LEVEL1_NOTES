What Are Hedge Funds?

Hedge Funds
Private pooled investment vehicles that can invest in a wide variety of products (equities, fixed income, derivatives, FX, private capital, real assets). Distinguished by their investment approach rather than the underlying investments. Objective: generate high returns in an absolute sense or on a risk-adjusted basis relative to portfolio-level volatility.

7 Distinguishing Characteristics of Hedge Funds:

  1. Less legal and regulatory constraints — lightly regulated vs. mutual funds
  2. Flexible mandates — permitting use of shorting and derivatives
  3. Larger investment universe — equities, credit, fixed income, commodities, futures, FX, loans, real estate
  4. Aggressive styles — concentrated positions; exposure to credit, volatility, and liquidity risk premiums
  5. Liberal use of leverage — through short selling, borrowing, derivatives, or combining all three
  6. Liquidity constraints — lockups and liquidity gates; not suitable for daily-liquidity products
  7. High fee structures — management and incentive fees ("2 and 20" or variants)

Key clarifications about hedge funds:

  • NOT an asset class — a variety of investment vehicles driven by disparate strategies
  • "Hedge fund" is a misnomer — they are not restricted to equities or hedging strategies; many amplify risks rather than purely hedge them
  • The investment strategy splits a portfolio such that each component hedges the other → internally neutralizes market risk
  • Available only to institutional and accredited investors (not retail)
  • Lightly regulated vs. mutual funds (which are highly regulated as public offerings)
  • Different from PE funds: hedge funds have shorter time horizons and invest in more liquid asset classes
  • Many hedge funds use an absolute return standard (not benchmark-relative) due to difficulty in benchmarking
  • Attractive for low correlation with traditional asset classes → diversification benefit
  • Soft lockup: redemption fee (payable to the fund, not manager) creates a path to redeem during lockup period — expensive but available

04

Equity Hedge Fund Strategies

The original hedge fund category. Focus on public equity markets; take long AND short positions in equity and equity derivative securities. Most use a bottom-up (company → industry → market) security-specific approach. Some use top-down (global macro → sector → company).

Equity Strategy — Long/Short Equity

Fundamental Long/Short: Long undervalued stocks + short overvalued stocks, intending to reverse positions to earn alpha. Typically net long bias but adjustable. May use index shorts (for market risk reduction) or single-name shorts (for alpha).

Fundamental Growth: Long high-growth companies + short low/negative-growth companies. Net long biased → non-zero beta → not market neutral.

Fundamental Value: Long undervalued/unloved companies with turnaround potential + short growth-overestimated companies. Spread between value and growth expectations drives performance.

Equity Strategy — Short Biased

Primarily short overvalued equity securities with limited or no long exposures. Uses quantitative, technical, and fundamental analysis. Managers are often forensic — exposing accounting flaws or unrecognized business problems. Contrarian by nature. Difficult long-term performance record (30+ years of generally positive markets).

Useful during periods of market stress (short positions profit in market downturns).

Equity Strategy — Market Neutral

Long undervalued + short overvalued equities, targeting beta ≈ 0 (market-neutral net position). Seeks stable, single-digit returns independent of market direction.

Requires leverage to generate meaningful returns — amplifies returns but increases risk during unexpected volatility.

Profit from individual security movements (undervalued rising, overvalued falling) while avoiding overall market moves.

05

Event-Driven Strategies

Bottom-up strategies that seek to profit from defined events expected to change valuations — typically involving changes in corporate structure (acquisitions, restructurings). May use long and short positions in common/preferred stocks, debt, and options. Generally long biased. Timeline: merger arb = 6–24 months; bankruptcy = years.

Event — Merger Arbitrage

Long the target company (acquired company stock at discount to announced takeover price) + short the acquirer.

  • Profit when deal spread narrows to closing price after transaction is consummated
  • Spread exists due to: timing uncertainty, legal/regulatory hurdles, risk acquirer walks away
  • Shorting acquirer also expresses risk of merger overpayment
  • Primary risk: Deal fails to close → fund holdings negatively impacted before position can unwind
  • Leverage commonly used to amplify modest spread returns → magnifies losses when deal fails
  • Least long biased of the event-driven strategies
Event — Distressed / Restructuring

Focus on securities of companies in or near bankruptcy.

  • Approach 1: Buy fixed-income securities at a significant discount to par but senior enough to be backed by sufficient corporate assets → expect recovery to par (or significant premium) in bankruptcy reorganization/liquidation
  • Approach 2: Buy a fulcrum security (debt expected to convert into equity upon restructuring) → hold or exit the new equity post-restructuring
Event — Special Situations

Buy equity of companies engaged in: security issuance or repurchase, special capital distributions, rescue finance, asset sales/spin-offs, or other catalyst-oriented situations.

Event — Activist

Manager secures sufficient equity holdings to influence corporate policy or direction by seeking a board seat.

  • Advocates for: divestitures, restructuring, capital distributions to shareholders, management changes, strategy changes
  • Distinct from private equity: operates primarily in public equity markets
💡 Fulcrum Security — Key Term

A debt instrument expected to be converted into new equity upon bankruptcy restructuring. The "fulcrum" is the point in the capital structure where debt flips to equity. Investors buy it cheaply before restructuring, receive equity, then either hold or exit at a higher valuation.

06

Relative Value Strategies

Seek to profit from pricing discrepancies between related securities based on unusual short-term relationships, with the expectation that the discrepancy will resolve over time. Conceptually market-neutral approaches.

Relative Value — Convertible Bond Arbitrage

Exploit mispricing between a convertible bond and its component parts (underlying bond + embedded call option).

  • Typically: Long convertible bond + short the issuer's common stock (based on the delta of the embedded call option)
  • Sensitive to bankruptcy risk → can hedge using equity put options or credit default swaps (CDS)
  • Conceptually market-neutral
Relative Value — Fixed Income (General)

Focus on relative value within fixed-income markets: sovereign debt (relative value rates) and investment-grade corporate debt (relative value credit).

  • Long-short trades between: two different issuers, corporate vs. government, different parts of same issuer's capital structure, or different parts of yield curve
  • Drivers: spread dynamics, currency dynamics, yield curve shape
Relative Value — Fixed Income (ABS, MBS, High Yield)

Focus on relative value in higher-yielding securities: ABS, MBS, high-yield loans/bonds, and their derivatives. Seek attractive secured coupon returns and exploit relative security/quality mispricings.

Relative Value — Multi-Strategy

Trades relative value within and across asset classes. Instead of one type of trade or one asset class, looks for any available investment opportunity — often with different "pods" of managers executing unique approaches.

  • Goal: deploy (and redeploy) capital efficiently and quickly across strategy areas as conditions change
  • More flexible than single-strategy funds
07

Opportunistic & Multi-Manager Strategies

Opportunistic — Global Macro

Top-down approach to identify economic trends. Trade based on expected movements in economic variables.

  • Trade opportunistically in: fixed income, equity, currency, and commodity markets
  • Use long AND short positions based on overall market direction → driven by major economic trends and events
  • Benefit most from periods of higher volatility
  • Active moves by central banks to smooth economic shocks → shrinks the investment sphere for macro funds
Opportunistic — Managed Futures (CTAs — Commodity Trading Advisers)

Actively managed funds making diversified directional investments primarily in futures markets using technical and fundamental strategies.

  • Historically focused on commodity futures (hence "CTAs") but now include equities, fixed income, FX, and commodity futures
  • Use trend-following and momentum models over different time horizons
  • Diversification benefit: Useful during strong trending markets and during extended market stress when fundamental strategies underperform — CTAs profit from holding short positions in falling markets
  • Risk: Mean-reverting markets create false momentum signals → extended drawdown periods before strong trends emerge
  • As CTAs migrated to more financial products (stock/bond futures), reliability of diversification benefits has diminished
  • Commodity futures unique feature: constant price tension between suppliers and consumers (high prices → reduce demand; low prices → reduce supply) → balance absent in stocks/bonds
Multi-Manager — Fund of Funds

A managed portfolio of hedge funds. Also classified under multi-manager. See Section 8 for full details.

08

Investment Forms & Vehicles

Legal structure: Typically organized as private limited partnerships or LLCs. GP = hedge fund manager. LPs = investors. Available only to limited number of investors meeting income and net worth requirements. Fund documents: private placement memorandum, partnership agreement, or articles of incorporation.

Direct Forms

Master-Feeder Structure

Most common HF structure. An onshore feeder fund and an offshore feeder fund both feed into a single master fund that invests the capital.

Purpose: tax efficiency. The offshore and onshore vehicles allow different investor types (domestic vs. international/tax-exempt) to access the same underlying portfolio.

Separately Managed Account (SMA)

For large institutional investors. Investor creates their own investment vehicle; assets held and registered in the investor's name. Day-to-day management delegated to the HF manager.

Advantages: Customizable, better transparency, efficient capital allocation, higher liquidity, investor retains enhanced control, lower fees.

Disadvantages: Operationally complex, requires greater governance oversight. Managers may only allocate the most liquid trades to SMAs (lower motivation without a stake in results). Fee incentives help mitigate this.

Fund of One

A hedge fund created for a single investor. Similar to SMA but structured as a dedicated fund vehicle. The investor retains more influence than in a typical commingled fund. Requires additional agreements and service providers.

Indirect Forms

Fund of Hedge Funds (FoF)

Managed portfolio of hedge funds — pools investor capital across a diversified portfolio of hedge funds.

Advantages: Direct diversification (strategies, regions, management styles); lower investment minimums; reduced lockup periods; better exit liquidity; manager conducts HF due diligence; may access closed funds.

Disadvantages: Extra layer of fees (often additional 1% mgmt + 10% incentive on top of underlying HF fees); fee layering reduces investor returns; greater liquidity may → weaker performance (redemptions during market turmoil).

Hedge Fund Replication ETFs

Seek to replicate HF investment styles through indexing or active management using liquid, publicly traded assets. No direct HF investment.

vs. Actual HFs:

  • Returns often fall short (cannot use leverage to same level; public = regulatory burden)
  • Cannot impose redemption restrictions

Advantages vs. HFs: Greater liquidity, lower fees, increased transparency. Seek to match monthly hedge fund index returns.

💡 SMA vs. Fund of One vs. Standard Fund

Standard commingled fund: Manager has a stake → high motivation; manager invests across all strategies; less investor influence. SMA: Manager has no stake → potentially lower motivation; investor gets most liquid trades only; maximum transparency. Fund of One: Dedicated fund for single investor; more influence than commingled; more structure required.

Fee Structures — "2 and 20" and "1 or 30"

Standard "2 and 20" Fee Structure
Management fee = 2% of AUM (charged to the fund entity)
Performance fee = 20% of net profits (charged to GP)

Investor net return = Gross return − management fee − performance fee

"1 or 30" alternative (newer structure):
Manager receives the GREATER of:
(a) 1% management fee OR
(b) 30% incentive fee on alpha/outperformance vs. benchmark
→ whichever is greater
"2 and 20": 2% management + 20% performance. Traditional standard structure.
"1 or 30": More investor-friendly in good years (only one fee paid); better alignment in bad years (1% covers expenses).
Fund of funds ADDS: typically +1% management fee + 10% incentive fee on top of underlying HF fees → significant fee layering.
High-water mark: manager earns performance fee only when NAV exceeds prior highest value net of fees.
⚠️ Fee Layering in Fund of Funds

FoF investors pay fees at TWO levels: the underlying hedge fund level (e.g., 2%+20%) AND the FoF level (e.g., 1%+10%). The incentive fee at the FoF level is typically calculated on profits net of management fees at both levels. This significantly reduces the investor's net return compared to direct HF investment. Fee layering can result in an investor effectively paying fees more than once for management of the same assets.

Return Sources & Hedge Fund Index Biases

Three Sources of Hedge Fund Returns

Market Beta

Broad market exposure that can be replicated using market index-based funds or ETFs. Not unique to hedge funds.

Strategy Beta

Beta attributable to the hedge fund's specific investment strategy applied across the broad market. Reflects systematic factors specific to the strategy (e.g., merger risk premium, credit risk premium).

Alpha (Manager Skill)

Manager-specific returns from: identifying mispriced securities/sectors, market timing, operational control over business models, and leverage to amplify results. The primary focus of hedge funds.

Primary sources of alpha:

  • Manager skill in specific stock selection
  • Utilizing higher-return strategies that minimize risks
  • Exploiting market inefficiencies (which may be short-lived)
  • Systematic and idiosyncratic alternative risk factors not captured by traditional asset pricing models

Return drag from fees: High fees reduce the alpha generated. When underperforming funds close and liquidate positions, capital redeemed may receive disadvantageous prices → further diminishes total return.

Hedge Fund Index Biases

⚠️ HF Index Data Is Voluntary Self-Reporting → Performance Likely Overestimated

Reporting is voluntary → introduces multiple biases. Most HF indexes rely on information reported by HF managers themselves.

Bias TypeDescriptionDirection of Effect
Survivorship Bias Funds that stop reporting (due to poor performance or closure) are removed from the index. Underperformers have limited incentive to continue reporting. Overstates returns (only winners remain)
Backfill Bias (Instant History) When a successful fund starts reporting, its prior stellar performance is backfilled into the index. Only funds with favorable returns typically join indexes. Overstates returns (only historically good funds add history)
Selection Bias Funds allocated to strategy peer groups inconsistently (by prospectus, historical analysis, or combination). Indexes may have inconsistent data sources. Distorts performance comparisons
Equal Weighting Bias Most HF indexes are NOT AUM-weighted — each fund gets equal weight. Large funds (often better managed) have no higher weight than tiny funds. Skews comparison vs. size-weighted index
Reporting Lag HF data published with a ~4-week delay. Indexes are non-investable and illiquid. Performance replication is difficult
💡 Best HF Benchmark: Fund-of-Funds Composite

Risk and return comparisons are typically made to fund-of-funds composite indexes (e.g., HFRI Fund of Funds Composite Index) to minimize return distortions. This approach: (1) controls for self-reporting and selection bias, (2) ensures the benchmark is investable, (3) reflects actual performance of portfolios of hedge funds. Returns may be slightly lower (extra FoF fee layer) but represent a fairer, more conservative, and more accurate estimate of average hedge fund performance.

When Hedge Fund Strategies Work Best

Market-Neutral, Relative Value, Event-Driven — Best When:
  • During market downturns (outperform equity markets)
  • When individual stock correlations fall (more relative value opportunities)
Weaker When:
  • When correlations are high → fewer relative value opportunities
  • When equities move uniformly higher → short positions drag returns
