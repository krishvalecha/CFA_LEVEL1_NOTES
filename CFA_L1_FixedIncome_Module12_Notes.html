Why Convexity — The Limitation of Duration Alone

In Module 11, we used modified duration to estimate percentage price changes: %ΔPV ≈ −AnnModDur × ΔYield. This is a linear approximation — it treats the price–yield curve as a straight line (the tangent at the current price).

The problem: the true price–yield relationship is curved (convex), not straight. For small yield changes, the linear approximation is close enough. But for larger yield changes, or for bonds with high duration, the error can be significant. Convexity captures this curvature — the second-order effect — and corrects the duration estimate.

Duration Alone — First-Order Linear Effect
  • Assumes a straight-line price–yield relationship
  • Gives the same absolute % change for +/− yield moves
  • Underestimates price gain when yields fall
  • Overestimates price loss when yields rise
  • Error grows with: (1) size of yield change, (2) longer maturity, (3) lower coupon/yield
Duration + Convexity — Second-Order Correction
  • Accounts for the curvature of the price–yield relationship
  • Convexity adjustment is ALWAYS positive → improves both up and down estimates
  • When yields fall: estimated gain is HIGHER than duration alone
  • When yields rise: estimated loss is LOWER than duration alone
  • More accurate for large yield moves or high-duration bonds
💡 The Geometry — Why the Convexity Adjustment is Always Positive

The price–yield curve is convex (bows upward toward the investor). The tangent line (= duration estimate) lies BELOW the actual price curve everywhere except at the current point. So the actual price is always ABOVE the duration estimate — whether yields rise or fall. This means: actual price gain > duration estimate, and actual price loss < duration estimate. The convexity adjustment (always positive) corrects for this by adding a positive term, pulling both estimates up toward the actual curve.

What Convexity Measures — The Second Derivative of Price

Convexity (AnnConvexity)
A measure of the second-order (non-linear) effect of yield changes on a bond's full price. Mathematically, it is the second derivative of the bond price with respect to yield, divided by price (d²PV/dr² / PV). It captures the curvature of the price–yield relationship — the rate at which duration (the slope) itself changes as yield changes. For option-free fixed-rate bonds, convexity is always positive.

First derivative of price w.r.t. yield = Duration (slope of price–yield curve at a given point)

Second derivative of price w.r.t. yield = Convexity (rate of change of slope = curvature)

Just as modified duration uses the first derivative, convexity uses the second derivative. Together, they provide a second-order Taylor expansion approximation of the bond's price change — far more accurate than the first-order (duration-only) estimate.

Why is convexity always positive for option-free bonds? Because the price–yield curve always bows upward (toward the investor) for such bonds. Duration decreases as yield rises and increases as yield falls — the curvature is always in the investor's favor. This is not true for callable bonds, which have negative convexity at low yields (the call option limits price appreciation).

Tabular Convexity Calculation — The 7-Column Method

The tabular method extends the 6-column MacDur table by adding a seventh column (Col. 7) that computes the convexity contribution of each cash flow. The sum of Col. 7, divided by the number of periods per year squared (m²), gives AnnConvexity.

Column 7 Formula — Convexity of Each Cash Flow
Col. 7 = t × (t + 1) × w_t × (1 + r_period)^(−2)

WHERE:
t = time to receipt of cash flow (in periods, from Col. 2)
t + 1 = one more than the time to receipt (introduces the second-order term)
w_t = PV weight of the cash flow (Col. 5 = PV_t / Full price)
r_period = yield-to-maturity per period (e.g., 3.2%/2 = 1.6% for semiannual)
(1+r)^−2 = discounting factor specific to convexity calculation

AnnConvexity = Σ(Col. 7) / m²

WHERE m = number of coupon periods per year (m=2 for semiannual)
So AnnConvexity = Σ(Col. 7) / 4 for semiannual bonds
Why t × (t+1)? The second derivative of a discounted cash flow with respect to yield introduces t and (t+1) as factors — it's the mathematical consequence of differentiating twice. This is where the "non-linearity" (squared time) enters, which is what makes convexity a second-order measure.

Key difference from duration: Duration uses t × w_t (Col. 6). Convexity uses t × (t+1) × w_t × (1+r)^−2 (Col. 7). The (1+r)^−2 factor adjusts for the periodicity of cash flows in the convexity calculation.

Annualizing: Σ(Col. 7) is in periodic units² (e.g., semiannual periods squared). Dividing by m² converts to annual years² — the standard annualized convexity quoted in the market.

This is why convexity is even more sensitive to maturity and the final large payment than duration is. A small extension in maturity increases convexity proportionally more than it increases duration.

Approximate Convexity — The Two-Price Method (Equation 2)

Just as approximate modified duration uses PV+, PV−, and PV₀ to estimate the slope numerically, approximate convexity uses the same three prices to estimate the curvature numerically. This approach is particularly useful for bonds with uncertain or changing cash flows (callable bonds, default-risky bonds) where the tabular method cannot be directly applied.

Equation 2 — Approximate Annualized Convexity
ApproxCon = (PV− + PV+ − 2×PV₀) / ((ΔYield)² × PV₀)

WHERE:
PV₀ = current full price at current YTM
PV+ = full price when YTM is increased by ΔYield
PV− = full price when YTM is decreased by ΔYield
ΔYield = yield change used (e.g., 0.0005 for 5bps, 0.0001 for 1bp)

Note: This uses the SAME three prices as approximate ModDur (Eq. 4 of Module 11).
Same prices, different formula → both duration AND convexity from one set of calculations.
Mathematical intuition: The numerator (PV− + PV+ − 2×PV₀) captures the degree to which the two off-center prices deviate from the center price. If price–yield were perfectly linear, PV− + PV+ would exactly equal 2×PV₀ → numerator = 0 → zero convexity. Any positive deviation reveals curvature (convexity). The (ΔYield)² in the denominator normalizes for the size of the yield perturbation.

Why use PV− + PV+? For ModDur, we needed PV− − PV+ (to get the slope direction). For convexity, we need to add them (to capture how both deviate from the center — the curvature is symmetric, so adding removes the linear/directional component and isolates the second-order effect).

ΔYield choice: Must be small enough to be meaningful but large enough to avoid floating-point errors. Typically 1bp to 10bps. Results should be nearly identical regardless of the specific ΔYield chosen within this range.

BRWA Bond — Approximate Convexity Worked Example

BRWA 5yr, 3.2% semi, at par. ΔYield = 5bps = 0.0005. Using Excel PRICE function.
PV₀ = PRICE(DATE(2025,10,15), DATE(2030,10,15), 0.032, 0.0320, 100, 2, 0) = 100.000000
PV+ = PRICE(DATE(2025,10,15), DATE(2030,10,15), 0.032, 0.0325, 100, 2, 0) = 99.770965
PV− = PRICE(DATE(2025,10,15), DATE(2030,10,15), 0.032, 0.0315, 100, 2, 0) = 100.229641

ApproxCon = (PV− + PV+ − 2×PV₀) / ((ΔYield)² × PV₀)
= (100.229641 + 99.770965 − 2×100.000000) / ((0.0005)² × 100.000000)
= (200.000606 − 200.000000) / (0.00000025 × 100)
= 0.000606 / 0.000025
= 24.23896

Compare to exact tabular result: 24.23895 ✓ (difference of 0.00001 — essentially identical)

Full Price Change Formula — Duration + Convexity Adjustment (Equation 1)

Equation 1 — Full Percentage Price Change with Convexity Adjustment
%ΔPV^Full ≈ (−AnnModDur × ΔYield) + [½ × AnnConvexity × (ΔYield)²]

WHERE:
First term: −AnnModDur × ΔYield = duration effect (linear, 1st order)
Second term: ½ × AnnConvexity × (ΔYield)² = convexity adjustment (2nd order)

The convexity adjustment:
• Is always POSITIVE (for option-free bonds) because (ΔYield)² > 0 always
• Grows with the SQUARE of the yield change → matters most for large moves
• Is the same whether yield rises or falls (ΔYield² is the same for ±ΔYield)

Net effect:
Yield RISES (+ΔYield): Duration term is negative; convexity adds back positive → loss is smaller
Yield FALLS (−ΔYield): Duration term is positive; convexity adds more positive → gain is larger
The ½ comes from the Taylor expansion: f(x+h) ≈ f(x) + f'(x)h + ½f''(x)h². ModDur is the first-order term (f'), convexity is the second-order term (f''). The ½ is a mathematical constant from calculus, not an arbitrary scaling factor.

When does convexity matter most? (1) Large yield changes (the (ΔYield)² grows fast — doubling the yield change quadruples the convexity adjustment). (2) Longer-maturity bonds (higher convexity → larger adjustment). (3) Lower-coupon or lower-yield bonds (same reason).

⚠️ Duration-Only Symmetry vs. Duration+Convexity Asymmetry

Duration alone gives the same estimate (±4.587%) for both +100bps and −100bps yield changes — symmetric. Adding convexity breaks this symmetry: the gain estimate (4.708%) exceeds the loss estimate (4.466%) in absolute value. This asymmetry is real and correct — it reflects the curvature of the price–yield relationship. A higher yield rise causes a smaller-than-linear price loss; a yield decline causes a larger-than-linear price gain. This is the mathematical expression of why convexity benefits investors.

Money Convexity — Convexity in Currency Units (Equations 3 & 4)

Equation 3 — Money Convexity
MoneyCon = AnnConvexity × PV^Full

WHERE:
AnnConvexity = annualized convexity statistic
PV^Full = full price (market value) of the bond or position

PARALLEL to MoneyDur = AnnModDur × PV^Full (Module 11)
MoneyCon scales AnnConvexity by the position's market value
→ Gives the second-order price change in currency units
Equation 4 — Currency Price Change Using MoneyDur + MoneyCon
ΔPV^Full ≈ −(MoneyDur × ΔYield) + [½ × MoneyCon × (ΔYield)²]

PARALLEL to Equation 1 (percentage), but in CURRENCY UNITS:
First term: −MoneyDur × ΔYield = currency duration effect
Second term: ½ × MoneyCon × (ΔYield)² = currency convexity adjustment

Both terms in the same currency as the bond position's market value.
Relationship between percentage and currency formulas:
Equation 1 (%) × PV^Full = Equation 4 (currency)
(AnnModDur × ΔYield) × PV^Full = MoneyDur × ΔYield
(½ × AnnConvexity × ΔYield²) × PV^Full = ½ × MoneyCon × ΔYield²
All the percentage changes, when multiplied by the portfolio value, become currency changes.

Convexity Properties — What Increases or Decreases Convexity

★ Same Relationships as Duration — Plus One Extra Factor

Convexity has the same directional relationships with bond features as duration. The only additional factor unique to convexity is cash flow dispersion.

Feature (increase in...)Effect on ConvexityExplanation
Coupon rate ↑ ↓ Decreases Higher coupons → more weight on early cash flows → less "spread" of payments → lower curvature. Same logic as duration: more early CF weight → less sensitivity at the far end where curvature builds.
YTM ↑ ↓ Decreases Higher yield → greater discounting of far-off cash flows → less weight on distant payments → lower weighted-average time² → lower convexity. Also: bond price is lower, so the percentage curvature is reduced.
Time-to-maturity ↑ ↑ Increases Longer maturity → large final payment is further away → t×(t+1) for the final period is much larger → dominates the Col. 7 sum → much higher convexity. Convexity scales approximately with maturity² for zero-coupon bonds.
Cash flow dispersion ↑ (same duration) ↑ Increases Unique to convexity. For two bonds with the same duration, the one whose cash flows are more spread out over time (higher dispersion) has greater convexity. A barbell portfolio (bonds at very short and very long maturities) has higher convexity than a bullet portfolio (bonds concentrated at one maturity), even if durations are matched.
💡 The Dispersion Insight — Barbell vs. Bullet Portfolios

This is a frequently tested concept. Two portfolios with equal modified duration can have different convexity if their cash flows are distributed differently over time. A barbell (concentrating bonds at very short and very long maturities) has more convexity than a bullet (concentrating around one specific maturity), for the same duration. Portfolio managers seeking to maximize convexity (and the associated price protection) prefer barbells — but the extra convexity is typically priced in (lower yield), so there's a cost-benefit tradeoff.

Convexity Is Valuable to Investors — And Has a Price

📈 When Yields Rise
  • More convex bond: price falls LESS than less convex bond
  • Duration effect (price loss) partially offset by curvature
  • More convex bond outperforms for the same yield increase
  • Example: +100bps → −4.466% (with convexity) vs. −4.587% (duration only)
📉 When Yields Fall
  • More convex bond: price rises MORE than less convex bond
  • Duration effect (price gain) amplified by curvature
  • More convex bond outperforms for the same yield decrease
  • Example: −100bps → +4.708% (with convexity) vs. +4.587% (duration only)
★ Key Insight — More Convex Bond Outperforms in ALL Scenarios

For two bonds with the same duration (same tangent line), the more convex bond always outperforms in both rising and falling rate environments — by falling less when yields rise and rising more when yields fall. This assumes both bonds are priced at the same YTM (same tangent line), so convexity is the only differentiator. In practice, the more convex bond is priced to reflect this advantage — it trades at a higher price (lower yield). Investors pay for convexity. If convexity is not reflected in the price, it represents a free risk-reduction benefit — but arbitrage quickly eliminates such mispricings.

⚠️ Convexity Advantage Requires Significant Yield Moves to Matter

For tiny yield changes (1–5bps), the convexity adjustment (½ × C × ΔY²) is negligibly small and doesn't meaningfully improve the duration estimate. Convexity only becomes important for larger moves — typically 25bps or more, and increasingly so for moves of 50bps, 100bps, or beyond. For the short 5yr BRWA bond, the improvement at ±100bps is 12bps. For the 30yr Romanian bond with convexity of 370, the improvement would be enormous. Always check whether the yield change is large enough to make convexity adjustment worthwhile.

Portfolio Duration & Convexity — Two Methods, One Preferred

Duration and convexity can be calculated for a portfolio of bonds, not just individual bonds. This allows portfolio managers to measure and manage the interest rate risk of their entire bond portfolio with a single set of numbers.

Method 1: Aggregate Cash Flow Timing
  • Treat the portfolio as a single bond with all cash flows combined
  • Compute the weighted-average time to receipt of all aggregate cash flows
  • Theoretically correct — uses actual portfolio cash flows
  • Impractical — requires knowing every cash flow from every bond at every future date; computationally intensive for large portfolios
  • Rarely used in practice
Method 2: Weighted Average of Individual Bonds
  • Compute each bond's duration and convexity separately
  • Weight by each bond's market value as a share of total portfolio market value
  • Portfolio Duration = Σ (w_i × ModDur_i)
  • Portfolio Convexity = Σ (w_i × Convexity_i)
  • Practically preferred — simple, intuitive, widely used
  • Limitation: implicitly assumes parallel yield curve shifts
Portfolio Duration and Convexity — Weighted-Average Method
Portfolio ModDur = Σ_i (w_i × ModDur_i)
Portfolio Convexity = Σ_i (w_i × Convexity_i)

WHERE:
w_i = market value of bond i / total portfolio market value
Σ w_i = 1 (weights sum to 1)

Portfolio %ΔPV ≈ (−Portfolio ModDur × ΔYield) + [½ × Portfolio Con × (ΔYield)²]
Weights are market-value weights, not par-value weights. A bond trading at a premium has a higher market value per $100 par than a bond trading at a discount — this difference matters when computing weights.

The approximation becomes more accurate when: (1) Yield differences between bonds in the portfolio are smaller, and (2) the yield curve is approximately flat. If bonds in the portfolio have very different YTMs, the weighted-average duration is a less accurate representation of actual price sensitivity.

Worked Example — BRWA + Romanian Bond Portfolio (Exhibit 6)

Portfolio: $50M par each of BRWA (5yr, 3.2%) and Romanian (30yr, 4.625%). Both USD-denominated.
BOND DETAILS:
BRWA: Price=100.000, YTM=3.20%, ModDur=4.58676, Convexity=24.23896
Romanian: Price=98.022, YTM=4.75%, ModDur=15.90637, Convexity=369.64203

MARKET VALUES:
BRWA market value: $50M × (100.000/100) = $50,000,000
Romanian market value: $50M × (98.022/100) = $49,011,224
Total portfolio value: $99,011,224

PORTFOLIO WEIGHTS (market value):
w_BRWA = $50,000,000 / $99,011,224 = 0.5050 (50.50%)
w_Romanian = $49,011,224 / $99,011,224 = 0.4950 (49.50%)

PORTFOLIO MODIFIED DURATION:
= (4.58676 × 0.5050) + (15.90637 × 0.4950)
= 2.31631 + 7.87365 = 10.19004

PORTFOLIO CONVEXITY:
= (24.23896 × 0.5050) + (369.64203 × 0.4950)
= 12.24067 + 182.97281 = 195.21348

ESTIMATED PORTFOLIO %ΔPV if ALL yields rise +100bps:
%ΔPV ≈ (−10.19004 × 0.0100) + [½ × 195.21348 × (0.0100)²]
= −10.19004% + 0.97607%
= −9.21397%

The portfolio loses approximately 9.21% if all yields rise by 100bps.

Limitations of Portfolio Duration — The Parallel Shift Assumption

⚠️ Critical Limitation — Parallel Yield Curve Shift Assumption

The weighted-average portfolio duration and convexity approach implicitly assumes that ALL yields in the portfolio change by the SAME amount in the SAME direction simultaneously — a "parallel shift" in the yield curve. In reality:

  • Parallel shifts are rare. The yield curve usually changes non-uniformly — short rates may rise while long rates fall (flattening), or long rates may rise more than short rates (steepening), or different parts may twist in different directions.
  • The BRWA + Romanian portfolio is particularly exposed to this. The two bonds have very different maturities (5yr vs. 30yr) — their yields may change by different amounts. If only long rates rise (30yr Romanian yield rises but 5yr BRWA yield stays unchanged), the portfolio duration estimate would be inaccurate.
  • The approximation is better when: bonds in the portfolio have similar maturities and similar yields, and the yield curve shift is approximately parallel.
  • Curve duration measures (effective duration, key rate duration) address this by measuring sensitivity to specific parts of the yield curve — covered in later modules.
  • The first method (aggregate cash flows) is theoretically superior because it doesn't require a parallel shift assumption — it works with the actual aggregate cash flows. But its impracticality for large portfolios makes the weighted-average method the standard in practice.
💡 When Does the Parallel Shift Assumption Matter Most?

It matters most for portfolios that are spread across the yield curve — long/short combinations, barbells, or portfolios mixing investment-grade and high-yield bonds (which have very different yield sensitivities). It matters less for portfolios concentrated at a single maturity (bullet portfolios) or portfolios where all bonds have similar yields and maturities. For the exam: always acknowledge the parallel shift assumption as the key limitation when discussing portfolio duration or convexity.

  • ✅ Convexity reduces estimation error dramatically for large yield moves. BRWA bond: at ±100bps, duration-only error ≈12bps; with convexity adjustment: <¼bp error. For the Romanian 30yr bond with convexity ~370, the improvement would be enormous.
  • ✅ More convex bond outperforms in ALL rate environments (same duration): gains more when yields fall, loses less when yields rise. This is why convexity is valuable to investors. If the higher convexity is not priced in, it's a free benefit — but markets usually price it in (lower yield for more convex bond).
  • ✅ Convexity properties — same direction as duration: Maturity ↑ → convexity ↑. Coupon ↓ → convexity ↑. YTM ↓ → convexity ↑. All three same direction as duration effects. PLUS: cash flow dispersion ↑ → convexity ↑ (unique to convexity — barbell vs. bullet).
  • ✅ Cash flow dispersion: Two bonds with the same duration but different cash flow spread → the one with more spread has higher convexity. Barbell portfolio > bullet portfolio convexity, for equal durations. This is why portfolio managers prefer barbells for convexity optimization.
  • ✅ Maturity amplifies convexity more than duration. In the Romanian vs. BRWA example: 6× longer maturity → 3.5× higher duration BUT 15× higher convexity. Because convexity uses t×(t+1) (approximately t²), it scales with maturity approximately quadratically for zero-coupon bonds.
  • ✅ Portfolio duration (Eq. Method 2): Portfolio ModDur = Σ (w_i × ModDur_i). Portfolio Convexity = Σ (w_i × Convexity_i). Weights = market value of bond i / total portfolio market value (NOT par value).
  • ✅ Portfolio price change estimate: Use portfolio ModDur and portfolio convexity in Eq. 1. BRWA + Romanian: portfolio ModDur = 10.190; portfolio convexity = 195.21. At +100bps: %ΔPV ≈ −10.19% + ½×195.21×0.01² = −9.214%.
  • ✅ Key limitation of weighted-average method: Assumes PARALLEL yield curve shifts (all yields change by same amount simultaneously). In reality: yield curves steepen, flatten, or twist. The approximation is most accurate when bonds have similar maturities/yields and the shift is approximately parallel.
  • ✅ Method 1 (aggregate CFs) is theoretically superior but impractical for large portfolios. Method 2 (weighted average of individual bonds) is used in practice despite its limitations.

The sign logic for Equation 1: Duration term: −ModDur × (+ΔYield) = negative (price falls when yield rises). Convexity term: +½ × C × (ΔYield)² = always positive (always adds back). For yield falls: −ModDur × (−ΔYield) = positive; convexity term still positive → both terms add → gain exceeds pure duration estimate. For yield rises: duration term is negative; convexity term is positive → partially offsets → loss is less than pure duration estimate.

Remember: both ApproxModDur and ApproxCon use the same PV₀, PV+, PV− inputs. ModDur uses (PV− − PV+) in the numerator. Convexity uses (PV− + PV+ − 2×PV₀) in the numerator. One subtraction, one addition — but same three prices.
