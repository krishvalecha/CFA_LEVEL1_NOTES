The Financial Analysis Framework

Financial analysis converts raw data into metrics that answer: How has the company performed? (past) — How will it perform? (future) — What is it worth? (valuation). A structured framework ensures analysis is purposeful rather than just mechanical computation. Phases 3 and 4 — processing and analyzing data — are the primary focus of this module.

The 6-Phase Framework

PhaseActivityKey Output
① Articulate PurposeDefine what questions the analysis will answer; understand client/institutional needsClear objective statement; report scope
② Collect DataGather financial statements, industry/macro data; site visits; management discussionsOrganized data tables; completed questionnaires
③ Process Data ★Adjust financial statements; build common-size statements; compute ratios; construct forecastsAdjusted FS; ratios; graphs; forecasts
④ Analyze/Interpret ★Evaluate processed data — not just WHAT happened, but WHY and whether it created valueAnalytical results and conclusions
⑤ CommunicateDevelop conclusions and recommendations; write report with context, governance, strategyAnalytical report; investment/credit recommendation
⑥ Follow-UpPeriodically revisit as new data arrive to determine if recommendations need revisionUpdated reports and recommendations
★ Core Principle — Computation vs. Analysis

The ratio is NOT "the answer." It tells you what happened, not why. A good analysis explains the cause of the ratio and whether the underlying driver is likely to persist. Always ask: does this result make business sense given the company's strategy and industry environment?

Examiner Focus

The exam tests your ability to compute ratios correctly AND interpret what they imply about a company's performance — not just recite formulas. Context is everything: a "good" ratio in one industry may be a red flag in another. The analysis framework questions typically appear in item sets where you must identify the correct phase or purpose.

02

Analytical Tools & Techniques

Evaluations require comparisons. A number is only meaningful relative to: the same company over time (trend analysis) or against other companies (cross-sectional analysis). Ratios and common-size statements remove size as a variable, enabling apples-to-apples comparisons regardless of company scale or reporting currency.

Ratio Analysis
  • Expresses one quantity in relation to another — controls for size
  • Validated by academic research (Altman Z-score for credit failure; Ou & Penman for stock returns)
  • No universal standard list — vendor formulas differ; always verify source
  • Ratios are indicators — they identify WHAT, not WHY
  • Use the same source/formula when comparing companies or over time
Common-Size Statements
  • Express every line item as % of a base — removes absolute size
  • Income statement base: revenue
  • Balance sheet base: total assets
  • Vertical: composition at a point in time
  • Horizontal: changes from base year or prior period
  • Combine both for full picture: what is the composition AND how is it changing?
Graphs
  • Pie chart: Composition at 1–2 points in time
  • Line chart: Change in a few items over longer time periods
  • Stacked column: Both composition AND change over time (most informative)
Regression Analysis

Identifies statistical correlations between variables. Examples: sales vs. GDP (cyclicality), sales vs. inventory (efficiency). Provides a quantitative basis for forecasting and highlights items deviating from historical norms.

Comparability Adjustments
  • Different currencies → translate at avg/period-end FX, or compare ratios directly
  • Different fiscal year ends → use trailing 12 months (TTM)
  • Different accounting methods → adjust FS before computing ratios
  • Interim vs. annual → annualize (quarterly turnover × 4)
⚠ Key Limitations of Ratio Analysis

1. Multi-segment companies: Aggregate ratios are distorted if divisions operate in very different industries — limits comparability with pure-play peers.

2. Need for consistency: One category of ratios may signal a problem while another suggests it is temporary. The whole picture matters.

3. Judgment-intensive: Ratios alone cannot value a company or determine creditworthiness — external context (industry, economy, strategy) is essential.

4. Accounting differences: FIFO vs. LIFO, straight-line vs. accelerated depreciation, operating vs. finance lease treatment — all distort cross-company comparisons. Adjustments may be required.

Average vs. Period-End Denominators — The Rule

When IS or CFO numerator + BS denominator: use AVERAGE of beginning and ending BS value. The IS covers a whole period; the BS is a single-date snapshot — averaging aligns the timing.
When both numerator and denominator are BS items: period-end is usually fine (same date).
Exception: In DuPont decomposition — if you use averages in one component ratio, use them in all component ratios for internal consistency.

03

Common-Size Analysis

Balance Sheet — Vertical

Each item ÷ total assets. Shows composition of the BS. Key questions: What mix of assets? How is the company financed? How does composition compare to peers?

Receivables jumping from 35% to 57% of assets: investigate — could be more credit sales, lower credit standards, aggressive revenue recognition, or a change in another asset category. Context determines the conclusion.

Balance Sheet — Horizontal

Each item ÷ same item in base year (or % change from prior period). Shows how items are changing over time. Two formats:

  • Index to base year: Period 1 = 1.00; subsequent years show relative size (0.74 = 26% decline from base)
  • % change vs. prior period: Year-over-year momentum; highlights recent acceleration or deceleration
Income Statement — Vertical

Each item ÷ revenue. Creates the common-size IS showing margin structure. Decompose revenue by segment if multiple sources exist — shifts in revenue mix affect cost structure and margins.

If salaries jump from 15% to 25% of revenue while EBITDA falls from 53% to 45%: Is the new revenue mix requiring more expensive staff? Was it worth the shift?

Cross-Statement Trend Comparisons
  • Revenue growth vs. AR growth vs. Inventory growth — mismatches are red flags
  • NI growing faster than Revenue → rising margins (but confirm it's from operations, not one-timers)
  • Revenue growing but CFO declining → earnings quality concern (aggressive accruals?)
  • Assets growing faster than Revenue → declining efficiency; watch for future impairments
Examiner Focus — Sunbeam Red Flag

Classic exam example: Sunbeam 1997 — Revenue +19%, Inventory +58%, Receivables +38%. Inventory and receivables should ideally grow slower than (or in line with) revenue. When they grow faster, it signals either operational inefficiency (obsolete inventory, slow collections) or aggressive accounting (premature revenue, channel stuffing). The SEC charged Sunbeam with both. This pattern is a standard exam trigger for "what does this suggest?"

Cross-Sectional & Trend Analysis

Cross-Sectional Analysis

Compares a company's metrics to peer companies or industry norms at the same point in time. Removes the absolute size problem. Enables relative ranking and benchmarking.

  • Check: does an outlier ratio reflect superior management or accounting manipulation?
  • Beware: multi-segment companies distort industry comparisons
  • Accounting differences distort cross-sectional comparisons — adjust first
Trend / Time-Series Analysis

Tracks a company's own ratios over time to identify improvement or deterioration. Often more insightful than a single-period snapshot.

  • Past trends are reliable guides mainly in stable macro/competitive environments
  • Even in volatile environments, history provides a baseline for setting expectations
  • Consistent ratios over time ≠ necessarily good — could reflect earnings smoothing via accounting policies

5 Contexts for Interpreting Ratios

#ContextWhy It Matters
① Prior PeriodCompany's own historical ratiosIs performance strengthening or deteriorating over time?
② ExpectationsAnalyst/management forecastsSurprises vs. expectations move stock prices; examine misses carefully
③ Industry PeersCross-sectional comparison to competitorsRelative positioning; beware of multi-segment distortion and accounting differences
④ Company GoalsStated strategy and internal targetsIs the strategy being executed? Are internal targets being met?
⑤ Economic CycleCurrent phase of the business cycleCyclical companies' ratios improve in expansions, weaken in recessions — adjust interpretation accordingly

Activity Ratios

Activity ratios (also called asset utilization or operating efficiency ratios) measure how effectively a company converts its assets into revenue and manages working capital. Higher turnover = better efficiency. All combine an IS/COGS numerator with an average balance sheet denominator.

Activity Ratios — Complete Formula Set
Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory
Days of Inventory (DOH) = 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover

Receivables Turnover = Revenue ÷ Average Receivables
Days of Sales (DSO) = 365 ÷ Receivables Turnover

Payables Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Trade Payables
Days of Payables = 365 ÷ Payables Turnover

Working Capital Turnover = Revenue ÷ Average Working Capital
Fixed Asset Turnover = Revenue ÷ Average Net Fixed Assets
Total Asset Turnover = Revenue ÷ Average Total Assets
Annualizing quarterly data: Quarterly IT = 3.18× per quarter. Annual equivalent = 3.18 × 4 = 12.72×/year. DOH (quarterly) = 90 ÷ 3.18 = 28.3 days (same as 365 ÷ 12.90 after annualizing — consistent either way).
Ideal numerator for receivables turnover: credit sales — but this is usually not disclosed, so analysts use total revenue. This slightly understates turnover for companies with large cash sales.
ACTIVITYInventory Turnover & DOH
IT = COGS ÷ Avg Inventory  |  DOH = 365 ÷ IT
High turnover / Low DOH: Efficient inventory management. But could also mean inadequate inventory → stockouts → lost sales. Cross-check: if revenue growth ≥ industry → efficiency. If revenue growth is lagging → stockout risk.
Low turnover / High DOH: Slow-moving inventory. Possible obsolescence, fashion risk, or demand decline. Look for rising inventory write-downs or falling gross margins as confirmation.
Critical trap: If DOH falls because of an inventory write-down (allowance for obsolescence), that is a valuation adjustment — NOT improved inventory management. Distinguish between the two.
ACTIVITYReceivables Turnover & DSO
RT = Revenue ÷ Avg Receivables  |  DSO = 365 ÷ RT
High DSO / Low turnover: Customers are taking longer to pay. May indicate relaxed credit standards, weak collection, or premature revenue recognition. Check receivables aging schedule — rising % of AR in 60+ day buckets is a red flag.
Low DSO / High turnover: Fast collections. But check if strict credit is restricting sales (compare revenue growth to industry).
Warning: If impairment provision is declining while receivables aging is deteriorating — company may be under-reserving to inflate earnings. Lenovo example: AR up 11% but sales up only 5.4%; 60+ day balances increasing; provision declining as % of AR — suggests extending customer credit to drive sales, potentially under-provisioning.
ACTIVITYPayables Turnover & Days of Payables
PT = COGS ÷ Avg Trade Payables  |  Days Payable = 365 ÷ PT
High days payable: Taking longer to pay suppliers. Could be: exploiting favorable credit terms (positive if liquidity is strong) OR struggling to pay (negative if liquidity is tight). Cross-check with liquidity ratios to distinguish.
Low days payable: Paying quickly — may be capturing early-payment discounts, or not fully utilizing available credit (opportunity cost).
Apple example: 112 days payable despite $70B+ cash — clearly a strategic use of supplier financing, not a distress signal. Never interpret payables in isolation.
ACTIVITYFixed Asset & Total Asset Turnover
Fixed Asset TO = Revenue ÷ Avg Net Fixed Assets  |  Total Asset TO = Revenue ÷ Avg Total Assets
Fixed asset turnover — age effect: Companies with newer assets (less depreciated → higher carrying value) show lower FA turnover than peers with older (more depreciated → lower carrying value) assets. This is a carrying-value artifact, not a true efficiency difference. Also erratic because revenue grows smoothly but capex is lumpy.
Total asset turnover: Low ratio can reflect genuine inefficiency OR capital-intensive industry OR new business not yet at full capacity. Also reflects management's strategic choice of capital-intensity vs. labour-intensity.
Declining total asset turnover over time: May signal future impairments, especially goodwill in acquisitive companies (overpaid for assets that aren't generating sufficient revenue).

Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios measure a company's ability to meet short-term obligations. Unlike activity ratios (which use averages), liquidity ratios use period-end balance sheet values — they are a point-in-time snapshot of the current liquidity position. Adequate liquidity varies by industry and company size.

Liquidity Ratios — Complete Formula Set
Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
Quick Ratio = (Cash + ST Marketable Investments + Receivables) ÷ Current Liabilities
Cash Ratio = (Cash + ST Marketable Investments) ÷ Current Liabilities
Defensive Interval Ratio = (Cash + ST Investments + Receivables) ÷ Daily Cash Expenditures

Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) = DOH + DSO – Days of Payables
[also called Net Operating Cycle]
Daily cash expenditures = (COGS + SG&A + R&D − D&A) ÷ 365. Exclude depreciation/amortization (non-cash). Taxes usually excluded.
Current ratio assumes inventory and receivables are liquid. If turnover ratios are low, this assumption fails — quick ratio is more reliable in that case.

Interpretation

RatioExcludes vs. CurrentHigher =Key Nuance
Current RatioIncludes everythingMore liquidOverstates liquidity if inventory is illiquid (low IT) or receivables are slow (high DSO)
Quick RatioExcludes inventory and prepaid expensesMore liquidBetter measure when inventory is slow-moving. Prepaid expenses can't be converted back to cash.
Cash RatioOnly cash and ST investmentsMost conservativeCrisis measure. In market crashes, even marketable securities can fall in value.
Defensive Interval—More days = safer"Burn rate" for established companies. Low vs. peers → investigate expected cash inflows.
CCC—Shorter/negative is betterNegative = company funded by suppliers. But verify context — distress vs. strategic leverage.
⚠ Trap: Negative CCC Is Not Always Positive

National Datacomputer had CCC of –275.5 days (2008) — not Apple-style supplier leverage, but because inventory had vanished (no cash to buy more) and payables were stretched to 295 days (couldn't pay). The company filed for bankruptcy. Key rule: always verify WHY the CCC is negative — strategic leverage (Apple) vs. financial distress (National Datacomputer).

Apple CCC — Negative = Strength, Not Distress

Apple FY2017: DSO 27 + DOH 9 − Days Payable 112 = CCC of −76 days. Apple collects from customers ~76 days before paying suppliers — effectively funded by its supply chain. With $70B+ cash, Apple has zero distress risk. The power to demand 112-day supplier terms reflects extraordinary bargaining leverage.

Examiner Focus — Contradictory Current vs. Quick Ratios

Classic exam trap: Current ratio rising (1.6 → 2.1) but quick ratio falling (1.0 → 0.8). Both have the same denominator (current liabilities) → the difference must be in an asset that's in current ratio but NOT quick ratio. That item is inventory. Confirm with rising DOH (30 → 55 days). Conclusion: liquidity is declining despite the improving current ratio — inventory is building up and it's the current ratio making the company look better than it is.

Solvency Ratios

Solvency ratios measure a company's ability to meet long-term obligations — the amount of debt in the capital structure (debt ratios) and the capacity to service that debt from earnings (coverage ratios). Solvency is fundamental to credit analysis and bond valuation.

Solvency Ratios — Complete Formula Set
═══ DEBT RATIOS (Balance Sheet) ═══
Debt-to-Assets = Total Debt ÷ Total Assets
Debt-to-Capital = Total Debt ÷ (Total Debt + Total Equity)
Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt ÷ Total Shareholders' Equity
Financial Leverage Ratio = Avg Total Assets ÷ Avg Total Equity
Debt-to-EBITDA = Total Debt (or Net Debt) ÷ EBITDA

═══ COVERAGE RATIOS (Income Statement) ═══
Interest Coverage = EBIT ÷ Interest Payments
Fixed Charge Coverage = (EBIT + Lease Payments) ÷ (Interest Payments + Lease Payments)
Total Debt (this module): Interest-bearing short-term + long-term debt only. Excludes AP, accrued expenses, and operating leases (unless stated).
Net Debt = Total debt − Cash − ST marketable investments. Assumption: cash could immediately repay debt. Can result in "net cash" if cash exceeds debt.
D/Capital vs. D/Equity: D/E of 1.0 = D/Capital of 50% (debt = equity in value). Simple conversion: D/Capital = D/E ÷ (1 + D/E).
Debt-to-EBITDA: Very common in loan covenants. Estimates how many years to repay all debt from operating cash flow proxy. Lower = better.
RatioFocusHigher Ratio →Key Point
Debt/AssetsBalance sheetWeaker solvency0.40 = 40% debt-financed. More debt = more financial risk.
Debt/CapitalBalance sheetWeaker solvencyTotal capital = Debt + Equity. D/E=1.0 → D/Capital=50%.
Debt/EquityBalance sheetWeaker solvencySome analysts use market value of equity for a more current picture.
Financial LeverageBalance sheetMore leveragedLeverage = 3 means €1 equity supports €3 assets. Used in DuPont as the "equity multiplier."
Debt/EBITDAB/S vs. ISMore leveragedCommon in bond covenants. Higher = more years to repay = more risk.
Interest CoverageIncome statementStronger solvency"Times interest earned." Coverage <1.5× is a serious concern. Used in loan covenants.
Fixed Charge CoverageIncome statementStronger solvencyAdds lease payments to both sides. Preferred for companies with significant operating leases. Also signals quality of preferred dividend.
Cash Flow Stability Determines Safe Debt Levels

Companies with stable, predictable cash flows (utilities, consumer staples) can carry much higher debt ratios safely. The same debt load in a company with volatile cash flows (tech, cyclicals) creates far greater default risk. Never interpret solvency ratios without considering the stability of the underlying cash flow stream.

Profitability Ratios

Profitability ratios measure the company's ability to generate profit from revenues and invested capital. Two groups: return on sales (margins — profit as % of revenue) and return on investment (profit relative to capital employed). For all profitability ratios: higher = better.

Profitability Ratios — Complete Formula Set
═══ RETURN ON SALES (Margins) ═══
Gross Profit Margin = Gross Profit ÷ Revenue
Operating Profit Margin = Operating Income (EBIT) ÷ Revenue
Pretax Margin = EBT (earnings before tax, after interest) ÷ Revenue
Net Profit Margin = Net Income ÷ Revenue

═══ RETURN ON INVESTMENT ═══
Operating ROA = Operating Income / EBIT ÷ Avg Total Assets
ROA = Net Income ÷ Avg Total Assets
ROA (interest-adjusted) = [Net Income + Interest Expense × (1 – Tax Rate)] ÷ Avg Total Assets
ROIC = EBIT × (1 – Effective Tax Rate) ÷ Avg (Total Debt + Total Equity)
ROE = Net Income ÷ Avg Total Equity
Return on Common Equity = (Net Income – Preferred Dividends) ÷ Avg Common Equity
ROA mismatch: Net income is the return to equity holders only, but total assets are financed by both debt and equity holders. Interest has already been deducted from NI. To fix: add back after-tax interest to NI. Alternatively, use operating ROA (EBIT/Avg Assets) which is pre-interest and pre-tax — measures return on all capital regardless of financing source.
ROIC: EBIT × (1−t) = NOPAT (net operating profit after tax). Divided by total invested capital (debt + equity). Best measure of how efficiently all capital — regardless of source — is deployed.
Consistency rule: Whichever ROA version you use, apply it consistently across companies and time periods.

Reading the Margin Waterfall

The margin hierarchy reveals exactly where profitability is gained or lost as you move down the income statement:

  • Gross margin ↑ but Operating margin flat: COGS/pricing improving, but SG&A rising proportionally
  • Operating margin ↑ faster than gross margin: Operating expense control improving (SG&A efficiency, scale benefits)
  • Operating margin ↑ but Pretax margin flat: Interest expense rising — more debt taken on (leverage increasing)
  • Pretax margin ↑ but Net margin flat: Effective tax rate rising (higher earnings in higher-tax jurisdictions?)
  • Pretax margin rising from non-operating income: Assess sustainability — is this a deliberate business shift, or a one-time event? Does not reflect operating improvement.
Gross Margin = Signal of Competitive Strength

Gross profit margin reflects two forces: (1) pricing power (brand, quality, technology moat — ability to charge premium prices) and (2) manufacturing/sourcing efficiency (supply chain, economies of scale). Companies with strong competitive advantages sustain high gross margins for years. Declining gross margins in a competitive industry are an early warning of future earnings pressure.

DuPont Analysis — Decomposing ROE

DuPont analysis breaks ROE into component ratios, each measuring a distinct aspect of performance. Instead of just knowing ROE = 5.92%, you understand why: Is it high margins? Efficient asset use? Leverage? This is one of the most tested topics in the FSA section and in equity analysis generally.

2-Component Decomposition

2-Way DuPont
ROE = ROA × Leverage
= (Net Income ÷ Avg Total Assets) × (Avg Total Assets ÷ Avg Shareholders' Equity)
No leverage → ROE = ROA (leverage ratio = 1.0). Adding debt amplifies ROE if cost of debt < return on assets. If borrowing cost > marginal ROA, additional leverage HURTS ROE — this is why overleveraged companies can destroy value.

3-Component Decomposition

3-Way DuPont
ROE = Net Profit Margin × Total Asset Turnover × Leverage
= (Net Income ÷ Revenue) × (Revenue ÷ Avg Total Assets) × (Avg Total Assets ÷ Avg Equity)
ROA = Net Profit Margin × Total Asset Turnover (note: Revenue cancels out). ROE = ROA × Leverage — consistent with 2-way.
This decomposition links ROE to: Profitability (margin), Efficiency (asset turnover), and Financial Structure (leverage).

5-Component Decomposition (Bloomberg / Industry Standard)

5-Way DuPont — Most Tested Version
ROE = Tax Burden × Interest Burden × EBIT Margin × Total Asset Turnover × Leverage

= (NI ÷ EBT) × (EBT ÷ EBIT) × (EBIT ÷ Revenue) × (Revenue ÷ Avg Assets) × (Avg Assets ÷ Avg Equity)
Tax Burden (NI/EBT): = 1 – effective tax rate. Higher value = lower taxes = more of pretax profit retained = better for ROE.
Interest Burden (EBT/EBIT): Higher value = less interest cost = lower leverage cost = better for ROE. Falls as debt increases.
EBIT Margin (EBIT/Revenue): Operating profitability before financing effects. Pure operating efficiency.
Total Asset Turnover (Revenue/Avg Assets): Overall efficiency — revenue generated per unit of assets.
Leverage (Avg Assets/Avg Equity): Financial structure amplification. Higher = more leveraged.

Examiner Focus — DuPont Interpretation

Anson's ROE improved from –0.62% to +5.92% over 4 years. Leverage was stable (~1.60×). So the entire gain was from ROA — driven by: rising net profit margin (–0.47% → 3.33%) AND rising asset turnover (0.84 → 1.11×). Both profitability and efficiency improved.

Key DuPont exam skill: When ROE changes, isolate which of the 5 components drove the change. Is it tax rate, interest burden, operating margin, efficiency, or leverage? Each has a different strategic implication.

Also tested: Two companies with the same ROE, different drivers — one via high margins (luxury brand), another via high turnover and thin margins (discount retailer). DuPont reveals the structural difference hidden by the single ROE number.

High Margin / Low Turnover Strategy

Luxury goods, pharmaceuticals, enterprise software. Premium prices → high net profit margins. Lower volume needed. Turnover is relatively low but acceptable. ROE driven primarily by profitability.

Low Margin / High Turnover Strategy

Grocery retail, discount retail, commodity businesses. Thin margins on enormous volume. Very high asset turnover compensates for thin margins. ROE driven primarily by efficiency and sometimes leverage.

Standard ratios apply broadly, but some aspects of performance are only meaningful within a specific industry. Industry-specific ratios capture these nuances. For early-stage companies not yet profitable, industry-specific metrics (subscribers, active users, occupancy) often matter more than traditional profitability ratios for valuation.

Forecasting Techniques

Ratio analysis feeds directly into financial forecasting. From a revenue forecast, apply expected common-size percentages to derive IS forecasts. Apply expected DSO to revenue to forecast AR; apply expected DOH to COGS to forecast inventory. Forecasts should reflect a range of outcomes, not just a single point estimate.

Sensitivity Analysis ("What If")

Changes one assumption at a time to show the range of possible outcomes. E.g., revenue growth of 5% vs. 10% vs. 15% — how does NI or FCF change? Identifies key value drivers and highlights which assumptions most affect the result. Useful for financing needs planning.

Scenario Analysis

Changes multiple assumptions simultaneously to reflect coherent scenarios (e.g., recession: lower revenue + margin compression + rising bad debts, all together). If scenarios are mutually exclusive with assigned probabilities → compute expected value and variance of outcomes.

Simulation (Monte Carlo)

Computer-generated analysis using probability distributions for each input. Runs thousands of scenarios simultaneously. Result: full distribution of outcomes with mean, median, percentiles. Most powerful but most complex — captures interaction effects between variables.

Exam Checklist — Module 11

  • ✅ 6-Phase framework: Articulate purpose → Collect data → Process data → Analyze/interpret → Communicate → Follow-up. Phases 3 and 4 are the primary module focus.
  • ✅ Ratio = indicator, not the answer. Tells you WHAT happened. Further analysis always required to determine WHY and whether it created value. Never stop at computing a ratio.
  • ✅ Average denominators: IS/CFO numerator + BS denominator → use AVERAGE BS. Both BS items → period-end usually fine. DuPont exception: if averaging one component, average all.
  • ✅ Vertical common-size IS: Each item ÷ Revenue. Vertical BS: Each item ÷ Total Assets. Horizontal BS: Each item indexed to base year OR % change vs. prior period. Combine horizontal BS data across statements (compare AR growth rate to revenue growth rate).
  • ✅ Sunbeam red flag pattern: Revenue +19%, Inventory +58%, Receivables +38% → inventory and receivables growing far faster than revenue → possible channel stuffing/premature revenue recognition. This is a standard exam trigger.
  • ✅ 5 interpretation contexts: Prior period, expectations, industry peers, company goals, economic cycle. Always frame ratio interpretation within at least one of these.
  • ✅ Activity formulas — memorize all 9: IT = COGS/AvgInv | DOH = 365/IT | RT = Rev/AvgRec | DSO = 365/RT | PT = COGS/AvgAP | DaysPayable = 365/PT | WC TO = Rev/AvgWC | FA TO = Rev/AvgNetFA | TA TO = Rev/AvgTA.
  • ✅ DOH declining from inventory write-down ≠ improved efficiency. A valuation allowance reduces carrying value → mechanically lowers DOH. This is an accounting adjustment, not a management improvement.
  • ✅ Current ratio rising + Quick ratio falling = inventory building up. DOH rising confirms. Despite the better-looking current ratio, overall liquidity is deteriorating. Inventory is the item that distinguishes the two ratios.
  • ✅ Cash Conversion Cycle = DOH + DSO − Days Payable. Shorter/more negative = generally better. BUT always validate context: Apple (negative from supplier power + $70B cash) vs. National Datacomputer (negative from vanishing inventory + 295-day payables + impending bankruptcy).
  • ✅ Defensive interval ratio: Numerator = Cash + ST investments + Receivables. Denominator = Daily cash expenditures (operating expenses excluding D&A, ÷ 365). Measures days company can operate without new cash inflows.
  • ✅ Solvency debt ratios — all higher = weaker: D/Assets, D/Capital, D/Equity, Financial Leverage (= Avg Assets/Avg Equity), D/EBITDA. Coverage ratios — higher = stronger: Interest Coverage (EBIT/Interest), Fixed Charge Coverage [(EBIT+Leases)/(Interest+Leases)].
  • ✅ Total debt in this module = interest-bearing short-term + long-term debt only (not AP, not accrued expenses). Net debt = Total debt − Cash − ST investments.
  • ✅ ROA mismatch: NI/Avg Assets understates return on ALL capital because NI is after interest (equity-only return). Fix: add back after-tax interest. Or use Operating ROA = EBIT/Avg Assets. Whichever version, apply consistently.
  • ✅ ROIC = EBIT × (1−t) ÷ Avg (Debt + Equity). Measures after-tax return on all invested capital regardless of financing source. Best measure of capital deployment efficiency.
  • ✅ 3-Way DuPont: ROE = Net Profit Margin × Total Asset Turnover × Leverage. (ROA = NPM × AT).
  • ✅ 5-Way DuPont: ROE = (NI/EBT) × (EBT/EBIT) × (EBIT/Rev) × (Rev/Assets) × (Assets/Equity). Tax burden (NI/EBT) = 1 − effective tax rate. Higher tax burden = LOWER tax rate = better. Interest burden (EBT/EBIT) = higher = LESS interest cost = better.
  • ✅ DuPont isolates the driver of ROE change. Anson Industries: ROE improved from −0.62% to +5.92% while leverage was stable. Driver = ROA improvement from both higher NPM and higher asset turnover.
  • ✅ Same ROE, different strategy: Luxury brand = high NPM + low turnover. Discounter = low NPM + high turnover. DuPont unmasks structural differences hidden by a single ROE number.
  • ✅ Industry ratios: Retail: same-store sales (organic growth), sales/sq m. Banking: NIM, capital adequacy, reserve ratio. Hotels: ADR, occupancy. Services: revenue/employee. Subscriptions: ARPU. Business risk: coefficient of variation.
  • ✅ 3 forecasting techniques: Sensitivity (1 variable at a time). Scenario (multiple variables simultaneously, with probabilities). Simulation/Monte Carlo (probability distributions for all variables, computer-run thousands of scenarios → full output distribution).

Top exam traps for Module 11:
(1) Ratio tells WHAT, not WHY — never stop at computation. Always interpret in context of strategy and industry.
(2) Average denominators when IS numerator + BS denominator. Period-end when both BS (with DuPont consistency exception).
(3) High DOH from inventory write-down ≠ improved inventory management — it is a valuation adjustment.
(4) Contradictory current/quick ratios → inventory is the distinguishing item. Rising DOH confirms inventory buildup.
(5) Negative CCC: always confirm context — strategic (Apple) vs. distress (National Datacomputer).
(6) ROA mismatch: NI is equity-only return but assets funded by all capital. Adjust numerator or use Operating ROA. Apply consistently.
(7) 5-way DuPont: Tax burden = NI/EBT (higher = LOWER tax rate = better). Interest burden = EBT/EBIT (higher = LESS interest cost = better). Know the interpretation direction for each component.
(8) Same ROE can come from very different strategies — DuPont unmasks the structure. Luxury brand (high margin/low turnover) vs. discounter (low margin/high turnover).
(9) Forecasting: sensitivity = 1 variable. Scenario = multiple variables. Simulation = probability distributions for all variables.
