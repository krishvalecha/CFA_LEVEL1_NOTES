The Portfolio Approach to Investing

Portfolio Approach
Evaluating individual securities in relation to their contribution to the investment characteristics of the whole portfolio — rather than evaluating each security in isolation. The central idea: what matters is how an asset behaves within a portfolio, not just by itself.

Why the Portfolio Approach Matters — Key Benefits

  1. Avoid disaster (downside protection): Don't put all eggs in one basket. Concentrated holdings create catastrophic loss exposure
  2. Risk reduction: Combining assets with less-than-perfect correlations reduces standard deviation for the same expected return
  3. Risk-return trade-off optimization: Different weight combinations produce different risk-return profiles — enabling customization

The Enron Case Study — Lesson in Concentration Risk

  • 1990-2000: Enron stock returned 27% per year vs. 13% for S&P 500
  • Enron 401(k): 62% of employee retirement assets in Enron shares (US$1.3 billion out of US$2.1 billion)
  • Employees could have diversified — only ~US$150M of shares were actually restricted
  • Between Jan 2001 – Jan 2002: Enron stock fell from ~US$90 to $0
  • Other examples: Owens Corning, Northern Telecom, Corning, ADC Telecommunications — all had >25% employee concentration and saw ~90% declines
⚠️ Key Lesson — Human Capital Concentration

Enron employees were doubly exposed: their human capital (wages, job security) AND financial capital (retirement portfolio) were both concentrated in one company. When a Enron-like failure occurs, you lose your job AND your savings. Never concentrate financial investments where your human capital is already invested.

The Diversification Ratio — Measuring Risk Reduction

★ Diversification Ratio ★
Diversification Ratio = σportfolio / σavg individual security
Measures risk reduction from equal-weight diversification.
Lower ratio = greater diversification benefit.
Example from reading: HKSE portfolio had σ = 17.9% vs. avg individual stock σ = 24.9% → ratio ≈ 0.72 (i.e., portfolio risk is only 72% of average individual risk — a 28% risk reduction).

Even stronger reduction is possible with assets from different industries (chosen for low correlation).
💡 Critical Insight — Portfolios Affect Risk More Than Returns

When you form an equally-weighted portfolio, the expected return is the SAME as the average of individual returns — but the standard deviation is LESS than the average of individual standard deviations (unless correlations are all +1). This is the free lunch of diversification: same return, less risk.

Modern Portfolio Theory & Downside Protection

Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)
The theoretical foundation for diversification-based investing, formalized by Harry Markowitz (1952). Main conclusion: investors should not only hold portfolios but should also focus on how individual securities are related to one another (correlation). Extended by Sharpe, Lintner, and Treynor (1960s) to derive the CAPM.

Key MPT Contributors

YearContributorContribution

1964William SharpeCAPM — role of systematic risk in pricing

Limitation of Diversification — Correlation Changes in Crises

Diversification works best in "normal" market conditions. When market turmoil hits, correlations can converge toward 1 — reducing or eliminating the diversification benefit:

  • During Q4 2007–Q1 2009 (Global Financial Crisis): equally-weighted portfolio of 5 global equity indexes returned −48.5%
  • All 5 indexes declined in unison during the crisis
  • The benefit reduced to "only avoiding being in the single worst-performing market"
  • Diversification offers no protection from worldwide contagion or total economic/market failure
⚠️ Exam Trap — Diversification Is Not a Panacea

A common exam question: "Is diversification always effective at reducing risk?" Answer: NO. Diversification reduces risk under NORMAL market conditions but may fail during severe market turmoil when correlations spike. It does NOT protect against systemic/global crises or total market failure.

💡 MPT's Key Insight — Correlation Matters More Than Individual Risk

Before MPT, investors evaluated assets one-by-one. MPT's revolutionary insight: two risky assets can combine into a less-risky portfolio if they have low/negative correlation. What matters is not just how risky each asset is, but how they move together. This insight led directly to the CAPM's focus on systematic (market) risk only.

The Portfolio Management Process — Three Steps

💡 Mnemonic — "PEF" for the Three Steps

Planning → Execution → Feedback. Remember this order for exam questions that ask about the sequence or for identification of a specific activity's step.

STEP 1 — PLANNING
  • Understand the client's needs: objectives (return) and constraints
  • Prepare the Investment Policy Statement (IPS) — written planning document
  • IPS contents: investment objectives, constraints, benchmarks (e.g., target return or index)
  • IPS should be reviewed and updated regularly: every ~3 years OR when major changes occur in client's objectives, constraints, or circumstances
Investment Policy Statement (IPS)
A written planning document describing a client's investment objectives and constraints. Acts as a roadmap for portfolio construction and a benchmark for future performance evaluation. Without an IPS, a manager cannot appropriately serve a client's needs.
STEP 2 — EXECUTION (Three Sub-Steps)
  • Asset Allocation: determine weights across asset classes (equity, fixed-income, alternatives, cash). Often driven by economic/capital market expectations
  • Security Analysis: identify attractive securities within each asset class
  • Portfolio Construction: assemble securities with appropriate weights given the IPS; trades executed (often by a separate buy-side trader)

Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up Analysis

Top-Down Analysis

Starts with macroeconomic conditions → evaluates markets/industries → identifies companies within favored industries.

Sequence: economy → industry → company.

Bottom-Up Analysis

Focuses on company-specific factors: management quality, business prospects, financials.

Sequence: company → may or may not consider industry/economy.

STEP 3 — FEEDBACK (Two Sub-Steps)
  • Portfolio Monitoring and Rebalancing: review composition; rebalance if weights drift from targets; adjust if client circumstances change
  • Performance Measurement and Reporting: evaluate whether objectives have been met; compare to benchmark; may trigger IPS review

Key insight — Asset allocation has the greatest impact on portfolio performance, compared to individual security selection and market timing. This is why the IPS → Asset Allocation chain is so important.

Summary of the Process — Master Flow

StepActivitiesOutputs
1. Planning Understand client needs · Prepare IPS Investment Policy Statement (IPS)
2. Execution Asset allocation · Security analysis · Portfolio construction Constructed portfolio
3. Feedback Monitoring & rebalancing · Performance measurement & reporting Performance report; potential IPS updates
⚠️ Exam Trap — Identifying the Step

Common exam question: "Which step is [specific activity]?" Watch for these common items:
• Rebalancing → Feedback (NOT Execution)
• Preparing the IPS → Planning
• Asset allocation → Execution
• Performance evaluation → Feedback
• Understanding the client → Planning

Individual Investors & Defined Contribution Plans

Individual Investor Goals

  • Short-term goals: children's education, major purchase (car, house), starting a business
  • Long-term goals: retirement income, wealth transfer to heirs
  • Growth vs. income needs: varies by life stage (younger investors typically growth-oriented; retirees typically income-oriented)
  • Pre-requisites: cash reserve, appropriate insurance, residence considerations before long-term investing
Defined Contribution Plan (DC Plan)
A retirement plan in the employee's name, usually funded by both employee and employer. The employee makes investment decisions and bears investment risk and inflation risk. The benefit depends on investment performance. Examples: 401(k) in the US, group personal pension schemes in the UK, superannuation plans in Australia.
💡 DC Plan — Who Bears the Risk?

In a DC plan, the EMPLOYEE bears all investment risk. The final retirement benefit depends on the employee's investment choices and market performance. The employer's obligation ends once the contribution is made. No guarantee of any particular retirement income.

Institutional Investors — Types & Characteristics

The Six Major Institutional Investor Types

① Defined Benefit (DB) Pension Plans
Company-sponsored plans offering employees a predefined retirement benefit. Employer makes contributions and bears investment risk. Assets must fund liabilities. Global pension assets > $41 trillion (end of 2017). US, UK, Japan = top 3 markets (76% of global pension assets).
② Endowments & Foundations
Endowments: funds of NON-PROFIT institutions (e.g., universities) to provide services.
Foundations: GRANT-MAKING entities.
US market ≈ $1.6 trillion. Typically very long time horizon, intended perpetual life, high risk tolerance. Large allocations to alternatives (Yale/Swensen model). Objective: maintain real capital + generate spending income.
③ Banks
Financial intermediaries (accept deposits, lend money). Invest excess reserves in conservative, short-duration fixed-income to earn an excess return above depositor interest obligations. Liquidity is paramount — must meet withdrawal requests. Short time horizon, low risk tolerance.
④ Insurance Companies
Receive premiums; invest to pay future claims. Two types:
Life insurers: long-term liabilities (policies over decades).
Property & Casualty (P&C): short-term liabilities (claims paid quickly).

General account: regulated, conservative fixed-income, matches liabilities.
Surplus account (assets − liabilities): higher-return assets like public/private equities, real estate, hedge funds.

High liquidity needs; low risk tolerance. Example: MassMutual has ~80% in bonds/mortgages/loans/cash.
⑤ Investment Companies (e.g., mutual funds)
Pooled investment vehicles. Characteristics vary by fund type (money market, bond, stock, balanced). Generally need high liquidity to meet redemptions.
⑥ Sovereign Wealth Funds (SWFs)
State-owned investment funds investing in financial/real assets. Do NOT typically manage specific liability obligations (unlike pensions). Goals: budget stabilization, future development projects. SWF assets > $7.6 trillion (March 2018) — more than doubled from 2007. Concentrated in Asia and natural-resource-rich countries.

💡 Pattern Recognition — Who Has What Profile?

Long horizon, high risk tolerance, LOW liquidity → Endowments & Foundations (can afford illiquid alternatives)
Short horizon, LOW risk, HIGH liquidity → Banks & P&C Insurers (must pay depositors/claims quickly)
Long horizon, LOW risk, HIGH liquidity → Life Insurers (long liabilities but claims must be paid on demand)
Long horizon, HIGH risk, varies on liquidity → DB Pension Plans (depends on funded status and maturity)

⚠️ Exam Trap — Endowments vs. Insurance Companies

Yale endowment: <5% in fixed income, >95% in growth/alternatives (real estate, equities, hedge funds).
MassMutual: ~80% in bonds/mortgages/loans/cash.
Completely opposite asset allocations because of completely opposite liability structures. Endowments have no defined liabilities; insurers must pay claims on demand. Tie-in: asset allocation should MATCH liability structure.

DB vs. DC Pension Plans — Master Comparison

⚠️ High-Priority Exam Topic

Defined Benefit vs. Defined Contribution is a classic CFA exam question. Memorize who bears risk, what's defined, and the direction of the industry shift.

Dimension Defined Benefit (DB) Plan Defined Contribution (DC) Plan
What is "defined" The retirement BENEFIT (guaranteed income) The CONTRIBUTION amount (what goes in)
Who bears investment risk EMPLOYER EMPLOYEE
Who bears inflation risk Employer (must fund defined benefit despite inflation) Employee
Who makes investment decisions Plan sponsor (employer/trustees) Employee
Account ownership Company-owned pension pool Individual employee's account
Benefit certainty Predefined, guaranteed income Depends on investment performance
Investor type Institutional investor Individual investor
Examples Traditional pension plans 401(k) (US), group personal pension (UK), superannuation (Australia)
Industry trend DECLINING (higher cost/risk to employer) GROWING (employers prefer this)
💡 Memory Trick — What's Defined = Who's Protected

DB — Benefit Defined: benefit is guaranteed → employee protected → employer takes risk.
DC — Contribution Defined: only the input is fixed → employee gets whatever the investment is worth → employee takes risk.

Think of it as: "What's promised is what's safe." In DB, the benefit is promised. In DC, only the contribution is promised.

Industry Shift: DC Gaining Share

Why are DB plans declining in favor of DC plans?

  • Lower cost to employers: employer's obligation ends after contribution
  • Lower risk to employers: investment risk transferred to employee
  • Simpler accounting: no future liability to estimate and disclose
  • Portable: employees can take DC plans with them between employers
  • Despite the shift, DB plans REMAIN SIZEABLE globally ($41 trillion in pension assets; 76% in US/UK/Japan)

The Asset Management Industry

At the end of 2017, the asset management industry managed more than US$79 trillion globally. Nearly 80% of professionally managed assets are in North America and Europe, but Asia and Latin America are the fastest-growing markets.

Buy-Side vs. Sell-Side

Buy-Side Firms

Asset managers — USE (buy) the services of sell-side firms. Manage portfolios for clients.

  • Mutual fund companies
  • Hedge funds
  • Pension fund managers
  • Insurance investment arms
Sell-Side Firms

Broker-dealers — SELL securities and provide research/recommendations to buy-side clients.

  • Investment banks (trading desks)
  • Broker-dealers
  • Independent research firms

Asset Manager Classifications

Classification AxisTypesDescription
Scope Specialist Focus on one asset class (e.g., EM equities) or style (e.g., quantitative)
Full-service Wide variety of asset classes and styles
Structure Multi-boutique Holding company owns several specialized asset management firms — keeps unique cultures while benefiting from shared services
Management Style Active Try to OUTPERFORM benchmark. Fundamental/quantitative research. Higher fees. ~80% of global AUM, ~94% of industry revenue
Passive Try to REPLICATE benchmark. Low costs. ~20% of global AUM but only ~6% of revenue due to low fees
Asset Type Traditional Long-only equity, fixed-income, multi-asset. Revenue = asset-based management fees
Alternative Hedge funds, private equity, venture capital. Revenue = management fees + performance fees ("carried interest"). Low AUM share but HIGH revenue share
Ownership Privately owned (most common) Usually LLCs or limited partnerships; owned by founders/key personnel
Publicly traded Less common but substantial AUM
Subsidiaries of financial conglomerates Banks/insurance companies offering asset management alongside other services
Smart Beta
A middle-ground strategy between pure passive and active. Uses simple, transparent, rules-based strategies based on factors like size, value, momentum, or dividend characteristics. Features somewhat higher management fees and higher portfolio turnover than pure passive market-cap-weighted strategies.

Three Key Industry Trends

① Growth of Passive Investing
  • Passive assets ≈ 20% of global AUM at end of 2017
  • Top 3 managers = 70% of passive industry
  • Concentrated in equity strategies
  • Drivers: LOW COSTS and difficulty of active managers generating alpha in efficient markets
② "Big Data" in Investing
  • 90% of world's data created in 2011-2013 (IBM)
  • Structured data: order books, returns
  • Unstructured: social media, satellite imagery, sensor data, geolocation
  • Machine learning used for both fundamental and quantitative strategies
  • Creates an "information arms race"
③ Robo-Advisers
  • $180 billion AUM at end of 2017
  • Automated platforms: planning, asset allocation, tax-loss harvesting, strategy selection
  • Drivers: mass affluent/younger demand, lower fees (~0.20% vs. 1% traditional), low barriers to entry
  • Use underlying ETFs/index funds
💡 Key Revenue Disparity — Active vs. Passive

Active = 80% of AUM but ~94% of revenue. Passive = 20% of AUM but only ~6% of revenue. Why? Passive fees are a tiny fraction of active fees (often 0.05% vs. 1%+). This explains why asset managers resist the passive shift — it dramatically compresses margins.

Mutual Funds — Structure & Mechanics

Mutual Fund
A commingled investment pool where investors each hold a pro-rata claim on the income and value of the fund. Value is measured as Net Asset Value (NAV), computed daily based on closing prices of securities in the portfolio. Worldwide open-end fund assets ≈ $50 trillion (Q1 2018).
★ Net Asset Value (NAV) per Share ★
NAV per share = (Total fund assets − liabilities) / Number of shares outstanding
Computed daily based on closing prices of all securities held.
For open-end funds: new investors buy at NAV; existing investors redeem at NAV.

Advantages of Mutual Funds

  • Low investment minimums — accessible to small investors
  • Diversified portfolios — instant diversification with one purchase
  • Daily liquidity (for open-end funds)
  • Standardized performance and tax reporting
  • Professional management

Open-End vs. Closed-End Funds — Master Comparison

Feature Open-End Fund Closed-End Fund

💡 Worked Example — Open-End Fund Mechanics

Fund has $12M NAV, 100,000 shares → NAV per share = $120.
• New investor wants to invest $0.96M → gets $0.96M/$120 = 8,000 NEW shares. Fund now has $12.96M and 108,000 shares.
• Existing investor redeems 5,000 shares → fund pays $600,000 (liquidates $600K of assets) and retires 5,000 shares.
• Other investors' NAV per share is unaffected by these transactions.

⚠️ Closed-End Fund Discount — Important Concept

Closed-end funds often trade at a DISCOUNT to NAV. Example: NAV per share = $100, but market price = $95 (5% discount). This is a market efficiency anomaly — theoretically you could buy all shares, liquidate the fund, and profit $5 per share. In practice, transaction costs and other frictions prevent arbitrage. (This is a topic revisited in Equity Module 3 — Market Efficiency.)

Load vs. No-Load Funds

No-Load Funds
  • NO sales charge for purchase or redemption
  • Only an annual fee (% of NAV)
  • Generally preferred by cost-conscious investors
Load Funds
  • Sales charge (a "load") on purchase and/or redemption PLUS annual fee
  • Usually sold through retail brokers who receive part of the upfront fee
  • Declining in popularity over time

Money Market Funds — CNAV vs. VNAV

Constant NAV (CNAV)

Share price maintained at $1 (or local equivalent). Focus on stability for cash-equivalent investors.

Variable NAV (VNAV)

Unit price can fluctuate. More transparent but potentially confusing for cash-substitute users.

⚠️ Money Market Funds Are NOT Bank Accounts

Although money market funds are often treated as bank account substitutes (since the 1980s), they are NOT insured the same way as bank deposits (e.g., FDIC in US). There is no guarantee of principal — money market funds have "broken the buck" (fallen below $1 NAV) during crises.

Lifecycle / Target-Date Funds

A specific type of hybrid/balanced fund designed around a target retirement date. The asset mix evolves automatically over time:

  • Early years (far from retirement): high equity allocation (e.g., 90% stocks / 10% bonds) — growth focus
  • As target date approaches: gradually shift to bonds (more conservative)
  • At target date: mostly fixed-income — capital preservation
  • Example: A 40-year-old in 2019 targeting age 67 → invest in a "2046" fund; manager handles the glide path
  • Popular in 401(k) plans as default options

Other Pooled Investment Products

Separately Managed Accounts (SMAs)

Separately Managed Account (SMA)
Also called "managed account," "wrap account," or "individually managed account." Managed exclusively for one individual or institution. Unlike mutual funds, assets are owned directly by the client. Allows customization for specific objectives, constraints, tax situation, and ESG preferences. Main disadvantage: much higher minimum investment.

Key features of SMAs:

  • Direct ownership (not pro-rata claim on a pool)
  • Customized for individual needs
  • Can incorporate ESG or socially responsible preferences (e.g., exclude tobacco/defense)
  • Used primarily by large institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals
  • Higher minimum investment than mutual funds

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)

Investment funds that trade on exchanges like stocks. Generally structured as open-end funds. Global ETF assets: $428B (2005) → $4.9 trillion (June 2018) — one of the fastest-growing products.

Hedge Funds

Hedge Fund
Private investment vehicles typically using leverage, derivatives, and long/short strategies. Origin: A.W. Jones & Co. (1949). Global hedge fund AUM: $3.3 trillion (May 2017). Not readily available to all investors — high minimum investment and restricted liquidity (quarterly withdrawals or long lock-ups).

Distinguishing Characteristics of Hedge Funds:

  • Short selling: directly or via options, futures, credit default swaps
  • Absolute return seeking: aim for positive returns in ALL market environments
  • Leverage: financial (borrowing) or implicit (derivatives)
  • Low correlation: some hedge funds historically exhibited low correlation with traditional assets
  • Fee structure: traditionally "2 and 20" — 2% management fee + 20% incentive fee on gains (though now under downward pressure)

Private Equity & Venture Capital Funds

Alternative funds that buy, optimize, and sell portfolio companies. Global PE AUM: $3.1 trillion (Dec 2017). Typical lifespan: 7-10 years (with contractual extensions).

Key characteristics:

  • "Hands-on" approach: financial engineering, new management, board changes, strategic development — not passive ownership
  • Structure: limited partnerships with:
    • General Partner (GP): the fund manager
    • Limited Partners (LPs): the investors
  • "Exit" or "Harvesting" stage: divest via merger, acquisition, or IPO
  • Revenue sources (four types):
    • Management fees: 1-3% of committed/invested capital (may step down over time)
    • Transaction fees: paid by portfolio companies for services; typically partially offset management fee
    • Carried interest: GP's share of profits (typically 20%); earned only after LPs recover initial investment
    • Investment income: on GP's own capital contributions

💡 Pattern — Liquidity vs. Returns Trade-off

The LESS liquid a product, the HIGHER expected returns needed to compensate investors:
Mutual Funds/ETFs (daily liquidity) → modest fees, lower returns
Hedge Funds (quarterly liquidity) → higher fees, aim for higher returns
PE/VC (7-10 year lock-up) → highest fees, aim for highest returns

This is the "liquidity premium" in action — investors demand more return for giving up liquidity.

  • ✅ Three industry trends: passive growth, big data, robo-advisers.
  • ✅ Robo-advisers: $180B AUM (2017). Fees ~0.20% vs. ~1% traditional. Services: planning, asset allocation, tax-loss harvesting, strategy selection.
  • ✅ Mutual fund: commingled pool. Value = NAV per share = (assets − liabilities) / shares. Computed daily.
  • ✅ Mutual fund advantages: low minimums, diversification, daily liquidity, standardized reporting.
  • ✅ Open-end fund: accepts new money, creates/redeems shares at NAV. Always trades AT NAV.
  • ✅ Closed-end fund: fixed share count, trades on exchange, CAN have premium/discount to NAV. ~1% of US mutual fund market.
  • ✅ Load funds: have sales charge (load). No-load funds: only annual fee. Load funds declining in popularity.
  • ✅ 4 types of mutual funds: Money Market, Bond, Stock, Hybrid/Balanced.
  • ✅ Money market funds: short-term (overnight to 90 days). CNAV (maintains $1) vs. VNAV (variable). NOT FDIC insured.
  • ✅ Stock funds: historically largest by AUM. Active (higher fees, higher turnover, lower tax efficiency) vs. Index (opposite). First index fund: Vanguard 1976.
  • ✅ Lifecycle/Target-Date funds: hybrid funds that glide from equity-heavy to bond-heavy over time based on retirement target date.
  • ✅ SMA: Separately Managed Account. DIRECT ownership (not pro-rata). Customized. HIGH minimum. Used by institutions/HNW.
  • ✅ ETF vs. mutual fund: ETF trades intraday, can short, can margin, dividends paid out. Mutual fund: once-a-day NAV, no short/margin, dividends reinvested.
  • ✅ Hedge funds: private, leverage, derivatives, long/short, absolute return focus. "2 and 20" fees. Restricted liquidity (quarterly or lock-up).
  • ✅ Hedge fund characteristics (5): short selling, absolute return, leverage, low correlation, fee structure.
  • ✅ Private Equity/VC: 7-10 year lifespan. GP (manager) + LP (investors). Hands-on approach. Exit via merger/acquisition/IPO.
  • ✅ PE/VC revenue (4 types): management fees (1-3%), transaction fees, carried interest (20%), investment income.
  • ✅ Carried interest: GP's 20% share of profits; earned only AFTER LPs recover their initial investment.

Portfolios affect RISK more than RETURNS

1. PLANNING: understand client → prepare IPS
2. EXECUTION: asset allocation → security analysis → portfolio construction
3. FEEDBACK: monitoring/rebalancing → performance measurement

═══ PENSION PLANS ═══
DB: Benefit defined; employer bears risk
DC: Contribution defined; employee bears risk
Trend: DB declining, DC growing

═══ NAV ═══
NAV per share = (Total assets − liabilities) / Shares outstanding

═══ FEE STRUCTURES ═══
Hedge funds: "2 and 20" (2% mgmt + 20% performance)
PE/VC: 1-3% mgmt + 20% carried interest
Robo-adviser: ~0.20% vs. traditional adviser ~1%

═══ OPEN-END vs. CLOSED-END MUTUAL FUNDS ═══
Open-end: accepts new $, creates/redeems at NAV, trades AT NAV
Closed-end: fixed shares, trades on exchange, may have premium/discount

═══ ETF vs. MUTUAL FUND KEY DIFFERENCES ═══
ETF: intraday trading, short/margin allowed, dividends paid
MF: once-a-day NAV, no short/margin, dividends reinvested
Key patterns to remember:
DB = Benefit fixed = Employer risk
DC = Contribution fixed = Employee risk
Endowment = long horizon, high risk, low liquidity
Bank = short horizon, low risk, high liquidity
Insurance = high liquidity needs + low risk (match liabilities)
Active: high AUM %, high revenue %. Passive: high AUM %, low revenue %.
Less liquid products command higher fees and higher expected returns (illiquidity premium).
