Company Research Reports

Financial Statement Model
A quantitative expression of an analyst's forward-looking views about a company's future earnings, cash flows, and financial position. Structured as projected financial statements. NOT a math problem with a correct answer — it is a justified, evidence-based view.
💡 3-Layer Company Analysis Framework

Layer 1 (this module): Company Analysis — Past & Present: business model, revenue drivers, profitability, working capital, capital structure. Layer 2: Industry & Competitive Analysis (Porter's Five Forces, PESTLE, market share). Layer 3: Company Forecasting: project financials, valuation, risks. Reports present all three layers culminating in a price target and buy/hold/sell recommendation.

Initial Research Report — Structure

Section Key Content
Front Matter Issuer name, security identifiers (ticker, CUSIP), analyst recommendation (buy/hold/sell) and target price, disclosures/disclaimers
Recommendation Analyst's recommendation + summary of key reasons supporting it
Company Description Business model and strategy; revenue/profit disaggregation by product, geography, segment
Industry Overview & Competitive Positioning Industry size, growth rate, key drivers, market share trends, profitability outlook; Porter's Five Forces; PESTLE analysis (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental); company's competitive position and strategy
Financial Analysis & Model Key revenue, cost, profitability, cash flow drivers; forecasts of drivers with supporting discussion; historical and forecasted financial statements
Valuation Company and security value estimates with target price; relative and present value approaches; sensitivity and scenario analyses
ESG Considerations ESG indicators and risks; ownership structure and management composition; executive compensation
Risks Material downside AND upside risks; how risks are incorporated in the financial analysis and valuation

Subsequent Research Report — Structure

Shorter than initial report. Primary audience = those already familiar with the issuer, needing an update. Typical structure for a post-earnings update:

  • Front Matter: analyst names, issuer name, identifier, recommendation, current price, target price, disclosures
  • Recommendation: current recommendation + summary of changes and support explanations
  • Analysis of New Information: actual quarterly results vs. expectations; analyst's interpretation; updated forecasts; historical and updated financial statements
  • Valuation: updated estimates; discussion of changes from prior report
  • Risks: update of risk factors; discussion of changes
💡 Sell-Side vs. Internal Reports

Sell-side (external distribution): comprehensive initial "initiation" report → followed by shorter update reports after earnings/news. Primary audience: those not yet familiar with the issuer. Internal reports: can be far shorter, delivered verbally or in a few slides. Audience already knows the company. The exam won't test format details deeply but may test which sections appear in initial vs. subsequent reports.

02

Determining the Business Model

The first step in company analysis. Determines key drivers of financial results and position, focuses the analyst on what needs investigation, and sets expectations. Appears in the "Company Description" section of the research report.

5 Business Model Elements & Key Questions

Element Key Question Corollary Questions
Products / Services What is the firm selling? Are products/services differentiated or commoditized? Are they consumable (recurring) or durable (one-time)?
Customers & Key Groups To whom does the company sell? Are customers few and concentrated (high dependence) or many and diversified? Are they B2B or B2C?
Sales Channels & Delivery How does the company reach and serve customers? Direct or indirect distribution? Physical stores, digital, agents, wholesalers? High or low customer acquisition cost?
Pricing & Payment Terms How much does the company charge and how are prices structured? Subscription, per-unit, membership, usage-based? Fixed or variable pricing? Pay upfront or deferred?
Resources, Suppliers & Partners What does the company buy and rely on? Are suppliers few and specialized (high dependence) or many and interchangeable? Are there critical partnerships or licenses?

Information Sources for Business Model Analysis

Issuer Sources (Free, Public)
  • Regulatory filings (annual + quarterly reports)
  • Earnings conference calls
  • Investor presentations and events
  • Press releases
  • Management / investor relations
  • Company website and properties
Third-Party Sources
  • Public: industry white papers, government economic/industry data, news outlets (general and industry-specific), social media
  • Proprietary: sell-side analyst reports, credit ratings, Bloomberg/FactSet, specialist consultancies (Rystad for energy, IQVIA/Evaluate for biopharma, Gartner/IDC for IT)
Proprietary Primary Research
  • Surveys commissioned by the analyst
  • Conversations with customers, competitors, suppliers
  • Product comparisons (use competitor products firsthand)
  • Other studies conducted directly by the analyst
03

Revenue Analysis & Drivers

Revenue Driver
A causative factor that explains the level of and changes in an output variable (here, revenue). Analysts identify drivers to understand historical performance and to build forward-looking forecasts.
Bottom-Up Approach

Decomposes revenue into granular drivers:

  • Sales volume × price per unit
  • Revenue by product line, segment, or geography
  • Each sub-segment further broken down into its own drivers

Example: Retail company → decompose into same-store sales growth + new store openings + average transaction value.

Top-Down Approach

Expresses revenue as a function of macro/market drivers:

  • Market size (total addressable market)
  • Company's market share
  • GDP growth and macroeconomic indicators

Example: Revenue = Total market size × Company's market share. Used in industry and competitive analysis module.

💡 Bottom-Up and Top-Down Are Complementary

Analysts use both approaches together — neither replaces the other. Bottom-up gives granularity and helps identify specific product or segment risks. Top-down provides macroeconomic context and a sanity check. A bottom-up forecast that implies implausible market share gains would be caught by a top-down cross-check.

04

Pricing Power

Pricing Power
A company's ability to set prices and other economic terms with customers without materially affecting its sales volumes. Primarily a function of market structure and a company's competitive positioning in its market.

Highly Competitive Markets — Low/No Pricing Power

  • Firms are price takers — price dictated by supply and demand forces
  • All firms sell at essentially the same price
  • Pricing above market → lose customers; pricing below market → price war (irrational)
  • Long-run returns on capital → approach cost of capital as competition forces price down to marginal cost
  • Exception: a low-cost producer (below marginal cost) can earn above-cost-of-capital returns long-run, but requires the cost advantage to be sustained over time
  • Attributes: little/no product differentiation, low barriers to entry, available substitutes, lack of customer loyalty, low switching costs
  • Examples: retail, oil & gas, bank loans/deposits, insurance, fresh food, off-patent pharma, transportation, some technology hardware
  • Commoditization: process by which markets become more competitive over time as new entrants arrive, innovation slows, and imitation spreads

Less Competitive Markets — Pricing Power Present

  • Monopolistic competition, oligopoly, or monopoly → some ability to raise prices without losing volume
  • Attributes: product differentiation (visible quality, convenience, wrap-around services, secondary resale market), barriers to entry, lack of economical substitutes, switching costs, high customer loyalty/retention
  • Examples: branded consumer goods, utilities, patented pharmaceuticals, software, restaurants, payment networks, medical devices, some real estate
  • Firms with pricing power use value-based pricing, cost-based pricing, and price discrimination strategies

How Analysts Evaluate Pricing Power

  1. Track prices over time — are prices rising, flat, or falling relative to competitors?
  2. Compare prices to costs (profit margins) — a company raising prices 3%/yr while costs rise 5%/yr is showing inability to pass on costs AND declining profitability. This is a signal of weak pricing power even if prices are rising
  3. Rising profitability over time = strongest signal of pricing power — demonstrates ability to retain economics for investors AND that competition is not eroding margins
  4. Movie theater example: unable to raise prices → streaming video is a cheaper substitute → customers switch. Pricing power constrained by availability of alternatives
⚠️ Exam Trap — Prices Rising ≠ Pricing Power

The exam may describe a scenario where a company is raising prices but costs are rising faster — this is evidence of WEAK pricing power, not strong. True pricing power means the company can raise prices faster than costs rise, producing expanding margins. Always compare price trends to cost trends, not just price trends in isolation.

Operating Cost Classifications

Three Types of Costs (High Level)

Operating Costs

Costs incurred in generating or relating to current period revenue: acquisition, production, sale, improvement and delivery of goods and services; management of business; regulatory compliance.

Investing Costs

Costs related to acquisition and production of long-term assets: tangible (property, equipment) and intangible (software, trademarks, patents). Capitalized then amortized.

Financing Costs

Payments to debt and equity investors as a return on their investment: interest payments, dividends.

GAAP Classification Quirks — Must Know

Cost Item Economic Intuition Income Statement Classification Cash Flow Statement
R&D Investing (builds future value) Operating — expensed immediately Operating
Depreciation & Amortization Investing (cost of using long-term assets) Operating — included in operating costs Non-cash add-back to Operating; Capital expenditures → Investing section
Interest Expense Financing (cost of debt) Other (not operating) — below EBIT US GAAP: Operating | IFRS: Operating OR Financing (company choice)
Income Taxes Separate (not operating/financing) Other (not operating) — below EBIT and interest Operating

Three Ways to Classify Operating Costs

By Behavior with Output

Variable costs: vary with output level (e.g., merchandise costs, direct labor, raw materials).

Fixed costs: do not change within a given output range in the short run (e.g., salaried employee compensation, D&A, software/IT, insurance, certain utilities). Stated as total dollar amount.

By Nature

What the cost is — describes the economic substance:

  • Compensation of employees
  • Raw materials
  • Merchandise
  • Office supplies
By Function

The purpose of the cost — required by IFRS/US GAAP for most issuers:

  • Cost of goods sold (COGS)
  • Sales and marketing (S&M)
  • General and administrative (G&A)
  • Research and development (R&D)
💡 Why Functional Classification Dominates in Practice

IFRS and US GAAP require issuers to classify costs by function (not fixed vs. variable). This is why all income statements look similar (COGS, SG&A, R&D) even when business models are very different. The fixed vs. variable breakdown is analytically more useful but less commonly disclosed — analysts must infer it from context, footnotes, and management commentary. The functional classification is what drives the standard profitability metrics (gross margin, EBITDA margin, EBIT margin).

Profitability Measures — Gross, EBITDA, EBIT

Gross Profit
Revenue
− Cost of Sales (COGS)
= Gross Profit
Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue
EBITDA
Revenue
− Cost of Sales
− Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D, etc.)
= EBITDA
EBITDA Margin = EBITDA / Revenue
EBIT (Operating Profit)
Revenue
− Cost of Sales
− Operating Expenses
− Depreciation & Amortization
= EBIT
EBIT Margin = EBIT / Revenue
Operating Profit Using Fixed/Variable Classification
Operating Profit = [Q × (P − VC)] − FC
Q = units of output sold | P = price per unit | VC = variable cost per unit | FC = total fixed costs
(P − VC) = Contribution Margin per unit
For profitability: contribution margin must be positive AND Q × (P − VC) must exceed FC

Gross margin ≈ contribution margin (since COGS is largely variable)
SG&A, R&D = largely fixed (may have variable components like sales commissions)
D&A = fixed (unless units-of-output depreciation method is used)
Metric What It Measures What It Excludes Key Use
Gross Profit / Gross Margin Profitability after direct production costs only SG&A, R&D, D&A, interest, taxes Approximate measure of contribution margin; reveals pricing power vs. variable cost of production
EBITDA / EBITDA Margin Operating profitability before non-cash charges and financing costs D&A, interest, taxes Proxy for operating cash generation; useful for comparing capital structures and asset intensities across companies; used in leverage ratios (Debt/EBITDA)
EBIT / Operating Profit Margin Operating profitability after all operating costs including D&A Interest expense, income taxes Measures operating efficiency; not affected by capital structure (pre-interest) or tax rates; most commonly compared across companies
Net Profit / Net Margin Bottom-line profitability after all costs Nothing — fully loaded Bottom line for shareholders; affected by capital structure and taxes; used in ROE calculation
⚠️ EBITDA ≠ Cash Flow from Operations

EBITDA is often used as a cash flow proxy, but it is NOT the same as operating cash flow. EBITDA ignores changes in working capital, which can be a significant use or source of cash. A company with positive EBITDA can still have negative operating cash flow if working capital is building rapidly. EBITDA also ignores maintenance capex needed to sustain the business. Use it as a rough proxy, not a substitute for actual cash flow analysis.

07

Degree of Operating Leverage (DOL)

Operating Leverage
The proportion of fixed costs in a company's operating cost structure. Higher fixed costs = higher operating leverage. Amplifies both profits (when revenue rises) and losses (when revenue falls).
★ Degree of Operating Leverage (DOL)
DOL = % Δ Operating Profit / % Δ Sales
DOL measures the sensitivity of operating profit to changes in sales revenue.
DOL > 1: operating profit changes by more than sales (amplification effect of fixed costs)
High DOL: high fixed costs relative to variable costs
Low DOL: mostly variable costs

Example: DOL = 3 means a 10% increase in sales → 30% increase in operating profit.
Same DOL = 3 with a 10% decrease in sales → 30% DECREASE in operating profit.

A firm can increase its DOL by: increasing fixed costs AND decreasing variable costs in its cost base.
High Operating Leverage
  • High proportion of fixed costs
  • Operating profit rises rapidly when sales grow (high contribution margin per unit flows through)
  • Operating profit falls rapidly when sales decline (fixed costs don't shrink)
  • Examples: airlines, media companies, software, capital-intensive manufacturers
  • High business risk but high upside potential
Low Operating Leverage
  • High proportion of variable costs
  • Operating profit rises more slowly with sales growth (costs also rise proportionally)
  • Operating profit falls more slowly with sales declines
  • Examples: pure retailers, trading companies, staffing firms
  • Lower business risk but lower operating profit sensitivity
💡 DOL — Operating Leverage vs. Financial Leverage

Operating leverage (DOL) = driven by the proportion of fixed operating costs in the cost structure. Amplifies operating profit changes relative to sales changes. Financial leverage (DFL) = driven by fixed financing costs (interest expense). Amplifies net income changes relative to operating income changes. Both are forms of leverage that magnify volatility — one before EBIT, one after. Together they determine the overall sensitivity of net income to sales.

08

Economies of Scale & Scope

Economies of Scale

Decline in costs per unit as output grows. Generally result from fixed costs being spread over more units of output. Also from increased bargaining power over suppliers as volume grows (drives down variable cost per unit too).

  • Evident from the business model (fixed cost structure)
  • Found empirically: compare company size (revenue) with operating costs as % of sales — falling cost ratios as revenue grows = scale economies
  • Example: warehouse retailer — SG&A as % of sales declines as more stores added and membership grows
Economies of Scope

Decline in costs per unit as number of product/business lines increases. Result from shared costs between product lines.

  • Found empirically: compare profitability of integrated company vs. standalone company
  • Example: financial services — commercial banking, consumer banking, brokerage, asset management, payment processing, investment banking all share: customer relationships, technology/IT, compliance, back-office operations → cost efficiencies drive large diversified financial firms
💡 Scale vs. Scope — Easy Distinction

Scale = doing MORE of the SAME thing → costs per unit fall. Scope = doing MORE DIFFERENT things → costs per unit fall due to shared infrastructure. Scale is about volume; scope is about variety. Both lead to cost advantages. Scale is usually a function of fixed costs; scope is usually a function of shared (semi-fixed) overhead.

Working Capital Analysis

Working capital analysis uses activity ratios to measure how efficiently a company manages its short-term assets and liabilities. The primary measures are:

  • Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC): the number of days between paying for inventory/inputs and collecting cash from customers
  • Net Working Capital (NWC) to Sales ratio: measures the minimum level of working capital investment required to support a given level of revenue
★ Cash Conversion Cycle
CCC = Days Inventory Outstanding + Days Sales Outstanding − Days Payable Outstanding
DIO = (Inventory / COGS) × 365 — how many days inventory is held before sale
DSO = (Accounts Receivable / Revenue) × 365 — how many days to collect from customers
DPO = (Accounts Payable / COGS) × 365 — how many days to pay suppliers

Short CCC: company collects fast, holds inventory briefly, pays suppliers slowly → needs LESS external financing
Long CCC: company needs MORE cash tied up in operations → higher financing need
Negative CCC: suppliers finance operations (e.g., large retailers collect cash before paying suppliers)
Short / Negative CCC (Good)
  • Requires less external financing
  • Negative NWC = suppliers are a source of financing (collect from customers before paying suppliers)
  • Examples: supermarkets, warehouse retailers, online retailers (customer pays upfront; suppliers paid later)
Long CCC (Capital Intensive)
  • Requires more external financing to fund operations
  • High inventory holdings or slow customer collections tie up cash
  • Examples: construction companies, aerospace manufacturers, distributors with long payment terms

NWC to Sales Ratio: Net Working Capital / Revenue

  • Determines the minimum level of investment (in addition to capital expenditures) that cannot be distributed to investors
  • High NWC/sales: company needs to invest heavily in working capital as revenue grows → constrains free cash flow
  • Negative NWC/sales: as revenue grows, negative NWC becomes MORE negative → working capital is a source of cash as the business grows (favorable)
10

Capital Investments & Capital Structure

Sources and Uses of Capital

Sources of Capital
  • Cash flows from operations (incl. NWC if negative)
  • Debt issuance
  • Equity issuance
  • Asset disposals
Uses of Capital
  • Cash and investments on hand
  • Net working capital (if positive)
  • Capital expenditures and additions to intangibles
  • Acquisitions
  • Debt paydown
  • Dividends and share repurchases

Evaluating Capital Investments

  • Goal: assess whether management has used investors' capital wisely → returns on invested capital (ROIC) vs. investors' required rate of return (WACC) over the long run
  • Independent analysts cannot access individual project IRRs/NPVs → must use aggregated measures to identify trends in value creation
  • Key return metrics: ROIC (Return on Invested Capital) and ROA (Return on Assets) = unlevered returns
  • Financial leverage converts unlevered returns (ROIC, ROA) into levered returns (ROE)
  • If ROIC > WACC over time → value creation for investors. If ROIC < WACC → value destruction

Capital Structure Risk Assessment Tools

  • Leverage ratios: Debt/EBITDA, Debt/Equity, Net Debt/EBITDA → measure burden of debt relative to earnings or equity
  • Coverage ratios: EBIT/Interest, EBITDA/Interest → measure ability to service debt from operating earnings
  • Credit ratings: third-party assessment of default risk (investment grade = BBB–/Baa3 or better; high yield below that)
  • Degree of Financial Leverage (DFL): measures sensitivity of net income to changes in operating income (see Section 11)
💡 ROE Decomposition — Linking Operating Performance to Shareholder Returns

ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier (DuPont decomposition). This links operating profitability (net margin), asset efficiency (asset turnover), and financial leverage (equity multiplier = Assets/Equity) into one comprehensive measure. Financial leverage (higher equity multiplier) amplifies ROE above the underlying unlevered returns (ROIC/ROA). Analysts use ROE decomposition to identify whether ROE is driven by operating improvement, efficiency gains, or just increased leverage.

11

Degree of Financial Leverage (DFL)

Financial Leverage
The use of debt (with fixed interest payments) in the capital structure. Amplifies net income changes relative to operating income changes. Analogous to operating leverage, but determined by financing costs (interest expense) rather than fixed operating costs.
★ Degree of Financial Leverage (DFL)
DFL = % Δ Net Income / % Δ Operating Income
DFL measures the sensitivity of net income to changes in operating income (EBIT).
Higher interest expense → higher DFL → net income more volatile relative to operating income
DFL = 1: no financial leverage (no debt/interest)
DFL > 1: interest expense is fixed, so net income swings more than operating income

Example: DFL = 2 means a 10% increase in EBIT → 20% increase in net income.
Same scenario: 10% decrease in EBIT → 20% DECREASE in net income.

DOL vs. DFL — Full Comparison

Dimension DOL (Operating Leverage) DFL (Financial Leverage)
Formula % Δ Operating Profit / % Δ Sales % Δ Net Income / % Δ Operating Income
Driven by Fixed operating costs (D&A, salaries, rent) Fixed financing costs (interest expense)
What it amplifies Operating profit changes relative to sales changes Net income changes relative to operating income changes
Increased by Higher fixed costs, lower variable costs Higher interest expense (more debt)
Income statement position Above EBIT (operating section) Below EBIT (financing section)
Risk type Business/operating risk Financial risk (default risk)
⚠️ Combined Leverage Effect

A company with BOTH high DOL and high DFL has extremely volatile net income relative to revenue changes. DOL amplifies operating income from sales changes; DFL then amplifies net income from operating income changes. The combined effect means a moderate sales decline could produce a catastrophic net income drop. High operating leverage PLUS high financial leverage = high overall risk. This combination is particularly dangerous in cyclical industries.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Financial statement model: a quantitative expression of an analyst's forward-looking views on a company's future financials. NOT a math problem with a single correct answer — a justified, evidence-based projection.
  • ✅ 3-layer analysis framework: (1) Company Analysis: Past & Present → (2) Industry & Competitive Analysis → (3) Company Forecasting. Reports culminate in valuation + recommendation + risks.
  • ✅ Initial research report sections: Front Matter, Recommendation, Company Description, Industry Overview & Competitive Positioning (Porter's Five Forces, PESTLE), Financial Analysis & Model, Valuation, ESG, Risks.
  • ✅ PESTLE: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental — framework for external industry influences in company research reports.
  • ✅ Subsequent report: shorter; audience already familiar with issuer; focuses on new information, changes to forecasts, and updated valuation.
  • ✅ Business model = first step. 5 elements: products/services (what), customers (who), sales channels/delivery (how), pricing/payment (how much/how paid), resources/suppliers/partners (what it buys). Analyzed using issuer, public third-party, proprietary third-party, and proprietary primary research sources.
  • ✅ Bottom-up revenue analysis: decompose into volume × price by product/segment/geography. Top-down: express as market size × market share. Both approaches used together.
  • ✅ Pricing power = ability to raise prices without losing volume. Primarily determined by market structure and competitive positioning. Key signal: rising profit margins over time = strong pricing power. Prices rising but margins falling = WEAK pricing power (costs rising faster).
  • ✅ Highly competitive markets (low pricing power): price takers, commoditized products, low barriers to entry, no switching costs, available substitutes. Long-run returns → cost of capital. Examples: oil & gas, retail, off-patent pharma, bank loans.
  • ✅ Markets with pricing power: monopolistic competition/oligopoly/monopoly. Attributes: product differentiation, barriers to entry, switching costs, customer loyalty, lack of substitutes. Examples: branded consumer goods, software, patented pharma, utilities, payment networks.
  • ✅ Commoditization: process where markets become more competitive over time (new entrants, slowing innovation, widespread imitation).
  • ✅ 3 ways to classify operating costs: (1) Behavior with output: fixed vs. variable. (2) Nature: what the cost is (materials, compensation). (3) Function: purpose (COGS, SG&A, R&D) — required by IFRS/US GAAP.
  • ✅ GAAP classification quirks: R&D = operating on income statement despite being economically "investing." D&A = operating on income statement despite being "investing." Interest expense = NOT operating on income statement (below EBIT); US GAAP cash flow = operating; IFRS = operating or financing. Income taxes = NOT operating on income statement; cash flow = operating.
  • ✅ Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS. Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue. Approximates contribution margin (COGS is mostly variable).
  • ✅ EBITDA = Revenue − COGS − Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D). EBITDA = EBIT + D&A. Used as proxy for operating cash generation; used in Debt/EBITDA and interest coverage ratios. NOT equal to operating cash flow (ignores working capital changes).
  • ✅ EBIT (Operating Profit) = Revenue − COGS − All Operating Expenses including D&A. EBIT Margin = EBIT / Revenue. Excludes interest and taxes → not affected by capital structure or tax rate → most useful for cross-company comparison.
  • ✅ Operating Profit Formula: Operating Profit = [Q × (P − VC)] − FC. Contribution margin = P − VC. Profitability requires: positive contribution margin AND Q × (P − VC) > FC.
  • ✅ DOL = % Δ Operating Profit / % Δ Sales. Driven by fixed operating costs. Higher fixed costs → higher DOL. A firm increases DOL by raising fixed costs and reducing variable costs. Amplifies both profits and losses relative to sales changes.
  • ✅ DFL = % Δ Net Income / % Δ Operating Income. Driven by fixed financing costs (interest expense). Higher debt → higher interest → higher DFL. Amplifies net income changes relative to operating income changes.
  • ✅ High DOL + High DFL = very high overall risk. Net income is highly sensitive to small changes in revenue. Dangerous in cyclical industries.
  • ✅ Economies of scale: costs per unit fall as output grows. Result from spreading fixed costs over more units. Detected by falling cost/sales ratios as revenue grows. Economies of scope: costs per unit fall as number of product lines grows. Result from shared costs between lines. Detected by comparing integrated vs. standalone profitability. Example: financial services sharing technology/compliance/back-office.
  • ✅ Cash Conversion Cycle = DIO + DSO − DPO. Short/negative CCC → needs less external financing. Negative CCC → suppliers finance operations. Long CCC → more cash tied up in working capital.
  • ✅ NWC to Sales: determines minimum working capital investment needed per dollar of revenue. Negative NWC/Sales = working capital is a source of cash as company grows.
  • ✅ Sources of capital: operating cash flows, debt issuance, equity issuance, asset disposals. Uses of capital: cash, NWC (if positive), capex/intangibles, acquisitions, debt repayment, dividends, buybacks.
  • ✅ Capital investment evaluation: compare ROIC to WACC over time. ROIC > WACC = value creation. ROIC < WACC = value destruction. Analysts use aggregated measures (not individual IRR/NPV which are management-only).
  • ✅ Capital structure risk tools: leverage ratios (Debt/EBITDA, Debt/Equity), coverage ratios (EBIT/Interest), credit ratings, DFL.
  • ✅ Financial leverage converts ROIC/ROA (unlevered) → ROE (levered). DuPont: ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier. Higher equity multiplier (more debt) boosts ROE but increases risk.
