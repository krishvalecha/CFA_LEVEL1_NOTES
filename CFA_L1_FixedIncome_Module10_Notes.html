Three Sources of Return from a Fixed-Rate Bond

Every dollar of return you earn from a fixed-rate bond comes from exactly one of three buckets. Understanding which bucket dominates depends entirely on your investment horizon and what happens to interest rates after you buy. This module is about understanding how these three sources interact — and how interest rate risk flows through them differently for different investors.

① Coupon + Principal Payments
  • Fixed and contractually promised
  • Certain (barring default)
  • Coupons received each period; principal at maturity
  • Not affected by interest rate changes
  • For a 6.2% bond: 6.2 per 100 par each year + 100 at maturity
② Reinvestment of Coupons
  • "Interest-on-interest" — compounding gain
  • Uncertain — depends on future reinvestment rates
  • Affected by rate changes after purchase
  • Rates rise → more reinvestment income
  • Rates fall → less reinvestment income
  • More important the longer the holding period
③ Capital Gain / Loss on Sale
  • Only relevant if bond is sold BEFORE maturity
  • Uncertain — depends on yields at time of sale
  • Rates rise → bond price falls → capital loss
  • Rates fall → bond price rises → capital gain
  • More important the shorter the holding period
  • Buy-and-hold investors: no capital gain/loss risk
💡 The Inverse Relationship — The Core Concept of this Module

Sources ② and ③ are inversely affected by interest rate changes. When rates rise: reinvestment income goes up, but bond price goes down (capital loss if sold). When rates fall: reinvestment income goes down, but bond price goes up (capital gain if sold). These two effects partially or fully offset each other — and the balance between them depends on your investment horizon relative to Macaulay duration.

Horizon Yield (Realized Rate of Return) — The General Approach
Total future value at horizon = FV(reinvested coupons) + Sale price (or par at maturity)

(1 + r)^N = [FV(reinvested coupons) + Sale price] / Purchase price

r = [(FV(reinvested coupons) + Sale price) / Purchase price]^(1/N) − 1

WHERE:
N = number of periods held (investment horizon)
r = annualized horizon yield (compounded)

EXCEL: FV of coupons = -FV(reinvestment rate, N periods, coupon, 0, 0)
Sale price = -PV(yield at sale, remaining periods, coupon, 100, 0)
Horizon yield (also called realized return or holding-period return) is the investor's actual IRR based on the price paid, total cash flows received, and the reinvestment rates actually achieved. It is NOT necessarily equal to the bond's stated YTM. The YTM at purchase is only a promise — the horizon yield is the reality.

Compounded annual growth rate: r = (Total future value / Purchase price)^(1/N) − 1. This is the correct formula — not a simple return.
02

Horizon Yield = YTM — The Three Assumptions You Must Know

★ Critical: Three Conditions for Horizon Yield = YTM
  • ① Hold to maturity: No early sale → no capital gain/loss risk
  • ② No default: All promised cash flows are received on schedule
  • ③ Reinvest all coupons at YTM: Every coupon payment is reinvested at exactly the bond's original YTM throughout its life

If ANY of these three assumptions fails, the horizon yield ≠ YTM. In practice, the reinvestment assumption almost always fails — reinvestment rates change as interest rates change.

⚠️ YTM is Not a "Guaranteed" Return — It's a Promise Under Perfect Conditions

The most common exam trap: students treat YTM as the investor's actual return. YTM is what you earn ONLY if you hold to maturity, face no default, AND reinvest every coupon at exactly YTM. Since reinvestment rates change over time, your realized return will differ from YTM unless you happen to always reinvest at exactly the original YTM rate. The module's examples prove this systematically.

The "Constant-Yield Price Trajectory" — Selling at Par

A bond sold at a price on its constant-yield price trajectory means there is no capital gain or loss — the bond is priced exactly where it "should be" given its original yield. The carrying value at any point = purchase price ± amortized discount/premium.

  • Bond purchased at par, YTM unchanged: carrying value = 100 at all times → no capital gain/loss when sold at any point
  • Bond purchased at discount: carrying value rises toward par over time (discount amortization)
  • Bond purchased at premium: carrying value falls toward par over time (premium amortization)
  • Capital gain/loss arises only when the sale price deviates from the carrying value (constant-yield price trajectory) — i.e., when yields have changed

Interest income = coupon receipt + reinvestment income + amortization of discount (or minus amortization of premium). This is the passage-of-time return.
Capital gain/loss = return from change in market yields (change in PV of the bond's cash flows).

03

Reinvestment Risk vs. Price Risk — The Inverse Relationship

📉 Reinvestment Risk

Definition: The risk that coupon payments will be reinvested at rates LOWER than the original YTM, reducing the total future value of reinvested cash flows.

Triggered by: Falling interest rates after purchase.

Who faces it most: Long-horizon investors (especially buy-and-hold). A buy-and-hold investor has ONLY reinvestment risk — no capital gain/loss risk since they hold to maturity.

Worst case: Rates fall dramatically → all coupons reinvested at very low rates → FV of coupons much lower than expected.

📈 Price Risk

Definition: The risk that bond prices will fall (due to rising yields), creating a capital loss if the bond is sold before maturity.

Triggered by: Rising interest rates after purchase.

Who faces it most: Short-horizon investors. An investor who sells before receiving a single coupon has ONLY price risk — no reinvestment risk at all.

Worst case: Rates rise dramatically immediately after purchase → large capital loss when selling at the lower market price.

The Inverse Relationship — Summary
Interest rates RISE → Reinvestment income ↑ AND Bond price ↓ (capital loss if sold)
Interest rates FALL → Reinvestment income ↓ AND Bond price ↑ (capital gain if sold)

These effects partially (or fully, at Macaulay duration) offset each other.

Long horizon investor: Reinvestment risk dominates → fears FALLING rates
Short horizon investor: Price risk dominates → fears RISING rates
Horizon = MacDur: Two risks exactly offset → immunized against rate changes
The key insight: The same interest rate change affects the two risks in opposite directions. This is why the investment horizon is at the heart of understanding which risk matters more. Neither risk is "better" to have — it depends on whether rates move for or against you. What matters is knowing which risk dominates your position so you know what to hedge.

Macaulay Duration — Definition, Concept & Properties

Macaulay Duration (MacDur)
The weighted-average time to receipt of a bond's cash flows, where each cash flow's weight is its share of the bond's total present value (full price). It is expressed in periods (years for annual coupon bonds; divide by 2 for semiannual bonds to get years). Named after Frederick Macaulay (Canadian economist, 1938). It is the investment horizon at which reinvestment risk and price risk exactly offset each other for a parallel yield curve shift.

What Macaulay duration tells you in plain English: If you hold the bond for exactly its Macaulay duration (in years), a one-time instantaneous parallel shift in the yield curve immediately after purchase will leave your horizon yield virtually unchanged — the gain/loss from reinvestment and the loss/gain from price change exactly cancel.

Why is MacDur less than maturity? Because it is a PV-weighted average of cash flows — the large final payment (coupon + principal) at maturity is discounted most heavily. The earlier coupons receive less weight because they arrive sooner. For a par bond, MacDur is always less than maturity (because coupons pull the average earlier than T). For a zero-coupon bond, MacDur = maturity exactly (only one cash flow: at T).

📉 When Rates Rise (Panel A of Exhibit 2)
  • Immediate: bond price drops → paper loss
  • Over time: "pull to par" effect → price recovers toward par
  • Reinvestment income grows as coupons are reinvested at higher rates
  • At MacDur: reinvestment gain = price loss → total return unchanged
  • Beyond MacDur: reinvestment gain > price loss → net gain
📈 When Rates Fall (Panel B of Exhibit 2)
  • Immediate: bond price rises → paper gain
  • Over time: "pull to par" → price falls back toward par
  • Reinvestment income shrinks as coupons reinvested at lower rates
  • At MacDur: reinvestment loss = price gain → total return unchanged
  • Beyond MacDur: reinvestment loss > price gain → net loss

Key Properties of Macaulay Duration

  • MacDur is always ≤ time to maturity. Equal only for zero-coupon bonds (MacDur = maturity). Always less for coupon bonds.
  • MacDur decreases as time passes (between coupon dates). After a coupon is paid, the time to receipt of all remaining cash flows shortens → MacDur falls.
  • MacDur falls after each coupon payment. With one fewer cash flow outstanding, the weighted average of remaining cash flows shifts earlier.
  • Higher coupon rate → lower MacDur. A bond with a higher coupon has more cash flows arriving early (larger weight on near-term periods) → lower weighted-average time.
  • Higher YTM → lower MacDur. Higher discount rate reduces the present value weight of distant cash flows (which are discounted more heavily) → weighted average shifts earlier.
  • Longer maturity → higher MacDur (generally). More distant cash flows shift the weighted average later.
  • Zero-coupon bond: MacDur = maturity. The only cash flow is at T, so the weighted average is T.
  • Macaulay duration is not static — it must be recalculated at each point in time as the bond moves toward maturity.

Macaulay Duration — Three Calculation Methods

Method 1: Weighted-Average Tabular Calculation (On Coupon Date)

The most intuitive approach. Build a table with 6 columns. The sum of column (6) is the Macaulay duration.

  1. Col 1: Period number (1, 2, 3, ... N)
  2. Col 2: Time to receipt (= period number when on a coupon date)
  3. Col 3: Cash flow (PMT for periods 1 to N−1; PMT + FV for period N)
  4. Col 4: Present value of each cash flow (CF / (1+r)^t)
  5. Col 5: Weight = Col4 / Sum of Col4 (= PV of CF / Full price)
  6. Col 6: Col2 × Col5 (time-weighted contribution to duration)

MacDur = Σ Col6 = sum of all (time to receipt × weight)

Method 2: Closed-Form Formula (Equation 3)

Equation 3 — Macaulay Duration Closed-Form
MacDur = { (1+r)/r − [1+r + N×(c−r)] / [c×((1+r)^N − 1) + r] } − t/T

WHERE:
r = yield-to-maturity per period (e.g., 0.062 for annual 6.2%)
N = number of periods to maturity (as of beginning of current period)
c = coupon rate per period (e.g., 0.062 for 6.2% annual)
t = days from last coupon payment to settlement date
T = days in the coupon period
t/T = fraction of period elapsed since last coupon (= 0 on a coupon date)
Special case: when c = r (priced at par, on coupon date):
When coupon rate = YTM and t/T = 0, the formula simplifies significantly. The (c−r) term in the numerator becomes 0, and the formula reduces to: MacDur = (1+r)/r − N/((1+r)^N − 1). For the BRWA bond: (1.062/0.062) − 10/(1.062^10 − 1) = 17.129 − 9.386 = 7.743 ≈ 7.7429 ✓

On coupon date: t/T = 0, so the last term drops out entirely. The formula gives the same result as the tabular method.

Method 3: Excel / Google Sheets DURATION Function

Excel DURATION Function
=DURATION(Settlement, Maturity, Coupon, Yld, Frequency, [Basis])

Settlement = settlement date in DATE form (e.g., DATE(2025,10,15))
Maturity = maturity date in DATE form (e.g., DATE(2035,10,15))
Coupon = annual coupon rate (e.g., 0.062)
Yld = annualized YTM (e.g., 0.062)
Frequency = payments per year (1=annual, 2=semiannual, 4=quarterly)
[Basis] = day-count convention (0 or omitted = 30/360, 1 = actual/actual)

EXAMPLE: =DURATION(DATE(2025,10,15), DATE(2035,10,15), 0.062, 0.062, 1, 0)
→ Returns: 7.7429
💡 Which Method to Use on the Exam?

The tabular method is most intuitive and asked for most frequently at Level 1 — you should know how to build the table and identify which row/column is which. The closed-form formula is rarely computed by hand on the exam but its structure (and what t, T, r, c, N represent) is tested conceptually. The DURATION Excel function is referenced for context but not directly testable. Know the table method thoroughly.

Macaulay Duration Between Coupon Dates — Example 5

When calculating Macaulay duration between coupon payment dates (not on a coupon date), the time to receipt of each cash flow is fractional. The same tabular method applies — the only difference is that each "time to receipt" shifts earlier by the fraction of the period that has already elapsed (t/T).

Between-Coupon-Date Adjustment — The t/T Factor
On a coupon date: Time to receipt of period k = k (whole number)
Between coupon dates: Time to receipt of period k = k − t/T

WHERE:
t = days since last coupon payment (e.g., 57 days)
T = total days in the coupon period (e.g., 360 for 30/360 convention)
t/T = fraction elapsed (e.g., 57/360 = 0.1583)

So if you're 57 days into the first period:
Period 1 time to receipt: 1 − 57/360 = 1 − 0.1583 = 0.8417
Period 2 time to receipt: 2 − 0.1583 = 1.8417
Period 10 time to receipt: 10 − 0.1583 = 9.8417

PV of each CF also increases (less discounting needed — closer to payment date)
Key result from Example 5: Settling 57 days after issuance, MacDur = 7.5845 vs. 7.7429 at issuance. The Macaulay duration fell — because 57 days of time has elapsed and all cash flows are now closer to receipt. This confirms that MacDur is not static: it falls continuously as time passes.

Duration Gap — Quantifying Your Interest Rate Risk Exposure

Equation 1 — Duration Gap
Duration Gap = Macaulay Duration − Investment Horizon

Positive duration gap → Investment horizon < MacDur → Price risk dominates → Fears RISING rates
Zero duration gap → Investment horizon = MacDur → Immunized → Neutral to rate changes
Negative duration gap → Investment horizon > MacDur → Reinvestment risk dominates → Fears FALLING rates
The duration gap quantifies the mismatch between your investment horizon and the bond's Macaulay duration. A portfolio manager trying to immunize a liability (match a payment due at a specific future date) wants a duration gap of zero — they would choose a bond or portfolio of bonds with Macaulay duration equal to their investment horizon.

⚠️ Important Caveat — MacDur Immunization is Not Perfect

Immunization (duration gap = 0) only works perfectly for a one-time, instantaneous, parallel shift in the yield curve. In practice: (1) rates shift multiple times and non-instantaneously; (2) the yield curve may twist (non-parallel shifts); (3) both MacDur and the investment horizon change over time, requiring rebalancing. Level 1 treats this as a single-shift approximation. Be careful not to claim perfect immunity — say "nearly immunized" or "largely offsetting."

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Three sources of return: (1) Coupon + principal payments [certain]; (2) Reinvestment income on coupons ["interest-on-interest", uncertain, rate-sensitive]; (3) Capital gain/loss on sale before maturity [uncertain, rate-sensitive, only if sold before maturity].
  • ✅ Horizon yield = YTM only if: (1) held to maturity; (2) no default; (3) all coupons reinvested at original YTM. Failure of ANY ONE of these → horizon yield ≠ YTM.
  • ✅ Horizon yield formula: r = [(FV coupons + Sale price) / Purchase price]^(1/N) − 1. Compute FV of coupons using Excel FV function at the reinvestment rate. Compute sale price using PV function at yield prevailing at sale.
  • ✅ Constant-yield price trajectory: The "fair value" path of a bond price assuming yields don't change. Selling on the trajectory = no capital gain/loss. Premium bonds: price falls toward par. Discount bonds: price rises toward par. Capital gain/loss = deviation from this trajectory.
  • ✅ Reinvestment risk: Risk that future coupon reinvestment rates fall below original YTM. Triggered by falling rates. Hurts long-horizon investors most. Buy-and-hold investors face ONLY reinvestment risk (no price risk).
  • ✅ Price risk: Risk that bond price falls (yields rise) creating a capital loss if sold. Triggered by rising rates. Hurts short-horizon investors most. Ultra-short investors (sell before first coupon) face ONLY price risk.
  • ✅ Inverse relationship: Rates rise → reinvestment income ↑ AND sale price ↓. Rates fall → reinvestment income ↓ AND sale price ↑. These offset each other — the balance depends on holding period vs. MacDur.
  • ✅ Long horizon > MacDur: Reinvestment risk dominates. Fears falling rates. Benefits from rising rates. Duration gap is negative.
  • ✅ Short horizon < MacDur: Price risk dominates. Fears rising rates. Benefits from falling rates. Duration gap is positive.
  • ✅ Horizon = MacDur: Immunized. The two risks exactly offset for a one-time parallel yield curve shift. Duration gap = 0. This is the concept of immunization.
  • ✅ Duration Gap = MacDur − Investment Horizon. Positive → price risk dominant. Zero → immunized. Negative → reinvestment risk dominant.
  • ✅ Macaulay duration definition: Weighted-average time to receipt of a bond's cash flows. Weights = PV of each CF / Full price. Expressed in years (for annual bonds). MacDur ≤ maturity always. MacDur = maturity for zero-coupon bonds only.
  • ✅ Tabular calculation method: 6-column table. Col 5 = PV weight. Col 6 = time × weight. Sum of Col 6 = MacDur. Know this cold. The final cash flow (coupon + par) typically accounts for ~58% of full price and dominates the duration.
  • ✅ Between-coupon-date calculation: Each time to receipt = period number − t/T. PV of each CF rises (less discounting required). Weights remain unchanged. MacDur is lower (time has passed). t = days since last coupon, T = days in the period.
  • ✅ MacDur is not static: It falls continuously as time passes toward maturity. Falls after each coupon payment. Must be recalculated to remain immunized over time (requires periodic rebalancing).
  • ✅ Higher coupon rate → lower MacDur (more cash flow weight on early periods). Higher YTM → lower MacDur (distant CFs discounted more heavily). Longer maturity → higher MacDur (generally). Zero coupon: MacDur = maturity.
  • ✅ BRWA bond key numbers: 10yr, 6.2% annual, par price. MacDur at issuance = 7.7429 years. MacDur at 57 days later = 7.5845 years. Hightest (8yr horizon) ≈ immunized.
  • ✅ Excel DURATION function: =DURATION(Settlement, Maturity, Coupon, Yld, Frequency, [Basis]). Basis: 0=30/360, 1=actual/actual.
  • ✅ Immunization caveat: Only perfectly offsets a one-time, instantaneous, parallel yield curve shift. Non-parallel shifts, multiple rate changes, or passage of time require rebalancing.

Duration Gap = MacDur − Investment Horizon
Gap > 0 → Horizon < MacDur → Price risk → Fears rising rates
Gap = 0 → Horizon = MacDur → Immunized → Neutral
Gap < 0 → Horizon > MacDur → Reinv. risk → Fears falling rates

═══ MACAULAY DURATION ═══
MacDur = Σ (time to receipt_k × weight_k)
weight_k = PV(CF_k) / Full price
MacDur ≤ maturity (= maturity for zero-coupon bonds)
MacDur falls as time passes; recalculate between coupon dates using t/T

═══ BETWEEN COUPON DATES ═══
Time to receipt of period k = k − t/T
t = days since last coupon; T = days in coupon period
Weights unchanged; times reduced; MacDur decreases

═══ CLOSED-FORM MacDur (Eq. 3) ═══
MacDur = {(1+r)/r − [1+r+N(c−r)] / [c((1+r)^N−1)+r]} − t/T
When c=r and t=0: simplifies to (1+r)/r − N/((1+r)^N−1)

═══ EXCEL FUNCTION ═══
=DURATION(Settlement, Maturity, Coupon, Yld, Frequency, [Basis])
Basis: 0=30/360, 1=actual/actual
