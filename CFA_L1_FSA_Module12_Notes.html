Financial Statement Model — Overview

A financial statement model (also called a pro forma model) is the foundation for most valuation work — it produces the future income statements, balance sheets, and cash flow statements that feed into DCF models, EPS-based valuations, EBITDA multiples, and credit analysis. The model is sales-based: the revenue forecast drives almost everything else.

What a Pro Forma Model Produces

Pro Forma Income Statement
  • Revenue (by segment: volume × price/mix × FX)
  • COGS → Gross profit → Gross margin %
  • SG&A / Distribution costs
  • EBIT, EBITDA (D&A add-back)
  • Interest expense, taxes → Net income
  • EPS (basic and diluted)
Pro Forma Cash Flow Statement
  • Starts with net income from IS
  • Add back D&A; adjust for working capital changes
  • Capex in investing section
  • Debt issuance/repayment, dividends, share activity in financing
  • Ending cash flows to balance sheet
Pro Forma Balance Sheet
  • Largely derived by linking IS and CFS
  • Retained earnings: prior year + NI − dividends
  • AR, Inventory, AP: derived from days ratios × revenue/COGS
  • Fixed assets: prior year + capex − D&A
  • Cash: ending balance from CFS
  • Must balance (Assets = Liabilities + Equity)

4-Step Income Statement Forecast Process

STEP 1
Revenue Forecast
Segment-level: Volume × Price/Mix × FX × Scope change. Most important input — drives everything downstream.
STEP 2
Operating Expense Forecast
COGS (as % of revenue = gross margin target). SG&A distribution and admin costs (as % of revenue or fixed absolute amounts).
STEP 3
Pro Forma EBIT
Check: compare consolidated EBIT to sum of segment operating profits. Segment approach = a "check" on consolidated model.
STEP 4
Non-Operating Items
Interest expense (debt × rate), income taxes (revenue × effective tax rate), shares outstanding → EPS computation.
Valuation Metrics From the Model

A financial statement model feeds all major valuation approaches: FCF-based DCF (requires EBIT, taxes, D&A, capex, working capital change), EPS-based P/E (net income ÷ shares), EBITDA multiples (EBIT + D&A), and book value per share (equity from the balance sheet). The same model, built once, serves all of these.

02

Building the Pro Forma Income Statement

Step 1 — Revenue Forecast

Revenue is the most critical forecast input. For each segment, revenue change is broken into four components that must be estimated separately:

  • Volume growth: Unit growth based on demand trends, industry capacity, competitive dynamics
  • Price/mix: Price changes per unit + revenue impact of selling more high-priced vs. low-priced items (mix shift). Price/mix is strongly accretive to gross margins.
  • Scope change: Impact of acquisitions/divestitures — kept separate from organic growth
  • FX impact: Revenue realized in foreign currencies translated at expected FX rates. For export-heavy companies (e.g., Rémy: >70% of revenues outside eurozone), FX can be very significant.
Organic Revenue Growth Formula
Organic Growth = [(1 + Volume Growth) × (1 + Price/Mix)] − 1

Example (Rémy Cognac FY2022E):
Volume = 7.0%, Price/Mix = 4.0%
Organic Growth = [(1.07) × (1.04)] − 1 = 11.3%

Total Revenue Growth = Organic Growth + FX Impact + Scope Change
Why multiply (1+v)(1+p) instead of adding? Because the price/mix change applies to the post-volume level of revenue, not the original. The combined effect is multiplicative, not additive. For small percentages the difference is minor, but technically the multiplicative formula is correct.
Organic growth excludes acquisitions and FX — this is the "clean" underlying business performance. Analysts and investors watch organic growth most closely.

Step 2 — COGS and Gross Margin

COGS is typically modeled as a percentage of revenue (i.e., 100% − gross margin %). The gross margin assumption is built from:

  • Historical gross margin trend
  • Expected volume leverage (higher revenue → fixed costs spread over more units → margin expansion)
  • Expected price/mix — premium product mix shifts → higher gross margin
  • Input cost inflation (raw materials, packaging, labour) — passed through or absorbed?
  • Management targets or strategic milestones (e.g., Rémy's FY2030 72% gross margin target)

Step 3 — SG&A and Operating Expenses

Distribution / Selling Costs

Typically modeled as % of revenue. If a company is investing in building out a distribution network, this % may rise before it can leverage the investment. Check for trend reversals (Rémy: rose from 26% to 38%, then declined to 34% — network build-out phase complete).

Administrative Expenses

Often modeled as: (a) fixed absolute amount growing at a fixed % per year (e.g., +1%/year), or (b) as % of revenue. In declining revenue periods, admin % of revenue rises because these are mostly fixed costs. As revenue recovers, admin % falls (operating leverage).

Other Operating Items — Zero by Default

Items like restructuring charges, impairment provisions, and divestiture gains/losses are highly irregular and unpredictable. Standard practice: forecast zero unless there is specific evidence of a known transaction. If an analyst includes these as recurring, it distorts the underlying earnings picture. They are disclosed separately on the IS precisely because they are non-recurring.

Step 4 — Non-Operating Items

ItemHow to ForecastKey Consideration
Interest Expense Beginning gross debt balance × interest rate Fixed rate: straightforward. Variable rate: reference rate + credit spread. Update as debt schedule changes.
Interest Income Average cash balance × expected yield Often near zero if company keeps cash in low-yield instruments. Forecast after completing the CFS cash projection.
Income Tax Pre-tax income × effective tax rate Use historical effective rate (not statutory rate) unless there's a known reason for it to change. Effective rate reflects jurisdiction mix, tax credits, deferred taxes.
Shares Outstanding Prior year ± net share issuance/repurchases Basic: common shares only. Diluted: add in-the-money options/convertibles less assumed repurchases. Weighted average for EPS calculation.

Segment Cross-Check

Always build a segment-level operating profit estimate and compare to the consolidated EBIT forecast. The two should reconcile (sum of segment current operating profits ≈ consolidated EBIT before non-recurring items). If there's a large gap, one of the assumptions is likely wrong. The segment approach also reveals what is driving the consolidated margin — e.g., in Rémy, almost all margin improvement came from the high-margin Cognac segment; the other two segments contributed little.

Pro Forma Cash Flow Statement & Balance Sheet

Cash Flow Statement — Key Forecasting Inputs

CFS SectionLine ItemHow Modeled
Operating (CFO) Net income Directly from forecasted IS
Operating (CFO) D&A % of prior year fixed assets (e.g., 4.2% of beginning PP&E). Add back to net income as non-cash.
Operating (CFO) Working capital changes Derived from change in AR, inventory, and AP computed via days ratios. WC increasing = cash use (negative). WC decreasing = cash source (positive).
Operating (CFO) Share-based compensation Non-cash; add back. Usually forecast flat at recent year level unless major change in comp structure.
Investing (CFI) Capex % of revenue (e.g., 5% of sales). Or absolute amount based on known investment plans.
Financing (CFF) Debt issuance / repayment Based on known maturity schedule or assumption of flat debt
Financing (CFF) Dividends Forecast flat at last known level, or apply expected payout ratio to net income
Financing (CFF) Share repurchases/issuance Zero unless management has disclosed a buyback program. Check shares outstanding schedule.

Working Capital Modeling — The Days Ratio Method

Working Capital Derived From Days Ratios
Accounts Receivable = (DSO ÷ 365) × Revenue
Inventories = (DOH ÷ 365) × COGS
Accounts Payable = (DPO ÷ 365) × COGS

Net Working Capital = AR + Inventory − AP
Change in WC = Current year NWC − Prior year NWC
CFS treatment: ↑ WC = Cash USED (negative in CFS) | ↓ WC = Cash PROVIDED (positive in CFS)
Key modeling practice: Hold days ratios (DSO, DOH, DPO) approximately constant at historical levels, unless there is a specific reason to expect a change (e.g., Rémy: DOH was temporarily elevated by COVID-19 inventory buildup → forecast DOH declining back toward normal as situation normalizes → positive CFS contribution from WC release).
Rémy's unusual working capital: Net WC = 105% of sales (FY2021) because cognac must age for years — extremely high DOH (~1,500 days). This is industry-specific, not a management problem.

Forecasted Balance Sheet — Linking Rules

Once IS and CFS are complete, the BS is largely mechanical linking:

  • Cash: Ending cash from CFS
  • AR / Inventory / AP: From days ratios applied to revenue/COGS forecasts
  • PP&E (net): Prior year net PP&E + Capex − D&A
  • Debt: Prior year + issuance − repayment (from CFS)
  • Common equity: Prior year + Net income + Share-based comp − Dividends ± Share activity
  • All other items: Held flat unless there is a specific reason to change them
  • Check: Total assets = Total liabilities + Total equity must hold in every forecast year. If it doesn't, a linking error exists.
Examiner Focus — BS Balance Check

The balance sheet must balance in every forecast period. Common errors: forgetting to add NI to retained earnings, linking capex as positive instead of negative, not updating AP/AR when COGS/Revenue forecasts change. In a model, retained earnings = prior year RE + NI − dividends. The accounting identity (A = L + E) is the key check that the entire model is correctly linked.

04

Valuation Inputs — Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF)

FCFF Formula — From the Pro Forma Model
FCFF = After-Tax EBIT + D&A + Change in Working Capital − Capex

Where:
After-Tax EBIT = EBIT × (1 − Effective Tax Rate)
Change in WC = Positive if WC decreasing (releasing cash); Negative if WC increasing (using cash)

Example (Rémy FY2022E):
EBIT = 272 | Tax rate = 32% | After-Tax EBIT = 185
D&A = 36 | Change in WC = +64 (WC declining → cash inflow) | Capex = –55
FCFF = 185 + 36 + 64 − 55 = 230 EUR million
FCFF vs. FCFE: FCFF = free cash flow to all capital providers (debt + equity). Used in enterprise value DCF. FCFE = FCFF − after-tax interest + net borrowings. Used in equity value DCF. FCFF is more commonly used in CFA context as it is unaffected by financing decisions.
Range of outcomes: Small changes in key assumptions (organic growth, EBIT margin, working capital %) produce a very wide range of FCFF estimates — highlighting why scenario analysis is critical (Rémy: base case $230M vs. Alt Scenario 1 $318M vs. Alt Scenario 2 $240M in FY2022E alone).
ROIC — The Competitive Advantage Metric

ROIC = Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT) ÷ (Operating Assets − Operating Liabilities). NOPAT = EBIT × (1 − tax rate). High and persistent ROIC is a strong indicator of competitive advantage (durable moat). Companies earning ROIC above their cost of capital create value; those below destroy it. Porter's five forces analysis helps explain WHY a company earns a particular level of ROIC.

Behavioral Biases in Analyst Forecasting

Experts in many fields make systematic forecasting errors arising from behavioral biases. Investment analysts are not immune. Studies show that 90% confidence intervals for forecasts are wrong as much as 40% of the time (vs. an expected 10% error rate). Knowing the five key biases — and their mitigants — is directly testable.

① Overconfidence Bias

Definition: Unwarranted faith in one's own forecasting ability. Manifests as overly narrow confidence intervals and excessive certainty in point forecasts. More common when making contrarian predictions (predicting what others don't expect).

Mitigant: Record forecasts and review them regularly — compare actual vs. predicted. Use scenario analysis ("where could I be wrong and by how much?") to widen confidence intervals and acknowledge uncertainty. Recognizing that forecast error rates are inherently high is the first step.

② Illusion of Control

Definition: A subset of overconfidence. Tendency to overestimate one's ability to control what cannot be controlled. In modeling, this manifests as building overly complex, highly granular models with data that isn't regularly disclosed — believing that more data and complexity equals more accuracy.

Why it fails: (1) Undisclosed data cannot be back-checked vs. actuals. (2) Complex models are overfitted to history and fail in novel environments. (3) Complexity buries key assumptions. (4) Opportunity cost — time modeling one company means fewer companies analyzed.

Mitigant: Restrict modeling variables to regularly disclosed data. Focus on the most impactful variables. Speak only with those likely to have unique/significant perspectives. Simpler, flexible models are often more robust.

③ Conservatism Bias (Anchoring & Adjustment)

Definition: Maintaining prior views by inadequately incorporating new information. Analysts use their previous forecast as an "anchor" and adjust too little — the updated forecast stays too close to the prior one even when evidence strongly warrants a larger change. Also called anchoring and adjustment.

Common form: Reluctance to lower estimates after disappointing results or competitor actions. Can also work in reverse — reluctance to raise estimates after positive surprises.

Mitigant: Regular team reviews of forecasts (e.g., quarterly). Build flexible models with fewer variables — easier to update when assumptions need to change. Mitigating overconfidence and illusion of control also reduces conservatism bias.

④ Representativeness Bias (Base-Rate Neglect)

Definition: Classifying new information based on superficial resemblance to past patterns, while ignoring the broader statistical base rate. Specifically: base-rate neglect is focusing on company-specific ("inside view") factors while ignoring industry-wide ("outside view") norms.

Inside view: Company-specific factors (specific product types, salesforce size, customer relationships).
Outside view: Industry or sector averages for gross margin, R&D%, operating margin, etc.

Best practice: Neither view is superior alone. Start with the base rate (outside view / industry averages), then adjust for specific factors that make the target company genuinely different. E.g., start with spirits industry average EBIT margin of 26%, then adjust for Rémy-specific factors (premium positioning, cognac focus, geographic mix).

⑤ Confirmation Bias

Definition: Tendency to seek out, notice, and weight information that confirms prior beliefs while ignoring or discounting contradictory evidence. In practice: structuring the research process to only hear positive views, speaking only to management (who are inherently optimistic) and like-minded analysts.

Classic example: 2007 annual report of a major European bank (published months before nationalization) used "good" 55 times, "strong/stronger/strongly" 78 times, "success/successful" 35 times — and "disappoint" 0 times, "bad" 0 times, "poor" 0 times.

Mitigants: (1) Seek out and read research from analysts with a negative view on the security. (2) Consult colleagues who are not economically or psychologically invested. (3) Be aware that management commentary will almost always be optimistic — weight it accordingly.

Examiner Focus — Bias Identification in Item Sets

Exam questions will describe an analyst behavior and ask you to identify the bias. Key distinguishing features:

Overconfidence = too-narrow range, excessive certainty. Illusion of control = more data/complexity = better (wrong). Conservatism = not updating enough after new info (anchor effect). Representativeness = ignoring industry base rates, classifying by superficial resemblance. Confirmation = only speaking to optimistic sources, ignoring contrary evidence. Conservatism and Confirmation are distinct: conservatism is about insufficient UPDATE; confirmation is about selective SEARCH.

Porter's Five Forces & Competitive Impact on Prices and Costs

Porter's five forces framework helps analysts assess the long-run profit potential of an industry by identifying how competitive forces divide the economic value created. Understanding a company's position within its industry allows analysts to estimate whether revenue growth and profit margins will be relatively high or low vs. history and vs. peers. Note: government/regulation is NOT a sixth force — it is best understood by analyzing how government policies affect the five existing forces.

The Five Forces

① Threat of Substitutes

High threat: Customers can easily switch to alternatives → limits pricing power, compresses margins. Low threat: Brand loyalty, unique product, no comparable alternatives → supports premium pricing and stable volumes. Example: Cognac consumers show high brand loyalty → Low threat → supports Rémy's pricing power.

② Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

High rivalry: Fragmented market, declining volumes, low differentiation → price wars → margin compression. Low rivalry: Concentrated oligopoly, stable or growing demand, differentiated products → price discipline, stable margins. UK beer: High rivalry (fragmented, declining volumes). Brazil beer: Low rivalry (AmBev 65% share, volume growth).

③ Bargaining Power of Suppliers

High supplier power: Concentrated suppliers, unique inputs, switching costs → cost inflation risk. Low supplier power: Many small suppliers, commodity inputs, easy substitution → company can push back on input cost increases. Cognac: many small independent vineyards and distillers → Low/medium supplier power. Beer: barley/hops/water = commodities → Low supplier power for both Brazil and UK.

④ Bargaining Power of Buyers

High buyer power: Concentrated customer base (e.g., dominant supermarket chains), private labels available, undifferentiated product → price pressure on producers. Low buyer power: Fragmented retail, specialty outlets, consumers willing to pay premium → pricing power retained. UK beer: High buyer power (Tesco, Sainsbury's, Walmart negotiate hard). Brazil beer: Low buyer power (fragmented supermarkets, small bar/restaurant buyers). Cognac: Low buyer power (retail wine shops don't coordinate purchasing).

⑤ Threat of New Entrants

High barriers to entry: Capital intensity, brand heritage requirements, regulatory constraints, distribution network → Low threat → incumbents retain market position. Low barriers: Easy entry → more competition over time → margin pressure. Cognac: production is geographically regulated, requires aging inventory (massive working capital), brand heritage essential → Very low threat of new entrants.

MarketSubstitutesRivalrySupplier PowerBuyer PowerNew EntrantsProfit Outlook
Cognac (Rémy)LowLowLow/MedLowLowHighly favorable — supports high ROIC
Brazil Beer (AmBev)MediumLowLowLowLowFavorable — 50% EBITDA margin

Porter's Forces → ROIC Link

There is no mechanical formula linking Porter's analysis to specific revenue growth or margin numbers — the link is probabilistic and judgmental. A company with all five forces favorable might be forecast to have above-average margins, but the exact margin requires additional analysis. The forces framework informs the assumptions, not mechanically determines them.

Modeling Inflation & Deflation

Inflation and deflation affect revenue and costs differently — and often at different rates even within a single company. The key analytical question is: can the company pass through cost increases to customers without losing volume? The answer depends on price elasticity of demand, industry structure, competitive dynamics, and strategy.

Inflation Impact on Revenue (Sales Projections)

Industry-Level Revenue & Inflation
  • Oligopoly with fragmented buyers (US beer, three-tier distribution) → can pass through cost increases → prices rise with CPI → revenue holds up
  • Concentrated buyers / fragmented producers (European beer, dominant supermarkets) → buyers push back → prices rise ~100bps below CPI → revenue squeezed
  • Industry structure is the primary determinant of pricing power
Company-Level Revenue & Inflation
  • Depends on price elasticity of demand for the specific product
  • Inelastic demand → price increases pass through cleanly → revenue benefits
  • Elastic demand → price increases reduce volume → revenue may fall even if unit prices rise
  • Consider geographic mix — different countries have different inflation rates → adjust pricing assumption per geography
  • Strategy matters: pass-through vs. absorb cost (sacrifice margin to gain market share)
Price–Volume Trade-Off in Inflation
If price raised 10% to offset 10% cost increase AND volume unchanged:
→ Gross margin % same, but absolute gross profit HIGHER (on higher revenue base)

If price raised 10% but volume falls (elastic demand):
→ Depends on elasticity: Revenue = Price × Volume
→ If elasticity > 1 (elastic): Revenue FALLS even as price rises
→ If elasticity < 1 (inelastic): Revenue RISES when price rises

Unilever example: Input prices surged in 2008 → large price increases → volume deteriorated significantly. Input prices fell 2009–2010 → prices lowered → volumes recovered strongly. Classic price-volume trade-off.
Key regression insight (UK food): For every 1pp increase in food price inflation → volume declines approximately 0.4%. This kind of historical regression gives a concrete empirical basis for modeling the volume impact of expected price changes.

Inflation Impact on Costs

Cost inflation analysis requires breaking the cost structure into categories and geographies:

  • Commodity inputs: Subject to market prices — weather (grain harvests), oil prices (packaging). Monitor commodity markets and long-term supply contracts.
  • Long-term fixed-price contracts and hedges: Delay the impact of input price changes. Industry that customarily hedges will see cost inflation slowly (company has time to react with price increases). Model by spreading the cost impact over the contract/hedge horizon.
  • Input substitution: Some companies can substitute cheaper inputs without affecting product quality (e.g., JDE coffee blending different bean types). This reduces exposure to spot price volatility for any single commodity.
  • Vertical integration: Companies that own their supply chain are less exposed to third-party input price volatility.
  • Short-term cost lever — A&P spending: Cutting advertising and promotional spend can offset rising COGS in the short term to protect margins, but risks weakening brand equity in the longer term.
⚠ High Inflation in Export Markets — FX Offsett Effect

High inflation in a company's export market implies that country's currency will likely weaken over time (purchasing power parity). So pricing gains from high local inflation get partially or fully offset by FX losses on translation. AB InBev Latin America South (Argentina, 2011): 24.7% organic revenue growth, of which only 2.1% was volume and the rest was price. No negative currency impact that year — but analysts treated this as a positive surprise, not a sustainable baseline. Most analysts normalize to a constant-currency growth rate after 1–2 years to avoid projecting inflation-driven revenue growth indefinitely.

Forecast Horizon & Long-Term Forecasting

The choice of forecast horizon is not arbitrary. It depends on: investment strategy time frame, industry cyclicality, company-specific factors (recent M&A, restructuring), and the analyst's employer's parameters. The forecast period should ideally be long enough for the business to reach a normalized, mid-cycle level of profitability.

Factors Determining the Forecast Horizon

FactorImplication for Horizon Length
Investment strategy time frameStated holding period (e.g., 3–5 years) → annual portfolio turnover 20–33%. Horizon should match strategy.
Industry cyclicalityCyclical industries need longer horizon to capture a full cycle and reach mid-cycle profitability
Recent M&A or restructuringExtend horizon enough to see benefits/synergies realized in financial statements
Employer/institutional preferenceSometimes fixed (e.g., always 3 years); analyst has no discretion

Normalized Earnings

Normalized earnings = the expected level of mid-cycle earnings in the absence of unusual or temporary factors. Temporary factors that distort current earnings include: stage of business cycle, recent M&A (integration charges), restructuring charges, and one-time items. Similarly, normalized free cash flow = mid-cycle CFO adjusted for unusual items minus recurring capex.

A longer forecast period allows the analyst to "see through" these temporary distortions and arrive at a sustainable earnings level. The terminal value in a DCF model should be based on normalized, mid-cycle cash flow — not a boom-year or trough-year figure.

Terminal Value Considerations

Why Terminal Value Matters So Much

Most DCF models use a perpetuity for terminal value: TV = FCFT × (1+g) ÷ (WACC−g). Because the perpetuity captures all value after the explicit forecast period, it often represents 60–80% of total enterprise value. A small change in the terminal growth rate has a disproportionate impact on valuation. Using a boom-year FCF → grossly overstated valuation. Using a trough-year FCF → grossly understated.

Long-Term Growth Rate Considerations
  • Cannot exceed the long-run GDP growth rate of the economy (a company can't grow faster than the economy forever)
  • Some companies can sustain above-GDP growth for extended periods (tech, emerging market leaders)
  • Some mature or declining industries grow below GDP (print media, traditional retail)
  • Product innovation and market expansion can extend above-average growth (Apple example)
  • Technology disruption can suddenly end above-average growth (Eastman Kodak example)

Inflection Points — The Greatest Forecasting Challenge

⚠ Anticipating Inflection Points

An inflection point is when the future will look significantly different from the recent past — when trend-based forecasting completely fails. The three main sources are:

1. Economic disruption: Sudden, unprecedented events (2008 GFC, COVID-19 pandemic). Even well-run companies with sound strategies can be thrown off course, especially those with high financial leverage.

2. Regulation: Government actions can have extreme, sudden, and unpredictable impacts. Affects utilities (intense regulation, slow tech change) and pharma (both heavy regulation AND technology) more than others.

3. Technology: Fast-growing innovators can become obsolete in months. Semiconductor companies must constantly keep up; utilities may not see significant tech change for decades. Classic example: Eastman Kodak — dominant in film photography, destroyed by digital cameras despite being the company that actually invented the digital camera.

Normalizing Revenue — Continental AG Method

For cyclical companies, use regression-based trend revenue as the normalized estimate. Plot 10+ years of actual revenue, run a linear regression (OLS), and use the fitted trend line as normalized revenue. Actual revenue above trend = above-cycle; below trend = below-cycle. Terminal value should be based on normalized (trend) revenue, not the last actual year if it's at a cyclical peak or trough.

═══ WORKING CAPITAL FROM DAYS RATIOS ═══
AR = (DSO ÷ 365) × Revenue
Inv = (DOH ÷ 365) × COGS
AP = (DPO ÷ 365) × COGS
WC increasing → Cash USED (negative in CFO) | WC decreasing → Cash PROVIDED (positive in CFO)

═══ FCFF ═══
FCFF = After-Tax EBIT + D&A + Change in WC − Capex
After-Tax EBIT = EBIT × (1 − effective tax rate)
ROIC = NOPAT ÷ (Operating Assets − Operating Liabilities) — high persistent ROIC = competitive advantage

═══ BEHAVIORAL BIASES & MITIGANTS ═══
Overconfidence → Scenario analysis; track forecast errors
Illusion of control → Limit to disclosed variables; simpler models
Conservatism → Regular team reviews; flexible, easy-to-update models
Representativeness → Combine inside view + outside view (base rate); start with industry avg, adjust
Confirmation → Seek negative opinion analysts; consult uninvested colleagues

═══ PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ═══
① Threat of substitutes ② Rivalry ③ Supplier power
④ Buyer power ⑤ Threat of new entrants
All Low → high long-run profit potential. Government is NOT a 6th force.
Link to forecasts: PROBABILISTIC, not mechanical.

═══ INFLATION KEY RULES ═══
Pricing power depends on: price elasticity + industry structure + strategy
Oligopoly/fragmented buyers → can pass through → revenue tracks costs
Concentrated buyers → price below inflation → margin squeeze
High export market inflation → currency weakens → FX offsets pricing gains → normalize after 1–2 years
Terminal value must be NORMALIZED (mid-cycle) — not peak, not trough
Top exam traps for Module 12:
(1) Organic growth = multiplicative: (1+Vol)(1+Price/Mix) − 1, not Vol + Price/Mix. The difference matters for exam calculations.
(2) Working capital INCREASING = cash USED (negative in CFS). Students often get the sign wrong. WC release = cash inflow.
(3) Forecast other operating expenses/restructuring = ZERO unless specific evidence of known transaction.
(4) Conservatism bias = insufficient updating after new info (anchoring). Confirm bias = selective search for confirming evidence. These are different and both testable.
(5) Representativeness best practice = start with industry base rate (outside view), then adjust for company-specific factors — neither view alone is best.
(6) Porter's analysis → probabilistic link to margins, not mechanical. Don't state "low rivalry = X% EBIT margin."
(7) Government is NOT a 6th force. Government affects the 5 forces — don't call it one.
(8) Terminal value must be normalized. Boom year → overstatement. Trough year → understatement. Especially important for cyclical industries.
(9) High inflation in export markets → currency depreciation will offset pricing gains over time. The AB InBev Argentina case was a positive surprise, not a repeatable pattern.
