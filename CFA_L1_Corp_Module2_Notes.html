Debt vs. Equity — Nature of Claims

Both debt and equity investors finance a corporation's assets, but they hold fundamentally different claims on those assets and cash flows. Understanding this distinction is the foundation of corporate finance analysis.

Debt (Debtholders / Lenders)
  • Finite maturity — has an end date
  • Fixed, promised payments — interest + principal on pre-specified dates
  • Priority claim — paid before any equity distributions
  • No voting/decision-making rights in the corporation
  • Can impose covenants (financial requirements, asset claims) in debt contracts
  • Interest payments are tax-deductible to the issuer
  • In liquidation: priority claim on assets
Equity (Shareholders)
  • Permanent capital — no maturity, no repayment obligation
  • Residual claim — whatever is left after all expenses, investments, debt payments
  • No promise of dividends — distributions at board of directors' discretion
  • Voting rights on important matters (board composition, mergers, liquidation)
  • Limited liability — max loss = amount invested
  • Dividends are NOT tax-deductible to the issuer
  • In liquidation: residual claim — last in line
Priority of Claims — Liquidation Waterfall
1st → Secured Creditors (backed by specific assets)
2nd → Unsecured Creditors / Bondholders
3rd → Suppliers, Employees, Tax (governments)
Last → Equity Shareholders (residual — may get nothing)
Remember: In liquidation, equity is ALWAYS last. Shareholders lose their entire investment if debtholder claims consume all remaining assets.
Key Structural Difference — Tax Treatment
Debt interest → Tax-deductible expense → reduces taxable income (creates a "tax shield")
Equity dividends → NOT tax-deductible → paid from after-tax profits
This tax shield is a key reason why firms prefer some degree of debt in their capital structure. Known as the "interest tax shield."
⚠ Exam Trap — Perspective Inversion on Risk

The risk perspective flips depending on whose viewpoint you take:
From the ISSUER's perspective: Debt is RISKIER than equity (committed fixed payments → bankruptcy risk if unpaid).
From the INVESTOR's perspective: Equity is RISKIER than debt (residual/uncertain returns vs. fixed/promised returns).
This is one of the most commonly tested distinctions in this module.

02

Risk & Return Asymmetry — Debt vs. Equity

Shareholder Payoff Profile

Shareholder value = max(V − D, 0) where V = firm value, D = value of debt.

  • If V > D (solvent): Shareholders receive the positive difference (V − D). Upside is theoretically unlimited as firm value grows.
  • If V < D (insolvent): Shareholders lose entire investment. Debtholders take control of firm assets.
  • Due to limited liability, shareholders cannot lose MORE than their initial investment — no personal liability for corporate debts.
Shareholder Payoff Function
Shareholder Payoff = max(V − D, 0)
V > D → Solvent → Shareholders earn (V − D) → Unlimited upside potential
V < D → Insolvent → Shareholders earn 0 → Entire investment lost
This asymmetry (capped downside, unlimited upside) is why shareholders PREFER riskier projects.

Debtholder Payoff Profile

  • If V > D (solvent): Debtholders receive exactly promised interest + principal — no more, regardless of how well the firm performs.
  • If V < D (insolvent): Debtholders recover what they can from remaining assets — may suffer losses.
  • Capped upside (limited to promised payment) + downside risk in insolvency.
Total Firm Value Identity
Firm Value (V) = Debtholder Payoff + Shareholder Payoff
Adding the shareholder payoff area and the debtholder payoff area always equals the total firm value diagonal. The two claims together account for 100% of firm value.

Financial Leverage Effect

Financial leverage = the greater use of debt relative to equity in the capital structure.

  • More leverage → amplifies equity returns in good times (shareholders keep larger % of a growing firm)
  • More leverage → amplifies equity risk in bad times (fixed debt obligations increase insolvency probability)
  • Leverage increases risk for both equity AND debt investors, but in different ways
  • Equity holders often prefer debt financing over new equity issuance to avoid dilution
Dilution
When a company issues additional shares, the fractional ownership of existing shareholders is reduced. This reduces earnings per share and the share of future profits each existing shareholder receives. Dilution may be offset if the new capital generates sufficient incremental profit.
💡 Memory Trick — The Leverage Knife

Think of financial leverage as a double-edged knife. It cuts both ways: it amplifies gains for shareholders when the firm does well (they keep more of a bigger pie), but it amplifies losses when the firm struggles (fixed debt payments must still be met from a shrinking pie). This is why increasing leverage makes equity riskier while debt investors dislike it too.

Risk/Return Summary Table

Dimension Debt (Bondholder) Equity (Shareholder)

Priority Senior (paid first) Junior (paid last)
Upside Potential Capped at promised payment Unlimited
Downside Risk Partial loss in insolvency Total loss of investment

03

Conflicts of Interest — Lenders vs. Shareholders

Both lenders and shareholders are paid from the same pool of corporate cash flows, but their different payoff profiles create structural conflicts of interest.

What Shareholders Prefer
  • Management pursues higher-risk, higher-return projects (limited downside due to limited liability; unlimited upside)
  • Maximum use of debt (leverage amplifies equity returns)
  • Higher cash dividends (even if it increases leverage)
  • Growth strategies, even if they increase insolvency risk
What Bondholders Prefer
  • Management invests in lower-risk, stable cash flow projects (ensures debt repayment)
  • Conservative leverage — limited debt usage
  • Cash retained to service debt, not paid as dividends
  • Contractual protections (covenants) imposed at inception

How Bondholders Protect Themselves

Since bondholders have no voting rights over management decisions, they rely on contractual protections (debt covenants) embedded in debt agreements:

  • Coverage requirements: Minimum cash flow coverage ratios for debt payments
  • Leverage limits: Cap on how much additional debt the firm can take on
  • Asset restriction covenants: Legal claims on specific assets if debt is unpaid
  • Dividend restrictions: Limit on cash dividends paid to shareholders

These restrictions prevent the firm from taking actions that benefit shareholders at the expense of debtholders' likelihood of repayment.

💡 The Core Conflict in One Sentence

Shareholders have "heads I win, tails I lose less" — they capture unlimited upside but limited downside (limited liability). Bondholders have "heads you keep your promise, tails I suffer" — they get no benefit from upside but bear downside risk from insolvency. This asymmetry is the root cause of all debtholder-shareholder conflicts.

⚠ Exam Trap — Debt Is Cheaper But Riskier for the Issuer

Debt financing has a lower cost to the issuer (lower interest rate than required equity return, plus tax deductibility of interest). But debt is riskier for the issuer because it creates committed obligations — failure to pay triggers bankruptcy. Never confuse "cheaper" with "safer" from the issuer's perspective.

04

Corporate Stakeholder Groups

Stakeholder
Any individual or group with a vested interest in a company — not just investors. The company depends on stakeholders, and stakeholders depend on the company, for economic success. Their short- and long-term goals may be in conflict.
Stakeholder Primary Interest / Goal Key Powers / Tools Potential Conflicts
Shareholders (Equity) Maximize firm value; residual cash flows; capital gains + dividends Voting rights; replace board of directors vs. bondholders (risk-taking); vs. managers (agency problem)
Debtholders / Lenders Timely repayment of interest + principal; cash flow stability Debt covenants; collateral; credit rating threat; restructuring leverage vs. shareholders (leverage and risk preferences)
Board of Directors Oversee management; advance shareholder interests; ensure compliance Hire/fire CEO; approve strategy; set executive compensation May entrench management; staggered boards limit shareholder power
Managers (CEO + Exec Team) Execute strategy; run operations; own job security + compensation Operational control; strategy formulation vs. shareholders (agency problem — may prioritize self-interest)
Employees Competitive pay, job security, safe conditions, development Labor unions; collective bargaining; productivity leverage vs. shareholders if cost-cutting affects workers
Customers Quality products/services at fair prices; safety; after-sales support Brand boycotts; switching to competitors; regulatory complaints Product safety vs. cost reduction; data privacy vs. business model
Suppliers Timely payment; long-term relationships; order certainty Withhold supply; extend/refuse credit; renegotiate terms Financial distress of issuer threatens supplier's own finances
Governments Tax revenue; economic output; employment; regulatory compliance; environmental standards Laws, regulations, fines, licenses, tax policy Compliance costs for companies; stricter ESG regulation
Society / Environment Minimize negative externalities; protect natural environment; community wellbeing Public pressure; NGO activism; reputational risk; litigation Environmental costs historically treated as externalities not borne by firms

Private Debtholders vs. Public Bondholders

Private Debtholders (Banks, Lessors)
  • Often hold debt to maturity
  • Direct access to management + non-public information
  • Lower information asymmetry
  • Single lender can be critical financing source (especially for small/mid-sized firms)
  • Greater influence — can refuse credit extension or relax restrictions
  • Wider variation in risk appetite and behavior
  • Some specialize in distressed/bankruptcy lending
Public Bondholders (Institutional Investors)
  • Rely on public information (financial statements)
  • Usually little to no influence over operations
  • Rely on debt contract terms negotiated at inception
  • More difficult to gain consent for changing existing agreement terms
  • Can exercise significant influence if firm must restructure public debt in distress
💡 Why Private Lenders Have More Power Over Small Firms

If a small company has only ONE bank as its lender, that bank's decision to extend or withdraw credit can make or break the business. A single private lender thus has far more leverage over a small firm than over a large corporation with access to multiple capital market financing options. This is why debt covenants from private lenders are often more restrictive for small companies.

05

Board of Directors — Structure & Governance

The board of directors is elected by shareholders to advance shareholders' interests by supervising corporate activities. Key responsibilities:

  • Hire the CEO and senior management
  • Monitor company and management performance
  • Set and oversee strategic direction
  • Ensure compliance with laws and governance standards
  • Set executive compensation (to align management with shareholder interests)

Director Types

Inside Directors
  • Founders, current or former managers
  • Deep knowledge of the business
  • May have conflicts of interest (e.g., entrench existing management)
  • Less likely to challenge management decisions
Independent Directors
  • No material relationship with company (no employment, no family ties)
  • Better represent minority shareholders
  • Most exchanges require a minimum proportion of independent directors
  • London Stock Exchange: ≥ 50% independent
  • Singapore Exchange: majority of non-executive directors

Single-Tier vs. Two-Tier Board Structures

Single-Tier Board (USA, UK)
  • One unified board responsible for both strategy and oversight
  • Includes inside + independent directors
  • Most common in Anglo-Saxon markets
Two-Tier Board (Germany, Continental Europe)
  • Managing Board — responsible for strategy and operations
  • Supervisory Board — oversees managing board; can appoint/dismiss board members; must approve key decisions
  • Supervisory board includes shareholders, employees, labor unions, public, government reps
  • Legally mandated in some countries (e.g., Germany)

Staggered Boards

Instead of electing ALL directors simultaneously, staggered boards elect directors in groups over consecutive years.

Disadvantage: Takes several years to replace a full board → limits shareholders' ability to effect rapid change of control → a form of entrenchment.

Advantage: Provides continuity and strategic consistency; avoids disruption from constant reassessment by new board members → reduces short-termism risk.

Common in: Australia, several European countries.

⚠ Exam Trap — Staggered Boards Are a Double-Edged Sword

Staggered boards reduce shareholder power (harder to replace bad management quickly) but also reduce short-termism (management doesn't need to worry about an immediate takeover). The CFA curriculum presents both sides — don't classify them as purely negative.

Shareholder vs. Stakeholder Theory of Corporate Governance

Shareholder Theory

Corporate governance exists to serve the interests of shareholders. All other parties' interests are considered only to the extent they affect shareholder value.

Associated with Milton Friedman (1970): "The Social Responsibility of Business Is to Increase Its Profits."

Stakeholder Theory

Corporate governance should consider ALL stakeholder interests — not just shareholders. ESG considerations should be explicit board objectives.

Challenges: complexity of balancing objectives; difficulty measuring non-shareholder goals; competitive disadvantage if rivals don't face same constraints; direct ESG compliance costs.

💡 Key Insight — They Aren't Necessarily at Odds

Friedman himself acknowledged that long-term shareholder value can align with stakeholder interests. Example: A firm providing community amenities attracts better employees, reduces wage costs, and reduces theft/sabotage — all benefiting shareholders in the long run. ESG is increasingly viewed as a long-term value creation issue, not just a cost.

07

ESG — Overview & Importance

ESG = Environmental Social Governance — a framework for evaluating non-traditional risk and value drivers of corporate issuers, now standard in investment analysis.

3 Reasons ESG Importance Has Grown

  1. Material financial impact has risen. Both shareholders AND debtholders have suffered substantial losses from environmental disasters, social controversies, and governance failures. ESG is no longer abstract — it moves stock prices and credit ratings.
  2. Growing investor demand. Particularly among younger clients who demand that newly acquired/inherited wealth and pension contributions be managed with ESG considerations in mind.
  3. Regulatory pressure. Governments increasingly prioritize climate change and social policies → revised regulations forcing corporate issuers to adapt business practices to meet more stringent ESG criteria.
Negative Externality
A cost created by a company but borne by third parties (society, environment) rather than by the company itself. Historically, environmental and social costs like pollution and air quality were externalities. Increased stakeholder awareness and regulation are forcing companies to internalize these costs — i.e., record them in their income statements.
ESG Factor Materiality Test
An ESG factor is MATERIAL when it is believed to have a SIGNIFICANT impact on a company's results or business model.
Not all ESG factors are material for all companies. Materiality varies greatly by industry. A coal miner faces material environmental risk; a software company faces material social (data privacy) risk.

ESG Factors Quick Reference

🌍 Environmental

Conservation of the natural world

  • Climate change
  • Air and water pollution
  • Biodiversity
  • Deforestation
  • Energy efficiency
  • Waste management
  • Water scarcity
👥 Social

Consideration of people and relationships

  • Customer satisfaction
  • Data protection & privacy
  • Gender and diversity
  • Employee engagement
  • Community relations
  • Human rights
  • Labor standards
🏛 Governance

Standards for running a company

  • Board composition
  • Audit committee structure
  • Bribery and corruption
  • Executive compensation
  • Lobbying
  • Political contributions
  • Whistleblower schemes
💡 Why Governance Is Easiest to Evaluate

Governance factors (voting rights, board composition, compensation) are widely available and quantifiable → relatively straightforward to evaluate. Environmental and social factors have evolved more slowly into investment analysis because identifying which factors are material, and when they will impact performance, is less clear. This is a key nuance tested in CFA exams.

08

Environmental Factors in Depth

Environmental materiality varies significantly across industries. For resource-intensive firms (mining, energy, agriculture), environmental factors often have a direct, material effect on operations. For others, the impact may be material but indirect.

Climate Change Risk — Two Types

Physical Risk
  • Damage to / destruction of assets by severe weather events
  • Expected to increase in frequency as climate changes
  • Can often be insured against or diversified
  • Examples: floods, hurricanes destroying factories or infrastructure
Transition Risk
  • Losses from the transition to a lower-carbon economy
  • Can result from new regulations or shifting consumer demand
  • Example: coal producer's revenue falls as utilities switch to renewables
  • Creates risk of stranded assets
  • Harder to insure or diversify vs. physical risk
Stranded Assets
Emission-intensive reserve assets (e.g., oil wells, coal reserves) at risk of becoming unviable before their useful life ends — due to new regulations or uneconomical prices caused by the energy transition. Even if an asset could produce from 2029 to 2059, early cessation may be forced, reducing its value. Difficult for analysts to assess due to regulatory and price uncertainties.

Emissions, Waste, and the "License to Operate"

Environmental mismanagement can result in:

  • Regulatory fines
  • Litigation costs
  • Clean-up expenses
  • Reputational risk
  • In extreme cases: permanent loss of license to operate
📋 Real-World Case — Vale Dam Disaster (Brazil, 2019)

Collapse of the Córrego do Feijão Mine dam → millions of tons of mud spilled → 270 lives lost → Paraopeba River contaminated. Vale accused of hiding dam instability for years. Executives and auditor charged with murder and environmental crimes. Vale fined millions by Brazilian government + agreed to pay USD 7 billion to victims' families in 2021. Share price crashed. Credit rating downgraded. Demonstrates how environmental mismanagement creates catastrophic financial + legal consequences.

Social Factors in Depth

Social factors concern a firm's practices and their impacts on employees, customers, and communities. Key social metrics include compensation, turnover, worker health, training, safety, diversity, data privacy, and community relations.

Why Social Factors Have Material Financial Impact

Minimizing social risk can lower costs and raise revenues through:

  • Higher employee productivity
  • Lower employee turnover
  • Reduced litigation potential
  • Reduced reputational risk
  • Brand loyalty from responsible products/services

Data Privacy and Security — A Key Social Risk

Addresses how companies gather, use, and secure personally identifiable information (PII) and metadata. Issues include:

  • Risk of theft or misuse of personal data
  • In internet/software industries: government requests may violate civil/political rights
  • Average cost of a data breach: USD 3.9 million (IBM/Ponemon Institute, 2019)
📋 Real-World Case — Equifax Data Breach (2017)

Lax cybersecurity at Equifax (US credit agency) → theft of identity/financial data of over 140 million US citizens. Equifax incurred hundreds of millions in expenses, faced numerous lawsuits and investigations. Demonstrates how social risk (data security) directly translates into financial losses.

📋 Real-World Case — Facebook / Cambridge Analytica (2018)

Facebook shared personal data of 80+ million users with a third-party consultancy without consent. Data used to psychologically profile users and influence US elections. Result: USD 5 billion government fine (one of the largest in tech sector). Significant decline in user trust. Company rebranded as Meta Platforms in 2021 — partly to distance from social/privacy controversies.

10

Governance Factors in Depth

Governance is the most quantifiable and directly analyzable ESG pillar. Key governance areas assessed by analysts:

  • Company ownership and voting structure
  • Relevance of board skills and experience to current/future company needs
  • Alignment of management compensation with company results
  • Strength of shareholder rights vs. peers
  • Company effectiveness in managing long-term risks and sustainability

Sources: proxy statements, annual reports, sustainability reports.

📋 Real-World Case — Siemens AG Bribery Scandal (2006–2008)

German police raided Siemens HQ → uncovered one of the largest corporate corruption cases in history. Bribery of foreign government officials was standard operating procedure for decades. Total bribery payments exceeded EUR 1 billion. Result: EUR 3+ billion in fines, disgorgement of profits, and other costs. Hundreds of employees dismissed. Both supervisory and managing board chairmen resigned. For the first time in 160 years, Siemens hired an external CEO. New governance policies: firmwide anti-corruption program; new board with operational insight; increased scrutiny of regional businesses. Demonstrates how governance failures create catastrophic, lasting financial and reputational damage.

💡 Governance as the Root of E and S Risks

The Vale dam disaster was traced to management hiding information about instability. The Siemens scandal reflected board failure to oversee regional operations. Poor governance is often the root cause of environmental and social failures too — not just a separate category. This is why analysts always start with governance when evaluating ESG risk.

Evaluating ESG Risks — Analytical Framework

ESG analysis process is similar for equity and credit (debt) analysts because both analyze the same underlying corporate cash flows — just with different claims on them.

How ESG Events Hit Equity vs. Debt Differently

ESG Event Impact Effect on Equity (Shareholders) Effect on Debt (Bondholders)
Significant adverse ESG event (short-term) Immediately and disproportionately affected — equity is residual, so it absorbs the first hit. Sharp share price decline (e.g., Vale, Equifax, Siemens). Less immediately affected than equity — fixed claims somewhat buffer short-term shocks. But cost of debt rises, credit rating downgraded.
Severe adverse ESG event (long-term) Long-term equity value destroyed if firm's ability to generate future cash flows is impaired. If ability to pay interest/principal is threatened → debtholders may force bankruptcy → potential losses in liquidation (Vale debt briefly cut to speculative grade).
Long-term risk (e.g., stranded assets) Long-dated equity heavily impacted by structural revenue decline. Long-maturity debt more affected than short-term debt (a coal company's 10-year bond is more exposed to stranded asset risk than its 12-month paper).

How Analysts Quantify ESG Factors

  1. Identify material ESG factors for the specific company and industry.
  2. Quantify financial impact — how do they affect projected cash flows (positively or negatively)?
  3. Adjust financial statements and ratios — reflect expected cash flow changes in forecasts.
  4. Adjust discount rate — higher ESG risk may raise the discount rate; lower ESG risk (competitive advantage) may lower it.
  5. Sensitivity / scenario analysis — weigh different outcomes for debt and equity holders under different ESG scenarios.
ESG Analytical Examples from the Module
Example 1 (Social Risk → Cost Increase):
High employee turnover at a hotel → analyst INCREASES forecast operating costs (lost productivity, higher search/training/temp worker costs, reduced customer satisfaction)

Example 2 (Environmental Opportunity → Lower Risk):
Food company transitions to sustainable ingredient source → analyst LOWERS discount rate (competitive advantage + reduced supply/regulatory risk)
ESG affects both the numerator (cash flows) AND the denominator (discount rate) of a DCF valuation.
⚠ Exam Note — ESG Is Not Just a Risk, It Can Be an Opportunity

Don't frame ESG purely as a risk mitigation exercise. Positive ESG factors can create competitive advantages: sustainable sourcing, better employee retention, stronger brand loyalty, lower regulatory risk, and access to ESG-focused capital. Analysts must evaluate both positive AND negative potential effects of material ESG factors.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Debt = finite, fixed, priority claim. Equity = permanent, residual, discretionary claim. Equity is paid LAST in all scenarios.
  • ✅ Risk perspective inversion: From ISSUER's view → debt is riskier (committed payments → bankruptcy risk). From INVESTOR's view → equity is riskier (residual/uncertain vs. fixed).
  • ✅ Interest on debt is tax-deductible (creates a tax shield). Dividends on equity are NOT tax-deductible.
  • ✅ Shareholder payoff = max(V − D, 0). Unlimited upside; total loss if insolvent. Debtholder payoff = capped at promised amount; loss only in insolvency.
  • ✅ Dilution: New share issuance reduces fractional ownership of existing shareholders. Shareholders often prefer debt to avoid dilution.
  • ✅ Conflict summary: Shareholders want riskier projects + more leverage. Bondholders want safer projects + less leverage + covenants protecting them.
  • ✅ Bondholders protect themselves via debt covenants (coverage ratios, leverage limits, dividend restrictions, asset claims) — NOT voting rights (they have none).
  • ✅ Private lenders → direct management access, non-public info, hold to maturity, high influence on small firms. Public bondholders → rely on public info, rely on contract terms, significant influence only in restructuring.
  • ✅ Board: Elected by shareholders → hires CEO → oversees management. Inside directors = founders/managers. Independent directors = no material relationship; better for minority shareholders.
  • ✅ Two-tier board (Germany, Continental Europe): Managing Board (strategy/ops) + Supervisory Board (oversight, can appoint/dismiss board). Supervisory board includes employees, unions, public.
  • ✅ Staggered boards: Limit shareholder ability to effect rapid control change, but provide strategic continuity.
  • ✅ Shareholder theory: maximize shareholder value. Stakeholder theory: consider all stakeholder interests. Not mutually exclusive — long-term shareholder value often aligns with stakeholder interests.
  • ✅ ESG materiality: An ESG factor is material when it significantly impacts the company's results or business model. Materiality varies by industry.
  • ✅ 3 reasons ESG importance rose: (1) material financial losses proven (2) investor demand, especially younger clients (3) regulatory pressure / climate policy.
  • ✅ Environmental: Physical risk (asset damage from weather, can be insured) vs. Transition risk (regulatory/demand shift to low-carbon economy, harder to insure). Stranded assets = transition risk.
  • ✅ ESG adverse events: Hit equity immediately and disproportionately (residual claim absorbs first). Debt more cushioned but faces rising cost of debt + credit downgrades; in extremis, bankruptcy losses.
  • ✅ Maturity matters for ESG debt risk: Long-term debt more exposed to long-term ESG risks (stranded assets) than short-term debt.
  • ✅ ESG affects valuation both ways: Adjusts cash flow forecasts (numerator) AND discount rate (denominator) in DCF analysis.
  • ✅ Governance = most quantifiable ESG pillar. E and S factors harder to assess (timing and materiality less clear).
  • ✅ Key cases to remember: Vale (environmental, 2019 Brazil dam) · Equifax (social/data, 2017) · Facebook/Cambridge Analytica (social/data) · Siemens (governance/bribery, 2006).
Quick Summary — The Three Perspectives on ESG
E = Physical risks (weather damage) + Transition risks (regulation/demand shift) + Stranded assets
S = Employee issues + Customer issues (data privacy) + Community relations → affect productivity, litigation, brand
G = Board composition, compensation, voting rights, anti-corruption → root cause of many E and S failures

Impact on Equity: Immediate, disproportionate
Impact on Debt: Delayed, cushioned — but cost of debt rises + credit downgrade + potential bankruptcy
Always connect ESG factors back to CASH FLOWS and DISCOUNT RATES — that is how the CFA exam tests them.
