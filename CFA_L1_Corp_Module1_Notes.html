Organizational Forms — Overview

Every business chooses a legal organizational form that defines how returns, risks, ownership, and operational responsibilities are distributed. There are three general forms common to most jurisdictions:

  1. Sole Proprietorship — single owner, simplest form
  2. Partnership — multiple owners pool resources (3 subtypes)
  3. Limited Company (Corporation) — most sophisticated; two subtypes: private & public

5 Key Attributes That Differentiate Forms

Mnemonic: "LOLOT" — Remember All 5 Attributes
L — Legal identity (separate entity or not?)
O — Owner–manager relationship (same or separated?)
L — Liability (unlimited or limited?)
O — cOst / Taxation (pass-through or double taxed?)
T — access To financing (limited or broad?)
These 5 attributes are the exam lens for comparing ANY organizational form.
💡 Big Picture — Why This Matters for Analysts

The organizational form determines how profits are taxed, how much liability owners bear, how easy it is to raise capital, and ultimately how attractive a business is as an investment target. The corporation (public limited company) dominates globally by revenues and assets because it best solves the financing and liability problems of the other forms.

02

Sole Proprietorship (Sole Trader)

The simplest organizational form. One person owns, funds, and manages the business entirely.

  • Legal identity: NOT a separate legal entity — owner and business are the same person legally
  • Owner–manager: Identical — owner IS the manager
  • Liability: Unlimited — owner personally responsible for ALL debts and obligations
  • Taxation: Pass-through — profits taxed as personal income of the owner
  • Access to financing: Very limited — constrained by owner's personal savings and creditworthiness
  • Formation: Often default form requiring no formal registration; dissolved on owner's death or cessation
Advantages
  • Simplest and cheapest to form
  • Full management control retained by owner
  • All profits go to owner
  • No separate tax filing for entity
  • High flexibility
Disadvantages
  • Unlimited personal liability
  • Growth severely limited by financing ability
  • Business ends at owner's death
  • No separation of ownership and management
  • Cannot easily bring in external investors
⚠ Exam Trap

Sole proprietorships comprise the largest number of businesses in most economies, but they are the smallest in terms of revenue and assets. Don't confuse "most numerous" with "most significant" economically.

03

Partnerships

Partnerships allow multiple owners to pool resources, share risk, and share returns. There are three common subtypes.

Like sole proprietorships, all partnership forms are pass-through entities for tax purposes — no tax at the entity level; profits/losses flow to partners and are taxed personally (even if not distributed).

Type 1 — General Partnership (GP)

  • Partners: Two or more general partners (GPs)
  • Liability: Unlimited for ALL partners — if one partner can't pay, others are fully liable
  • Management: All partners share management responsibilities
  • Governance: Governed by a written (or verbal/incidental) partnership agreement
  • Taxation: Pass-through — taxed only at personal level
  • Financing: Limited to what partners can contribute

Type 2 — Limited Partnership (LP)

  • Structure: At least one General Partner (GP) + one or more Limited Partners (LPs)
  • GP liability: Unlimited — the GP manages and bears full risk
  • LP liability: Limited — losses capped at amount invested; personal assets protected
  • Management: GP manages; LPs typically have no management responsibilities
  • Profit sharing: All partners share per agreement; GPs typically receive a larger share for their greater risk
  • Taxation: Pass-through
  • Transferability: Often restricted by partnership agreement
💡 Memory Trick — LP Structure

Think: "GPs run it, GPs own it fully. LPs fund it, LPs hide behind it." The limited partner's liability is literally limited to what they put in — like a passive investor with a liability shield.

Type 3 — Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

  • Key distinction: No general partner required — ALL partners have limited liability
  • Management: Partners share management; typically appoint managing partners
  • Use case: Professional services firms (law, accounting, engineering, architecture)
  • Restrictions: In some jurisdictions (e.g., USA), only permitted for professional services; limits on partner count and equity investment
  • Taxation: Pass-through
⚠ Exam Trap — Pass-Through Taxation Timing

In ALL partnership forms, income is taxed at the personal level regardless of whether it was actually distributed. Partners pay tax on their share of profits even if the money stayed in the business and was reinvested. This is a key disadvantage vs. corporations.

04

Limited Companies (Corporations)

Limited companies offer all the advantages of limited partnerships plus additional features that enable much greater access to financing and growth. Two subtypes exist in most jurisdictions.

Private Limited Company

  • Liability: Limited for ALL owners (shareholders/members)
  • Ownership: Divided into shares (more easily tradeable than partnership interests)
  • Management: Owners (shareholders) elect a board of directors; board appoints professional managers
  • Restrictions: Legal limits on number of owners in many jurisdictions; ownership transfer often requires a vote
  • Taxation: Pass-through (like partnerships) in many jurisdictions — taxed only at shareholder level

Names by jurisdiction: LLC / S-Corp (USA) · GmbH (Germany) · SARL (France) · G.K. (Japan) · Company with Limited Liability (China)

Public Limited Company (Corporation)

  • Liability: Limited for all shareholders
  • Ownership restrictions: None — no legal limit on number of owners or transferability
  • Management: Full separation — shareholders elect board → board appoints executive management
  • Taxation: Double taxation — taxed at the corporate level AND again at shareholder level on dividends distributed
  • Best suited for: Companies seeking to go public, raise large amounts of capital, or retain profits for reinvestment (retained profits avoid the second layer of tax)

Names by jurisdiction: C-Corp (USA) · Corporation (China) · Société Anonyme / SA (France) · AG (Germany) · K.K. / Stock Company (Japan)

💡 Why Double Taxation Isn't Always a Dealbreaker

If profits are retained and reinvested (not distributed as dividends), the shareholder-level tax does NOT apply. This makes corporations attractive for growth-oriented firms. Also, in jurisdictions where corporate tax rates are lower than personal income tax rates, it can be advantageous to "store" profits in the business.

⚠ Critical Exam Distinction — "Public Limited" ≠ "Publicly Listed"

A public limited company (the legal form) is NOT obligated to list its shares on an exchange. It is simply the form most suitable for doing so. A company can be a public limited company (e.g., a C-Corp in the USA) and still remain privately held (unlisted). The terms "public" and "private" when describing listed vs. unlisted status are SEPARATE from the legal form distinction.

💡 The Progression Logic

Each form solves a problem with the previous one: Partnership → solves sole prop's capital/risk problem. LP → solves unlimited liability for passive investors. LLP → solves GP's unlimited liability. Private Ltd → adds ownership separation and share transferability. Public Ltd → removes all ownership restrictions and enables the widest capital access. Each step up the ladder trades simplicity for financing power.

Key Features of Corporate Issuers

Corporate issuers = corporations that raise capital in financial markets. They raise more capital from investors than even governments worldwide. Understanding them is essential for financial analysts.

1. Legal Identity

Corporation
A legal entity separate and distinct from its owners, formed by filing articles of incorporation with a regulatory authority. Can enter contracts, hire employees, sue and be sued, borrow and lend money, make investments, and pay taxes — just like an individual.

Large corporations operate across many jurisdictions and are subject to regulatory requirements wherever:

  • The company is incorporated
  • The business is conducted
  • The company finances itself

These requirements cover: registration, financial/non-financial reporting & disclosure, capital market activities (issuance, trading, investment).

2. Owner–Manager Separation

The most distinctive feature of the corporate form. Shareholders ≠ Managers.

  • Shareholders elect a board of directors
  • Board appoints executive management (e.g., CEO)
  • CEO is accountable for investing, financing, and operating decisions
  • Directors and managers have a primary duty to act in the best interests of shareholders and all stakeholders

Shareholder recourse: If board/management fails shareholders, they can exercise voting rights to replace the board of directors. This is the primary mechanism of shareholder power.

Corporate Governance Chain
Shareholders → elect → Board of Directors → appoints → Executive Management (CEO)
Shareholders → Board → Management. Each tier is accountable to the one above it. Shareholders are the ultimate principals.
💡 Why Separation Enables Larger Capital Access

Because investors don't need to be involved in management, the universe of potential investors is vastly larger. A retiree in Mumbai can own shares in Apple without knowing anything about chip design or supply chains. This is the fundamental innovation of the corporate form.

3. Owner / Shareholder Liability

  • Limited liability: Maximum loss = value of shares (can fall to zero, no further)
  • Shareholders are NOT responsible for corporate debts unless they separately and specifically guarantee them
  • Risk and return shared in proportion to share ownership (unless charter specifies otherwise)
  • Ownership divided into shares of smaller unit size → easy to buy/sell incremental interests
  • Some corporations issue multiple classes of shares with different risk/return characteristics

4. External Financing

Corporations are financed in two fundamental ways:

Equity Financing
  • Selling shares to investors
  • Reinvesting profits (retained earnings)
  • Investors = shareholders
  • Returns via dividends (distributions of profit)
  • No fixed repayment obligation
  • Residual claim on assets
Debt Financing
  • Loans, bonds, and leases
  • Must be repaid on a pre-specified date with interest
  • Interest is a fixed obligation
  • Senior claim on assets vs. equity
  • Interest tax-deductible (tax shield)
Financial Balance Sheet Structure
Assets (Left Side) = Liabilities + Equity (Right Side)

Assets: Cash | Short-Term Claims | Long-Term Assets
Liabilities: Short-Term Obligations | Long-Term Obligations
Equity: Shareholders' Equity
The "Economic" balance sheet extends this to include intangible/hard-to-quantify items: human capital, supplier/customer relationships, governance structures, and community claims.
Financial vs. Economic Profit
Financial Profit (Net Income) = Revenue − All Financial Costs − Taxes

Economic Profit = Net Income − Required Rate of Return on Equity
Economic profit measures return IN EXCESS of what owners could have earned elsewhere (opportunity cost of equity). This is the true wealth-creation metric. Economic profit > 0 means the firm is creating value above the required return.
💡 Key Insight — Economic vs. Accounting Profit

A firm can have positive accounting (financial) profit yet zero or negative economic profit if returns don't exceed the required rate of return on equity. This distinction is critical for valuation and is the foundation of EVA (Economic Value Added) analysis tested in later CFA topics.

Financing providers include: Individuals, mutual funds, pension funds, banks, governments, non-profits, and other corporations.

Forms of financing: Can be raised as a private contractual agreement between issuer and investor, OR as a security — a standardized, potentially tradeable instrument on a public exchange.

07

Corporate Taxation

Double Taxation of Corporate Profits

Double Taxation Structure — Public Limited Company
Step 1: Corporation earns profit → pays CORPORATE TAX on profits
Step 2: Remaining after-tax profit distributed as dividends → shareholder pays PERSONAL TAX on dividends

Result: Same profits taxed TWICE — once at corporate level, once at personal level
This applies when profits ARE distributed. If profits are RETAINED and reinvested → no second layer of tax applies.

Important caveats on double taxation:

  • In some countries, shareholders get a personal tax credit for their share of taxes already paid by the corporation (imputation system)
  • In other countries, shareholders pay no personal tax on dividends if the corporation has already paid tax on those earnings
  • In some countries, corporations pay no corporate tax at all
  • Tax treatment varies greatly by jurisdiction — always jurisdiction-specific

Why Corporations Remain Attractive Despite Double Tax

Corporate Form Advantages on Tax
  • Retained earnings NOT distributed → only taxed once (corporate level)
  • If corporate tax rate < personal income tax rate → advantageous to store profits in business
  • Partnerships/sole props taxed on ALL profits regardless of distribution — no deferral option
  • Enables tax-efficient reinvestment of profits for growth
Corporate Form Tax Disadvantages
  • Distributed profits taxed twice
  • More complex tax compliance
  • Taxable profits ≠ financial statement profits (tax codes differ from accounting standards)
  • Subject to multiple tax jurisdictions if operating globally
⚠ Exam Trap — Pass-Through Timing vs. Corporate Retained Earnings

Sole proprietors and partners are taxed on profits whether or not they are distributed. Corporate shareholders (public limited companies) are taxed on dividends only when actually received. This asymmetry makes corporations more attractive for growth businesses that reinvest earnings.

Publicly vs. Privately Owned Corporate Issuers

For corporations, "public" and "private" (or "listed" and "unlisted") are defined by whether the company's shares are listed and tradeable on an exchange.

Stock Exchange
A rules-based, open access market venue where financial instruments are traded, with price and volume transparency accessible by issuers, investors, and their intermediaries.
Free Float
The shares that are actively traded on exchanges — not held by insiders, strategic investors, or sponsors. Expressed as a percentage of total shares outstanding. A low free float means most shares are locked up by controlling owners.

💡 Why Stay Private? Benefits of Private Status

1. Fewer shareholders = accountable to fewer stakeholders · 2. Early-stage investors can earn very high returns if the company succeeds · 3. No expensive compliance/disclosure requirements · 4. Preserves control by incumbent owners and management · 5. Access to private equity/venture capital has grown, reducing need for public markets.

Disclosure Requirements — Key Facts

  • USA (NYSE/NASDAQ): Audited financial statements + other info filed quarterly with SEC; accessible via EDGAR (Electronic Data Gathering, Analysis, and Retrieval)
  • European Union: Financial statements in ESEF (European Single Electronic Format) filed at least semiannually in registry of domicile
  • Public companies must also disclose: major changes in voting rights, management/director stock transactions
  • Annual reports regularly exceed 100 pages
  • Private companies: generally not subject to same disclosure — though anti-fraud rules and tax filing obligations apply to all entities
⚠ Exam Trap — Short-Termism Claim

It is commonly claimed that public company status causes short-termism (focus on quarterly earnings vs. long-term value). The module notes that empirical evidence for short-termism among listed companies is thin at best. Don't take this argument at face value — the CFA curriculum is skeptical of it.

09

Going Public & Going Private — Mechanisms

3 Ways a Private Company Goes Public

1. IPO (Initial Public Offering)
  • Most common method
  • Company meets exchange listing requirements
  • Engages investment banks to underwrite the sale
  • New shares issued → proceeds go to the company
  • Shares begin trading on exchange after IPO
2. Direct Listing
  • No underwriter
  • No new shares issued → no capital raised
  • Existing shares listed; price set by market
  • Shares sold by existing shareholders
  • Faster and cheaper than IPO
  • Only viable for large, established, recognized brands
3. Acquisition (SPAC / Reverse Merger)
  • Private co. acquired by public co. → exposure to private co.
  • SPAC: "blank check" shell company that IPOs, holds cash in trust, then acquires a private company
  • SPAC has finite time (e.g., 18 months) to complete acquisition; unused proceeds returned
  • Replaces older reverse merger process
IPO vs. Direct Listing — Key Differences
IPO: Underwriter engaged + New shares issued + Capital raised → Company gets proceeds
Direct Listing: No underwriter + No new shares + No new capital → Existing shareholders sell
Direct listing suits large, established companies with brand recognition (e.g., Spotify, Coinbase). IPO suits companies actively seeking new capital for growth.
SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company)
A shell ("blank check") company that: (1) raises capital via its own IPO, (2) places proceeds in trust, (3) acquires an unspecified private company within a set timeframe (e.g., 18 months). Once acquisition is complete, the private target becomes public. If no acquisition is made → proceeds returned to investors.

Going from Public to Private (Take-Private / Go-Private)

  • Investors (typically private equity) acquire all publicly traded shares and delist from the exchange
  • Must pay a premium above current share price
  • Often financed with debt → known as a Leveraged Buyout (LBO)
  • Motivation: investors believe value-creating actions (management changes, asset sales, restructuring, cost savings) can exceed premium + financing costs
  • After value creation, company may be taken public again several years later
💡 LBO Logic — Why Use Debt?

Using debt to finance the acquisition amplifies equity returns (financial leverage). If the private equity firm can buy the company, improve it, and sell it at a higher valuation, the debt repayment is manageable and equity returns are magnified. This is the core economics of leveraged buyouts.

Public vs. Private Company Trends

Emerging Economies
  • Growing number of public companies
  • Higher growth rates → need for public capital
  • Transition to open market structures
  • Foreign capital inflows driving listings
  • State-owned enterprises gradually transferred to private sector via IPO
Developed Economies
  • Declining number of listed companies
  • More M&A reducing independent companies
  • More private capital (VC/PE) available
  • Avoiding costly compliance of public listing
  • Founders prefer to preserve control

The Varieties of Corporate Owners

Corporate shareholders are not just individual investors. They include a wide range of entities, each with different objectives:

Owner Type Examples Key Characteristics
Individual Investors Retail investors, employees Diverse objectives; vote via shares; may hold directly or through funds
Institutional Investors Pension funds, mutual funds, insurance companies Hold on behalf of individuals; large AUM; significant voting power; financial return focus
Other Corporations Strategic investors, parent companies, cross-holdings May have strategic objectives beyond financial return; can be controlling shareholders
Governments Sovereign wealth funds, state-owned enterprises (SOEs) May have public interest objectives; often in infrastructure, energy, telecoms; common in emerging markets
Non-Profits Foundations, endowments, universities Financial + societal/non-financial objectives; some grown to significant size

Government-Owned Corporations (State-Owned Enterprises)

  • Governments create legally separate corporations while maintaining 100% or partial ownership
  • Have board of directors, management structure, audited financials for transparency to taxpayers
  • Profits or losses must be reported; losses financed from government budget
  • Developed economies: Common in postal systems, railways, airports (infrastructure)
  • Emerging economies: Dominant in major domestic industries (commodities, energy, banking)
  • As economies seek private sector capital → SOEs gradually transferred via IPO
  • Example: Netherlands (KPN), UK (Royal Mail), Germany (Deutsche Post) — postal services privatized
  • Telecommunications, power generation: shifted from government monopolies to private sector from 1980s onward → greater innovation, diversification, and renewable energy investment
💡 USPS — A Useful Exam Example

The US Postal Service (USPS) is structured like a government-owned corporation (board governance, audited financials, borrowing authority) but is legally NOT a corporation. This distinction matters. It is a government agency with corporate-like features.

⚠ Exam Note — Private Placements

Private company investors typically invest via private placements — shares offered through a legal document to select investors. Investors must usually be qualified / accredited / sophisticated investors — those deemed by regulators to be able and willing to assume greater risk of a non-public offering. Public companies can also conduct private placements, subject to regulatory constraints.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ 5 attributes of organizational forms: Legal identity, Owner–manager, Liability, Taxation, Access to financing (LOLOT)
  • ✅ Sole proprietorship: No separate legal entity. Unlimited liability. Pass-through tax. Simplest but most constrained.
  • ✅ General partnership: ALL partners have unlimited liability. If one can't pay, others cover the full amount.
  • ✅ Limited partnership: GP = unlimited liability + management. LP = limited liability, no management. Both pass-through tax.
  • ✅ LLP: No GP — ALL partners limited liability. Professional services only in some jurisdictions.
  • ✅ Pass-through tax: Applies to sole props, ALL partnership types, and private limited companies (most jurisdictions). Income taxed at personal level EVEN IF NOT DISTRIBUTED.
  • ✅ Public limited company (corporation): Double taxation (corporate + dividend level), but retained earnings avoid the second layer.
  • ✅ Public limited company ≠ publicly listed. A C-Corp can remain private (unlisted). The form is separate from listing status.
  • ✅ Corporate governance chain: Shareholders → elect Board → Board appoints Management (CEO).
  • ✅ Limited liability: Max shareholder loss = value of shares. Cannot lose more than invested unless personally guaranteeing debts.
  • ✅ Economic profit = Net income − Required rate of return on equity. Can be >0 (value creation), =0 (normal return), or <0 (value destruction) even with positive accounting profit.
  • ✅ Free float = shares actively traded on exchange (excluding insider/strategic holdings).
  • ✅ IPO vs. Direct Listing: IPO has underwriter + new shares + capital raised. Direct listing: no underwriter, no new shares, no capital raised.
  • ✅ SPAC: Blank check company, IPOs first, then finds private target within time limit; unused proceeds returned to investors.
  • ✅ Take-private / LBO: Pay premium + use debt to acquire all public shares → delist → restructure → potentially re-list later.
  • ✅ Developed markets: Declining listed companies (M&A, private capital, control preference). Emerging markets: Growing listed companies (capital needs, SOE privatizations).
  • ✅ Private placement investors must typically be accredited/sophisticated investors. Public companies can also do private placements.
  • ✅ US public companies: file quarterly with SEC via EDGAR. EU: at least semiannually in ESEF format.
