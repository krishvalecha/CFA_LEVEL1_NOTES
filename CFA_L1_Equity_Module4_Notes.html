01

Equity vs. Debt — Core Difference

Debt Securities
  • A liability of the issuing company
  • Company is contractually obligated to repay principal at maturity
  • Company is contractually obligated to pay interest periodically
  • Investors seek interest income
  • Creditors have priority claim over equity in liquidation
Equity Securities
  • NOT a liability — represents ownership
  • Company is NOT obligated to repay the amount received from shareholders
  • Company is NOT obligated to make periodic payments (dividends are discretionary)
  • Shareholders have a residual claim on assets after all liabilities paid
  • Investors seek total return = capital appreciation + dividends

Historical return context (1900–2017, real compounded):

  • Government bills: real return <1% per year (barely kept pace with inflation)
  • Government bonds: real return <2% per year in most countries
  • Equities: real return ~3.5% per year in most markets; world average ~5.2%; world ex-US ~5%
  • Best performers: South Africa, Australia, then US, New Zealand, Sweden
  • Reinvested dividends dramatically amplify long-run equity returns: US$1 in US equities (1900) → US$1,402 real with reinvested dividends vs. US$11.9 price appreciation only (6.4% vs. 2.1% real CAGR)
💡 Market Cap / GDP as a Valuation Indicator

The ratio of equity market capitalization to GDP is used as a rough indicator of whether a market is over, under, or fairly valued relative to its long-run average. Example: US equity markets = ~51% of global market cap but only ~25% of global GDP → potential over-representation. After 2008 crash, US market cap/GDP fell to 59% vs. long-run average of 79%.

02

Common Shares & Voting Rights

Common Shares (Ordinary Shares / Common Stock)
The predominant type of equity security. Represent an ownership interest giving holders: (1) claim on operating performance, (2) participation in governance via voting rights, (3) residual claim on net assets in liquidation. Dividends are discretionary — not contractually obligated.

Callable and Putable Common Shares

Callable Common Shares

Give the issuer the right to buy back shares from shareholders at a price determined at issuance. Riskier for investors — call price limits upside. Companies pay higher dividends to compensate investors for call risk.

Putable Common Shares

Give the shareholder the right to sell shares back to the issuer at a price specified at issuance. Less risky for investors — put price sets a floor on value. Companies pay lower dividends because investors accept less return for the downside protection.

Voting Rights

Voting Type How It Works Who Benefits Example
Statutory Voting Each share = one vote per candidate. Votes cannot be concentrated — must be spread evenly across all candidates Majority shareholders — large holders can dominate every seat 100 shares, 4 directors → max 100 votes per candidate
Cumulative Voting Total voting rights = shares owned × number of directors being elected. Shareholder can allocate ALL votes to ONE candidate Minority shareholders — can concentrate all votes on one preferred candidate for board representation 100 shares, 4 directors → 400 total votes, all 400 can go to 1 candidate
💡 Cumulative vs. Statutory Voting — Quick Memory Trick

Statutory = spread votes like a statute says — equal distribution. Cumulative = accumulate all your votes and aim them at one person — better for minorities. The exam will test which benefits minority shareholders: always cumulative voting.

Dual-Class Share Structures

Companies can issue different classes of common shares (Class A, Class B) with different ownership rights. Common uses:

  • Viacom example: Class A = voting stock (1 vote/share); Class B = non-voting stock. Family controls >70% of votes through Class A ownership. Both classes share equally in dividends and liquidation
  • Ford example: Class A (public) = 60% voting rights; Class B (Ford family) = 40% voting rights. But Class B gets liquidation priority ($0.50, then extra $1.00, then equal)
  • Dual-class structures allow founders/families to maintain control while raising public capital
  • Proxy voting: shareholders can designate another party to vote on their behalf when unable to attend annual meeting
⚠️ Exam Trap — Voting Rights ≠ Economic Rights

In dual-class structures, voting rights and economic rights can be separated. Class B (non-voting) at Viacom trades close to Class A in price even with no votes — shares are economically equivalent. The exam may ask about which class has more economic value vs. which has more voting control. Also note: dividends are paid equally across classes (in most structures) even when voting rights differ substantially.

03

Preference Shares (Preferred Stock)

Preference Shares
Rank above common shares for dividend payments and liquidation distributions. Generally do NOT carry voting rights (unless explicitly granted). Have characteristics of both debt AND equity — fixed dividends (like debt), but dividends are not contractual obligations (like equity). Can be perpetual, callable, or putable.

Preference shares vs. debt:

  • Like debt: dividends are fixed and generally higher than common dividends
  • Unlike debt: dividends are NOT contractual obligations — company can skip them (subject to accumulation rules)
  • Like equity: can be perpetual (no maturity), can be callable or putable
  • Generally treated as fixed income by analysts when dividends are clearly payable in the foreseeable future

Types of Preference Share Features

Cumulative vs. Non-Cumulative

Cumulative: unpaid dividends accumulate and must all be paid before any common dividend can be paid. Provides stronger protection to preferred shareholders. Lower risk → lower dividend yield.

Non-cumulative: unpaid dividends are forfeited permanently — they do not accumulate. However, company still cannot pay common dividends in the current period unless preferred dividends have been paid first. Higher risk → higher dividend yield.

Participating vs. Non-Participating

Participating: receive standard preferred dividend PLUS additional dividend if company profits exceed a pre-specified level. Can also receive extra assets in liquidation above par value. Effectively shares in company upside. More common for smaller, riskier companies where liquidation is a concern.

Non-participating: entitled only to the fixed dividend and par value in liquidation. No share in excess profits.

Convertible Preference Shares

Shareholders can convert preferred shares into a specified number of common shares at a predetermined conversion ratio set at issuance.

Advantages: (1) Higher dividend than common shares. (2) Opportunity to share in company profits. (3) Benefits from rise in common share price through conversion option. (4) Less volatile than common shares (known dividend payments provide floor).

Why used in VC/private equity: popular for early-stage, high-risk companies. Provides income (dividends) while waiting for company to go public, then allows conversion to common at IPO to capture upside. Also used when IPO may be years away (longer private equity holding periods of ~10 years).

Callable vs. Putable Preference Shares

Callable preference: issuer can redeem shares at predetermined price. Riskier for investors — limits upside. Pays higher dividend to compensate.

Putable preference: investor can sell back to issuer at predetermined price. Less risky for investors — floor on value. Pays lower dividend because downside protected.

04

Preference Shares — Master Comparison

Feature Type A Type B Risk to Investor Dividend Effect
Dividend Accrual Cumulative — unpaid dividends accumulate Non-cumulative — unpaid dividends forfeited Cumulative = lower risk Cumulative → lower yield; Non-cumulative → higher yield
Profit Sharing Participating — fixed dividend + share of excess profits Non-participating — fixed dividend only Participating = more upside; common in smaller/riskier companies Participating → lower base yield (upside compensates)
Conversion Convertible — can convert to common at set ratio Non-convertible — fixed preferred forever Convertible = less volatile, more upside potential Convertible → lower yield (option has value)
Issuer Redemption Callable — issuer can redeem at set price Non-callable — no issuer call right Callable = higher risk (limits upside) Callable → higher yield (compensate for call risk)
Investor Put Right Putable — investor can sell back at set price Non-putable — no put right Putable = lower risk (price floor) Putable → lower yield (downside protected)
💡 Risk-Yield Relationship Memory Rule

More protection for investor → lower risk → lower required dividend yield. Less protection (more risk) → higher required dividend yield. Callable = riskier for investor = higher yield. Putable = safer for investor = lower yield. Cumulative = safer for investor = lower yield. This logic is consistent across all security types in the curriculum.

⚠️ Exam Trap — Common vs. Preference Share Risk

Preference shares are LESS risky than common shares because: (1) dividends are fixed and known, (2) preferred gets paid before common, (3) liquidation claim is above common. BUT preference shares CAN be riskier than other preference shares depending on features (e.g., non-cumulative is riskier than cumulative). The exam may ask you to rank risk — always: secured debt < unsecured debt < preferred < common.

Public vs. Private Equity Securities

Public Equity
  • Listed on public exchanges
  • Active secondary market → liquid
  • Market-determined quoted prices
  • Mandatory financial disclosure → transparent
  • More capital-raising opportunities
  • Higher corporate governance scrutiny
  • Costly regulatory/reporting requirements
  • Management pressure for short-term results (quarterly targets)
Private Equity
  • Not listed on exchanges — no active secondary market
  • Highly illiquid — no market-quoted prices
  • Issued primarily to institutional investors via private placements
  • Fair value difficult to determine — limited financial disclosure
  • Long holding periods: originally 3–5 yrs; now ~10 years
  • Management can focus on long-term value creation
  • Eliminates costly public-company requirements
  • Lower corporate governance effectiveness (per some studies)

Three Types of Private Equity Investments

Type Description Stage Exit Strategy Key Features
Venture Capital (VC) Provides seed/start-up capital, early-stage financing, or mezzanine financing to early-stage companies requiring capital for expansion Pre-IPO, start-up to growth IPO on stock market or sale to other investors. Typical exit: 3–10 years from start-up VC investors include friends/family, wealthy individuals, PE funds. Securities not publicly traded → long-term commitment required. Exit return = price at IPO or sale
Leveraged Buyout (LBO) Group of investors uses large amounts of debt to purchase all outstanding common shares of a publicly traded company → takes it "private" Mature public company → taken private → later re-IPO Re-IPO of restructured company in primary market Target companies: large undervalued assets (sold to reduce debt) + high cash flows (service debt). MBO = Management Buyout (management is primary acquirer). Ultimate goal: restructure, then re-IPO
PIPE (Private Investment in Public Equity) Public company sells sizeable equity stake to private investor(s) quickly to raise capital Already public company needing fast capital Investor holds publicly traded shares after registration Used when company needs rapid capital (expansion, high debt, operational distress). Shares often sold at significant discount to market price. Private investor gets a bargain; company gets quick capital
💡 LBO vs. MBO — Easy Distinction

Both are leveraged buyouts using significant debt to take a company private. The difference: in an MBO, the acquiring group is primarily the company's own existing management. In a generic LBO, the acquirer is an outside private equity partnership or investor group. All MBOs are LBOs; not all LBOs are MBOs.

Why companies choose private equity over public listing:

  • Eliminate short-term performance pressure (quarterly analyst targets)
  • Eliminate costly regulatory/exchange filing requirements
  • Eliminate investor relations departments and quarterly conference calls
  • Enable longer-term strategic restructuring and operational improvement
  • Private equity holding periods have lengthened: 3–5 years (1980s–90s) → ~10 years (current)
  • Convertible preference shares increasingly used in longer-duration PE investments → provides income + upside via conversion at eventual IPO
06

Non-Domestic Equity Securities

Why Invest Globally? Why Are There Barriers?

Benefits of Global Investing
  • Diversification — add assets with lower correlation to domestic holdings
  • Access to faster-growing markets (emerging economies)
  • Expanded investment universe → better risk/return opportunities
  • Dual listing benefits: more liquid, broader shareholder base, greater transparency, greater investor awareness
Foreign Ownership Restrictions — 3 Reasons
  1. Limit foreign control: prevent foreigners from acquiring majority interest in domestic companies (strategic/national security concerns)
  2. Protect domestic investors: give locals opportunity to own shares in foreign companies operating domestically (e.g., IKEA in Asia-Pacific)
  3. Reduce capital flow volatility: prevent sudden large capital outflows (e.g., 1997–98 Asian Financial Crisis → dramatic equity declines and currency devaluations in Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea)

Methods to Invest in Foreign Equity

Method Description Advantages Disadvantages
Direct Investing Buy shares directly on foreign exchange in foreign currency Direct ownership; access to full local market Currency risk; must know foreign settlement/clearing rules; less transparency; less liquidity in some markets
Depository Receipts (DR) Security representing foreign shares, traded on local exchange in local currency No currency conversion needed; familiar market rules; no unfamiliar settlement; dividend payments in local currency May have voting limitations (unsponsored); exchange rate still affects underlying value
Global Registered Share (GRS) Single common share traded on multiple exchanges worldwide in different currencies Most flexible — same share traded anywhere; no currency conversion needed for purchase/sale; actual ownership interest (not a receipt) Complex to administer; few companies have issued them (Daimler Chrysler 1998, UBS AG 2011)
BLDR (Basket of Listed DRs) ETF consisting of a portfolio of depository receipts tracking a DR index Diversified DR exposure; trades like a stock; can be bought on margin, sold short, used in hedging/arbitrage ETF expense ratio; tracks index not individual companies

Depository Receipts — Key Mechanics

  • A depository receipt (DR) = security that trades like an ordinary share on a local exchange but represents an economic interest in a foreign company
  • Created when: foreign shares deposited in a depository bank in the local country → bank issues receipts representing those shares
  • DR may represent 1 share, multiple shares, or a fraction of a share (ratio set at creation)
  • Price of DR affected by: company fundamentals, market conditions, analyst recommendations, and exchange rate movements
  • Short-term price discrepancies between DR and underlying shares = quick arbitrage opportunity
  • Depository bank responsibilities: custodian + registrar; handles dividends, stock splits, taxable events, transfer agent functions
  • Largest depository banks: Bank of New York Mellon (largest), Deutsche Bank, JPMorgan, Citibank
Sponsored DR

Foreign company has direct involvement in issuance. Investors retain voting rights. Subject to greater reporting requirements. Must be registered with SEC (in US). All investor rights same as direct owners.

Unsponsored DR

Foreign company has NO involvement. Depository purchases shares in foreign market and issues receipts via brokerage firms. Depository bank retains voting rights (not investors). Less regulatory requirements.

GDR vs. ADR — Key Differences

GDR (Global Depository Receipt)
  • Issued outside the company's home country AND outside the United States
  • NOT subject to foreign ownership/capital flow restrictions of home country
  • Primarily traded on London and Luxembourg exchanges (timelier, lower cost)
  • Most denominated in USD (some in GBP, EUR)
  • Cannot be listed on US exchanges but CAN be privately placed with US institutional investors
ADR (American Depository Receipt)
  • USD-denominated security trading on US exchanges
  • Oldest type of DR — first created in 1927
  • Most commonly traded DR type
  • Enable foreign companies to raise capital from US investors
  • An ADR is one form of GDR; NOT all GDRs are ADRs
  • ADS (American Depository Share) = the underlying share on which the ADR is based; ADR = the certificate trading on US markets
  • Currently: >2,000 DRs from 80+ countries trade on US exchanges; total value ~US$2 trillion (~15% of all US equity trading)

Level I (Unlisted) No No Form F-6 only OTC (Over-the-Counter) Low None
Level II (Listed) Yes No (existing shares only) Form F-6 NYSE, NASDAQ, AMEX High Yes — must meet size/earnings

💡 ADR Level Memory Trick — "1 = OTC only, 2 = listed no raise, 3 = listed + raise"

Think of levels as increasingly demanding: Level I is the easiest (just OTC, minimal filing). Level II is exchange-listed but still can't issue new shares to raise capital. Level III is the full package — listed + capital raising. Rule 144A is the "private back door" — no listing, no SEC, but large institutional investors only. The key exam question: which level can raise capital publicly? Only Level III.

Risk & Return Characteristics of Equity Securities

Sources of Return

★ Equity Total Return Formula
Total Return, Rₜ = (Pₜ − Pₜ₋₁ + Dₜ) / Pₜ₋₁
Pₜ = sale price at end of period t | Pₜ₋₁ = purchase price at beginning of period t
Dₜ = cash or stock dividends received during period t

Two sources for domestic investors: (1) Price change (capital gain/loss) + (2) Dividend income
THREE sources for foreign investors: (1) Price change + (2) Dividend income + (3) Foreign exchange gain/loss

FX example: Japanese stock returns 10% in Japan. If yen depreciates 10% vs. USD → US investor's ADR return ≈ 0%
If yen appreciates 10% vs. USD → US investor's ADR return ≈ 20%

Reinvested dividends — the power of compounding:

US$1 invested in US equities in 1900 → US$1,402 real value in 2016 with reinvested dividends (6.4% real CAGR) vs. only US$11.9 price appreciation only (2.1% real CAGR). For comparison: bonds → US$9.8 (2.0% real); bills → US$2.60 (0.8% real). Reinvested dividends are critical for long-run wealth accumulation.

Risk of Equity Securities

  • Risk = uncertainty of future cash flows → greater uncertainty → greater price volatility → higher risk
  • Measured by: standard deviation of expected total return
  • Estimated via: historical returns and standard deviation OR probability-weighted scenario analysis

Risk Ranking: Common vs. Preference Shares

Dimension Preference Shares Common Shares
Dividend certainty Higher — fixed, known dividend Lower — discretionary, variable
Payment priority Paid first before common Paid last — residual claim
Liquidation claim Known and fixed (par/face value) Whatever is left (could be nothing)
Return source Mostly dividends (known and stable) Mostly price appreciation (uncertain) + dividends (discretionary)
Overall risk Lower Higher
Expected return Lower (less risk = less return) Higher (more risk = more return)

Callable vs. Putable Risk (All Equity)

Feature Risk to Investor Dividend/Yield Why
Callable (common or preferred) Higher than non-callable Higher dividend to compensate Call price caps investor's upside; issuer will call when it's favorable to them (bad for investor)
Putable (common or preferred) Lower than non-putable Lower dividend (investor accepts less) Put price establishes a floor → limits downside; investor pays for this protection through lower yield
Cumulative preferred Lower than non-cumulative Lower dividend Unpaid dividends accumulate → stronger investor protection
Non-cumulative preferred Higher than cumulative Higher dividend Forfeited dividends = more risk for investor

Book Value vs. Market Value vs. Intrinsic Value

Book Value of Equity

= Total Assets − Total Liabilities (on balance sheet). Represents historical operating and financing decisions. Increases when company retains net income. Management can directly influence it. Reflects accounting choices (depreciation method, inventory method). Also called shareholders' equity.

Market Value of Equity

= Number of shares outstanding × current market price per share (market capitalization). Reflects collective investor expectations about amount, timing, and uncertainty of future cash flows. Management can only indirectly influence it. Rarely equals book value. Higher market/book ratio = investors expect more future growth opportunities.

Intrinsic Value (Fundamental Value)

= Present value of future projected cash flows. Can be estimated (e.g., DDM, DCF) but never known for certain — requires judgment on amount, timing, and risk of cash flows. Investors profit by finding gaps between intrinsic value estimate and current market price.

Book Value vs. Market Value — Key Relationship
Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio = Market Price per Share / Book Value per Share

P/B > 1.0 → market believes company has future growth opportunities above book assets
P/B < 1.0 → market values company below its accounting book value (distress signal)
High P/B industry (tech): investors expect many profitable future investments
Low P/B industry (heavy equipment/mature): fewer growth opportunities expected
Do NOT compare P/B ratios across industries — differences reflect industry growth outlooks, not company quality.
Compare within same industry only. A higher P/B than industry peers → investors see above-average growth potential.

Why book value and market value rarely equal each other:

  • Book value = historical cost-based accounting → does not reflect current market values of assets or earning potential
  • Market value = forward-looking → reflects NPV of all future growth opportunities
  • Example: Warren Buffett's observation on Berkshire Hathaway — in 1964, book value overstated value (textile business worth less than book). Today, many businesses worth far more than book value. Intrinsic value = "discounted value of cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life"
⚠️ Exam Trap — ROE Increase Is Not Always Positive

ROE can increase for bad reasons: (1) Net income falls more slowly than equity (both declining — bad). (2) Company takes on debt to repurchase shares → equity decreases → ROE mechanically increases but equity is now riskier. Always examine the source of ROE change. The DuPont decomposition (separate reading) is used to analyze ROE components. An increasing ROE is only clearly positive if driven by genuine operational improvement.

ROE, Cost of Equity & Required Return

Return on Equity (ROE) — Two Formulas

★ ROE Formula 1 — Using Average Book Value (More Common)
ROEₜ = NIₜ / Average BVEₜ = NIₜ / [(BVEₜ + BVEₜ₋₁) / 2]
NIₜ = net income available to ordinary shareholders in year t (after preferred dividends)
Average BVEₜ = average of beginning and ending book value of equity for year t
Use when: company experiences volatile year-end book values, or industry convention uses average BVE
★ ROE Formula 2 — Using Beginning Book Value
ROEₜ = NIₜ / BVEₜ₋₁
BVEₜ₋₁ = book value of equity at the beginning of year t (= end of year t−1)
Assumes only beginning equity was used to generate net income during the year
Use when: book values are relatively stable over time, or computing ROE annually over a multi-year period

What ROE measures: how effectively management uses equity capital invested by shareholders to generate profits. ROE of 32.5% means the company generates $0.325 profit per $1.00 of shareholder equity.

ROE can increase because:

  • Net income increases faster than equity (best outcome)
  • Net income decreases slower than equity (less favorable)
  • Debt-financed share repurchases decrease equity denominator (increases risk)

ROE limitations: affected by accounting method choices (depreciation, inventory) → makes cross-company comparison difficult. Also hard to compare if accounting methods changed over time.

Cost of Equity vs. Required Return — Three Key Concepts

Concept Definition Who Determines It Key Point
Cost of Equity The minimum expected rate of return a company must offer investors to purchase its shares in the primary market and maintain share price in the secondary market Determined by market forces; estimated using DDM or CAPM Used as proxy for investors' minimum required return. If actual return falls below this, share price drops until yield rises to meet requirement
Investors' Required Rate of Return The minimum return investors demand for the risk they take. Based on expected future cash flows (uncertain) → may differ across investors Set by individual investors based on risk perception May differ from company's cost of equity because expectations vary. Company uses cost of equity as proxy for this
Accounting ROE Net income / book value of equity — measures historical accounting efficiency of equity use Determined by accounting results and book equity Backward-looking (historical). Does not directly equal cost of equity or required return. Used to evaluate management effectiveness
Cost of Debt vs. Cost of Equity — Key Contrast
Cost of Debt = periodic interest/coupon rate (known and contractual → easy to estimate)
Cost of Equity = not contractual → harder to estimate. Common models:
→ DDM (Dividend Discount Model): Cost of equity based on expected dividends
→ CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model): Cost of equity based on systematic risk (beta)

WACC = weighted average of after-tax cost of debt + cost of equity
WACC = minimum required return company must earn on long-term investments to satisfy all capital providers
If investors require a HIGHER return than company's cost of equity → they sell shares → price drops → yield rises → equilibrium restored
Cost of equity is always higher than cost of debt (equity is riskier, and interest is tax-deductible)
💡 Why Cost of Equity May Differ from Investors' Required Return

The company's cost of equity is a single number used in WACC. But individual investors have heterogeneous expectations about future cash flows, so their required returns differ. The company sets its cost of equity as the best estimate of what the market requires — a proxy for the average investor's required return. In equilibrium, cost of equity ≈ market-required return.

★

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Equity vs. debt: Debt = liability + contractual obligation (principal + interest). Equity = NOT a liability, NOT contractual. Equity = residual claim. Equity investors seek total return; debt investors seek interest income.
  • ✅ Common shares: ownership interest, voting rights, residual claim in liquidation. Dividends are discretionary (not obligatory). Shareholders vote via proxy when unable to attend meeting.
  • ✅ Statutory voting: 1 share = 1 vote per candidate; must distribute evenly. Favors majority. Cumulative voting: total votes = shares × directors being elected; can concentrate all votes on 1 candidate → favors minority shareholders.
  • ✅ Callable common/preferred: issuer can redeem at set price → riskier for investor → higher dividend to compensate. Putable common/preferred: investor can sell back at set price → less risky → lower dividend.
  • ✅ Preference shares: rank above common for dividends and liquidation. Fixed dividend (like debt) but NOT contractual (like equity). Generally NO voting rights unless granted. Generally treated as fixed income when dividends clearly payable.
  • ✅ Cumulative preferred: unpaid dividends accumulate; must all be paid before common gets any. Non-cumulative: forfeited permanently but company still can't pay common until current-period preferred is paid.
  • ✅ Participating preferred: receives standard dividend PLUS share of excess profits if profits exceed threshold. More common in small/risky companies. Non-participating: fixed dividend + par value only.
  • ✅ Convertible preferred: can convert to common at predetermined ratio. Advantages: higher income than common, participation in upside, less volatility. Popular in VC/PE because provides income while waiting for IPO, then converts at IPO.
  • ✅ Risk ranking: preference shares < common shares in risk (and in expected return). Fixed dividends, payment priority, known liquidation claim → all reduce uncertainty vs. common shares.
  • ✅ Private equity types: (1) Venture capital — seed/early-stage capital, exit via IPO or sale (3–10 years). (2) LBO — debt-financed buyout of public company → taken private → restructured → re-IPO. MBO = management is primary acquirer. (3) PIPE — public company sells private stake quickly, often at discount to market price.
  • ✅ LBO target characteristics: undervalued assets (sold to reduce debt) + high cash flows (service debt). Goal: restructure and re-IPO.
  • ✅ Private vs. public equity: Private = illiquid, no market price, institutional investors only, long holding periods (~10 years), management focuses on long-term, lower governance. Public = liquid, market-priced, active secondary market, mandatory disclosure, short-term performance pressure.
  • ✅ 3 reasons for foreign ownership restrictions: (1) limit foreign control, (2) protect domestic investors' opportunity to own local companies, (3) reduce capital flow volatility (e.g., 1997–98 Asian crisis).
  • ✅ Depository receipt (DR): trades like ordinary share on local exchange; represents economic interest in foreign company. Created: foreign shares deposited in depository bank → bank issues receipts.
  • ✅ Sponsored DR: foreign company involved; investors retain voting rights; greater disclosure requirements. Unsponsored DR: no foreign company involvement; depository bank retains voting rights.
  • ✅ GDR: issued outside home country AND outside US; not subject to home country restrictions; mainly traded London/Luxembourg; mostly USD-denominated; cannot list on US exchanges but can be privately placed with US institutional investors.
  • ✅ ADR: USD-denominated, trades on US exchanges, first created 1927. ADR is one form of GDR; not all GDRs are ADRs. ADS = underlying share (trades in domestic market); ADR = certificate (trades on US market).
  • ✅ ADR levels: Level I = OTC only, no SEC registration, no capital raising. Level II = listed (NYSE/NASDAQ/AMEX), no capital raising, full SEC. Level III = listed + capital raising via public offerings, full SEC. Rule 144A = unlisted, no SEC, private placement to QIBs only. Key: ONLY Level III can raise capital publicly.
  • ✅ GRS (Global Registered Share): actual common share traded on multiple exchanges in different currencies — no currency conversion needed; most flexible but rare (Daimler Chrysler 1998, UBS 2011).
  • ✅ BLDR: ETF of depository receipts tracking a DR index. Can be bought on margin, sold short, used in hedging/arbitrage — trades like an individual stock.
  • ✅ Total return formula: Rₜ = (Pₜ − Pₜ₋₁ + Dₜ) / Pₜ₋₁. Foreign investors add FX component: yen appreciates 10% + stock +10% = ~+20% total. Yen depreciates 10% + stock +10% = ~0% total.
  • ✅ Reinvested dividends: dramatically amplify long-run returns. 1900–2016: $1 → $1,402 (with reinvested dividends) vs. $11.90 (price only). Never ignore the compounding effect of dividends in long-horizon analysis.
  • ✅ Book value of equity = Total Assets − Total Liabilities (balance sheet). Management directly controls it (via net income and retained earnings). Historical cost-based.
  • ✅ Market value of equity = shares outstanding × market price. Reflects collective investor expectations about future cash flows. Management only indirectly influences it. Forward-looking.
  • ✅ Intrinsic value = PV of future cash flows. Can only be estimated (never known). Warren Buffett: "discounted value of cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life." Highly subjective — changes with cash flow estimates and interest rates.
  • ✅ P/B ratio: Market price / Book value per share. P/B > 1 → investors expect future growth. Compare only within same industry. Higher P/B vs. peers → above-average growth expectations.
  • ✅ ROE Formula 1: NIₜ / Average BVEₜ = NIₜ / [(BVEₜ + BVEₜ₋₁) / 2]. Use when volatile book values or industry convention. ROE Formula 2: NIₜ / BVEₜ₋₁. Use when stable book values. NI = after preferred dividends.
  • ✅ ROE increase is NOT always positive: debt-financed buybacks reduce equity → ROE rises mechanically but equity is riskier. Net income falling slower than equity → both declining → bad. Must examine source via DuPont analysis.
  • ✅ Cost of equity vs. investors' required return: Cost of equity = minimum return company must offer to maintain share price; estimated via DDM or CAPM; used as proxy for investors' required return. They may differ due to heterogeneous investor expectations. In equilibrium, cost of equity ≈ market required return.
  • ✅ Cost of debt vs. cost of equity: Cost of debt = contractual, easy to estimate (coupon rate). Cost of equity = not contractual, must be modeled (DDM, CAPM). Cost of equity > cost of debt always (equity is riskier; interest is tax-deductible).
  • ✅ WACC = weighted average of after-tax cost of debt + cost of equity = minimum required return company must earn on long-term investments to satisfy all capital providers. Companies invest in projects with expected returns > WACC.

Total Return: Rₜ = (Pₜ − Pₜ₋₁ + Dₜ) / Pₜ₋₁

ROE (Average BVE): ROEₜ = NIₜ / [(BVEₜ + BVEₜ₋₁) / 2]
ROE (Beginning BVE): ROEₜ = NIₜ / BVEₜ₋₁

Statutory Voting: Max votes per candidate = Number of shares owned
