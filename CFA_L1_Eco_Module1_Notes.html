01

Core Concepts & Revenue Fundamentals

Revenue & Cost Definitions

Revenue Concepts
  • TR = Price × Quantity
  • AR = TR / Q = Price (always)
  • MR = ΔTR / ΔQ
  • Perfect competition: P = MR = AR
  • Imperfect competition: MR < P
Profit Concepts
  • Accounting Profit = TR – Accounting Costs
  • Economic Profit = TR – Total Economic Costs
  • Normal Profit = Economic Profit = 0
  • Economic cost = Accounting cost + Opportunity cost
⚠ Exam Trap

Normal profit ≠ zero accounting profit. A firm earning normal profit covers ALL opportunity costs including return on capital — it just earns zero economic profit. Accounting profit is still positive.

The Golden Rule: Profit Maximization

Profit Maximization Condition
MR = MC  AND  MC must be rising (not falling)
The second condition is critical — there can be two points where MR = MC; only the one where MC is rising is the profit-maximizing output.
💡 Memory Trick

Think of it as: "Where MR meets rising MC." The rising MC condition rules out the other intersection point Q' on the left side of the cost curve.

Perfect vs. Imperfect Competition — Revenue Curves

Perfect Competition: Firm is a price taker. Demand curve is perfectly horizontal at market price. TR curve is a straight line (linear) with slope = P. MR = AR = P at all output levels.

Imperfect Competition (Monopoly): Firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve. Price is a function of Q: P = f(Q). TR first rises (elastic demand, positive MR) then falls (inelastic demand, negative MR). MR curve lies below the demand curve.

💡 Trick to Remember MR Relationship

For a linear demand curve: the MR curve has the same intercept but twice the slope (falls twice as fast). So if demand is P = 450 – Q, then MR = 450 – 2Q. This is directly tested in Cournot problems!

02

Breakeven & Shutdown Points

Key Definitions

Breakeven Point
P = ATC (minimum ATC)
TR = TC → Economic profit = 0 (normal profit only). Firm SHOULD continue operating.
Shutdown Point
P = AVC (minimum AVC)
If P falls below min AVC, shut down immediately. Losses exceed fixed costs.

The Shutdown Decision Logic

P > ATC → Positive economic profit. Operate and earn profit.
AVC < P < ATC → Negative economic profit BUT continue operating in short run. Revenue covers all variable costs + part of fixed costs (sunk). Loss is less than fixed cost alone.
P < AVC → Shut down immediately. Cannot even cover variable costs. Losses are worse than just paying fixed costs.
💡 Intuition for Shutdown Rule

Fixed costs are sunk — you pay them whether you operate or not. The only question is: does producing cover variable costs? If yes, every unit sold contributes something toward those fixed costs. If no (P < AVC), you're better off not producing at all.

⚠ Exam Trap — Short Run vs. Long Run

In the short run: A firm may operate at a loss if P > AVC (fixed costs are sunk).
In the long run: A firm must earn at least zero economic profit or it exits the market. ALL costs are variable in the long run — nothing is sunk.

Profit / Loss Visual Summary

Condition Profit Status Short-Run Action Long-Run Action
P > ATC Positive economic profit Operate New firms enter → P falls
P = ATC Normal profit (breakeven) Operate Long-run equilibrium
AVC < P < ATC Negative economic profit Operate (P covers AVC) Exit industry
P < AVC Loss exceeds fixed costs Shut down Exit industry
🧠 Mnemonic — "SB" = Shutdown Below, Breakeven Best

Shutdown = below min AVC  |  Breakeven = at min ATC
The curve order from bottom to top: AVC → ATC → (with SMC passing through both minima). SMC crosses AVC at AVC's minimum, then crosses ATC at ATC's minimum.

03

Economies & Diseconomies of Scale

Short Run vs. Long Run

Short Run
  • At least one factor is fixed (capital, plant size)
  • Firm adjusts only variable inputs (e.g., labor)
  • Subject to diminishing marginal returns
  • Firm is always operating in short run
Long Run
  • All factors are variable
  • Firm can enter or exit the market
  • Firm chooses optimal plant size
  • Firm is always planning in long run

LRAC (Envelope Curve)

The Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC) curve is an "envelope" of all short-run average total cost (SATC) curves — it picks the minimum SATC for each output level across all possible plant sizes.

The firm chooses the plant size (small, medium, large) that minimizes cost at its planned output level. The LRAC connects the lowest points across all SATC curves.

Economies vs. Diseconomies of Scale

Economies of Scale
  • LRAC falls as output increases
  • Cost per unit decreases
  • LRAC slope is negative
  • Causes: specialization, bulk buying, technology, better management information, waste reduction, increasing returns to scale
  • Example: Walmart, electric utilities
Diseconomies of Scale
  • LRAC rises as output increases
  • Cost per unit increases
  • LRAC slope is positive
  • Causes: management complexity, product overlap, communication breakdowns, supply constraints, decreasing returns to scale
  • Example: Old General Motors
Minimum Efficient Scale
Minimum point on the LRAC curve
This is the optimal long-run firm size under perfect competition. Market forces drive firms to this point — firms not operating here are threatened long-term.
💡 LRAC Shape in Reality

The LRAC curve is often U-shaped or "hockey stick" shaped: economies of scale first (LRAC falls) → constant returns (flat range) → diseconomies of scale (LRAC rises). The flat portion is where the firm enjoys maximum efficiency over a range of output.

🧠 Mnemonic — From Most to Least Competitive: "PM-OM"

As you move right: fewer sellers, more pricing power, higher barriers, more economic moat.

5 Determinants of Market Structure

  1. Number and relative size of firms — more firms = more competition
  2. Degree of product differentiation — more differentiation = more pricing power
  3. Pricing power of the seller — price maker vs. price taker
  4. Barriers to entry and exit — capital requirements, patents, high exit costs
  5. Degree of non-price competition — advertising, branding, features

Porter's Five Forces (Analyst Perspective)

Porter's Five Forces is a practical framework for analysts assessing competitive dynamics and firm profitability:

  • Threat of new entrants
  • Power of suppliers
  • Power of buyers (customers)
  • Threat of substitutes
  • Rivalry among existing competitors
Note: "Power of suppliers" is the only force without a direct parallel in economic theory — but it has high practical relevance. The concept of an "economic moat" (deep competitive advantage = high barriers + locked-in customers) is rooted in Porter's framework.
05

Monopolistic Competition

Key Characteristics

  • Large number of buyers and sellers
  • Products are close substitutes but differentiated (each firm tries to appear unique)
  • Low barriers to entry and exit
  • Some pricing power due to differentiation
  • Non-price competition: advertising, branding (e.g., Harley-Davidson, Coca-Cola)
  • Downward-sloping demand curve (not perfectly flat like perfect competition)

Short-Run Equilibrium

Short-Run Profit Max
Produce where MR = MC → find Q₁
Read price P₁ from the Demand Curve at Q₁
Economic profit = (P₁ – C₁) × Q₁  where C₁ = ATC at Q₁. Can be positive, zero, or negative in SR.

Long-Run Equilibrium

Positive economic profit attracts new entrants → demand for each existing firm shrinks → economic profit falls to zero.

In long-run equilibrium: P₁ = C₁ = ATC at the optimal output Q₁. Economic profit = 0 (same as perfect competition in this regard).

Key difference from perfect competition: Q₁ in monopolistic competition is less than the minimum-ATC output (Q₂). The firm operates with excess capacity — it is not at the most efficient point on its cost curve.

⚠ Key Exam Point — Excess Capacity

In long-run equilibrium, a monopolistically competitive firm operates to the LEFT of minimum ATC. This means output is lower and price is higher than in perfect competition — this is the "cost" of variety and product differentiation.

💡 Why is Monopolistic Competition "Not So Bad"?

Even though prices are higher and output lower than perfect competition, monopolistic competition generates product variety and drives innovation (Schumpeter's innovation incentive). Think: restaurants, clothing, tech gadgets — variety has real consumer value.

06

Oligopoly & Pricing Strategies

Key Characteristics

  • Few firms dominating the market
  • Pricing decisions are interdependent — each firm considers rivals' reactions
  • High barriers to entry (capital, patents, brand)
  • Substantial pricing power — long-run economic profits possible
  • Products may be homogeneous (petroleum, cement) or differentiated (beverages, cereals)
  • No single well-defined supply function (unlike perfect competition)

Pricing Strategy 1 — Kinked Demand Curve

Core assumption: Rivals will match a price cut but ignore a price increase.

This creates two demand segments for the firm:

  • Above the prevailing price: Demand is highly elastic (rivals don't follow price increases → firm loses customers rapidly)
  • Below the prevailing price: Demand is inelastic (rivals match price cuts → firm gains few extra customers)

Result: A "kink" in the demand curve at the prevailing price, and a discontinuous (gap) in the MR curve. The MC can shift within this gap without changing the optimal price or output.

⚠ Limitation of Kinked Demand Curve

The kinked demand curve explains price stability in oligopolies but cannot determine the original prevailing price. It's an incomplete pricing theory — useful, but limited.

Pricing Strategy 2 — Cournot Assumption

Each firm assumes rivals will keep their output constant as it changes its own output (no strategic retaliation assumed). Best suited for quantity-setting duopolies.

Cournot Duopoly — Step-by-Step Method

1
Write total demand: QD = a – P → rearrange to P = a – QD = a – q₁ – q₂
2
Find TR for each firm: TR₁ = P × q₁ = (a – q₁ – q₂)q₁
3
Find MR for each firm: differentiate TR with respect to own output:
MR₁ = a – 2q₁ – q₂   MR₂ = a – q₁ – 2q₂
4
Set MR = MC for each firm and solve simultaneously (since q₁ = q₂ by symmetry in equal-cost duopoly).
5
Plug Q back into demand to find equilibrium price P.
Cournot Numeric Example (from module)
QD = 450 – P, MC = 30
P = 450 – q₁ – q₂  →  MR₁ = 450 – 2q₁ – q₂ = 30
By symmetry: q₁ = q₂ → 450 – 3q₁ = 30 → q₁ = 140
Q = 280, P = 450 – 280 = 170

Market Equilibrium Comparison

(P = MC)
170 Cournot Oligopoly
(2 firms, Q=280)
240 Monopoly
(Q=210, MR=MC)
💡 Cournot Key Insight

As the number of firms increases toward infinity, Cournot equilibrium approaches perfect competition. As the number decreases toward 1, it approaches monopoly. More firms → lower price, higher output.

Pricing Strategy 3 — Nash Equilibrium

Definition: A state where no firm can increase its profit by unilaterally changing its strategy, given the strategies of all other firms.

Each firm does the best it can given what rivals are doing. Actions are non-cooperative (each firm maximizes its own profit, not joint profit).

Nash Payoff Matrix (ArcCo vs BatCo)
Both Low: ArcCo=50, BatCo=70
ArcCo Low / BatCo High: ArcCo=80, BatCo=0
ArcCo High / BatCo Low: ArcCo=300, BatCo=350 ← Nash Equilibrium
Both High: ArcCo=500, BatCo=300
Nash Equilibrium: ArcCo High / BatCo Low → BatCo improves to 350 by going low when ArcCo plays high. Neither can unilaterally improve further → equilibrium.
💡 Why Not Both High Price? — The Collusion Temptation

Both high gives maximum joint profit (800), but it requires trust. BatCo can "cheat" and earn 350 vs. 300. This is the core instability of cartels — every member has an incentive to cheat. Collusion is most stable when: few firms, homogeneous products, similar costs, small/frequent orders, severe retaliation threat.

Stackelberg Model

Key difference vs. Cournot: Cournot = simultaneous decisions. Stackelberg = sequential decisions.

The leader firm moves first, choosing output. The follower firm observes and responds. The leader has a first-mover advantage — it earns more than in Cournot, while the follower earns less. The leader can overproduce to force the follower to scale back ("top dog" strategy).

Collusion & Cartels

When firms collude openly and formally → they form a cartel (e.g., OPEC). Collusion is generally illegal in most countries.

6 Factors that increase collusion success:

  1. Fewer, more similarly sized firms
  2. Homogeneous products
  3. Similar cost structures across firms
  4. Small and frequent orders (less incentive to cheat)
  5. Severe threatened retaliation
  6. Low degree of external competition

Long-Run Tendencies in Oligopoly

Economic profits attract new entrants over time → dominant firm's market share erodes → profits decline. Example: US Steel had 66% market share in 1901, falling to 42% by 1925.

Key rule: Price wars hurt all participants — any market share gain is temporary. Long-run pricing strategy must account for rivals' reactions. Innovation can maintain leadership but is costly.

⚠ Exam Trap — No Well-Defined Supply Curve in Oligopoly

Like monopolistic competition, oligopolists do NOT have a well-defined supply function. Optimal price and quantity depend jointly on demand conditions AND rivals' strategies — not just cost curves alone.

Identifying Market Structure & Concentration Measures

Concentration Ratio (CR)

Concentration Ratio Formula
CR(N) = Sum of market shares of the largest N firms
Range: 0% (perfect competition) to 100% (monopoly). Simple to compute. Common versions: CR3, CR4, CR10.
Advantages
  • Simple and fast to calculate
  • Easy to understand and communicate
  • Useful for quick market assessment
Disadvantages
  • Doesn't capture barriers to entry
  • Doesn't reflect demand elasticity
  • Insensitive to mergers among top firms
  • High CR doesn't automatically mean monopoly power

Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI)

HHI Formula
HHI = Σ (market shareᵢ)²
Market shares expressed as decimals (0 to 1).
HHI = 1 → Pure monopoly (one firm, 100% share)
HHI = 1/M → M firms with equal shares (e.g., 5 equal firms → HHI = 0.20)
HHI near 0 → highly competitive market

Numerical Example from Module:
Market shares: 35%, 25%, 20%, 10%, 2.5% × 4

CR3 = 35 + 25 + 20 = 80%    CR4 = 35 + 25 + 20 + 10 = 90%

HHI (top 3) = 0.35² + 0.25² + 0.20² = 0.1225 + 0.0625 + 0.04 = 0.225

After merger of top 2 firms (60% share):

CR3 = 60 + 20 + 10 = 90% (barely changed!)

HHI = 0.60² + 0.20² + 0.10² = 0.36 + 0.04 + 0.01 = 0.410 (nearly doubled!)

💡 Why HHI > CR for Mergers

When two large firms merge, CR barely moves (sums stay similar), but HHI jumps dramatically because squaring amplifies large shares. HHI is much more sensitive to market concentration changes — this is why regulators prefer it.

⚠ Limitations of Both Measures

Neither CR nor HHI accounts for barriers to entry or demand elasticity. A firm may be the only one in a market (high CR/HHI) yet behave competitively if new entrants can easily enter. The threat of entry can discipline pricing even without actual competition. Econometric elasticity estimation is theoretically superior but practically difficult due to endogeneity and data limitations.

Quick Reference — Analyst Application

When analyzing a firm for investment, ask:

  1. How many sellers? → Determines base structure
  2. Is the product differentiated? → Determines pricing power
  3. Are there regulatory constraints (monopoly case)? → Limits profit potential
  4. What is the threat from new entrants? → Determines long-run profit sustainability
  5. Does a proposed merger change HHI significantly? → Antitrust risk

Monopolists and oligopolists → potential for large margins and positive cash flows → attractive for equity investors but subject to regulatory risk.
