Standard I(A): Knowledge of the Law

The foundational compliance standard. Covers the relationship between the Code and applicable law, how to determine which governs, how to handle violations by others, and cross-border jurisdiction rules. 01

The Standard — Verbatim Text

Standard I(A) — Knowledge of the Law

Members and Candidates must understand and comply with all applicable laws, rules, and regulations (including the CFA Institute Code of Ethics and Standards of Professional Conduct) of any government, regulatory organization, licensing agency, or professional association governing their professional activities. In the event of conflict, Members and Candidates must comply with the more strict law, rule, or regulation. Members and Candidates must not knowingly participate or assist in and must dissociate from any violation of such laws, rules, or regulations.

I(A) is the foundational "compliance" standard. It establishes three separate obligations that must all be met:

  1. Understand and comply with applicable laws, rules, and regulations governing professional activities
  2. When laws conflict, comply with the more strict law or regulation
  3. Must not knowingly participate or assist in — and must dissociate from — any violation

The Code and Standards are included in the definition of "applicable law" — meaning members and candidates must comply with the Code even when it is stricter than local law, or when no local law applies.

02

Core Concepts — What "Applicable Law" Means in Practice

Scope of the Obligation

Members and candidates must understand laws and regulations of every country and jurisdiction where they engage in professional activities — which includes trading, investment advice, research, and other investment services. The obligation is to understand and comply on a reasonable and good faith basis, not to become a legal expert on every rule in every jurisdiction.

The standard does NOT require:

  • Becoming a compliance expert
  • Detailed knowledge of all laws that could potentially govern activities
  • Detailed knowledge of regulations outside the member's professional role (e.g., a portfolio manager doesn't need detailed knowledge of financial audit regulations)
💡 Role-Specific Knowledge

The Handbook clarifies that the knowledge obligation is role-specific. A junior analyst without supervisory responsibilities does not need detailed knowledge of employment law. A CFO responsible for regulatory reporting must know financial audit regulations in depth. The test: does the law directly relate to your professional responsibilities? If yes, you must know it.

Changing Regulations — Staying Current

During periods of regulatory change — which are frequent in financial services — members and candidates must remain vigilant. Sources of regulatory change include new financial products, uncovered ethical missteps, new communication platforms (social media, mobile apps), and global market developments. Members must update procedures and practices to remain compliant as requirements evolve.

Examiner Focus — Social Media and New Communication Channels

Both the LES and Handbook emphasise that new communication technologies (social media, mobile applications, web platforms) create ongoing compliance obligations. Failure to stay current on regulations governing these channels = I(A) violation. See Example 8 (White): posting investment recommendations on social media without understanding the regulatory framework governing such communications = I(A) violation. This is explicitly a "failure to maintain knowledge of the law" scenario.

03

Relationship between the Code and Applicable Law

The core rule: always follow the more strict of (a) applicable law or (b) the Code and Standards. This is the central decision framework for I(A) and it has several important implications:

  • If applicable law is stricter than the Code → follow applicable law
  • If the Code is stricter than applicable law → follow the Code
  • If there is no applicable law → follow the Code
  • The Code is the floor — you can never go below it regardless of what local law permits
The More Strict Rule — Decision Framework
Step 1: What does applicable law require?
Step 2: What does the Code and Standards require?
Step 3: Which imposes the greater restriction / greater protection of investors?
Step 4: Follow THAT one.

"More strict" = greater restrictions on the member OR greater protection for investors
Applicable law = floor if stricter; Code = floor if stricter
No applicable law = Code is the minimum standard
Key example: Applicable law may NOT require disclosure of referral fees. The Code DOES require disclosure. Because the Code imposes greater investor protection, members must disclose referral fees even if local law doesn't mandate it. The Code wins.
Conduct that violates the Code but is legal

Members and candidates must NOT engage in conduct that violates the Code even if it is otherwise legal. The Code sets ethical standards above the minimum legal threshold. Legality is the floor, not the ceiling.

Conduct that is illegal but Code-compliant

Cannot exist — if conduct is illegal, it cannot be Code-compliant. Applicable laws or regulations are themselves incorporated into what members and candidates must follow under I(A).

⚠️ Legal Counsel Does Not Absolve Responsibility

A key principle from Example 1 (Allen): seeking and receiving advice from legal counsel does NOT absolve a member or candidate from the obligation to comply with the law or regulation. If legal counsel says "it would be difficult to prove you were involved in wrongdoing" that is not the same as saying "what you did was legal" — and even if it were legal, members must still comply with the Code. Always seek independent legal opinion AND determine whether the regulator should be notified.

Examiner Focus — The One Pattern That Solves All Jurisdiction Questions

The table has 10 scenarios but ONE underlying logic: always follow the more strict of (a) whichever law is "applicable" based on the jurisdiction rule and (b) the Code and Standards. You don't need to memorise 10 rows. Step 1: determine which law is "applicable" (consider choice-of-law clauses). Step 2: compare that law's strictness to the Code. Step 3: follow whichever is stricter. When the applicable law points to a jurisdiction with NO law, the Code fills the gap by default.

The Karramba example (Example 5/Jameson): Jameson is registered in Karramba where IPO participation is prohibited. She is a US citizen at a US firm. She believes US law and the Code apply. WRONG — she is registered and working in Karramba, so Karrambian law applies. Since Karrambian law is stricter (total IPO prohibition vs. Code's disclosure requirement), she must follow Karrambian law. Being a US citizen or working for a US firm doesn't override where you are actually registered and operating.

05

Participation in or Association with Violations by Others

I(A) applies not only to your own conduct but to situations where you knowingly participate, assist, or are associated with violations by colleagues, employers, or clients. The standard uses "know or should know" — wilful blindness is not a defence.

Two key obligations:

  • Responsibility for knowing: Members and candidates are presumed to have knowledge of all applicable laws, rules, and regulations. If you know or should know your conduct contributes to a violation, I(A) applies.
  • Obligation when you discover a violation: You must not just report it — you must dissociate if the violation is not remedied.
💡 Who Counts as "Others" Under This Standard?

The Handbook specifies: colleagues, employers, and clients. This is important — if a client is engaged in illegal activity (e.g., money laundering, as in Pellie in Module 5), the member has an obligation under I(A) to comply with applicable laws (e.g., AML requirements) even when doing so conflicts with the client relationship. The duty of loyalty to clients under III(A) does not override the obligation to comply with the law.

⚠️ Inaction = Participation

This is one of the most tested principles in I(A). Inaction combined with continuing association with those involved in illegal or unethical conduct may be construed as participation or assistance in the illegal or unethical conduct. Simply knowing about a violation and continuing to work alongside it is not passive — it is a form of participation. The obligation is to actively dissociate.

Whistleblowing — Reporting to Regulators

CFA Institute distinguishes between two reporting obligations:

  • Reporting violations of the Code to CFA Institute: Strongly encouraged but not compelled. A failure to report is less likely to be a violation than a failure to dissociate.
  • Reporting to government/regulatory organizations: NOT compelled by the Code and Standards unless applicable law mandates such disclosure. However, whistleblowing may be prudent in certain circumstances, and members should consult legal and compliance advisers.

In cases where employer conduct is harming clients (as in the Kuznetsov vignette in Module 5), reporting to regulators to protect client interests is justified and does not violate IV(A) Loyalty to Employer — client interests override employer loyalty when the employer is actively harming clients.

06

Dissociation — How to Do It Correctly

When a member or candidate has reasonable grounds to believe that imminent or ongoing activities are illegal or unethical, dissociation — not just reporting — is required. Dissociation is a process, not a single action.

Step 1 — Direct Discussion

Attempt to stop the behavior by discussing it directly with the person(s) committing the violation. If this fails, escalate.

Step 2 — Internal Escalation

Bring it to the attention of a supervisor or the firm's compliance department. If this fails to produce adequate remediation, move to Step 3.

Step 3 — Dissociate

Step away from the activity. Forms of dissociation: remove name from reports/recommendations, ask for a different assignment, refuse to continue advising a client, or in extreme cases, leave the firm.

💡 Document the Violation

When dissociating from an activity that violates the Code and Standards, members and candidates should document the violation and urge their firms to attempt to persuade the perpetrators to cease the conduct. Documentation protects the member from being accused of participation and creates a record of the dissociation effort.

Examiner Focus — Adequacy of the Remedy

The Mandracken vignette (Module 5) is the canonical test of this concept. The key insight: if the proposed corrective action is inadequate, the member must dissociate — not implement the inadequate fix. In Mandracken, the supervisor proposed a partial fix (lower rates for new clients only, no remediation for past clients). This is inadequate. Mandracken must refuse to participate in client interactions using the fraudulent fee schedule. Implementing the inadequate fix while continuing to interact with clients = continuing participation in the violation. The standard is not "did the employer make some attempt to fix it?" — it is "is the remedy adequate to eliminate the violation?"

Examiner Focus — Escalation Before Extreme Steps

Brown (Example 2) discovers that a preliminary prospectus is misleading. The correct sequence: (1) report to appropriate supervisory persons in the firm, (2) if the matter is not remedied AND the employer does not dissociate from the underwriting, THEN Brown must sever all connections with the underwriting. Dissociation is graduated — you don't skip straight to leaving the firm. However, reporting to the board or regulators is also possible at later stages. The critical point: while you are pursuing intermediate steps, you must not continue to participate in the activity.

07

Investment Products and Cross-Border Compliance

Members and candidates involved in creating, maintaining, or distributing investment products must be mindful of the laws and regulations of:

  • The country of origination — where the product is created
  • The countries of distribution — where the product will be sold

Supervisors of product creation must understand applicable laws in all relevant jurisdictions. Members should make reasonable efforts to confirm that affiliated firms distributing their products also comply with distribution-country laws.

💡 The Islamic Law Example — Religious and Cultural Laws

The Janney example from the LES illustrates an important nuance: in a globalised market, members and candidates must be aware of not just governmental laws but also cultural and religious legal frameworks (e.g., Sharia-compliant investment criteria). If a product is distributed in a region where such considerations are material to investors, proactive disclosure of whether the product meets those criteria is good practice and protects both the firm and the investor. This is not strictly a compliance requirement but reflects the spirit of I(A)'s full disclosure and investor protection mandate.

08

Recommended Procedures for Compliance

For Members and Candidates
  • Stay informed: Establish procedures to be regularly informed about changes in applicable laws, rules, and regulations. Participate in internal or external CE programmes.
  • Review procedures: Regularly review the firm's written compliance procedures to ensure they reflect current law and are adequate.
  • Maintain current files: Keep readily accessible current reference copies of applicable statutes, rules, and regulations.
  • Seek advice: When in doubt, seek advice from compliance personnel or legal counsel. For potential violations by colleagues, consult the firm's compliance department.
  • Dissociate and document: When dissociating from a violation, document it.
  • Know distribution laws: Understand applicable laws for countries where investment products will be distributed.
For Firms (Members Should Encourage)
  • Code of ethics: Adopt a code of ethics for all employees — ethical culture starts at the top. CFA Institute has published the Asset Manager Code of Professional Conduct as a model.
  • Provide information on applicable laws: Distribute pertinent information highlighting applicable laws and regulations to employees; provide access from a central location.
  • Reporting procedures: Establish written protocols for reporting suspected violations of laws, regulations, or company policies — including anonymous reporting mechanisms where required.

Worked Examples — LES + Handbook

Example 1
Allen — Notification of Known Violations
VIOLATION
Allen works at a brokerage firm on a securities underwriting. A company official tells Allen that the financial statements filed with the regulator overstate the issuer's earnings. Allen consults the firm's general counsel, who says it would be "difficult for the regulator to prove" Allen's involvement in wrongdoing.
Allen must report this to his supervisor, seek an independent legal opinion, AND determine whether the regulator should be notified. He cannot rely on the general counsel's limited assurance as justification for inaction.
🔑 Core Rule
Legal counsel's opinion that wrongdoing is hard to prove does NOT satisfy the obligation to comply with the law. Two separate obligations: (1) comply with the law — which he isn't doing by remaining silent, and (2) consider notifying the regulator. Inaction after receiving comfortable legal advice = still a violation.
Example 2
Brown — Dissociating from a Violation (Underwriting)
DISSOCIATION REQUIRED
Brown's employer is the principal underwriter for convertible debentures. Brown discovers that the company concealed severe third-quarter losses from the preliminary prospectus, which has already been distributed.
Brown must report to appropriate supervisory persons. If the matter is not remedied AND the employer does not dissociate from the underwriting, Brown must sever ALL his connections with the underwriting. He should also seek legal advice about additional reporting obligations.
🔑 Core Rule
Knowing the prospectus is misleading = knowing the violation. Continuing association without dissociating = participation. The escalation must be attempted first, but if the firm doesn't act, Brown cannot continue. He cannot just "note his objection" and remain associated — full dissociation is required.
Example 3
Washington — Dissociating from Misleading Performance Material
VIOLATION IF CONTINUES
Washington's firm advertises its 10-year performance by omitting accounts that left the firm, inflating the return figure. The description of the composite says all firm accounts are included. Washington is asked to use this promotional material when soliciting business.
Using the inflated performance number = assisting in violating I(A). Washington must dissociate: bring it to her supervisor or compliance, and if the firm won't recalculate, she must refrain from using the misleading promotional material and notify the firm of her reasons. If the firm insists, she should consider whether dissociation requires seeking other employment.
🔑 Core Rules
(1) A member need not have originally created the violation to be responsible for it — using the misleading material creates its own violation. (2) The Handbook note: "she will assist in violating Standard I(A) if she were to use the inflated performance number" — the word "will" (not "might") reflects that using the material is itself a violation, not merely a risk. (3) Also implicates III(D) Performance Presentation.
Example 4
Collins — Following the Highest Requirements (Developing Country)
CODE GOVERNS
Collins is an investment analyst working in a developing country with minimal securities laws — no prohibition on insider trading. He possesses material nonpublic information.
Collins must comply with the Code and Standards, which are stricter than the local rules. He must follow Standard II(A) Material Nonpublic Information regardless of the absence of local insider trading prohibitions.
🔑 Core Rule
Absence of local law does not create permission to act contrary to the Code. The Code fills the vacuum — it is the minimum standard that always applies. The phrase "must abide by the requirements of the Code and Standards, which might be more strict than the rules of the developing country" is exactly the more-strict rule in action.
Example 5
Jameson / Karramba — Registered Adviser, Stricter Local Law
VIOLATION
Jameson is a US citizen at a US-based investment adviser, registered and working as an investment adviser in the island nation of Karramba. Karramba prohibits registered investment advisers from participating in IPOs. Jameson believes she only needs to follow US law and the Code's requirement to disclose IPO participation.
Jameson is in violation of I(A). As a registered investment adviser in Karramba, she is subject to Karrambian law. Karrambian law prohibits IPO participation entirely — which is stricter than the Code's disclosure-only requirement. She must follow the stricter Karrambian law, regardless of her US citizenship or employer's domicile.
🔑 Core Rule
Registration and place of business determine applicable law — not citizenship or employer domicile. The Code's IPO disclosure requirement is LESS strict than Karramba's total prohibition. "More strict" = total prohibition > disclosure-only requirement. So Karrambian law controls.
Example 6
White — Failure to Maintain Knowledge of the Law (Social Media)
VIOLATION
White posts investment information, performance reports, and investment opinions on her Facebook and X (Twitter) accounts. Before she started doing this, the local regulator issued new requirements governing online electronic communication. Her posts conflict with these recent regulatory announcements.
White violated I(A). Her communications do not comply with existing applicable regulation governing social media. The Handbook notes she must also be aware of I(E) Competence — appropriate knowledge of laws directly applicable to professional activities is required under both standards.
🔑 Core Rule
"Stay current" is an ongoing obligation, not a one-time check. Regulations evolve, especially in areas like social media and electronic communication. Failing to monitor and update compliance with evolving regulations = I(A) violation. Note the double violation potential: I(A) for non-compliance, I(E) for failure to maintain competence in applicable law.
Example 7 (Handbook-Only)
Scherzer — Knowledge of Applicable Law is Role-Specific
NOT A VIOLATION
Scherzer is a portfolio manager who assists with the firm's annual financial audit but whose primary role is portfolio management. He fails to properly collect and disclose certain information required by financial audit regulations while assisting the CFO.
Scherzer did NOT violate I(A). His primary professional responsibilities relate to portfolio management. Standard I(A) does not require Scherzer to have detailed knowledge of financial audit regulations — those are the CFO's domain. He can rely on the CFO to guide him on what information is required.
🔑 Core Rule
The Handbook's key addition: I(A) knowledge obligations are role-specific. You must know laws that directly relate to your professional responsibilities — but you are not required to be an expert on all laws that touch your workplace. The CFO, who is responsible for financial audit compliance, must know those regulations in depth. Scherzer, as portfolio manager, need not.
Example 8 (LES)
Blume — Reporting Suspected Unethical Activity
ENCOURAGED ACTION
Blume is a junior portfolio manager who suspects a broker is being paid for new business through inappropriate research payment allocations. The broker receives research payments but has no apparent research product and the portfolios don't invest in the broker's country. Her supervisor dismisses her concerns.
Blume should follow the firm's policies and procedures for reporting potential unethical activity, which may include further discussions with her supervisor or someone in a designated compliance department. She should communicate her concerns appropriately while advocating for disclosure between the new broker relationship and the research payments.
🔑 Core Rule
CFA Institute strongly encourages reporting potential violations but the Code does not compel reporting to regulatory organizations unless applicable law mandates it. However, this example shows the process: follow firm procedures, use compliance channels, and advocate for proper disclosure. Silence combined with continued association would risk participation in the violation.

Exam Checklist — Standard I(A): Knowledge of the Law

  • ✅ Three-part obligation: (1) Understand and comply with applicable laws. (2) When laws conflict, follow the more strict. (3) Do not knowingly participate, assist, or associate — must dissociate.
  • ✅ Applicable law includes the Code and Standards. Members must comply with the Code even when local law imposes no such requirement.
  • ✅ The more strict rule: Always follow whichever is stricter — applicable law or the Code. "More strict" = greater restrictions on the member OR greater investor protection.
  • ✅ No applicable law = follow the Code. Absence of local law does not permit conduct that violates the Code. The Code fills the vacuum.
  • ✅ Legally permitted ≠ Code-permitted. Members must not engage in conduct that violates the Code even if the conduct is otherwise legal.
  • ✅ Legal counsel's opinion does NOT absolve compliance obligation. Seek advice but also independently determine whether to report and how to comply. (Allen example)
  • ✅ Jurisdiction follows registration and place of business — not citizenship or employer domicile. (Karramba/Jameson example)
  • ✅ Knowledge obligation is role-specific. Must know laws that directly govern your professional responsibilities — not an expert on all workplace laws. (Scherzer example)
  • ✅ Inaction = participation. Continuing association with a known violation without dissociating = assisting in the violation.
  • ✅ Dissociation is a process: (1) Direct discussion → (2) Supervisor/compliance → (3) Step away from the activity (remove name, change assignment, refuse client, or leave firm). Document throughout.
  • ✅ Adequacy of the remedy matters. If the corrective action proposed is inadequate, the member must dissociate — not implement the inadequate fix. (Mandracken principle)
  • ✅ Whistleblowing to regulators is NOT compelled by the Code unless applicable law mandates it. But it may be prudent. Consult compliance/legal advisers.
  • ✅ CFA Institute reporting is strongly encouraged but not compelled. Failure to report is less likely to be a violation than failure to dissociate.
  • ✅ Using misleading materials created by others = your violation. You need not have created the violation to be responsible for perpetuating it. (Washington example)
  • ✅ Social media and new technology create ongoing compliance obligations. Failing to stay current on evolving regulations governing these channels = I(A) violation.
  • ✅ Cross-border products: Must know laws of both country of origination and countries of distribution. Confirm affiliated distributors also comply with distribution-country laws.
