Prepayment Risk — Contraction & Extension

Prepayment risk is the risk that the principal (or a portion of principal) is repaid at a different pace than the contractually scheduled repayment plan. MBS cash flows are uncertain because borrower behavior — early repayments, delinquencies, refinancing — depends on income, interest rates, loan terms, and property sales.

Prepayment risk has two components, both driven by changes in the general level of interest rates:

Contraction Risk — Rates Fall
  • Definition: Borrower repays principal faster than scheduled — shortening the MBS life.
  • Trigger: Interest rates decline → homeowners refinance at lower rates. Especially common in the US (few prepayment penalties).
  • Two adverse effects for investors:
    1. Must reinvest principal proceeds at the new, lower interest rates (reinvestment risk).
    2. The embedded prepayment option reduces potential price appreciation — the bond is "called away" from the investor when rates fall, just when its price would have risen most.
  • Memory: Rates ↓ → refis ↑ → MBS shortens → you get your money back at the worst time.
Extension Risk — Rates Rise
  • Definition: Borrower repays principal more slowly than scheduled — lengthening the MBS life.
  • Trigger: Interest rates rise → homeowners less likely to refinance. May also delay new home purchases. Prepayments slow or stop.
  • Adverse effect for investors: Higher discount rates reduce the present value of future cash flows. The investor is locked into receiving below-market cash flows for a longer period than expected — at exactly the time rates are higher.
  • Memory: Rates ↑ → no refis → MBS lengthens → you're stuck at below-market rates for longer.
💡 The Prepayment Risk Paradox — Investors Lose Both Ways

MBS investors face a double disadvantage: when rates fall, they experience contraction risk (principal returned early, must reinvest at lower rates — price appreciation capped). When rates rise, they experience extension risk (stuck with below-market cash flows for longer — duration extends painfully). This is called negative convexity: the MBS underperforms a standard bond in both rate directions. This is the fundamental pricing challenge of MBS compared to plain vanilla bonds.

⚠️ US vs. Europe — Prepayment Penalty Differences

In the US: few mortgages have prepayment penalties → refinancing is easy → contraction risk is high when rates fall. This is why US MBS markets need sophisticated structures (CMOs, PAC tranches) to manage prepayment risk. In Europe: prepayment penalty mortgages are common → they compensate lenders for the difference between contract rate and prevailing rate → reduces borrower incentive to prepay → lower contraction risk. Know this geographic difference for the exam.

Time Tranching — Redistributing Prepayment Risk

Time Tranching — Definition
  • Time tranching creates bond classes with different expected maturities from the same collateral pool. Principal repayments flow first to one tranche until fully repaid, then to the next tranche sequentially.
  • This redistributes prepayment risk rather than eliminating it — short tranches bear contraction risk protection while long tranches bear extension risk protection, and each tranche insulates others.
  • The classic implementation is the sequential-pay CMO: principal goes to Tranche A first (retires it), then to Tranche B, then C, etc.
  • A securitization can have both credit tranching (subordination) AND time tranching (different maturities) simultaneously — this is quite common.
RLT MBS — Time Tranching Example (Residential Loan Trust)
Tranche A: EUR 825m | MRR + 0.50% | Maturity: 1 year (senior + shortest)
Tranche B: EUR 100m | MRR + 1.50% | Maturity: 2 years
Tranche C: EUR 50m | MRR + 2.50% | Maturity: 3 years
Tranche D: EUR 25m | Variable | Maturity: 4 years (junior + longest)

PREPAYMENT IMPACT (if rates fall in Year 2 → immediate prepayments):
Tranche A: Repaid in 1 year regardless (already shortest — no change)
Tranche B: Repaid LESS THAN 2 years (prepayments accelerate principal → contraction risk)
Tranche C: Repaid LESS THAN 3 years (same — contraction risk for longer tranches too)
Tranche D: Repaid LESS THAN 4 years (same)

KEY: Each tranche's principal repayment date is uncertain because it depends on actual prepayment rates.
💡 Credit Tranching vs. Time Tranching — Side by Side

Credit tranching (subordination): addresses who bears credit losses first. Junior tranches absorb defaults before senior tranches. Purpose: reduce credit risk for senior investors.

Time tranching (sequential-pay): addresses who receives principal first. Short-maturity tranches are fully retired before long-maturity tranches receive principal. Purpose: redistribute prepayment risk — short tranches get protection from extension risk; long tranches get protection from contraction risk.

Most real-world MBS combine both — a single securitization can have multiple tranches with different maturities AND different seniority (credit rank).

Mortgage Loan Fundamentals

Mortgage Loan — Core Definition & Features
  • Mortgage loan: A loan secured by the collateral of a specified real estate property. The lender holds a first lien and security interest in the property — the right to seize collateral if the borrower doesn't pay as agreed.
  • Foreclosure: Upon default, the lender can foreclose on the property and sell it to recover the outstanding loan balance. Proceeds may be insufficient to cover the full outstanding balance.
  • Down payment: The borrower typically pays a portion of the purchase price upfront. The remainder is the mortgage loan. The ratio of loan amount to property value is the LTV ratio.
  • Fully amortizing mortgages: Each payment includes both interest AND principal. Over time, the interest portion decreases and the principal portion increases (standard mortgage structure). Total monthly payment remains constant.
  • Prepayment option: The borrower's right to repay all or part of the outstanding principal before maturity. This creates prepayment risk for the lender/investor.

Recourse vs. Non-Recourse Loans — Critical Distinction

Recourse Loan

Lender has a claim against the borrower personally for any shortfall between the outstanding mortgage balance and proceeds from foreclosure sale. Lender can go after the borrower's other assets and income.

Effect: Borrower is less likely to strategically default — they remain personally liable for the gap even after losing the property.

Common in: Most European countries, and some US states.

Non-Recourse Loan

Lender can ONLY look to the property (collateral) for recovery. No personal claim against the borrower for the deficiency. If the property sale proceeds < outstanding balance, lender absorbs the loss.

Effect: Creates incentive for strategic default when mortgage is "underwater" (LTV > 100%) — borrower may walk away even if they could afford to pay.

Common in: Many US states (determined state by state).

⚠️ Strategic Default on Underwater Mortgages — GFC Example

During the 2008–09 GFC, nearly 10.7 million US residential properties (23%) were underwater (LTV > 100%). The key GFC factors: loose credit underwriting 2004–2006 + inflated home prices + declining prices + high leverage on non-recourse loans. With non-recourse loans, borrowers had an incentive to walk away even if they could pay — strategic default. States with the highest negative equity: Nevada (65%), Arizona (48%), Florida (45%), Michigan (37%), California (35%). Investors default at 2–3% higher rates than owner-occupiers in negative equity — especially in non-recourse states.

Key Ratios — LTV, DTI, and DSC (CMBS)

Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV) — Residential & Commercial
LTV = Loan Amount ÷ Property Value (or Appraised Value)

Example: EUR600,000 loan ÷ EUR800,000 property = 75% LTV

LTV ↓ → Borrower equity ↑ → Less likely to default → More protection for lender
LTV ↑ → Less equity cushion → Higher default risk → Less collateral protection
LTV > 100% → "Underwater mortgage" → Strategic default risk (especially non-recourse loans)
LTV changes over time: as the borrower makes principal payments → outstanding balance ↓ → LTV ↓. But if property value falls → LTV ↑ (can go above 100% = underwater).
Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI) — Residential
DTI = Monthly Debt Payment ÷ Monthly Pre-tax Gross Income

Example (from LES): Monthly payment = EUR3,795.90 | Annual income = EUR141,000
DTI = EUR3,795.90 ÷ (EUR141,000 ÷ 12) = EUR3,795.90 ÷ EUR11,750 = 32.31%

Low DTI → Good balance of income vs. debt → Can sustain additional debt → Lower default risk
High DTI → Too much debt relative to income → Struggling to service debt → Higher default risk
Lenders want LOW DTI before extending mortgage loans.
DTI uses MONTHLY gross (pre-tax) income in the denominator. It compares ongoing monthly debt obligations to the borrower's earning capacity. Prime loans → low DTI. Subprime loans → high DTI.
Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSC / DSCR) — Commercial Only
DSC = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Debt Service

NOI = Rental Income − Cash Operating Expenses − Replacement Reserves
(NOI excludes: principal + interest payments, capital expenditures, depreciation, amortization)
Debt Service = Annual interest payments + principal repayments

DSC > 1.0× → Property cash flows COVER debt service (property is self-sustaining)
DSC < 1.0× → Property cash flows INSUFFICIENT to cover debt → Default risk high
Higher DSC → More comfortable coverage → Lower lender/investor risk

Example (LES): NOI = GBP7,590,000 | Debt Service = GBP4,717,169
DSC = 7,590,000 ÷ 4,717,169 = 1.61× ✓ (sufficient coverage)
DSC is used for COMMERCIAL mortgages (CMBS). The commercial analog to DTI for residential mortgages. Commercial mortgages are non-recourse → lender can ONLY look to property cash flows → DSC is the primary measure of repayment capacity.
💡 Residential vs. Commercial — Which Ratio to Use?

Residential mortgages → LTV + DTI. Capacity assessment: can the individual borrower service the debt from personal income? DTI measures this. LTV measures collateral cushion.

Commercial mortgages → LTV + DSC. Capacity assessment: can the property itself generate enough cash flow to service the debt? DSC measures this. LTV measures collateral cushion. Critically: commercial mortgages are non-recourse → the property's cash flow IS the repayment source → DSC is paramount.

WAC and WAM — Pass-Through Pool Metrics

WAC (Weighted Average Coupon) and WAM (Weighted Average Maturity)
WAC = Σ [ iₙ × (CBₙ ÷ Σ CB) ]
(weight = each mortgage's current balance / total current balance of all mortgages in pool)

WAM = Σ [ MMₙ × (CBₙ ÷ Σ CB) ]
(weight = same as WAC; MMₙ = months to maturity for mortgage n)

Example (5 mortgages, total current balance = EUR1,149,000):
A: 2.50% × (238/1,149) B: 3.30% × (380/1,149) C: 2.80% × (87/1,149)
D: 4.00% × (132/1,149) E: 3.70% × (312/1,149)
WAC = 3.29%

WAM: 180×(238/1,149) + 480×(380/1,149) + 240×(87/1,149) + 120×(132/1,149) + 312×(312/1,149)
WAM = 313 months
Pass-through rate (coupon to investor) = WAC minus servicing/admin/guarantee fees. The investor receives the "net coupon" or "net interest" — always lower than the WAC. The difference (servicing spread) covers all pool administration costs.
Weighted average life (WAL) accounts for both scheduled and unscheduled (prepayment) principal repayments — a better measure of when an MBS will actually be paid off than WAM. WAM uses scheduled maturities only.

⚠️ Non-Agency RMBS Nearly Disappeared After the GFC

Due to tighter regulatory standards and increased scrutiny post-GFC, non-agency RMBS (private label MBS) almost completely vanished from the market. Pre-GFC, they securitized subprime and non-conforming mortgages with inadequate credit enhancement. Now returning in small volumes — primarily for non-conforming (jumbo) loans and residential home improvement loans (including subordinated solar ABS liens). Know: non-agency = no government guarantee = dependent on private credit enhancements for investor protection.

Mortgage Pass-Through Securities

Mortgage Pass-Through — Core Definition
  • Created when mortgage lenders pool mortgages and sell securities backed by that pool to investors.
  • Cash flows from the pool — monthly principal, interest, and prepayments — are passed through to security holders proportionally.
  • A pool can contain several thousand mortgages (large, diversified) or just a few (small, concentrated).
  • Pass-through rate = coupon rate received by investors. Always LOWER than the WAC of the pool because administrative/servicing/guarantee fees are deducted. The investor receives the "net coupon" or "net interest."
  • Mortgages in the pool are heterogeneous — different rates, balances, maturities. Hence WAC and WAM are used to summarize pool characteristics.
  • Weighted Average Life (WAL): The measure of when the MBS is expected to be paid off, accounting for both scheduled principal and prepayments. More useful than WAM for MBS valuation since WAM ignores prepayments.
Pass-Through Structure — Cash Flow Example (from LES, Exhibit 2)
Mortgages pool → SPE (Pass-Through Entity)

ASSETS: Mortgages | WAC = 4.55%
LIABILITIES: RMBS pass-through securities | Pass-through rate = 4.00%
Servicing fees = 0.55% (the spread between WAC and pass-through rate)

Cash flows from borrowers: Principal + Interest + Prepayments
→ Less servicing and admin fees (0.55%)
→ Passed to RMBS investors as: Principal + coupon (4.00%) + prepayments less fees

The servicing fee covers: collecting monthly payments, forwarding to investors,
sending payment notices, maintaining records, initiating foreclosure, tax info,
plus any guarantee fees charged by the issuer or financial guarantor.

Collateralized Mortgage Obligations (CMOs) — All Structures

CMO — Overview
  • CMOs securitize mortgage pass-through securities or multiple pools of loans, and are structured to redistribute cash flows to different tranches — creating securities with different exposures to prepayment risk.
  • CMOs cannot eliminate prepayment risk — they can only redistribute it. The total prepayment risk of the collateral pool is preserved; it is just shifted across tranches.
  • Key principle: the more senior a tranche, the less exposure it has to both prepayment risk AND default risk.
  • CMO tranching structures reduce the uncertainty around the size and timing of payments investors receive.
  • Features are designed to meet the specific needs of institutional investors with different duration/risk preferences.

① Sequential-Pay CMO

①
Mechanism: All principal payments go to Tranche A first, until Tranche A's principal is fully repaid (retired). Then all principal payments go to Tranche B, then C, etc. All tranches receive interest throughout. This is pure time tranching.
②
Prepayment risk redistribution: Tranche A (short) is protected against extension risk — it receives all principal first, so even in a slow prepayment environment, it is retired before B and C. Tranches B and C are protected against contraction risk — they don't receive principal until A is fully retired, so fast prepayments only accelerate A's retirement, not theirs.
③
Investor matching: Pension fund needing long-duration → Tranche C. Endowment financing near-term projects → Tranche A. Long-term risk-tolerant investor → Tranche C (highest prepayment risk, most uncertainty).
Sequential-Pay CMO — LES Example (Exhibit 6)
Collateral: USD100m RMBS pass-through | WAC = 4.55% | WAM = 360 months | Pass-through rate = 4%

Tranche A: USD50m | Coupon: 3.75% | Expected maturity: 0–5 years
Tranche B: USD30m | Coupon: 3.95% | Expected maturity: 6–15 years
Tranche C: USD20m | Coupon: 4.05% | Expected maturity: 16–30 years

Cash flow rules:
Years 0–5: A receives principal+interest; B and C receive interest only
Years 6–15: B receives principal+interest; C receives interest only
Years 16–30: C receives principal+interest

Note: Higher tranche coupon rates reflect longer maturity + more prepayment risk (term structure).

② PAC Tranche (Planned Amortization Class)

PAC Tranche + Support Tranche
  • PAC tranche: Makes scheduled and fixed principal payments over a predetermined period — IF prepayment rates stay within a specified range (minimum and maximum PSA band).
  • If prepayments fall within the PAC band: all prepayment risk is absorbed by the support tranche — PAC investors receive their scheduled payments with high certainty.
  • PAC tranches offer greater predictability and stability of cash flows — ideal for insurance companies, pension funds, and other liability-matching investors.
  • Support tranche: Acts as a buffer. If prepayments are faster than the upper PAC band → support tranche absorbs excess (contraction risk). If prepayments are slower than the lower PAC band → support tranche is starved of principal (extension risk). Support tranche investors bear far more prepayment risk in exchange for higher yields.
  • If actual prepayments move outside the PAC band, the PAC tranche itself faces prepayment risk — but this requires extreme market conditions.

💡 IO vs. PO — The Rate/Prepayment Relationship Is Opposite

PO (Principal-Only): Rates ↓ → prepayments ↑ → principal returned faster → PO price ↑. Rates ↑ → prepayments slow → PO price ↓.
IO (Interest-Only): Rates ↓ → prepayments ↑ → less outstanding balance → less interest earned → IO cash flows ↓ → IO price ↓. Rates ↑ → prepayments slow → more interest for longer → IO price ↑.
IO has a positive relationship with interest rates (unusual for fixed income). IOs are used to HEDGE portfolios against rising rates. POs are used to make a bullish bet on falling rates / accelerating prepayments.

Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities (CMBS)

CMBS — Core Definition & Collateral
  • CMBS are backed by a pool of commercial mortgages on income-producing properties: apartment buildings (multifamily), office buildings, industrial/warehouse properties, shopping centers, hotels, healthcare facilities.
  • Repayment comes from leases and other property revenue. Commercial loans are originated either to finance property acquisitions or refinance prior mortgages.
  • CMBS securitization process is highly similar to RMBS. However, two critical features distinguish CMBS: call protection and balloon maturity provision.
  • Commercial mortgages are non-recourse → lender can ONLY look to the income-producing property for repayment. This makes DSC the primary underwriting metric.
  • Concentration risk: CMBS pools may contain only a few loans — one default can significantly impact all CMBS investors (unlike RMBS with thousands of small, diversified loans).

① Call Protection — CMBS vs. RMBS

The biggest structural difference from RMBS: CMBS offer call protection — protection against early prepayments. This makes CMBS trade more like corporate bonds than RMBS. Call protection comes at two levels:

A
Structural Call Protection (tranche level): Sequential-pay tranches — a lower-rated tranche cannot be paid down until all higher-rated tranches are completely retired. Principal losses are always borne by junior tranches first. This provides a structural barrier to prepayment affecting senior tranches.
B1
Loan-Level Call Protection — Prepayment Lockout: A contractual covenant that prohibits ANY prepayments during a specified period. Absolute protection during the lockout window.
B2
Loan-Level Call Protection — Prepayment Penalty Points: Predetermined penalties the borrower must pay if they want to refinance. One "point" = 1.00% of the outstanding loan balance. Makes prepayment economically unattractive.
B3
Loan-Level Call Protection — Defeasance: Borrower CAN prepay but must purchase a portfolio of government securities that fully replicates the remaining cash flows (principal + interest + balloon balance) of the loan. When the last obligation is paid off, the portfolio value is zero — no cash left over. The cost of buying this replicating portfolio = cost of defeasing the loan, paid by the issuer. This compensates the lender fully while allowing the borrower to exit.
💡 Defeasance — The Elegant CMBS Call Protection Mechanism

Defeasance is clever: the borrower doesn't just pay a penalty — they must substitute an exact government bond portfolio for the remaining loan cash flows. From the CMBS SPE's perspective, nothing changes — it continues to receive the exact same scheduled payments, now from government bonds instead of the original mortgage. The borrower gets to exit the loan; the CMBS investor gets no cash flow disruption. The cost of assembling the government bond portfolio is the "price" of defeasing.

② Balloon Maturity Provision & Balloon Risk

Balloon Risk — A Type of Extension Risk Unique to CMBS
  • Commercial mortgage structure: Unlike self-amortizing residential mortgages, commercial loans are NOT fully amortizing. They pay interest + some principal throughout the loan term, but the bulk of the principal is due as a large balloon payment at maturity (e.g., end of year 5 or year 10).
  • Balloon risk: The risk that the borrower FAILS to make the balloon payment at maturity and defaults. Three reasons this can happen:
    1. Borrower cannot refinance (deteriorating DSC or LTV ratios make lenders unwilling to extend new credit).
    2. Market conditions prevent refinancing (tight credit environment).
    3. Borrower cannot sell the property to generate sufficient proceeds.
  • Workout period: When balloon payment fails, the lender may extend the loan and modify its terms over a "workout period." This extension of the loan life makes balloon risk a form of extension risk.
  • Why balloon risk matters: CMBS investors (especially junior tranche holders) face significant extension risk that is different in nature from RMBS extension risk — it's triggered by borrower inability to refinance or sell, not just interest rate levels.

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Prepayment risk = risk that principal is repaid at a different pace than scheduled. Two components: (1) Contraction risk — repaid faster (rates fall → refinancing). (2) Extension risk — repaid slower (rates rise → no refinancing).
  • ✅ Contraction risk (rates fall): MBS life shortens. Investor reinvests at lower rates. Price appreciation capped (bond is "called" away). US mortgages especially vulnerable (few prepayment penalties). Europe: prepayment penalties common → lower contraction risk.
  • ✅ Extension risk (rates rise): MBS life lengthens. Investor locked into below-market cash flows for longer, discounted at higher rates → lower PV. Both risks make MBS exhibit negative convexity.
  • ✅ Time tranching = bond classes with different expected maturities. Principal payments flow sequentially (shorter tranche first). Redistributes (does NOT eliminate) prepayment risk. Short tranches protected against extension risk by longer tranches. Long tranches protected against contraction risk by short tranches.
  • ✅ Credit tranching vs. time tranching: Credit tranching = who bears LOSSES first (junior tranches). Time tranching = who receives PRINCIPAL first (short tranches). Both can exist simultaneously in one securitization.
  • ✅ LTV = Loan Amount ÷ Property Value. Lower LTV → more borrower equity → less default risk. LTV > 100% = underwater mortgage → strategic default risk (especially non-recourse loans).
  • ✅ DTI = Monthly Debt Payment ÷ Monthly Pre-tax Gross Income. Used for RESIDENTIAL. Low DTI → can service debt. High DTI → overextended. Subprime loans: high LTV + high DTI.
  • ✅ DSC = NOI ÷ Debt Service. Used for COMMERCIAL (CMBS only). NOI = Rental income − cash operating expenses − replacement reserves. DSC > 1.0× → sufficient cash flow coverage. Commercial mortgages are non-recourse → property cash flow is the ONLY repayment source → DSC is critical.
  • ✅ WAC = Σ[interest_rate_n × (CB_n ÷ total CB)]. Weighted by current balance. WAM = Σ[months_to_maturity_n × (CB_n ÷ total CB)]. Weighted by current balance. Pass-through rate = WAC minus servicing/admin/guarantee fees ("net coupon").
  • ✅ Agency RMBS: Sector 1 (federal agency — full faith + credit guarantee); Sector 2 (GSEs — GSE guarantee, fee charged, not full government backing). Agency = conforming mortgages, virtually no credit risk. Non-agency RMBS = private label, no government guarantee, subprime/non-conforming collateral, relies on credit enhancements. Largely disappeared after GFC.
  • ✅ Mortgage pass-through security: Pool of mortgages → SPE → RMBS issued to investors. Monthly principal + interest + prepayments passed through (minus servicing fees). Pass-through rate < WAC (fees deducted). Heterogeneous pool → use WAC and WAM to summarize.
  • ✅ Sequential-pay CMO: All principal to Tranche A until retired → then B → then C. Interest paid to all throughout. Short tranche: protected against extension risk; long tranche: protected against contraction risk. CMO distributes prepayment risk — doesn't eliminate it.
  • ✅ PAC tranche: Fixed scheduled principal payments if prepayment rate stays within a specified PSA band. Support tranche absorbs all prepayment variability within the band. PAC = most predictable cash flows of any CMO tranche (good for liability matching). Support tranche = highest prepayment risk.
  • ✅ Z-tranche (accrual bond): No cash interest until preset date — interest accrues to principal. Then both P+I paid. Avg life 20+ years. No reinvestment risk (no coupon to reinvest). Very hard to value. Last in sequential or PAC structure. Frees up cash for other tranches during accrual period.
  • ✅ PO (Principal-Only): Receives all principal; no coupon. Value ↑ when rates fall (faster prepayments). Value ↓ when rates rise (slower prepayments). Bought at steep discount; return comes from capital appreciation as principal is received.
  • ✅ IO (Interest-Only): Receives all interest; no principal (no face value). Value ↑ when rates rise (slower prepayments → more interest for longer). Value ↓ when rates fall (faster prepayments → less interest). IOs HEDGE against falling rates — unusual positive relationship with rates. Used to hedge MBS portfolios against reinvestment risk.
  • ✅ CMBS: Backed by commercial mortgages on income-producing properties. Non-recourse (lender limited to property). Key metrics: LTV + DSC. Concentration risk: fewer loans than RMBS → one default = significant impact. Must analyze each loan, property, and borrower separately.
  • ✅ CMBS call protection: Structural (sequential-pay tranches) + Loan-level (prepayment lockout | prepayment penalty points (1 point = 1% of balance) | defeasance (borrower buys govt bonds replicating remaining cash flows)). Result: CMBS trades like a corporate bond (predictable cash flows) rather than RMBS.
  • ✅ Balloon risk: Commercial loans have balloon payment at maturity (not fully amortizing). Balloon risk = borrower fails balloon payment → lender extends loan via workout period → balloon risk is a form of EXTENSION risk. Causes: can't refinance, tight credit, can't sell property.
  • ✅ CMBS vs. RMBS prepayment risk: CMBS: low contraction risk (call protection) + high extension/balloon risk. RMBS: high contraction risk (no prepayment penalties, especially US) + high extension risk (when rates rise). Both have extension risk but from different mechanisms.
  • ✅ Recourse distinction: Recourse (Europe, some US states) — lender claims borrower's other assets for shortfall → reduces strategic default. Non-recourse (many US states) — lender limited to property → enables strategic default when underwater. Commercial mortgages = non-recourse in almost all cases.
★ COMPLETE QUICK REFERENCE SUMMARY ★
═══ PREPAYMENT RISK ═══
Contraction: Rates ↓ → refis ↑ → MBS shortens → reinvest at lower rates + price appreciation capped
Extension: Rates ↑ → refis ↓ → MBS lengthens → locked in below-market rates longer
Both cause NEGATIVE CONVEXITY — MBS underperforms plain bonds in both rate directions
US: few prepayment penalties → high contraction risk. Europe: penalties common → lower risk.

═══ KEY RATIOS ═══
LTV = Loan Amount ÷ Property Value [residential + commercial; lower = safer]
DTI = Monthly Debt Payment ÷ Monthly pre-tax gross income [residential only]
DSC = NOI ÷ Debt Service [commercial CMBS only; DSC > 1.0× = sufficient coverage]
NOI = Rental income − cash operating expenses − replacement reserves
WAC = Σ[rate_n × CB_n/ΣCB] [pool's weighted average coupon]
WAM = Σ[months_n × CB_n/ΣCB] [pool's weighted average maturity]
Pass-through rate = WAC − servicing/admin/guarantee fees (investor's net coupon)

═══ CMO TRANCHE CHEAT SHEET ═══
Sequential A (short): LOW extension risk | HIGH contraction risk
Sequential C (long): HIGH extension risk | LOW contraction risk
PAC: Fixed schedule within PSA band | Support bears ALL prepayment variability
Support: HIGHEST prepayment risk of any tranche (bears both sides for PAC)
Z-tranche: No cash until preset date; accrues to principal; 20+ yr avg life; no reinvestment risk
PO: Principal only; Value ↑ rates ↓ (faster prepayment); Value ↓ rates ↑
IO: Interest only; Value ↑ rates ↑ (slower prepayment = more interest); Value ↓ rates ↓
Residual: Excess cash; equity-like; highest risk; hedge funds only

═══ CMBS CALL PROTECTION (4 TYPES) ═══
① Structural: Sequential-pay (junior not paid until senior retired)
② Lockout: No prepayments allowed during specified period
③ Penalty points: 1 point = 1% of outstanding balance (economic deterrent)
④ Defeasance: Buy govt bonds replicating remaining cash flows (most complete protection)
Result: CMBS trades like a corporate bond (low contraction risk)

═══ BALLOON RISK (CMBS ONLY) ═══
Commercial loans NOT fully amortizing → large balloon payment at maturity (yr 5 or 10)
Balloon risk = fail balloon payment → workout period (loan extended) → TYPE OF EXTENSION RISK
Causes: can't refinance (bad DSC/LTV) | tight credit market | can't sell property

═══ CMBS vs RMBS — KEY DIFFERENCES ═══
CMBS: Low contraction risk | High balloon/extension risk | Concentration risk | Trades like bonds
RMBS: High contraction risk (US) | High extension risk (rates rise) | Diversified pool | Negative convexity
Sequential-pay CMO one-liner: Short tranche protected from extension (longer tranches buffer it). Long tranche protected from contraction (shorter tranches absorb fast prepayments first). CMO redistributes but does NOT eliminate prepayment risk.
PAC one-liner: PAC is the predictable tranche; support is the shock absorber. Within the PSA band → support takes all variability. Outside the band → PAC itself faces prepayment risk.
IO vs. PO memory: IO and PO behave OPPOSITE to each other. When rates fall → PO ↑, IO ↓. When rates rise → PO ↓, IO ↑. IOs hedge MBS portfolios; POs are bullish rate bets.
