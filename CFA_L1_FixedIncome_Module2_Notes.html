Cash Flow Structures — Overview

A bond's cash flow structure is determined by how its principal is repaid and how its interest is calculated and paid. The bullet bond is the most common structure — the foundation against which all others are compared.

StructurePrincipal RepaymentInterestKey Examples
Bullet Bond 100% at maturity (lump sum) Fixed periodic coupon (interest only) Most government and corporate bonds
Fully Amortizing Equal portions every period; nothing at maturity Declining interest + rising principal = level payment Residential mortgages, commercial real estate loans
Partially Amortizing Partial over life + balloon payment at maturity Declining as principal reduces; higher than bullet early Some corporate bonds, commercial mortgages
Sinking Fund Issuer sets aside funds periodically to retire portions early Fixed or variable; interest paid on remaining principal Some government bonds, certain corporate bonds
Waterfall (ABS/MBS) Sequential by tranche priority (senior → junior) Interest paid to all tranches simultaneously; principal sequential Asset-backed securities, mortgage-backed securities
Floating-Rate Note (FRN) At maturity (like bullet) Variable = MRR + credit spread; resets periodically Bank loans, corporate FRNs
Step-Up Bond At maturity Increases by specified amounts on scheduled dates Some corporate and sovereign bonds
Index-Linked Bond Inflation-adjusted at maturity (capital-indexed) Fixed rate on adjusted principal, or inflation-adjusted coupon TIPS (US), linkers (UK/Australia/Canada)
Zero-Coupon Bond Par at maturity No periodic payments; issued at discount to par Short-term government T-bills; strips
Deferred Coupon Bond At maturity No interest for first few years; higher coupon later Construction project financing; some high-yield issuers
PIK Bond At maturity (but principal grows) Paid by increasing the principal outstanding, not cash Highly leveraged issuers; LBO financing

Amortizing Debt — Structures & Trade-offs

★ Fully Amortizing Periodic Payment Formula
A = (r × Principal) / [1 − (1 + r)^(−N)]

Where:
A = periodic payment amount (constant throughout)
r = market interest rate per period
Principal = original loan/bond principal
N = total number of payment periods

BRWA example: Principal = 300, r = 1.6% per period, N = 10
A = (0.016 × 300) / [1 − (1.016)^(−10)] = 32.7 per period
The payment A is CONSTANT each period (level annuity). However, what changes each period:
— Interest portion = r × Remaining principal (DECLINES each period as principal reduces)
— Principal portion = A − Interest portion (INCREASES each period)
This mirrors the structure of mortgage loan amortization schedules.

Comparison: Bullet vs. Partially vs. Fully Amortizing (BRWA Example, USD millions)

0.5 → 4.54.8 (interest only)18.75 (interest + partial principal)32.7 (level payment)
5.0 (maturity)304.8 (coupon + full principal)168.75 (coupon + balloon 50% principal)32.7 (final level payment)
Amortization Benefits for Investors
  • Lower credit risk: borrower's outstanding liability shrinks over time — less left to default on
  • Higher near-term cash flows vs. bullet bonds
  • More predictable return of capital
Amortization Risks for Investors
  • Higher reinvestment risk: higher near-term cash flows must be reinvested at prevailing (potentially lower) market rates
  • Reduces effective duration — price sensitivity to rate changes lower than equivalent bullet

Sinking Fund

A sinking fund arrangement has the issuer periodically setting aside funds in an escrow account to retire portions of the bond's principal early. Mechanics:

  • Issuer directs the bond trustee to redeem a predetermined principal amount selected at random from investors
  • Alternatively, issuer may have the right to repurchase bonds at a predetermined fixed price (a call-like feature)
  • Reduces credit risk (same as amortization — principal reduced over time)
  • Increases reinvestment risk (same reasoning — investors receive early principal returns)
  • Used primarily by government issuers and some corporate issuers

Waterfall Structure (ABS / MBS)

A waterfall determines the timing of principal repayments to investor classes with different seniority claims. Most common structure:

  • Interest: paid to ALL tranches simultaneously (no preference)
  • Principal: paid sequentially — most senior tranche (A) is fully repaid first, then Tranche B, then Tranche C
  • Payment shortfalls are borne by the most junior tranches first — Tranche A has lowest credit risk, Tranche C has highest
  • Used in asset-backed securities (ABS) and mortgage-backed securities (MBS)
💡 Key Distinction — Sinking Fund vs. Call Provision

Both retire bond principal early, but they differ in mechanism. Sinking fund: issuer systematically sets aside funds in escrow; bonds redeemed periodically at random from investors. Call provision: issuer exercises the option to redeem at a time of its choosing (typically when rates fall). Sinking funds are planned from inception; calls are opportunistic.

Variable Interest & Special Coupon Structures

★ FRN Coupon Calculation
FRN coupon rate = MRR + Credit Spread

Antelas AG FRN example (Quarter 15):
MRR = 0.25%, Spread = 250 bps = 2.50%
Coupon rate = 0.25% + 2.50% = 2.75%
Quarterly interest = (2.75% × EUR 250,000,000) / 4 = EUR 1,718,750
FRN structure: bullet principal (repaid at maturity). Only the coupon varies.
MRR resets periodically (quarterly in this example). Credit spread is fixed at issuance.
Banks prefer floating-rate loan assets because they match their variable-rate deposit liabilities (natural hedge).

Special Coupon Structures

Step-Up Bond
Coupon increases by specified amounts at specified future dates according to a predetermined schedule. Protects investors against rising rates. May also provide an incentive for issuers to call the bond before the step-up takes effect (issuer prefers to refinance rather than pay the higher coupon). NOT linked to a market rate — purely schedule-driven.
Credit-Linked Coupon (Leveraged Loans)
Coupon adjusts based on changes in the issuer's credit quality — typically linked to financial ratios (leverage, interest coverage) or credit ratings. If credit quality deteriorates, investors receive a higher coupon to compensate. Allows less creditworthy issuers to borrow at lower initial spreads while giving investors protection against credit deterioration. Most common in leveraged loans (loans to lower-credit-quality issuers).
Payment-in-Kind (PIK)
Issuer pays periodic interest by increasing the principal outstanding rather than paying cash. Allows cash-constrained issuers to preserve cash. Principal grows over time. Higher interest rate than cash-pay equivalent to compensate investors for greater principal risk. Most common in highly leveraged issuers (LBOs).
Index-Linked Bond ("Linker")
Interest and/or principal payments linked to a specified index, most commonly a consumer price index (inflation). Two sub-types: (1) capital-indexed (principal adjusts; coupon = fixed % of adjusted principal) and (2) interest-indexed (fixed principal; coupon adjusts for inflation). Issued primarily by sovereign governments. Protect against inflation erosion of real returns.

Capital-Indexed vs. Interest-Indexed Bonds

Capital-Indexed Bond (e.g., TIPS)
  • Principal adjusts periodically based on inflation index
  • Coupon = fixed rate × inflation-adjusted principal
  • Both coupon AND maturity payment adjust with inflation
  • Protects real value of principal
  • If deflation: principal declines, BUT investors receive max(adjusted principal, par) at maturity
  • Examples: US TIPS, UK index-linked gilts, Australian, Canadian bonds
  • Issued primarily by SOVEREIGN governments
Interest-Indexed Bond
  • Fixed nominal principal at maturity (no adjustment)
  • Only the coupon is index-linked (inflation adjustment)
  • Essentially an FRN where the MRR = rate of inflation
  • Does NOT protect real value of principal
  • More commonly issued by private financial intermediaries (not governments)
💡 Real vs. Nominal Interest Rate

Nominal interest rate: the stated rate; does not adjust for inflation.
Real interest rate ≈ Nominal rate − Inflation rate (Fisher approximation)

Fixed-income payments are nominal cash flows — their purchasing power declines as inflation rises. Index-linked bonds solve this by adjusting payments for inflation, protecting the investor's real purchasing power. TIPS investors hold the inflation risk at the government level rather than bearing it themselves.

⚠️ FRN vs. Fixed Bond — Interest Rate Risk Comparison

FRN: Lower interest rate risk (coupon adjusts with MRR, so price stays near par). BUT still has full credit risk — if issuer's creditworthiness deteriorates, FRN price falls regardless of MRR changes.

Fixed-coupon bond: Higher interest rate risk (price falls when rates rise). Also has credit risk.

Key insight: FRNs eliminate MRR risk but NOT credit spread risk. If the credit spread widens (issuer deteriorates), the FRN's price falls even if MRR is unchanged.

Zero-Coupon & Deferred Coupon Structures

Zero-Coupon Bond
  • No periodic interest payments
  • Also called: discount bond / pure discount bond
  • Issued at a discount to par; repays par at maturity
  • Investor's return = par − issue price (cumulative interest at maturity)
  • No reinvestment risk (no coupons to reinvest) — ideal for funding fixed future obligations
  • If rates fall, zero-coupon investors don't face shortfall from reinvestment risk (unlike fixed-coupon bondholders)
  • Common issuers: governments with tenor ≤12 months (T-bills)
  • Also created by "stripping" individual payments from sovereign bullet bonds
  • OID = par − issue price = Original Issue Discount (tax implications!)
Deferred Coupon Bond
  • No interest for the first few years
  • Higher coupon paid from a specified future date through maturity
  • Extreme form: zero-coupon bond (zero coupon = complete deferral)
  • Usually priced at a discount (higher coupon later insufficient to offset forgone early interest)
  • Issuers: companies conserving cash post-issuance, or financing construction projects not yet generating income
  • May signal lower credit quality (issuer cannot afford early cash interest)
💡 Zero-Coupon Bonds — No Reinvestment Risk

With a standard fixed-coupon bond, if interest rates fall after purchase, coupon payments must be reinvested at the new (lower) rates — the realized return falls below the YTM. Zero-coupon bonds have no coupons to reinvest, so the realized return exactly equals the YTM (assuming held to maturity and no default). This makes them ideal for liability matching — e.g., funding a known future obligation (pension payment, tuition) without reinvestment uncertainty.

Callable Bonds — Issuer-Favorable Embedded Option

📞 Callable Bond — Right to Redeem Early
Who Holds the Right?
ISSUER — the right to redeem (call) all or part of the bond before maturity. Issuer-favorable option.
Why Issuers Want Call Features
  • Flexibility to refinance if market interest rates fall — retire high-coupon debt and re-issue at lower rates
  • Allows debt management without market buybacks
Call Protection Period
Period during which the call feature CANNOT be used. Protects investors for an initial period. VIVU example: call cannot be exercised for the first 3 years.
Call Price Schedule (VIVU Example)
  • Year 3–4: 103.25% of par (highest premium — most investor protection)
  • Year 4–5: 102.50% of par
  • Year 5–6: 101.75% of par
  • Year 6–7: 101.00% of par (declining — closer to maturity = less valuable call protection)
Effect on Price and Yield
  • Callable bond price is capped at the call price — if market rates fall, non-callable price rises freely but callable price cannot exceed the fixed call price → negative convexity / price compression
  • If YTM rises above coupon: call is not exercised → callable behaves like a non-callable bond
  • If YTM falls below coupon: call is likely → price capped at call price, investor loses upside
  • Callable bonds must offer a higher yield than otherwise identical non-callable bonds to compensate investors for call risk (uncertain maturity + capped price appreciation)
Make-Whole Call
A special call provision requiring the issuer to compensate investors at a price based on the YTM of a sovereign bond of similar maturity — usually well above market price. Rarely exercised. Gives flexibility to repurchase debt without using market; offers issuers operational flexibility with minimal economic impact.
★ Callable Bond Pricing Principle
Price of Callable Bond < Price of Equivalent Non-Callable Bond

Yield of Callable Bond > Yield of Equivalent Non-Callable Bond

Value of call option (to issuer) = Price (non-callable) − Price (callable)

Call risk to investor = uncertain maturity + price capped at call price
The issuer PAYS for the call option through the LOWER PRICE at which it can sell the callable bond (i.e., it must offer a higher yield).
Investor is COMPENSATED for the call option via the higher yield on the callable bond.

Putable Bonds — Investor-Favorable Embedded Option

📤 Putable Bond — Right to Sell Back Early
Who Holds the Right?
INVESTOR (bondholder) — the right to sell (put) the bond back to the issuer at a predetermined price on specified dates. Investor-favorable option. Put price is usually par value.
Why Investors Want Put Features
  • Protection if interest rates rise — investor can put the bond back at par rather than holding a depreciated bond
  • Proceeds reinvested at the new, higher market rates
  • Provides a price FLOOR (= put price) for the bond
Effect on Price and Yield
  • Putable bond price is floored at the put price (usually par) — if market rates rise and prices fall, the bond cannot fall below the put price
  • If YTM < coupon rate (price > put price): put is less valuable → putable behaves like a non-putable bond
  • If YTM > coupon rate (price < put price): put price is activated as a floor → limits investor's downside
  • Putable bonds carry a lower yield than equivalent non-putable bonds because the put option has value to investors — the lower yield compensates the issuer for granting the put right
Issuer Impact
If investors exercise the put, the issuer is forced to refinance at current (higher) yields — at an inconvenient time from the issuer's perspective.
★ Putable Bond Pricing Principle
Price of Putable Bond > Price of Equivalent Non-Putable Bond

Yield of Putable Bond < Yield of Equivalent Non-Putable Bond

Value of put option (to investor) = Price (putable) − Price (non-putable)

Put price (usually par) = price FLOOR for the putable bond
The investor PAYS for the put option through the LOWER YIELD on the putable bond (accepts lower return).
Issuer is COMPENSATED for granting the put option by being able to issue at a lower yield.
💡 Call vs. Put — Quick Comparison

Call option: Issuer's right → issuer-favorable → callable bond has LOWER price, HIGHER yield → limits investor's price appreciation → creates call risk

Put option: Investor's right → investor-favorable → putable bond has HIGHER price, LOWER yield → provides investor with price floor → forces issuer to refinance at bad time

Memory trick: Call = Cheap (lower price) for investor | Put = Pricier for investor (higher price)

Convertible Bonds — Debt with Equity Upside

🔄 Convertible Bond — Right to Convert to Equity
Who Holds the Right?
INVESTOR (bondholder) — the right to exchange the bond for a predetermined number of common shares at a set conversion price. Investor-favorable option. If exercised, debt is replaced with equity (not retired with cash).
Why Issuers Use Convertibles
  • Growth companies with limited current cash flow can offer very low coupon in exchange for the conversion right
  • Ability to raise equity at a higher price (conversion price > current share price)
  • Investors accept lower yield now for potential equity upside
Effect on Price and Yield
  • Convertible bonds carry a much lower coupon/yield than equivalent non-convertible bonds — the conversion right has value
  • If share price is well below conversion price: bond trades like a regular bond (conversion worthless)
  • If share price well above conversion price: bond tracks its conversion value (trades like equity)
  • As share price rises, conversion value grows → bond price appreciation
★ Convertible Bond Key Formulas (ZTGB Example)
Conversion Ratio = Bond par value / Conversion price
                   = EUR 1,000 / EUR 42.00 = 23.81 shares per bond

Conversion Value = Conversion ratio × Current share price

Conversion Premium = (Conversion price − Current share price) / Current share price
                      = (EUR 42 − EUR 28) / EUR 28 = 50%

Example (1 year later, share price = EUR 35):
Conversion value = 23.81 × EUR 35 = EUR 833.35 per EUR 1,000 bond
Bond trading at par (EUR 1,000) > conversion value (EUR 833.35)
→ Investor prefers to HOLD the bond (not convert yet)
ZTGB issued at 1.25% coupon — far below what a standard ZTGB bond of same maturity would require → investors accepted lower yield for conversion rights.
Conversion premium = 50% means the share price must rise 50% before conversion is immediately profitable.
Conversion period: any business day starting 1 year from settlement through maturity.
⚠️ When to Convert vs. Hold — Decision Rule

Compare the conversion value to the bond's market price:
— If conversion value < bond price → do NOT convert (hold bond for interest + principal)
— If conversion value > bond price → CONVERT (get more value from shares than the bond is worth)

In the ZTGB example: conversion value = EUR 833.35 < bond price = EUR 1,000 → investor holds the bond.

Special Provisions — Warrants, CoCos

Warrant
An option attached to a bond (not embedded) that entitles the bondholder to buy the issuer's stock at a fixed exercise price until an expiration date. Key distinctions from convertibles: (1) warrants are SEPARATELY TRADEABLE in financial markets (unlike embedded options); (2) warrant exercise results in new share issuance for cash (not conversion of the bond); (3) warrants are attached, not embedded. Used as yield enhancement to investors. Traded on exchanges such as Deutsche Börse and Hong Kong Stock Exchange.
Contingent Convertible Bonds (CoCos)
A type of convertible bond issued primarily by European banks. Key distinctions from standard convertibles:
  • Conversion direction: Standard convertibles convert when share price RISES (investor's choice for upside). CoCos convert when the bank's capital falls BELOW a minimum regulatory threshold (automatic, not investor's choice)
  • Trigger: Specific regulatory event — e.g., core Tier 1 capital ratio falls below minimum. NOT based on equity price levels
  • Purpose: Automatically convert debt to equity when the bank is stressed → increases capital → reduces likelihood of default → limits systemic risk
  • Compensation: CoCos pay a higher yield than otherwise similar bonds (investor bears the risk of automatic conversion at a bad time)
  • Conversion is NOT at investor's discretion — happens automatically
Warrant vs. Embedded Convertible
  • Warrant: separately tradeable on an exchange
  • Embedded option: cannot be traded separately from the bond
  • Warrant exercise: buy NEW shares for cash
  • Convertible exercise: exchange bond for existing/new shares
  • Both give access to issuer's equity upside
CoCo vs. Standard Convertible
  • Standard convertible: investor's discretion; converts when shares rise
  • CoCo: automatic; converts when bank capital falls (downside event)
  • CoCo trigger: regulatory capital breach (NOT equity price)
  • CoCo purpose: systemic risk mitigation (banking regulation)
  • CoCo yield: HIGHER (compensates for automatic conversion risk)

Legal & Regulatory Considerations

Bond TypeDefinitionRegulatory StatusKey Features / Examples
Domestic Bond Issued by an entity incorporated in the SAME country as the issuance Full local regulations, listing, and disclosure requirements apply BRWA (US company) issuing USD bonds in the US. Most liquid in their respective local markets.
Foreign Bond Issued by an entity incorporated in a DIFFERENT country from the issuance Subject to local regulations of the country where issued BRWA issuing EUR bonds in Germany or CNY bonds in China. Often matches currency to foreign operations (natural hedge).
Eurobond Issued internationally in a currency different from the domestic currency of the issuance country (e.g., USD bonds issued in London) Fewer listing, disclosure, and regulatory requirements than domestic or foreign bonds. Not registered with any single country's regulator. Named for their denomination: Eurodollar (USD), Euroyen (JPY), euro-denominated Eurobonds. Usually unsecured. IIF USD bonds issued in Singapore. Can be denominated in any currency. Cannot be sold to US retail investors at issuance (not SEC-registered), except to large qualified institutional investors.
Global Bond Issued simultaneously in the Eurobond market AND at least one domestic bond market Subject to both Eurobond and domestic bond regulations World Bank is a regular issuer. Ensures sufficient demand from all investor types. Provides widest investor access.
💡 Critical: Currency Denomination Matters More Than Where Issued

The currency in which a bond is denominated has a stronger effect on its price than where the bond is issued or traded. This is because market interest rates of the denomination currency drive pricing. Example: IIF's 5-year USD Eurobond coupon = 1.50%, while its Indonesian rupiah bonds of similar tenor had YTM = 6.97%. Same issuer, same maturity — dramatic difference because of the currency.

Sukuk
Fixed-income instruments compliant with Islamic law (shari'a), which prohibits interest payments and financing of non-shari'a-compliant activities (alcohol, gambling). Instead of interest, sukuk pay periodic rental cash flows from underlying assets. First non-Muslim world sovereign sukuk: UK in 2014 (GBP 200 million, 5-year, listed on London Stock Exchange). Issued by supranational entities (Islamic Development Bank), sovereign (Turkey), and corporate (Saudi Aramco) issuers.
💡 Eurobond Market Origin & Key Features

Created in the 1960s primarily to bypass legal, regulatory, and tax constraints imposed in the United States. Key characteristics: (1) issued outside any single country's jurisdiction; (2) usually unsecured; (3) may be in any currency including issuer's domestic currency; (4) underwritten by international financial intermediaries; (5) historically bearer bonds (no ownership records — now all registered bonds); (6) sold primarily to European, Middle Eastern, and Asian investors. Romania's 30-year EUR bond (2019) was issued as a Eurobond + Rule 144A private placement (allows qualified US institutional investors).

⚠️ International vs. Domestic Bond Terminology

Many market participants refer to foreign bonds, Eurobonds, and global bonds collectively as "international bonds" (as opposed to domestic bonds). Eurobonds were historically bearer bonds (only the clearing system knew the owner — no name records). Now, all bonds (domestic, foreign, Eurobond) are registered bonds with ownership recorded by name or serial number.

Tax Considerations

Tax treatment of fixed-income securities varies significantly by jurisdiction and bond type. Three key areas:

  1. Interest income — usually taxed as ordinary income (same rate as wages)
  2. Capital gains/losses — different rate from interest income; may vary by holding period
  3. Original Issue Discount (OID) — special rules for zero-coupon bonds and bonds issued at a discount

Interest Income Tax Treatment

Country / JurisdictionInterest TaxCapital Gains TaxSpecial Notes
United States — Treasuries Federal income tax applies Yes (long-term at lower rate if held >1 year) EXEMPT from state and local tax → Treasuries more attractive to investors in high-tax states
United States — Municipal Bonds Often exempt from federal income tax AND state/local tax of issuing state Generally yes Tax exemption encourages local residents to buy state/local bonds
United Kingdom Income tax on accrued and paid interest Exempt from capital gains tax No CGT on bond gains
Eurobonds (general) Gross interest payments (no withholding at source) Varies by investor's home country Gross payment gives investors more flexibility on where/how taxes are paid
Some domestic bonds Net interest (paid after withholding tax deducted) Varies Tax deducted at source before investor receives payment

Capital Gains on Bonds

When a bond is sold before maturity at a price different from the purchase price, a capital gain or loss is recognized:

  • Long-term capital gain (held >1 year in US): taxed at a lower rate than ordinary income
  • Short-term capital gain (held ≤1 year in US): taxed at ordinary income rate (same as interest)
  • Not all countries have a separate capital gains tax or holding period distinction

Original Issue Discount (OID) — Zero-Coupon Bonds

★ Original Issue Discount Calculation
OID = Bond par value − Issuance price

Example:
Par = USD 25,000,000 | Issue price = USD 92.83 per USD 100 par
OID = 25,000,000 − (0.9283 × 25,000,000) = USD 1,792,500

US treatment: prorated OID included as taxable INCOME each year
                  → pay income tax annually; no capital gains at maturity

No-OID jurisdiction: no income recognized during holding period
                         → pay capital gains tax on full OID at maturity
OID = the interest that accrues to the investor over the bond's life via price appreciation from discount to par.
US OID tax provision: tax is paid on accrued interest annually even though no cash is received (phantom income).
Non-OID jurisdiction (e.g., Japan): no annual tax; large lump-sum capital gains tax at maturity instead.
This creates a significant after-tax return difference between investors in different jurisdictions.
💡 Premium Bond Tax Treatment

For bonds purchased at a premium above par, some jurisdictions allow investors to deduct a prorated portion of the premium from taxable income each year (amortize the premium). This is often a choice, not a requirement. Alternatively, investors can take no deduction annually and recognize a capital loss at maturity when the bond is redeemed at par (below the purchase price). The optimal choice depends on the investor's tax situation and expected holding period.

💡 Corporate Issuers — Interest Tax Deductibility

For corporate issuers, interest expense on bonds and loans is tax-deductible (unlike dividends on equity). This creates a tax shield on debt financing — the after-tax cost of debt is lower than the stated interest rate. This is a key reason companies prefer debt to equity from a tax perspective and is a fundamental concept in capital structure theory (covered in Corporate Issuers).

Exam Checklist — Must-Know Points

  • ✅ Bullet bond = standard structure: interest only during life, full principal at maturity. Most government and corporate bonds.
  • ✅ Fully amortizing: equal periodic payments (level annuity); interest portion declines, principal portion increases each period. Formula: A = (r × Principal) / [1 − (1+r)^(−N)]. Example: mortgages.
  • ✅ Partially amortizing: partial principal each period + balloon payment at maturity. Combines bullet and fully amortizing.
  • ✅ Amortization benefits investors: lower credit risk (principal reduces). Harms investors: higher reinvestment risk (early cash flows reinvested at potentially lower rates).
  • ✅ Sinking fund: issuer sets aside funds in escrow to retire principal early (randomly selected bonds). Reduces credit risk; increases reinvestment risk. Government and some corporate use.
  • ✅ Waterfall (ABS/MBS): interest paid to ALL tranches; principal paid SEQUENTIALLY (senior first). Shortfalls borne by JUNIOR tranches. Tranche A = lowest credit risk.
  • ✅ FRN coupon = MRR + Credit Spread. MRR resets; spread is constant. FRNs have lower interest rate risk but still have full credit risk.
  • ✅ Step-up bonds: coupon increases at scheduled future dates. Protects investors against rising rates; incentivizes issuers to call before step-up.
  • ✅ Credit-linked coupon: adjusts based on issuer credit quality/ratios. Most common in leveraged loans. Allows less creditworthy issuers to borrow at lower initial spreads.
  • ✅ PIK bonds: interest paid by increasing principal outstanding (not cash). Used by highly leveraged issuers; higher interest rate to compensate investors.
  • ✅ Capital-indexed bonds (e.g., TIPS): principal adjusts with inflation index; coupon = fixed rate × adjusted principal. Deflation protection: receive max(adjusted principal, par). Issued by sovereigns.
  • ✅ Interest-indexed bonds: fixed nominal principal; only coupon is inflation-linked. Essentially FRN where MRR = inflation. More common from private financial intermediaries.
  • ✅ Zero-coupon bonds = discount bonds = pure discount bonds. No periodic interest; issued at discount; sole return = par − issue price. No reinvestment risk. OID = par − issue price. Created by "stripping" sovereign bullet bonds.
  • ✅ Deferred coupon bonds: no interest early on, higher coupon later. Usually at discount to par. Signals potential lower credit quality. Zero-coupon = extreme deferred coupon case.
  • ✅ All embedded options (call, put, convert) cannot be traded separately from the bond. Value estimated by comparing to same bond without the provision.
  • ✅ Callable bonds: ISSUER's right. Callable price < non-callable price. Callable yield > non-callable yield. Call protection period = period during which call cannot be exercised. Price capped at call price (negative convexity). Make-whole calls rarely executed.
  • ✅ Putable bonds: INVESTOR's right. Putable price > non-putable price. Putable yield < non-putable yield. Put price = floor for bond price. Investor exercises when rates rise.
  • ✅ Convertible bonds: INVESTOR's right to convert to shares. Low coupon accepted in exchange for equity upside. Conversion ratio = par / conversion price. Conversion value = ratio × current share price. Convert when conversion value > bond price.
  • ✅ Conversion premium = (conversion price − current share price) / current share price. ZTGB example: (42−28)/28 = 50%.
  • ✅ Warrants are ATTACHED (not embedded) — separately tradeable. Entitles bondholder to buy issuer's shares at exercise price. Traded on exchanges (Deutsche Börse, HKEX).
  • ✅ CoCos: issued by banks; convert automatically when capital falls below regulatory minimum (Tier 1). NOT at investor's discretion. Purpose: limit systemic risk. Higher yield than similar bonds.
  • ✅ Domestic bonds: same country issuer and market. Foreign bonds: different country issuer. Eurobonds: issued outside any single jurisdiction; fewer regulations; can be any currency. Global bonds: Eurobond + domestic simultaneously.
  • ✅ Eurobonds were created in the 1960s to bypass US regulatory constraints. Cannot be sold to US retail investors at issuance (not SEC registered). Originally bearer bonds; now all registered bonds.
  • ✅ Currency denomination > where issued for price determination. Market rates of the denomination currency drive bond pricing.
  • ✅ Sukuk: shari'a-compliant bonds; pay rental income not interest; backed by specific assets. First non-Muslim sovereign: UK (2014).
  • ✅ Bond interest → ordinary income tax. Capital gains → usually different (lower) rate; US long-term CG rate lower if held >1 year.
  • ✅ OID = par − issue price. US: prorated as taxable income annually (pay tax without receiving cash). Non-OID jurisdictions: no annual tax; capital gains tax on OID at maturity.
  • ✅ US Treasuries exempt from state/local tax. US municipal bonds often exempt from federal tax and state tax of issuing state. UK bonds exempt from capital gains tax.
  • ✅ Premium bonds: some jurisdictions allow annual amortization of premium as a deduction (reduces taxable income). Alternative: take capital loss at maturity.

═══ AMORTIZATION ═══
Periodic payment A = (r × Principal) / [1 − (1 + r)^(−N)]
Fully amortizing: level payment; interest ↓ + principal ↑ each period
Balloon payment = partial amortization + lump sum at maturity

═══ FRN ═══
Coupon = MRR + Credit Spread  [MRR resets; spread fixed at issuance]
Lower interest rate risk than fixed bonds; still has full credit risk

═══ INDEX-LINKED ═══
Real rate ≈ Nominal rate − Inflation rate (Fisher approximation)
Capital-indexed (TIPS): principal adjusts; pays max(adjusted par, par) at maturity
Interest-indexed: fixed principal; only coupon adjusts

═══ ZERO-COUPON ═══
OID = Par − Issue price (= cumulative interest earned at maturity)
No reinvestment risk; ideal for liability matching

Callable: ISSUER's right → price ↓, yield ↑ vs. non-callable; capped at call price
Putable: INVESTOR's right → price ↑, yield ↓ vs. non-putable; floored at put price

                conversion premium = (conv. price − share price) / share price
Convert when: conversion value > bond market price
CoCo: automatic at regulatory trigger (Tier 1 capital breach); NOT investor's choice
Warrant: ATTACHED (separately tradeable); entitles holder to BUY shares at exercise price

Amortizing vs. Bullet: Amortizing lowers credit risk but raises reinvestment risk — these two always go together.
Callable vs. Putable: Call = issuer's option = lower price/higher yield for investor. Put = investor's option = higher price/lower yield.
Convertible key metrics: Conversion ratio (shares per bond) = par / conversion price. Conversion value = ratio × current share price. Convert only if conversion value > bond price.
CoCo trigger: regulatory capital breach — not market prices. Automatic conversion; investor has no choice; higher yield compensates.
OID tax: US taxes annually (phantom income); others tax as capital gain at maturity — major after-tax return difference.
