CFA LEVEL 1 · STANDARD IV · MODULE 3

Standards IV(B): Additional Compensation & IV(C): Responsibilities of Supervisors

IV(B) covers the requirement to obtain written employer consent before accepting any outside compensation, benefits, or consideration that could create a conflict with employer interests — including nonmonetary benefits. IV(C) covers the supervisor's obligation to prevent and detect violations, establish adequate compliance systems, and respond appropriately when violations occur. Both sources synthesised — all examples including LES-only additions.

IV(B)

Standard IV(B): Additional Compensation Arrangements — The Standard

Standard IV(B) — Additional Compensation Arrangements

Members and Candidates must not accept gifts, benefits, compensation, or consideration that competes with or might reasonably be expected to create a conflict of interest with their employer's interest unless they obtain written consent from all parties involved.

IV(B) is about protecting the employer's knowledge of and consent to arrangements that could compromise the member's or candidate's loyalty, objectivity, and full attention to the employer's interests. The standard applies before accepting — not after.

Three key terms: "written consent" = any documented communication (including email). "all parties involved" = both the employer AND the party offering the additional compensation. "competes with or might reasonably be expected to create a conflict" = a broad prospective standard — not just actual conflicts.

B2

What IV(B) Covers — Scope of the Obligation

Types of Compensation Covered
  • Direct compensation by clients (e.g., performance bonuses, gifts, travel)
  • Indirect compensation from third parties (e.g., research access, board membership perks)
  • Nonmonetary benefits (e.g., fitness club memberships, accommodation use, entertainment)
  • Performance incentives offered by clients
  • Board directorships with associated benefits
  • Outside consulting arrangements
  • Any benefit that could reasonably create a conflict with the employer's interest
Why Disclosure Is Required
  • Additional arrangements may affect loyalties and objectivity
  • May create potential conflicts of interest the employer needs to assess
  • Allows employer to evaluate the actions and motivations of the member or candidate
  • Employer is entitled to full knowledge of all compensation/benefit arrangements to assess the true cost of services being provided
  • Nonmonetary compensation creates conflicts in the same manner as monetary compensation (Jones/Exercise Unlimited example)
💡 Part-Time Employment — LES Addition

The LES adds that for members hired on a part-time basis, who by definition may work for multiple firms, the parameters around providing competitive services should be addressed and negotiated during the contracting and hiring process. IV(B) applies to limitations identified at that time. Part-time status doesn't create an automatic right to provide competitive services — the employer's consent is still required.

Examiner Focus — Nonmonetary = Same Obligation as Monetary

Both the Whitman/Cochran (Monaco trip) and Jones/Exercise Unlimited (fitness membership) examples establish the same principle: the absence of monetary compensation does not remove the disclosure obligation. Cochran's Monaco trip offer is a performance-based benefit that creates an incentive to favour her account. Jones's unlimited family fitness membership creates an incentive to recommend Exercise Unlimited stock. In both cases, the conflict of interest arises from the benefit's effect on the member's objectivity — not from the type or form of the benefit. Nonmonetary benefits must be disclosed to and consented to by the employer in the same way monetary ones are.

B3

Recommended Procedures — IV(B)

Members and candidates must make an immediate written report to their supervisor and compliance officer specifying any proposed additional compensation before accepting it. The report must include:

  • The nature of the compensation (what form it takes)
  • The approximate amount of the compensation
  • The duration of the arrangement
  • Any performance incentives offered by clients

The party offering the additional compensation should acknowledge and confirm the details in the disclosure. Both parties — the employer and the offering party — must consent. "Written consent" includes documented emails.

B4

IV(B) Worked Examples

Example 1
Both
Whitman/Cochran — Monaco Trip as Performance Bonus
VIOLATION
Cochran proposes to Whitman (her portfolio manager) that if her portfolio achieves 15% before taxes, Whitman and his wife can fly to Monaco at her expense and use her condominium for a week in January. Whitman does not inform his employer and takes the Monaco vacation.
Violation of IV(B). Whitman must inform his employer in writing of this supplemental, contingent compensation arrangement before accepting. The arrangement creates an incentive to favour Cochran's account over others he manages for Adams Trust Company.
🔑 Core Rules
(1) Contingent performance benefits = additional compensation requiring written employer consent. (2) The trip is not yet taken — disclosure must happen before acceptance. (3) The conflict: favouring Cochran's account to trigger the 15% threshold could detract from performance on other client accounts. The employer needs to know about this incentive structure to monitor for bias.
Example 2
Both
Jones — Board Membership Fitness Benefits
VIOLATION
Jones (senior portfolio manager) sits on the board of Exercise Unlimited and receives unlimited family fitness membership in exchange for his board services. He recommends Exercise Unlimited stock for appropriate client accounts but does not disclose the arrangement to his employer because he receives no monetary compensation for the board service.
Violation of IV(B). Nonmonetary compensation creates a conflict of interest in the same manner as monetary compensation. Jones has an incentive to recommend Exercise Unlimited stock because the stock's performance affects the company's ability to continue offering his family's fitness benefits.
🔑 Core Rule
"No monetary payment" ≠ no conflict. The value of the fitness membership to Jones and his family creates a real incentive to favour Exercise Unlimited in his recommendations. The employer needs to know about this arrangement to assess whether Jones's recommendation was objective or influenced by the personal benefit.
Example 3
Both
Hollis — CEO Meeting with Prior Approval, Post-Dinner Disclosure
NO VIOLATION
Hollis (oil analyst) is invited to visit the CEO of ABC Oil he has been covering. ABC offers a company plane and accommodation. Hollis gains appropriate prior approvals, accepts the meeting, but declines the offered travel arrangements. He joins the CEO for dinner at an upscale restaurant, which he discloses to his director of research upon returning.
No violation. Hollis sought prior approval before accepting the meeting, declined the potentially problematic travel arrangements, and disclosed the dinner (which arose spontaneously at the meeting location) upon his return. The employer can now assess whether the dinner has any impact on Hollis's objectivity in future ABC Oil research.
🔑 Core Rule
Prior approval + declining lavish benefits + prompt post-hoc disclosure of spontaneous benefits = full compliance. The dinner was not pre-arranged (no location was specified in advance) so prior disclosure wasn't possible — post-hoc disclosure immediately upon return satisfies IV(B). The employer's knowledge allows them to monitor for bias in subsequent reports.
IV(C)

Standard IV(C): Responsibilities of Supervisors — The Standard

Standard IV(C) — Responsibilities of Supervisors

Members and Candidates must make reasonable efforts to ensure that anyone subject to their supervision or authority complies with applicable laws, rules, regulations, and the Code and Standards.

IV(C) applies to any investment professional who has employees subject to their control or influence — regardless of whether those employees are CFA members, charterholders, or candidates. The obligation runs to all employees supervised, not just investment professionals or those who are members. Supervisory authority includes delegated supervisors — responsibilities include instructing those subordinates on compliance methods.

The standard imposes a dual obligation: (1) prevent violations through effective compliance systems, and (2) detect violations through monitoring and review. Both are required.

C2

System for Supervision — Prevention

Members with supervisory responsibility must understand what an adequate compliance system looks like for their firm and make reasonable efforts to ensure that appropriate compliance procedures are:

  • Established — in place before violations occur (not reactive)
  • Documented — in a clearly written, accessible manual tailored to the firm
  • Communicated to covered personnel
  • Followed — monitored and enforced on an ongoing basis
⚠️ Reporting + Warning ≠ Adequate Response to a Violation

Once a supervisor learns that an employee has violated or may have violated laws or the Code and Standards, the supervisor must promptly initiate an assessment to determine the extent of the wrongdoing. These steps alone are NOT sufficient: (1) relying on the employee's own account of the violation, (2) relying on the employee's assurances that it won't happen again, (3) reporting up the chain and warning the employee to stop. Pending the outcome of the investigation, the supervisor must also take steps to ensure the violation will not be repeated — such as placing limits on the employee's activities or increasing monitoring.

Adequate compliance procedures must include:

  • Clearly written, accessible manual tailored to the firm's operations
  • Designated compliance officer with clear authority, responsibility, and resources
  • Description of supervision hierarchy and assignment of duties
  • System of checks and balances
  • Scope of the procedures clearly defined
  • Procedures to document monitoring and testing
  • Permissible conduct outlined
  • Procedures for reporting violations and sanctions
  • Periodic updates to stay legally adequate
  • Professional conduct evaluations as part of performance reviews
💡 Inadequate Compliance System — What To Do

If a member with supervisory responsibility clearly cannot discharge their supervisory obligations because of an absent or inadequate compliance system, they should: (1) bring the inadequacy to the attention of the firm's senior managers and recommend corrective action, and (2) if the system remains inadequate, decline in writing to accept supervisory responsibility until the firm adopts reasonable procedures. The LES version specifies this should be in writing. Simply noting the inadequacy verbally is not sufficient.

C3

Supervision Includes Detection

Beyond prevention, supervisors must also make reasonable efforts to detect violations. Key principles:

  • Detection obligation = establishing written compliance procedures AND ensuring those procedures are followed through periodic review
  • Safe harbour: If a member has adopted reasonable procedures and taken steps to institute an effective compliance programme, they may NOT be in violation of IV(C) if violations occur despite those efforts
  • But: the occurrence of violations may indicate compliance procedures are inadequate — triggering a review
  • Critical condition: A member may be in violation of IV(C) if they know or should know that the procedures designed to promote compliance are NOT being followed — even if those procedures are theoretically adequate
Examiner Focus — "Should Know" Violations of IV(C)

The IV(C) "should know" standard is often tested. Mason (Example 2/3 LES) notices the increased trading activity in Highland stock but does nothing — she "should know" this requires investigation. Bartolucci (Example 7 Handbook) knows D'Addario is providing price quotes to customers as part of the brokerage business but doesn't develop policies and procedures governing those quotes — he "should know" this activity required procedures. In both cases, the supervisor's own awareness of an unusual pattern (increased activity, price quote activity) creates a "should know" obligation to investigate. Wilful blindness = IV(C) violation.

C4

Building a Compliance Culture — Education, Training, Incentives

Code of Ethics vs. Compliance Procedures

These are distinct and should be kept separate. Code of ethics: fundamental, principle-based ethical concepts in plain language applicable to all employees — unencumbered by detailed procedures or legal boilerplate. Compliance procedures: implement the code in day-to-day operations with specific rules and processes. Mingling the two dilutes the ethical principles' effectiveness and creates inaccessibility. Both are important but each serves a different purpose.

Education and Training

Regular ethics and compliance training is critical to building a culture of integrity. Training helps employees: (1) recognise areas prone to ethical and legal pitfalls, (2) identify circumstances and influences that impair ethical judgment, and (3) link ethical conduct to the long-term success of the business. The LES notes: "No amount of ethics education will deter someone determined to commit fraud for personal enrichment" — but the vast majority of investment professionals are genuinely trying to do the right thing, and training supports them.

Incentive Structures

Compensation and incentive structures that reward revenue generation without regard to how it is achieved can incentivise unethical behaviour even from otherwise ethical people. Supervisors must examine whether incentive structures encourage profits at the expense of ethically appropriate conduct. Effective structures tie compensation and incentives to how outcomes are achieved (ethical conduct), not only how much revenue is generated. The Mason example (Example 3) shows a specific conflict: Mason's compensation includes commission revenue from the trading department — creating a personal incentive to look away from trading irregularities.

C5

IV(C) Worked Examples — All (LES + Handbook)

Example 1
Both
Mattock — No Procedures to Prevent Trading on Pre-Publication Recommendation Change
VIOLATION
Mattock (head of research at H&V) orally advises executives of her pending buy-to-sell recommendation change as required by H&V's compliance procedures. However, she did not implement procedures designed to prevent those executives from trading or selectively disclosing the information before the recommendation is published.
Violation of IV(C). Mattock's failure to implement procedures to prevent dissemination of or trading on the information by those informed of changed recommendations before publication. The firm must have procedures for reviewing or recording any trading in a corporation that has been the subject of an unpublished change in recommendation.
🔑 Core Rule
Compliance with one aspect of a procedure (orally advising executives) doesn't satisfy the full obligation. Mattock's firm's procedure required advance notification to executives — but that notification created a pre-publication MNPI situation without any procedures to prevent trading on it. The supervisor must ensure the entire chain of compliance is addressed.
Example 2 (LES) / Example 2 (Handbook)
Both (different scenarios)
Mason — Compensation Tied to Trading = Conflict in Supervisory Role
VIOLATION
Mason is responsible for supervising securities law compliance in the trading department. Part of her compensation is based on commission revenues from that department. She notices increased Highland stock trading by trainee Edwards but does nothing to investigate or halt it.
Violation of IV(C). Mason's failure to investigate or halt unusual trading activity and failure to supervise the trainee's activities. The compensation structure creates a conflict: her personal financial interest (commission-based compensation) conflicts with her supervisory obligation to investigate potentially problematic trading.
🔑 Core Rule
"Supervisors must be especially sensitive to actual or potential conflicts between their own self-interests and their supervisory responsibilities." When a supervisor's compensation depends on revenue generated by the activity they are supposed to supervise, the conflict is direct. Wilful inaction when unusual patterns are noticed = IV(C) violation.
Example 2 (LES-Only) / Not in Handbook
LES Only
Miller — Not Reviewing Memo for MNPI Before Disseminating
VIOLATION
Research director Miller receives a memo from analyst Ginn recommending a local company Excelsior, which is rumored to be a merger target. Miller passes the memo to portfolio managers without reviewing it, noting he hasn't reviewed it. The portfolio managers buy Excelsior stock. It later emerges that Ginn's only source was her brother, an analyst at the acquiring conglomerate — and the information was MNPI.
Violation of IV(C). Miller violated his supervisory obligation by disseminating the memo without checking that Ginn had a reasonable and adequate basis for her recommendations and was not relying on MNPI. Noting "I haven't reviewed this" does not absolve the supervisory responsibility — it highlights the failure.
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only)
Supervision includes verifying the quality and compliance of subordinate work before acting on it or passing it to others. Explicitly noting that you haven't reviewed a memo and then distributing it anyway = conscious abdication of supervisory responsibility. This also implicates Standard V(A) (Diligence and Reasonable Basis) for Miller's failure to verify the basis for the recommendation.
Example 3 (Handbook) / Example 4 (LES)
Both
Claudius/Henry — No Compliance Procedures for Futures Trading = Post-Trade Account Manipulation
VIOLATION
Tabbing (portfolio manager) places S&P 500 futures orders without pre-designating accounts and without completing internal trade tickets. After observing market movements, she assigns favourable executions to the employee profit-sharing plan (CEPSP) and unfavourable ones to client funds. No written procedures or compliance manual exists for futures trading; the compliance department doesn't review it.
Claudius/Henry violated IV(C) by failing to adequately supervise Tabbing and failing to establish recordkeeping and reporting procedures. The absence of written operating procedures for futures trading — and the compliance department's failure to review this activity — allowed Tabbing's post-trade cherry-picking to occur undetected.
🔑 Core Rule
Absence of compliance procedures = IV(C) violation even before any underlying misconduct occurs. The supervisor is responsible for establishing, documenting, communicating, and following compliance procedures for all employee trading activity. The fact that Claudius/Henry didn't know about Tabbing's manipulation is not a defence — they should have had systems in place to detect it.
Examples 4/5 (LES + Handbook)
Both
Blair + Saunders — Dismissing Rasmussen's Concerns About Hedge Fund Reporting
BOTH VIOLATE IV(C)
Rasmussen raises concerns that the hedge fund's reported performance doesn't reflect a significant market decline she observed. Blair (her supervisor/head of trading) and Saunders (compliance officer) both dismiss her concerns and tell her to stay away from the issue.
Both Blair and Saunders violated IV(C). Supervisors have the responsibility to review concerns brought forth by employees and to establish and encourage an ethical culture in the firm. Dismissing Rasmussen's question without investigation violates IV(C) and undermines the firm's ethical operations.
🔑 Core Rule
The supervisor's obligation includes receiving and investigating employee concerns — not just actively detecting violations through their own monitoring. When an employee brings a potential compliance issue to a supervisor's attention, dismissing it without investigation is itself a supervisory failure. Both the direct supervisor (Blair) and the compliance officer (Saunders) share this obligation.
Examples 6+7 (LES-Only)
LES Only
Witt + Papis — Inadequate Procedures at Research Firm / Inadequate Oversight of CIO
BOTH: INADEQUATE SUPERVISION
Witt: On-Line and Informed research analyst bases a report on news articles and conventional wisdom under time pressure, without adequate analysis. His supervisor allows it to be posted. Papis: CIO of a state retirement fund changes the real estate adviser to his business school classmate Nagle's new firm without apparent diligence or review — the only notice appears in the next annual report.
Witt's supervisor: Violated IV(C) — allowing a research report without adequate basis to be posted highlights a lack of compliance procedures. Must work with management to develop an appropriate review process. Papis's situation: Highlights the need for supervision and review at all levels — including the CIO level. The firm should have appropriate policies to detect arbitrary decisions made at senior management level. Also implicates V(A) Diligence, V(B) Communication with Clients, and VI(A) Disclosure of Conflicts (Papis's personal relationship with Nagle).
🔑 Core Rules (LES-Only)
Witt: Compliance procedures must ensure that research meets quality standards before distribution. Time pressure is not a justification for bypassing the review process. Papis: IV(C) applies at all levels — even senior management decisions require oversight and review procedures. An arbitrary change in adviser without diligence is exactly the type of decision that appropriate firm-level supervision should detect and question.
Example 5 (Handbook) / Example 8 (LES)
Both
Graf — No Social Media Policies Before Encouraging Social Media Use
VIOLATION
Graf (Burdette's supervisor) encourages Burdette to expand FIM's social media presence but has not established any policies or procedures governing online communications. Burdette posts her buy recommendation on LinkedIn/X before it's distributed to all clients.
Graf violated IV(C) by failing to establish reasonable procedures to prevent unauthorized dissemination of company research through social media networks. Cannot encourage use of a channel without establishing appropriate compliance guardrails first.
🔑 Core Rule
A supervisor who encourages an employee to engage in a new activity without establishing the compliance framework for that activity = IV(C) violation. The sequence must be: (1) establish policies and procedures for the new activity, (2) train employees on those policies, then (3) allow the activity. Graf skipped steps 1 and 2 and went directly to step 3. Also see III(B) for the fair dealing violation by Burdette.
Example 6 (Handbook)
Handbook Only
Bartlett — Compliance Officer Fails to Supervise Branch Suitability
VIOLATION
Bartlett (compliance officer for all branches of FFWM) allows Sokol to direct 100+ retail clients into a highly speculative Feeder Fund without conducting suitability assessments — some clients have low risk tolerances. Alpha Fund loses 35% of value, causing ~$16M in client losses.
Bartlett violated IV(C). His inadequate policies, procedures, training, and supervision allowed Sokol to recommend the Feeder Fund without conducting proper suitability analysis. Bartlett failed to adopt and implement written policies designed to prevent investment advisers from recommending complex financial products to unsuitable clients.
🔑 Core Rule (Handbook-Only)
The compliance officer is a supervisor for IV(C) purposes. A compliance officer's failure to establish adequate suitability procedures — with the result that clients are systematically recommended unsuitable investments — violates IV(C) even if the compliance officer had no direct knowledge of each specific recommendation. The obligation is to have systems that would have prevented this pattern.
Example 7 (Handbook)
Handbook Only
Bartolucci — No Policies for Price Quotes Allows D'Addario Fraud
VIOLATION
Bartolucci (BOAC CEO) knows D'Addario is providing price quotes to customers as part of the brokerage business but doesn't develop policies or procedures governing the provision of price quotes. D'Addario enters a fraudulent arrangement with Amity Point to provide artificially inflated MBS price quotes in exchange for business — allowing Amity Point to report inflated NAVs.
Bartolucci violated IV(C). Failed to develop, promote, and train employees on adequate policies governing the provision of price quotes to customers. Failed to supervise D'Addario to prevent and detect illegal and unethical activities.
🔑 Core Rule (Handbook-Only)
Even when the CEO is not aware of the specific misconduct (D'Addario's fraudulent arrangement), IV(C) requires policies governing the activities that could give rise to misconduct. Bartolucci knew that price quotes were being provided to customers — he should have known this activity required procedures governing accuracy and arm's-length standards. The "should have known" obligation is the supervisor's burden.
Example 9 (LES-Only)
LES Only
Chen Wang — Time Pressure Overrides Review Process
VIOLATION
Chen leads the research department with a team of 10 analysts. Huang Mei submits a sell recommendation for ZYX Bank late at night, based partly on an independent blogger's analysis she doesn't disclose. Due to the late submission and confidence in Huang's prior work, Chen distributes the report without following the firm's approved review process.
Chen violated IV(C). The delayed submission and quality of prior work do not remove Chen's requirement to uphold the designated review process. A member with supervisory responsibility must make reasonable efforts to see that appropriate procedures are established, documented, communicated, and — critically — followed.
🔑 Core Rule (LES-Only)
Time pressure is NOT a justification for bypassing established review processes. The review process exists precisely to catch cases like this — where a rushed analyst relies on an undisclosed external source without attribution. The supervisor's confidence in an analyst's past performance does not justify bypassing review for current work. Each piece of research requires independent verification through the approved process.

Combined Exam Checklists — IV(B) and IV(C)

Standard IV(B): Additional Compensation Arrangements

  • ✅ Prohibition: Cannot accept gifts, benefits, compensation, or consideration that competes with or could create a conflict with employer interests — without written consent from all parties.
  • ✅ Written consent = any documented communication including email. From ALL parties = both employer and the party offering the compensation.
  • ✅ Nonmonetary benefits: Create conflicts in exactly the same manner as monetary compensation. Board membership perks, fitness memberships, travel, accommodation = all covered. (Jones)
  • ✅ Disclosure must occur before accepting — not after taking the benefit. (Whitman took the Monaco trip without prior disclosure = violation)
  • ✅ What to disclose: Nature of compensation + approximate amount + duration + any performance incentives. Confirmed by the offering party.
  • ✅ Contingent performance benefits (e.g., Monaco trip if 15% return achieved) = additional compensation requiring written consent. Creates incentive to favour the offering client. (Whitman)
  • ✅ Prior approval + declining lavish offers + prompt post-hoc disclosure of spontaneous benefits = compliant approach. (Hollis)
  • ✅ Part-time employees: Must negotiate competitive service limitations during the hiring process. IV(B) applies to those limitations.

Standard IV(C): Responsibilities of Supervisors

  • ✅ Applies to any investment professional with employees subject to their control or influence — regardless of whether those employees are CFA members, charterholders, or candidates.
  • ✅ Dual obligation: PREVENT violations (through effective compliance systems) + DETECT violations (through monitoring and review).
  • ✅ Procedures must be: Established + Documented + Communicated to covered personnel + Followed + Monitored + Enforced.
  • ✅ Procedures must be in place BEFORE violations occur — not established reactively after a problem is discovered.
  • ✅ When a violation is discovered: (1) Prompt thorough investigation to determine extent, (2) Limit/increase monitoring of alleged offender PENDING investigation, (3) Review procedures for necessary changes. Merely reporting + warning ≠ sufficient.
  • ✅ Employee's own account or assurances ≠ sufficient — supervisor must independently investigate the extent of wrongdoing.
  • ✅ Inadequate compliance system: Bring to senior management's attention. If still inadequate, decline IN WRITING to accept supervisory responsibility.
  • ✅ Detection safe harbour: If adequate procedures adopted and effective compliance programme instituted, may not violate IV(C) if violations occur anyway. But occurrence of violations may indicate inadequate procedures.
  • ✅ "Should know" standard: Wilful blindness = violation. If supervisor notices unusual patterns and does nothing = IV(C) violation. (Mason, Bartolucci)
  • ✅ Dismissing employee concerns without investigation = IV(C) violation. (Blair + Saunders/Rasmussen)
  • ✅ Code of ethics ≠ compliance procedures. Both are needed but must be kept separate — code should be plain-language principles; procedures implement those principles in daily operations.
  • ✅ Encouraging an activity without establishing compliance policies for it = IV(C) violation. (Graf encouraging social media without social media policies)
  • ✅ Time pressure does NOT justify bypassing review processes. (Chen Wang)
  • ✅ Compensation conflicts impair supervision: When supervisor's income depends on activity they supervise, extra vigilance required. (Mason)
  • ✅ Compliance officer is a supervisor for IV(C) purposes — responsible for firm-wide compliance system adequacy. (Bartlett)

Top IV(B) exam traps:
(1) "He received no monetary payment for the board service, so no IV(B) issue" — nonmonetary benefits (fitness membership) create conflicts in the same way as monetary ones
(2) "She disclosed the Monaco trip arrangement before taking it, so no violation" — must also OBTAIN WRITTEN CONSENT, not just disclose
(3) "The dinner was spontaneous so no prior disclosure was possible" — correct; prompt post-hoc disclosure is compliant when prior disclosure wasn't possible (Hollis)

Top IV(C) exam traps:
(1) "The supervisor reported the violation and warned the employee to stop" — reporting + warning alone are insufficient; must also limit the employee's activities pending investigation
(2) "The supervisor had adequate procedures in place, so they can't be held liable for violations" — safe harbour applies only if the supervisor also has reasonable steps to ensure procedures are actually followed
(3) "She encouraged social media but couldn't anticipate what Burdette would post" — must establish procedures BEFORE encouraging the activity
(4) "He trusted Huang's prior work quality so no need to review this report" — prior performance doesn't justify bypassing the established review process for current work
