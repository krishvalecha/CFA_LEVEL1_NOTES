Capital Market Theory & the Capital Market Line

Homogeneity of Expectations
The assumption that all investors have the same expectations for prices, cash flows, and other investment characteristics. Under this assumption, all investors arrive at the same optimal risky portfolio — the market portfolio. If relaxed, investors arrive at different optimal portfolios. This is a KEY CAPM assumption.

Why homogeneity matters: The intrinsic value of any asset depends on estimates of (a) future cash flows and (b) required rate of return. Different analysts value these differently — leading to different "optimal" portfolios. Homogeneity forces everyone to the same conclusion → a single market portfolio.

Asset Price Formula (Intrinsic Value)
P = Σ [CFt / (1 + rt)t]
CFt = cash flow at end of period t
rt = required return/discount rate for period t
Different analysts use different CFs and rts → different intrinsic values → different "optimal" portfolios without homogeneity.

Passive vs. Active Portfolio Management

Passive Portfolios
  • Assume market prices are unbiased (informationally efficient market)
  • Replicate market indexes (S&P 500, Nikkei 300, CAC 40)
  • Index funds / ETFs
  • Low costs — no security analysis needed
  • Examples: S&P 500 index fund, Nikkei 300 ETF
Active Portfolios
  • Rely on own valuations, not market prices
  • Overweight undervalued / underweight (or short) overvalued
  • Higher costs (research, transactions)
  • Examples: most open-end mutual funds, hedge funds
  • Value add is debated but most analysts believe it exists

What is the "Market"?

Theoretically: includes ALL risky assets — stocks, bonds, real estate, commodities, even human capital.

Practically: a broad market index is used as a proxy (e.g., S&P 500 for US equities).

  • S&P 500 ≈ 80% of US equity market cap; US markets ≈ 40% of world equity → S&P 500 ≈ 32% of worldwide equity
  • The proxy doesn't include non-US stocks, bonds, real estate, or other asset classes
  • Some assets are not tradable (human capital, Taj Mahal); some are tradable but not investable (e.g., restricted shares)

The Capital Market Line (CML)

CAPITAL MARKET LINE (CML)
  • Definition: A SPECIAL CASE of the Capital Allocation Line (CAL) where the risky portfolio is THE MARKET PORTFOLIO
  • Only applies to efficient portfolios (those on the efficient frontier that are well-diversified)
  • X-axis: total risk (σp) · Y-axis: expected return E(Rp)
  • Intercept: Rf (risk-free return, zero risk)
  • Slope: [E(Rm) − Rf] / σm = market price of risk
  • Tangent to the Markowitz efficient frontier at the market portfolio (M)
★ CAPITAL MARKET LINE EQUATION ★ (Memorize!)
E(Rp) = Rf + [(E(Rm) − Rf) / σm] × σp
Slope = Sharpe ratio of the market = Market price of risk
Also called the "reward-to-variability ratio" or "reward-to-total-risk ratio"
Straight line form y = a + bx: intercept Rf, slope = (E(Rm)−Rf)/σm
Portfolio on the CML — Risk & Return
Let w₁ = weight in risk-free asset, (1 − w₁) = weight in market

E(Rp) = w₁ Rf + (1 − w₁) E(Rm)
σp = (1 − w₁) σm
Why σp is linear: risk-free asset has σf = 0 AND Cov(Rf, anything) = 0.
Two of three terms in the variance formula drop out → portfolio risk is proportional to market risk.
⚠️ Why the CML is Straight (Key Conceptual Point)

Combining TWO RISKY assets generally gives a CURVED opportunity set (because of non-zero covariance). But combining the risk-free asset + risky portfolio gives a STRAIGHT LINE because σf = 0 and Cov(Rf, Rm) = 0 — the cross-product terms vanish in the portfolio variance formula.

Leveraged Portfolios & Different Borrowing Rates

Lending vs. Borrowing Portfolios

Position on CMLWeight in RfWeight in MarketName
At Rf (y-intercept)100%0%100% lending — zero risk
Between Rf and M0% < w₁ < 100%0% < w₂ < 100%Lending portfolio
At M (market)0%100%Fully invested in market
Right of Mw₁ < 0% (negative)> 100%Borrowing / leveraged portfolio

Different Borrowing and Lending Rates — "Kinked" CML

In reality, investors cannot borrow at the risk-free rate — they must pay a higher borrowing rate Rb. This creates a kinked CML with two different slopes:

Kinked CML — Two Equations
Lending segment (w₁ ≥ 0, left of M):
E(Rp) = Rf + [(E(Rm) − Rf)/σm] × σp

Borrowing segment (w₁ < 0, right of M):
E(Rp) = Rb + [(E(Rm) − Rb)/σm] × σp
Because Rb > Rf, the borrowing segment has a SMALLER slope than the lending segment.
The CML "kinks" downward at the market portfolio (M).
Leveraged investors get a worse risk-return trade-off than lending investors.
💡 Visualizing the Kinked CML

Picture the CML as a straight line from Rf through M and beyond. Now, at point M, the line bends downward (to a flatter slope) because beyond M you're using the higher borrowing rate Rb. Leverage is still possible but offers less incremental return per unit of risk.

Systematic vs. Nonsystematic Risk

Systematic Risk
  • Also called: market risk, non-diversifiable risk
  • Affects the ENTIRE market/economy
  • CANNOT be diversified away
  • Examples: interest rates, inflation, economic cycles, political uncertainty, widespread natural disasters
  • PRICED by the market → investors earn a return for bearing it
  • Measured by beta (β)
Nonsystematic Risk
  • Also called: company-specific, industry-specific, diversifiable, idiosyncratic risk
  • Pertains to a single company or industry
  • CAN be diversified away
  • Examples: failed drug trial, airliner crash, CEO scandal
  • NOT PRICED → no extra return expected
  • Measured by residual/error variance (σ²e)
★ TOTAL RISK DECOMPOSITION ★ (Variance-Based)
Total variance = Systematic variance + Nonsystematic variance

σ²i = β²i σ²m + σ²e

σi = √(β²i σ²m + σ²e)
βi σm = systematic risk (std. dev.)
σe = nonsystematic risk (std. dev. of residual error)
Important: The equality is about VARIANCES (squared terms), not standard deviations!
For a well-diversified portfolio: σ²e ≈ 0, so total risk ≈ β σm (systematic only).

Why Nonsystematic Risk Is NOT Priced (Economic Argument)

Suppose nonsystematic risk WERE priced (earned a positive return):

  1. Investors would buy assets with high nonsystematic risk to earn that return
  2. They'd then diversify away the nonsystematic risk (combining with low-correlation assets)
  3. They'd now earn the nonsystematic return WITHOUT bearing the risk
  4. Demand for such diversifiable risk would surge → price rises to infinity → return falls to zero
  5. Conclusion: in equilibrium, the return on nonsystematic risk must be ZERO
💡 Key Principle — Only Systematic Risk Is Compensated

Risk-averse investors should hold only well-diversified portfolios because bearing nonsystematic risk earns them no reward. The entire CAPM framework is built on this insight: expected return depends ONLY on an asset's systematic risk (beta), not its total risk.

⚠️ Exam Trap — Variances Add, Not Std Devs

Total variance = systematic variance + nonsystematic variance. You cannot simply add standard deviations. If systematic σ = 0.12 and nonsystematic σ = 0.05, total σ = √(0.12² + 0.05²) = √(0.0144 + 0.0025) = √0.0169 = 0.13 — NOT 0.17.

Return-Generating Models

Return-Generating Model
A model that provides an estimate of the expected return of a security given certain parameters. Most general form is a multi-factor model. The CAPM is a special case — a single-factor model where the only factor is the market.

Types of Factor Models

Macroeconomic

Uses economic factors: GDP growth, interest rates, inflation, productivity, employment, consumer confidence. Preferred for having economic meaning.

Fundamental

Uses company fundamentals: earnings, earnings growth, cash flow, R&D, advertising, patents. Preferred for having economic meaning.

Statistical

Uses historical return data to find explanatory factors. May have NO economic connection (e.g., "Super Bowl winner's conference" as a factor). Prone to data mining — results can be spurious.

General Multi-Factor Return-Generating Model
E(Ri) − Rf = βi1[E(Rm) − Rf] + Σj=2k βij E(Fj)
LHS: expected excess return over risk-free rate
βij = factor loadings (sensitivity of asset i to factor j)
E(Fj) = expected value of factor j
All models contain the market factor as at least one of the factors.

Fama-French and Carhart Models

Fama-French 3-Factor Model (1992) + Carhart 4th Factor (1997)
E(Rit) = αi + βi,MKT·MKTt + βi,SMB·SMBt + βi,HML·HMLt + βi,UMD·UMDt
MKT = excess return on the market portfolio
SMB = Small Minus Big (size factor — small caps outperform large caps)
HML = High Minus Low (value factor — high book-to-market outperforms low)
UMD = Up Minus Down (momentum factor — past winners outperform past losers)

Empirical finding: βMKT coefficient often NOT significantly different from zero — the other three factors explain most of the variation!

The Single-Index Model

★ Single-Index Model ★
E(Ri) − Rf = βi [E(Rm) − Rf]
Only ONE factor: the market return.
This is effectively the CAPM equation.
Derived from CML by substituting σi = βiσm (which holds for well-diversified portfolios).

The Market Model

★ MARKET MODEL ★ (used to estimate beta via regression)
Ri = αi + βi Rm + ei
αi = intercept (theoretically αi = Rf(1 − βi))
βi = slope = beta (estimated via regression of historical returns)
ei = error term (company-specific/residual return)

Most common way to estimate beta: regress asset returns on market returns; slope = beta.
Used to compute ABNORMAL RETURNS on specific dates: abnormal return = Ri − (αi + βiRm)
💡 Example — Walmart's Market Model

Regress Walmart's daily returns on S&P 500 daily returns. Say we get α = 0.0001, β = 0.90. On a day when market rose 1% and Walmart rose 2%:
Expected = 0.0001 + 0.90 × 0.01 = 0.0091 = 0.91%
Abnormal return = 2% − 0.91% = 1.09%
Walmart outperformed its expected return by 1.09% that day.

⚠️ Single-Index Model vs. Market Model — Subtle Difference

Single-Index Model: theoretical; uses excess returns; αi = Rf(1 − βi).
Market Model: empirical version; uses raw returns; αi estimated freely from data.
In practice these are treated as nearly equivalent. Market model is easier to estimate and is the standard for computing beta.

Beta — The Central Measure

Beta (β)
Measures an asset's sensitivity to market movements — the portion of an asset's risk that cannot be eliminated through diversification. Beta captures SYSTEMATIC RISK only. Mathematically, it's the covariance of the asset's return with the market, standardized by market variance.
★ BETA — THE FORMULA (Memorize!) ★
βi = Cov(Ri, Rm) / σ²m

= ρi,m × σi × σm / σ²m

= ρi,m × (σi / σm)
Three equivalent forms — use whichever the question gives you inputs for.
ρi,m = correlation between asset i and market
σi = std. dev. of asset i, σm = std. dev. of market
Cov(Ri, Rm) = covariance of asset with market
σm² in denominator is VARIANCE (not std dev)
💡 Worked Examples

Example 1 — From correlation and std devs: ρ = 0.70, σi = 0.25, σm = 0.15. β = 0.70 × (0.25/0.15) = 0.70 × 1.667 = 1.17.

Example 2 — From covariance and variance: Cov = 0.026250, σ²m = 0.02250. β = 0.026250 / 0.02250 = 1.17. (Same answer — equivalent formulas.)

Interpreting Beta Values

Beta ValueInterpretationExample
β = 1Moves with the market — market has β = 1 by definitionA broad index fund
β > 1More volatile than market (aggressive). Expected return > marketTech stocks, small caps
0 < β < 1Less volatile than market (defensive). Expected return < market but > RfUtilities, consumer staples
β = 0No correlation with market → risk-free assetT-bills
β < 0Moves opposite to market (rare). Expected return < RfGold (sometimes), insurance, put options
Why Market Beta = 1 (and Risk-Free Beta = 0)
βm = ρm,m × σm / σm = 1 × 1 = 1
(correlation of anything with itself = 1)

βf = Cov(Rf, Rm) / σ²m = 0 / σ²m = 0
(risk-free asset has zero covariance with everything)
The AVERAGE beta of all stocks = 1 because the market itself has β = 1 (and the market is the average of all stocks by cap weight).

Portfolio Beta — Weighted Average

★ Portfolio Beta ★
βp = Σ wi βi     where Σ wi = 1
Unlike standard deviation, beta IS a simple weighted average.
This is because beta is linear in asset weights (unlike variance, which has cross-terms).
Example: 60% in β=1.2 asset + 40% in β=0.8 asset → βp = 0.6(1.2) + 0.4(0.8) = 0.72 + 0.32 = 1.04

Estimation of Beta — Practical Issues

  • Beta is estimated by regressing historical asset returns on historical market returns (market model)
  • Shorter periods (e.g., 12 months): capture CURRENT systematic risk better, but less statistically accurate
  • Longer periods (3-5 years): more statistically accurate, but may not reflect current state if company has changed
  • Different data frequencies (daily vs. monthly) produce different beta estimates
  • 3-year beta ≠ 5-year beta ≠ daily beta ≠ monthly beta — estimate depends on sample period and frequency
  • In developed markets, most stocks have correlation > 0.70 with market (for S&P 500: > 0.90)
  • Consistently negative betas are RARE due to broad market effects on all assets
⚠️ Exam Trap — Variance in the Beta Formula

The denominator of β = Cov(Ri, Rm) / σ²m is market VARIANCE, NOT market standard deviation. If σm = 15%, then σ²m = 0.0225 (not 0.15). Pay attention to whether the question gives you variance or std dev.

Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) & Security Market Line

★★★ THE CAPM — THE MOST IMPORTANT FORMULA IN PORTFOLIO THEORY ★★★
E(Ri) = Rf + βi [E(Rm) − Rf]
E(Ri) = required/expected return on asset i
Rf = risk-free rate
βi = beta of asset i (systematic risk)
E(Rm) − Rf = market risk premium
βi × [E(Rm) − Rf] = asset's own risk premium

Two assets with same beta → same expected return, regardless of anything else about the assets.

CAPM Assumptions (6 Key Assumptions)

#AssumptionMeaning
1Risk-averse, utility-maximizing, rational investorsInvestors dislike risk, want more wealth, evaluate info rationally. Degrees of aversion can differ.
2Frictionless marketsNo transaction costs, no taxes, no short-selling costs/restrictions, can borrow/lend at Rf.
3Single-period horizonAll investor decisions made for one common period. No multi-period strategy.
4Homogeneous expectationsAll investors use same inputs and arrive at same valuations → same optimal risky portfolio (the market).
5Infinitely divisible investmentsCan invest any fraction in any asset (continuous, not discrete).
6Price takers (competitive markets)No single investor large enough to affect prices.
💡 Mnemonic — CAPM Assumptions: "RF HIP"

Rational (and risk-averse, utility-maximizing) · Frictionless markets · Homogeneous expectations · Infinitely divisible · Price takers · (and single-period horizon)

The Security Market Line (SML)

SECURITY MARKET LINE (SML)
  • Graphical representation of CAPM
  • X-axis: beta (β) — systematic risk
  • Y-axis: expected return E(Ri)
  • Intercept: Rf
  • Slope: [E(Rm) − Rf] = market risk premium
  • Applies to ALL securities and portfolios — whether efficient or not
  • Passes through (β=0, Rf) and (β=1, E(Rm))
SML — Two Key Points
At β = 0: E(R) = Rf (y-intercept)
At β = 1: E(R) = E(Rm) (the market portfolio)
Slope = E(Rm) − Rf = market risk premium
The SML is the CAPM drawn as a line.

Using CAPM for Portfolio Expected Return

Portfolio CAPM Equation
E(Rp) = Rf + βp [E(Rm) − Rf]

where βp = Σ wi βi
Because beta is a weighted average, CAPM works for any portfolio by simply using portfolio beta.
This makes CAPM very convenient for practical applications.

CAPM Applications

1. Expected Return / Valuation

Use CAPM return as the discount rate to value assets (stocks, bonds, real estate) by discounting future cash flows.

2. Capital Budgeting

Use CAPM to estimate required rate of return for projects based on project's beta. Computes NPV of investments.

3. Performance Appraisal

Compare actual portfolio return with CAPM-predicted return. Used to evaluate managers.

4. Security Selection

Compare analyst's expected return vs. CAPM's required return. Above SML = undervalued (buy); below SML = overvalued (sell/short).

5. Cost of Capital

Used by regulatory commissions to set fair returns for regulated utilities.

6. Insurance Premiums

Used to set actuarially fair insurance premiums based on systematic risk exposure.

💡 Negative Beta Example — Insurance

Insurance has a negative beta: it pays out when the market tanks (you lost your house in a disaster when economy is also struggling). CAPM gives it a NEGATIVE expected return — yet people buy it because it reduces overall portfolio risk. This is consistent with CAPM: assets that reduce systematic risk can have expected returns below Rf.

CML vs. SML — Master Comparison

⚠️ Critical — Don't Confuse CML and SML!

These are two of the most frequently confused concepts on the CFA exam. They are DIFFERENT lines with DIFFERENT axes, slopes, and applicability. Memorize the distinctions cold.

Dimension Capital Market Line (CML) Security Market Line (SML)
What it plots E(R) vs. σ (total risk) E(R) vs. β (systematic risk)
X-axis Standard deviation (σp) Beta (βi)
Y-axis E(Rp) E(Ri)
Intercept Rf Rf
Slope (E(Rm) − Rf) / σm = Sharpe ratio E(Rm) − Rf = market risk premium
Equation E(Rp) = Rf + [(E(Rm)−Rf)/σm] × σp E(Ri) = Rf + βi × [E(Rm)−Rf]
Applies to Only EFFICIENT portfolios (well-diversified) ALL assets and portfolios (efficient or not)
Passes through Rf and the market portfolio M (0, Rf) and (1, E(Rm))

Individual assets on it? NO — individual assets are BELOW CML (have nonsystematic risk) YES — all correctly-priced assets lie ON SML

💡 Mnemonic — CML vs. SML

CML → "C"omplete/Comprehensive portfolios → total risk (σ) → only efficient portfolios
SML → "S"ingle Securities → systematic risk (β) → all securities

When you see σ → CML. When you see β → SML. Simple.

Why individual stocks are BELOW the CML but ON the SML:

  • An individual stock has TOTAL risk = systematic + nonsystematic
  • CML plots return vs. TOTAL risk → the stock has more total risk than its systematic risk alone
  • For the stock's systematic risk level, the CML would predict a higher return than the stock actually earns → stock is BELOW CML
  • SML plots return vs. SYSTEMATIC risk only → nonsystematic risk is ignored → correctly-priced stocks lie ON SML

Beyond CAPM — Limitations & Extensions

Theoretical Limitations of the CAPM

  • Single-factor model: Only beta matters. Very restrictive — ignores all other potentially relevant factors (size, value, momentum, etc.)
  • Single-period model: Does not account for multi-period decisions. Can lead to myopic choices (e.g., default now to maximize one-period return, ignoring future consequences)

Practical Limitations of the CAPM

  • True market portfolio unobservable: Should include ALL assets (human capital, closed-economy assets), but most aren't measurable (Roll's critique)
  • Market proxy varies: Different analysts use different market proxies (S&P 500, MSCI World, etc.) → different return estimates. CAPM theory requires a unique market portfolio
  • Beta estimation issues: Long history needed (3-5 years); historical beta may not reflect current/future beta. Ex ante theory applied with ex post data
  • Poor empirical predictor: Tests of CAPM show that returns are NOT determined by beta alone. Weak empirical support
  • Homogeneous expectations unrealistic: Investors really do have different expectations → multiple optimal portfolios and SMLs exist in practice

Extensions of the CAPM — Two Categories

Theoretical: Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT)

Developed by Stephen Ross. Multi-factor linear model based on no-arbitrage condition:

E(Rp) = RF + λ₁βp,1 + ... + λKβp,K

  • λj = risk premium for factor j
  • βp,j = sensitivity to factor j
  • Does NOT specify what the factors are
  • Factors need not be common across assets
  • Theoretically elegant but rarely used in practice (hard to identify factors)
Practical: Fama-French-Carhart 4-Factor

Empirically successful — predicts returns better than CAPM for US stocks:

Ri − Rf = α + βMKT·MKT + βSMB·SMB + βHML·HML + βUMD·UMD

  • SMB: small caps beat large caps
  • HML: value beats growth
  • UMD: winners beat losers (momentum)
  • Coefficient on MKT often insignificant → market risk doesn't matter much after controlling for other factors
  • Not based on equilibrium theory (unlike CAPM)
⚠️ APT vs. CAPM Key Distinction

APT is based on no-arbitrage (if arbitrage opportunities exist, they'd be exploited away). CAPM is based on market equilibrium (all investors hold optimal portfolio). APT is more flexible but doesn't tell you WHICH factors to use. CAPM is rigid but specific.

The Four Performance Appraisal Ratios

① The Sharpe Ratio

Uses total risk (σ). Also called the "reward-to-variability ratio". Equals the slope of the CAL.

★ Sharpe Ratio ★
SR = [E(Rp) − Rf] / σp    (ex ante)

SR = (R̄p − R̄f) / σ̂p    (ex post)
Interpretation: excess return per unit of TOTAL risk.
Higher = better (assuming positive numerator).
Limitations:
(1) Uses total risk (only systematic risk is priced) — best when portfolio is the entire investment (not added to another portfolio)
(2) Ratio value alone is meaningless — only useful for comparison
(3) Rankings break down if numerator is negative (less negative ≠ better when ratio is negative)
② The Treynor Ratio

Uses beta (systematic risk). Fixes Sharpe's main issue — only systematic risk is priced, so divide by beta instead of σ.

★ Treynor Ratio ★
TR = [E(Rp) − Rf] / βp    (ex ante)

TR = (R̄p − R̄f) / β̂p    (ex post)
Interpretation: excess return per unit of SYSTEMATIC risk.
Appropriate when portfolio is part of a broader diversified portfolio.
Limitations:
(1) Does NOT work with negative beta (denominator must be positive)
(2) Does not give economic significance of over/underperformance
(3) Numerator must also be positive for rankings to work
③ M² (M-Squared / Modigliani-Modigliani RAP)

Risk-adjusted performance measure — expressed in percentage points. Same rankings as Sharpe ratio but more interpretable. Developed by Franco Modigliani and Leah Modigliani (1997).

★ M² Formula ★
M² = [E(Rp) − Rf] × (σm/σp) + Rf

Equivalently: M² = SR × σm + Rf
Idea: Leverage/de-leverage the portfolio until its σ equals σm. Then compare the adjusted return to market return.
σm/σp = portfolio-specific leverage ratio

M² Alpha = M² − Rm = risk-adjusted outperformance vs. market (in percentage points)
Positive M² alpha → outperformed market on risk-adjusted basis.
💡 M² Worked Example

R̄f = 4%, R̄p = 14%, σp = 25%, σm = 20%.
Sharpe = (14 − 4)/25 = 0.4
M² = 0.4 × 20 + 4 = 12.0%
If market return = 10%, M² alpha = 12% − 10% = +2.0% outperformance on risk-adjusted basis.

④ Jensen's Alpha (α)

Uses systematic risk (beta). Measures the difference between actual return and CAPM-predicted return. Most commonly used measure for evaluating institutional managers, pension funds, and mutual funds.

★ Jensen's Alpha ★
αp = Rp − {Rf + βp [E(Rm) − Rf]}

αp = Actual Return − CAPM Required Return
α > 0: portfolio outperformed CAPM prediction → superior risk-adjusted returns → skilled manager
α < 0: portfolio underperformed → unskilled manager
α = 0: portfolio earned exactly what CAPM predicts
By definition: α of the market = 0

Jensen's alpha is the maximum you should pay a manager. An alpha of 2% means the manager adds 2% of value.
Graphical interpretation: vertical distance from the SML.

Performance Ratios — Master Comparison

Ratio Risk Measure Formula When to Use Interpretation
Sharpe Total risk (σ) (Rp − Rf) / σp Portfolio is full investment (undiversified investor) Ratio; ranks portfolios; not absolute
Treynor Systematic (β) (Rp − Rf) / βp Portfolio is part of diversified portfolio Ratio; ranks portfolios; not absolute
M² Total risk (σ) SR × σm + Rf Same use as Sharpe; easier to interpret Percentage return; directly comparable to market
Jensen's α Systematic (β) Rp − [Rf + βp(Rm−Rf)] Most common for evaluating mutual funds/managers Percentage alpha; directly shows over/underperformance
💡 Key Pairings and Relationships

Sharpe & M² use TOTAL risk (σ) → rank portfolios IDENTICALLY
Treynor & Jensen's α use SYSTEMATIC risk (β) → related but can rank differently
Sharpe is the slope of the CAL
M² is a rescaled Sharpe (expressed as a % return instead of a ratio)
Jensen's α is the vertical distance from the SML

⚠️ Exam Traps on Performance Ratios

1. Treynor ratio doesn't work for negative beta (denominator issue).
2. Sharpe/Treynor rankings break if numerator is negative (a less-negative ratio isn't necessarily better when it comes from higher risk).
3. Sharpe uses σ, Treynor uses β — pay attention to which risk measure the question provides.
4. M² and Sharpe always rank the same; Treynor and Jensen's α use beta but can disagree.
5. Use Sharpe when the portfolio is the investor's ENTIRE portfolio; use Treynor/Jensen's when it's PART of a larger diversified portfolio.

Applications: Portfolio Construction & Security Selection

Security Characteristic Line (SCL)

SECURITY CHARACTERISTIC LINE (SCL)
  • Plots excess security return vs. excess market return (both over Rf)
  • X-axis: Rm − Rf (excess market return)
  • Y-axis: Ri − Rf (excess security return)
  • Slope: βi (beta of the security)
  • Intercept: αi (Jensen's alpha — ex post)
Security Characteristic Line Equation
Ri − Rf = αi + βi(Rm − Rf)
Estimated by regressing (Ri − Rf) on (Rm − Rf).
Slope gives β; intercept gives Jensen's α.
Related to but different from SML: SCL plots returns over time for ONE security; SML plots expected return vs. β across securities.

Security Selection Using the SML

Core principle: Compare an analyst's expected return to the CAPM-required return. Position relative to the SML tells you if a security is a buy/sell/hold.

💡 SML Example

Given Rf = 5%, E(Rm) = 15%:
Asset A: R = 11%, β = 0.5 → CAPM predicts 5 + 0.5(15−5) = 10%. Expected 11% > required 10% → ABOVE SML → BUY.
Asset B: R = 12%, β = 1.0 → CAPM predicts 5 + 1.0(10) = 15%. Expected 12% < required 15% → BELOW SML → SELL/short.
Asset C: R = 20%, β = 1.2 → CAPM predicts 5 + 1.2(10) = 17%. Expected 20% > required 17% → ABOVE SML → BUY.

Implications of CAPM for Portfolio Construction

Practical steps based on CAPM insights:

  1. Start with a broad index (e.g., S&P 500) as the base portfolio — treat it as "the market"
  2. Diversification basics: most nonsystematic risk is eliminated with ~30 randomly selected, diverse securities
  3. Evaluate candidate securities: for any non-index security, calculate its α using CAPM with the index as the market
  4. Add securities with positive α: they offer excess return relative to their beta
  5. Overweight undervalued index members: securities within the index with positive α should be overweighted
  6. Underweight/short overvalued securities: those with negative α
  7. Weight by Information Ratio: weights proportional to αi / σ²e,i (higher α and lower nonsystematic risk = more weight)
Information Ratio (for security selection weighting)
IRi = αi / σe,i
αi = Jensen's alpha of security i
σe,i = nonsystematic (residual) risk of security i

Higher IR = more valuable security. Measures abnormal return per unit of nonsystematic risk added to a well-diversified portfolio.
The optimal weight for a non-market security in a portfolio is proportional to αi / σ²e,i.
💡 Why ~30 Securities Diversifies Most Nonsystematic Risk

As you add securities (randomly selected, representing different asset classes), nonsystematic variance falls rapidly. By ~30 securities, nearly all of it is gone. BUT this only works if securities are randomly selected and diverse. 30 tech stocks won't diversify industry-specific risk. Hence the CAPM takeaway: use broad index funds to get the market efficiently.

Last-Minute Exam Checklist

  • ✅ Homogeneity of expectations: CAPM assumption that ALL investors have same probability beliefs → single optimal risky portfolio = market portfolio.
  • ✅ Passive portfolios: replicate market indexes; low cost; assume market is informationally efficient. Active portfolios: use own valuations; higher costs.
  • ✅ The "market": theoretically ALL risky assets (including human capital); practically a broad proxy like S&P 500.
  • ✅ CML EQUATION: E(Rp) = Rf + [(E(Rm)−Rf)/σm] × σp. Slope = Sharpe ratio = market price of risk.
  • ✅ CML plots: E(R) vs. σ (TOTAL risk). Applies ONLY to efficient portfolios (well-diversified). Individual stocks lie BELOW CML.
  • ✅ CML is a straight line because Rf has σ=0 AND Cov=0 with market — the cross-term in variance formula vanishes.
  • ✅ Lending portfolio: between Rf and M (positive w in Rf). Borrowing portfolio: right of M (negative w in Rf, leveraged).
  • ✅ Kinked CML: when borrowing rate Rb > lending rate Rf, the CML has a LOWER slope to the right of M.
  • ✅ Systematic risk: market risk, non-diversifiable, PRICED. Examples: interest rates, inflation, business cycles. Measured by β.
  • ✅ Nonsystematic risk: company/industry-specific, diversifiable, NOT PRICED. Examples: drug trial failure, strike. Can be eliminated through diversification.
  • ✅ Total variance = Systematic variance + Nonsystematic variance: σ²i = β²iσ²m + σ²e. Equation is in VARIANCES, not std devs.
  • ✅ Why nonsystematic risk isn't priced: if it were, investors would diversify, get the return without the risk, demand would push price up, and return would fall to zero.
  • ✅ Return-generating models: can be multi-factor; types = macroeconomic, fundamental, statistical. All include market as at least one factor.
  • ✅ Statistical factor models are prone to data mining (e.g., Super Bowl winner as predictor — no economic meaning).
  • ✅ Fama-French-Carhart 4-factor model: MKT, SMB (size), HML (value), UMD (momentum). Historically, βMKT often not significant.
  • ✅ Single-index model: E(Ri) − Rf = βi[E(Rm) − Rf]. Same as CAPM equation.
  • ✅ Market model: Ri = αi + βiRm + ei. Used to ESTIMATE BETA via regression and compute abnormal returns.
  • ✅ BETA FORMULA: βi = Cov(Ri, Rm)/σ²m = ρi,mσiσm/σ²m = ρi,m(σi/σm).
  • ✅ Market beta = 1, risk-free beta = 0. Average beta of all stocks = 1.
  • ✅ β > 1: aggressive/volatile. β < 1: defensive. β < 0: moves opposite market (rare).
  • ✅ Portfolio beta IS a weighted average: βp = Σ wiβi. Unlike std dev, beta has no cross-terms.
  • ✅ Beta estimation tradeoff: shorter period = reflects current risk but less accurate; longer period = more accurate but may be stale.
  • ✅ CAPM EQUATION: E(Ri) = Rf + βi[E(Rm) − Rf]. Cornerstone formula.
  • ✅ Market risk premium = E(Rm) − Rf. Asset's own risk premium = βi × market risk premium.
  • ✅ CAPM Assumptions (6): risk-averse rational investors · frictionless markets · single holding period · homogeneous expectations · infinitely divisible · price takers.
  • ✅ SML EQUATION: Same as CAPM — E(Ri) = Rf + βi[E(Rm) − Rf]. SML is CAPM drawn as a line.
  • ✅ SML plots: E(R) vs. β (SYSTEMATIC risk). Applies to ALL securities, efficient or not. Slope = market risk premium.
  • ✅ CML vs. SML — KEY DIFFERENCE: CML has σ on x-axis (total risk, efficient portfolios only); SML has β on x-axis (systematic risk, ALL assets).
  • ✅ CAPM applications: valuation, capital budgeting, performance appraisal, security selection, cost of capital, insurance pricing.
  • ✅ CAPM theoretical limitations: single-factor, single-period.
  • ✅ CAPM practical limitations: unobservable true market (Roll's critique), varying proxies, beta estimation issues, poor empirical fit, unrealistic homogeneity.
  • ✅ APT (Ross): multi-factor, based on no-arbitrage. Flexible but doesn't specify factors. Rarely used practically.
  • ✅ SHARPE RATIO: (Rp − Rf) / σp. Total risk. Slope of CAL. Good for undiversified investors.
  • ✅ TREYNOR RATIO: (Rp − Rf) / βp. Systematic risk. Good for part-of-larger-portfolio contexts. Fails for negative beta.
  • ✅ M²: [E(Rp) − Rf](σm/σp) + Rf. Rescaled Sharpe; expressed as % return. Same ranking as Sharpe.
  • ✅ M² alpha = M² − Rm = risk-adjusted outperformance in percentage points.
  • ✅ JENSEN'S ALPHA: αp = Rp − [Rf + βp(Rm − Rf)]. Actual − CAPM required. Uses beta. Positive = outperformed.
  • ✅ Jensen's α of market = 0 by definition. Alpha is max you should pay a manager.
  • ✅ Sharpe and M² rank portfolios IDENTICALLY. Both use total risk (σ).
  • ✅ Treynor and Jensen both use systematic risk (β) but can rank differently.
  • ✅ Numerator positive required for Sharpe/Treynor rankings to work correctly.
  • ✅ Security Characteristic Line (SCL): Ri − Rf = αi + βi(Rm − Rf). Plots excess security return vs. excess market return over time.
  • ✅ SML security selection: above SML = undervalued (buy), on SML = fairly valued, below SML = overvalued (sell/short).
  • ✅ ~30 randomly diverse securities diversifies most nonsystematic risk.
  • ✅ Information Ratio: IR = αi / σe,i. Measures abnormal return per unit of nonsystematic risk. Higher = more valuable security.
  • ✅ Optimal weight in a non-market security is proportional to αi/σ²e,i.

E(Ri) = Rf + βi × [E(Rm) − Rf]
Market risk premium = E(Rm) − Rf
Asset risk premium = βi × [E(Rm) − Rf]

═══ CML (efficient portfolios only) ═══
E(Rp) = Rf + [(E(Rm) − Rf)/σm] × σp
CML slope = Sharpe ratio of market = market price of risk

═══ BETA ═══
βi = Cov(Ri, Rm) / σ²m
βi = ρi,m × σi × σm / σ²m
βi = ρi,m × (σi / σm)
βmarket = 1, βRf = 0
βp = Σ wi βi (weighted average)

═══ RISK DECOMPOSITION ═══
σ²i = β²i σ²m + σ²e
Total variance = Systematic + Nonsystematic
σi = √(β²iσ²m + σ²e)

═══ MARKET MODEL (regression) ═══
Ri = αi + βiRm + ei
Abnormal return = Ri − (αi + βiRm) = ei

═══ SINGLE-INDEX MODEL ═══
E(Ri) − Rf = βi[E(Rm) − Rf]

═══ FAMA-FRENCH-CARHART 4-FACTOR ═══
Ri − Rf = α + βMKT·MKT + βSMB·SMB + βHML·HML + βUMD·UMD

Sharpe: SR = (Rp − Rf) / σp  [total risk]
Treynor: TR = (Rp − Rf) / βp  [systematic risk]
M²: M² = SR × σm + Rf = (Rp − Rf)(σm/σp) + Rf
M² alpha: M² − Rm
Jensen's α: αp = Rp − [Rf + βp(Rm − Rf)]

═══ SECURITY CHARACTERISTIC LINE (SCL) ═══
Ri − Rf = αi + βi(Rm − Rf)

═══ INFORMATION RATIO ═══
IRi = αi / σe,i
Optimal non-market weight ∝ αi / σ²e,i

E(Rp) = Rf + λ₁βp,1 + λ₂βp,2 + ... + λKβp,K
Critical distinctions to cement:
CML uses σ (total risk), efficient portfolios only.
SML uses β (systematic risk), ALL securities.
Sharpe & M² use σ; Treynor & Jensen's α use β.
Portfolio BETA is weighted avg; portfolio STD DEV is NOT (except when ρ=+1).
Total VARIANCE = systematic + nonsystematic (variances add, not std devs).
βmarket = 1 always; βRf = 0 always.
