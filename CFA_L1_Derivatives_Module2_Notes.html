Forward Contracts

Forward Contract
An OTC derivative in which two counterparties agree that the buyer will purchase an underlying from the seller at a future date at a pre-agreed fixed price (the forward price). A firm commitment — both parties are obligated. No cash is exchanged at inception. Final settlement occurs at maturity T only (single future exchange). More flexible than futures: custom size, maturity, underlying, credit terms.

Key notation:

  • F₀(T) = forward price agreed at t=0 for delivery at t=T (subscript = when price is set; parenthesis = when exchange occurs)
  • S₀ = spot price of the underlying at inception (t=0)
  • S_T = spot price of the underlying at maturity (t=T)
  • No upfront payment → payoff = profit (no premium to subtract)
  • Buyer = long forward; benefits from rising underlying prices
  • Seller = short forward; benefits from falling underlying prices

Forward Payoff Profiles — Buyer & Seller

Forward Payoff & Profit Formulas
BUYER (Long Forward):
Payoff = Profit = S_T − F₀(T)
S_T > F₀(T) → Buyer GAINS [S_T − F₀(T)] > 0
S_T < F₀(T) → Buyer LOSES [S_T − F₀(T)] < 0

SELLER (Short Forward):
Payoff = Profit = F₀(T) − S_T
S_T < F₀(T) → Seller GAINS [F₀(T) − S_T] > 0
S_T > F₀(T) → Seller LOSES [F₀(T) − S_T] < 0
Why payoff = profit for forwards? No upfront payment is made at t=0. The only cash flow occurs at maturity T — the net difference between the spot price and the forward price. There is no premium to deduct from the payoff.

Symmetric payoff: Buyer's gain = Seller's loss (zero-sum game). This symmetric, linear payoff is the defining feature of all firm commitments.
✅ Long Forward — Buyer
  • Agrees to BUY underlying at F₀(T) at time T
  • Benefits when S_T RISES above F₀(T)
  • Unlimited upside (no cap on S_T)
  • Unlimited downside (S_T could fall to zero)
  • No premium paid — payoff = profit
  • Forward buyer benefits like a long position in the spot market
📉 Short Forward — Seller
  • Agrees to SELL underlying at F₀(T) at time T
  • Benefits when S_T FALLS below F₀(T)
  • Upside limited to F₀(T) (S_T can't fall below 0)
  • Unlimited downside (S_T could rise infinitely)
  • No premium received — payoff = profit
  • Forward seller benefits like a short position in the spot market
💡 Firm Commitments Are Linear (Linear Derivatives)

Forward contracts (and all firm commitments) have a linear payoff profile — payoff is a straight-line function of the underlying price at maturity. A $1 increase in S_T always increases the buyer's payoff by exactly $1 and decreases the seller's payoff by exactly $1. This linearity is why firm commitments are called linear derivatives. Compare to options (non-linear/asymmetric — see Section 5).

⚠️ Forward vs. Spot Market — Same Economic Exposure, Different Cash Flows

A long forward position creates similar exposure to buying the underlying in the spot market, but with key differences. In the spot market, you pay S₀ today and receive a return of (S_T − S₀) at time T. In the forward, you pay nothing today and receive (S_T − F₀(T)) at time T. The forward price F₀(T) is set above S₀ (the spot price) to account for the cost of carry — the financing cost of holding the underlying from today to T. Both approaches give similar total economic exposure, but the timing and amount of cash flows differ.

Futures Contracts & Mark-to-Market (MTM)

Futures Contract
A standardized forward contract traded on an exchange. All terms (contract size, underlying specifications, maturity dates, delivery type) are set by the exchange. Futures combine: (1) contract uniformity, (2) organized exchange with rules and regulations, and (3) a central clearing facility — providing both greater liquidity and protection against counterparty default.

Futures price notation:

  • f₀(T) = futures price agreed at inception (t=0) for delivery at T
  • f_t(T) = futures price on day t during the contract's life
  • f_T(T) = final futures settlement price at maturity (converges to spot price S_T)
  • No upfront payment — same as forwards
  • Same final payoff as forward: Buyer = S_T − f₀(T); Seller = f₀(T) − S_T
  • Key difference from forwards: daily MTM settlement of gains and losses

Mark-to-Market (MTM) / Daily Settlement

The most important feature distinguishing futures from forwards is the daily mark-to-market (MTM) process, also called daily settlement. At the end of each trading day, the clearinghouse:

  1. Calculates the settlement price (end-of-day average of final trading prices)
  2. Marks all open contracts to this settlement price
  3. Credits the accounts of those with MTM gains (day's price moved in their favor)
  4. Debits the accounts of those with MTM losses (day's price moved against them)

This daily settlement effectively resets the futures contract each day — rather than accumulating a single large settlement at maturity, gains and losses are transferred continuously throughout the contract's life.

Margin System — Three Key Concepts

ConceptDefinitionKey Detail
Initial Margin Required minimum deposit when entering a futures contract. Deposited by BOTH buyer and seller into a futures margin account held at the exchange. No cash exchange at inception — but margin must be deposited. E.g., LME gold: $4,950 per 100-oz contract. This is not the full contract value — it's a small fraction (leverage).
Maintenance Margin Minimum balance that must be maintained in the futures margin account throughout the contract's life. Set BELOW the initial margin. E.g., LME gold: $4,500 (below the $4,950 initial margin). If the margin balance falls below this level, a margin call is triggered.
Variation Margin / Margin Call When the margin account falls below the maintenance margin, the exchange issues a margin call — a demand to immediately deposit funds to restore the account to the initial margin level. Must be deposited immediately. If a counterparty fails to meet the margin call, the position is closed out and the clearinghouse covers any remaining shortfall from its insurance fund.
Margin Call Trigger & Amount
Margin call triggered when: Margin Balance < Maintenance Margin

Amount of margin call = Initial Margin − Current Margin Balance
(Must replenish back to INITIAL margin, not just maintenance margin)

Example: Initial = $4,950 | Maintenance = $4,500 | Balance falls to $4,362
→ Margin call triggered ($4,362 < $4,500)
→ Must deposit $4,950 − $4,362 = $588 (back to initial, not just maintenance)
Key trap: Margin calls restore the account to INITIAL margin (not maintenance margin). Always top up to the initial margin level.
Daily gain/loss per contract: (Closing futures price − Previous day's futures price) × Contract size
Positive = gain for buyer / loss for seller. Negative = loss for buyer / gain for seller.
💡 Additional Futures Safeguards

Price limits: Some futures contracts restrict daily price moves to a band around the previous settlement price. Trading halts if price moves outside the band until two parties agree within the band. Purpose: prevent panic-driven runaway moves and give the clearinghouse time to call margins.
Circuit breakers: Brief intraday trading pause if a price limit is hit.
Intraday margin calls: For large positions or high volatility, exchanges may issue margin calls intraday (not just at end of day) and may increase required margins.
Open interest: Total number of outstanding futures contracts. At maturity, open interest is settled via cash or physical delivery. Alternatively, a position can be closed before maturity by entering an offsetting contract (buyer sells the contract back).

💡 Forward vs. Futures — Same Final Payoff, Different Timing

If a forward and a futures contract have the same underlying, same maturity, and same initial price, the final cumulative settlement is identical. The difference is TIMING: the forward settles in a single payment at maturity, while the futures settles via daily MTM throughout the contract's life. Due to the time value of money, these are not strictly equivalent — but for short maturities and low interest rates, the difference is negligible. Also: in forwards, the financial intermediary bears counterparty risk; in futures, the clearinghouse guarantees performance.

Whether Procam used a forward or a futures contract, the final loss is $1,163. The futures just distributed that loss across multiple days via daily MTM. The forward would settle the full $1,163 in one payment at maturity. Net economic outcome: identical (ignoring time value of money effects on margin flows, which are small for short maturities).

Swaps

Swap Contract
A firm commitment under which two counterparties exchange a series of cash flows in the future. One cash flow stream is typically variable (floating, reset each period based on a market reference rate); the other is typically fixed (the swap rate). Unlike forwards and futures which have a single future exchange, swaps involve multiple future exchanges over the swap's tenor.

Interest Rate Swap — The Most Common Swap

In an interest rate swap, one party pays a fixed rate (the swap rate) and receives a floating rate (MRR), while the other pays floating and receives fixed. The two parties are:

  • Fixed-rate payer / Floating-rate receiver: Pays fixed swap rate; receives MRR. Benefits when MRR rises above swap rate.
  • Floating-rate payer / Fixed-rate receiver: Pays MRR; receives fixed swap rate. Benefits when MRR falls below swap rate.

Notional principal: NOT exchanged — it is used only for calculating the fixed and floating interest payment amounts. This is a key difference from a loan.

Net payment: Counterparties exchange only the net difference between the fixed and floating payment each period (not gross amounts).

Interest Rate Swap — Key Mechanics
Each period net payment (from fixed-rate payer's perspective):
Net paid = (Swap Rate − MRR) × Notional × Period fraction
If MRR > Swap Rate → Fixed-rate payer RECEIVES net (floating payment exceeds fixed)
If MRR < Swap Rate → Fixed-rate payer PAYS net (fixed payment exceeds floating)

Swap rate (fixed leg) is determined by:
→ Setting fixed leg PV = floating leg PV (using forward MRRs to project future floating payments)
→ Result: swap rate is the constant fixed yield that makes the two legs equal in PV at inception

Value at inception = ZERO (by design — like a forward contract)
Value changes over time as market rates change (similar to bond price changes)
Swap as a series of forwards: An interest rate swap can be viewed as a bundle of forward rate agreements (FRAs) — one for each payment date. Each payment date has an implied forward MRR; the fixed rate is set to make the PV of all these forward rates equal the PV of all fixed payments.
Esterr example from Module 1: Esterr pays fixed, receives CORRA (MRR). Net effect: Esterr effectively fixes its floating-rate loan at a blended fixed rate = (swap fixed rate + credit spread on loan). If CORRA rises, the swap compensates Esterr — its floating loan cost rises, but so does its swap receipt.

💡 Using Swaps to Change Duration Without Trading Bonds

An investment manager with a long-duration bond portfolio can use a pay-fixed interest rate swap to effectively shorten duration. By paying fixed and receiving floating: if rates rise (reducing bond prices), the swap generates a gain (since the floating received exceeds the fixed paid when rates are high). This offsetting gain from the swap partially compensates the bond portfolio losses from rising rates — reducing the portfolio's net interest rate sensitivity (duration) without buying or selling bonds.

Options — Core Concepts & Terminology

Options are the primary contingent claim. The option buyer has the right but not the obligation to transact; the option seller (writer) has the obligation to fulfill the transaction if the buyer exercises. Because of this asymmetry, the option buyer pays an upfront option premium to the seller at t=0.

TermDefinition
Option Premium (c₀ or p₀)Upfront payment from option BUYER to option SELLER at t=0. Compensates seller for taking on the one-sided obligation. For calls: c₀. For puts: p₀.
Exercise Price (Strike Price, X)The pre-agreed price at which the buyer can buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying. The "trigger" price. May be above, at, or below the forward price.
Maturity Date (T)The date on which the option expires. European options may only be exercised AT maturity. American options may be exercised at any time before or at maturity.
ExerciseThe act of the option buyer deciding to transact the underlying at the exercise price. Exercised only when profitable.
Call OptionGives the buyer the RIGHT TO BUY the underlying at X. Buyer exercises when S_T > X.
Put OptionGives the buyer the RIGHT TO SELL the underlying at X. Buyer exercises when S_T < X.
European OptionCan only be exercised AT maturity (t=T). Note: "European" refers to exercise style, not geography.
American OptionCan be exercised at ANY time from inception to maturity. Note: "American" refers to exercise style, not geography.

Moneyness — In, At, or Out of the Money

In-the-Money (ITM)
  • Call ITM: S_t > X (underlying above strike)
  • Put ITM: S_t < X (underlying below strike)
  • Intrinsic value > 0 — worth exercising immediately
  • Option has both intrinsic value AND time value
At-the-Money (ATM)
  • Call ATM: S_t = X
  • Put ATM: S_t = X
  • Intrinsic value = 0
  • Option price = time value only
Out-of-the-Money (OTM)
  • Call OTM: S_t < X
  • Put OTM: S_t > X
  • Intrinsic value = 0
  • Option price = time value only
  • Would not be exercised if maturity today
💡 European vs. American Options — "Labels" Don't Mean Geography

European options are exercisable ONLY at maturity. Most index options and many OTC options are European. The CFA exam's Module 2 focuses on European options (all payoff formulas below assume exercise only at T).
American options are exercisable ANY TIME up to and including maturity. Most individual stock options traded on US exchanges (CBOE, etc.) are American-style. American options are always worth at least as much as their European equivalents (extra flexibility cannot reduce value). The exam may note this distinction but the primary calculations use European-style payoffs.

Call Options — All Four Positions

Call Option Payoff & Profit — All Equations
LONG CALL (Buyer) — Equations 1 & 2:
Payoff: c_T = max(0, S_T − X)
Profit: Π = max(0, S_T − X) − c₀

SHORT CALL (Seller) — Mirror of buyer:
Payoff: −c_T = −max(0, S_T − X) = min(0, X − S_T)
Profit: Π = −max(0, S_T − X) + c₀
X = exercise price (strike) | S_T = underlying price at maturity | c₀ = call premium paid at t=0
max(0, S_T−X) ensures payoff is NEVER negative for the buyer (the "floor" at zero).
The buyer's breakeven: S_T = X + c₀ (underlying must exceed strike by at least the premium paid to profit)
✅ Long Call — Buyer
  • Pays premium c₀ at t=0 (upfront cost)
  • Has the RIGHT (not obligation) to BUY at X
  • Exercises when S_T > X (option in-the-money)
  • Does NOT exercise when S_T ≤ X (expires worthless)
  • Payoff = max(0, S_T − X) — always ≥ 0
  • Profit = max(0, S_T − X) − c₀
  • Breakeven: S_T = X + c₀
  • Max loss = c₀ (premium paid — known upfront)
  • Upside = unlimited (S_T has no ceiling)
  • Benefits: rising underlying price
📉 Short Call — Seller (Writer)
  • Receives premium c₀ at t=0 (max profit)
  • Has the OBLIGATION to sell at X if buyer exercises
  • Buyer exercises against seller when S_T > X
  • Keeps full premium when S_T ≤ X (buyer doesn't exercise)
  • Payoff = −max(0, S_T − X) — always ≤ 0
  • Profit = −max(0, S_T − X) + c₀
  • Breakeven: S_T = X + c₀
  • Max gain = c₀ (premium received)
  • Downside = unlimited (S_T can rise infinitely)
  • Benefits: stable or falling underlying price

Call Option — Worked Examples

Call Option: c₀ = $5, X = $30, Exercise style: European
Scenario 1: S_T = $40 (ITM — buyer exercises)
Long Call: Payoff = max(0, 40−30) = $10 | Profit = $10 − $5 = +$5
Short Call: Payoff = −$10 | Profit = −$10 + $5 = −$5

Scenario 2: S_T = $25 (OTM — buyer does NOT exercise)
Long Call: Payoff = max(0, 25−30) = $0 | Profit = $0 − $5 = −$5 (loss = premium)
Short Call: Payoff = $0 | Profit = $0 + $5 = +$5 (gain = premium)

Scenario 3: S_T = $35 (breakeven)
Long Call: Payoff = max(0, 35−30) = $5 | Profit = $5 − $5 = $0 (breakeven)
Short Call: Payoff = −$5 | Profit = −$5 + $5 = $0 (breakeven)
⚠️ Short Call — The Most Dangerous Option Position

The short call position has unlimited downside risk — theoretically, the underlying price could rise to infinity, and the call seller must deliver at the strike price X regardless. This is why writing (selling) uncovered (naked) call options is considered one of the riskiest positions in derivatives. The maximum gain is capped at the premium received (c₀), but losses are unbounded. Institutions that sell calls typically hedge the position by holding the underlying stock (covered call strategy).

Put Options — All Four Positions

Put Option Payoff & Profit — All Equations
LONG PUT (Buyer) — Equations 3 & 4:
Payoff: p_T = max(0, X − S_T)
Profit: Π = max(0, X − S_T) − p₀

SHORT PUT (Seller) — Mirror of buyer — Equations 5 & 6:
Payoff: −p_T = −max(0, X − S_T)
Profit: Π = −max(0, X − S_T) + p₀
X = exercise price | S_T = underlying price at maturity | p₀ = put premium paid at t=0
max(0, X−S_T): payoff is positive only when S_T < X (put is in-the-money — buyer SELLS above market)
Breakeven for long put buyer: S_T = X − p₀ (underlying must fall below strike by at least the premium)
✅ Long Put — Buyer
  • Pays premium p₀ at t=0
  • Has the RIGHT to SELL underlying at X
  • Exercises when S_T < X (put in-the-money)
  • Does NOT exercise when S_T ≥ X (expires worthless)
  • Payoff = max(0, X − S_T) — always ≥ 0
  • Profit = max(0, X − S_T) − p₀
  • Breakeven: S_T = X − p₀
  • Max loss = p₀ (premium paid)
  • Max gain = X − p₀ (if S_T falls to 0)
  • Benefits: falling underlying price
  • Used as portfolio insurance against price declines
📉 Short Put — Seller (Writer)
  • Receives premium p₀ at t=0 (max profit)
  • Has OBLIGATION to BUY underlying at X if exercised
  • Buyer exercises against seller when S_T < X
  • Keeps full premium when S_T ≥ X (buyer doesn't exercise)
  • Payoff = −max(0, X − S_T) — always ≤ 0
  • Profit = −max(0, X − S_T) + p₀
  • Breakeven: S_T = X − p₀
  • Max gain = p₀ (premium received)
  • Max loss = X − p₀ (if S_T falls to 0)
  • Benefits: stable or rising underlying price
  • Short put resembles a long position in the underlying

Put Option — Worked Examples

Put Option: p₀ = $4, X = $50, European style
Scenario 1: S_T = $35 (ITM — buyer exercises)
Long Put: Payoff = max(0, 50−35) = $15 | Profit = $15 − $4 = +$11
Short Put: Payoff = −$15 | Profit = −$15 + $4 = −$11

Scenario 2: S_T = $60 (OTM — buyer does NOT exercise)
Long Put: Payoff = max(0, 50−60) = $0 | Profit = $0 − $4 = −$4 (loss = premium)
Short Put: Payoff = $0 | Profit = $0 + $4 = +$4 (gain = premium)

Scenario 3: S_T = $46 (breakeven: X − p₀ = 50 − 4 = $46)
Long Put: Payoff = max(0, 50−46) = $4 | Profit = $4 − $4 = $0 (breakeven)
Short Put: Payoff = −$4 | Profit = −$4 + $4 = $0 (breakeven)

💡 The Option Buyer's Payoff Is ALWAYS ≥ 0

This is the fundamental property of contingent claims: the option buyer's PAYOFF is always zero or positive — never negative. The buyer will simply choose not to exercise if doing so would result in a loss. However, the buyer's PROFIT (payoff minus premium paid) CAN be negative — if the payoff doesn't recover the upfront premium. Maximum loss for any option buyer = the premium paid. This asymmetry (buyer's limited loss, seller's limited gain) is the defining contrast with firm commitments.

Option Value Components — Intrinsic Value & Time Value

Option Price Decomposition (Before Maturity, t < T)
Option Price (c_t or p_t) = Intrinsic Value + Time Value

Call Intrinsic Value = max(0, S_t − X)
Put Intrinsic Value = max(0, X − S_t)

Time Value = Option Price − Intrinsic Value

At maturity (t = T): Time value = 0 → Option price = Intrinsic value only
Intrinsic value = the option's exercise value RIGHT NOW if it could be exercised immediately. For OTM and ATM options, intrinsic value = 0 (not negative — the buyer simply wouldn't exercise).

Time value = the additional value above intrinsic value, reflecting the possibility that the option may become more profitable before expiry. ALWAYS positive before maturity (t < T). Declines to zero as maturity approaches (time value decay / theta decay).

Why time value is positive: The longer the time to maturity, the more time for a favorable price move in the underlying. This increases both the probability and potential magnitude of exercise — increasing the option's value.
MoneynessIntrinsic Value (Call)Time ValueOption Price
ITM (S_t > X)(S_t − X) > 0Positive (but lower for deep ITM)Intrinsic + Time value
ATM (S_t = X)0Maximum time value (highest uncertainty)= Time value only
OTM (S_t < X)0Positive (reflects remaining time for recovery)= Time value only
💡 At Maturity — Time Value Always Decays to Zero

As an option approaches maturity, its time value erodes to zero — this is called time decay (or theta). At the final expiration moment, the option is worth exactly its intrinsic value (exercise it or let it expire worthless). This is why option sellers (who received the premium upfront) benefit from the passage of time — the option they sold becomes worth less each day, all else equal. Option buyers suffer from time decay — they need a sufficiently large and fast price move to overcome the erosion of time value.

⚠️ Option Credit Risk — One-Sided

For options, counterparty credit risk is one-sided: once the premium is paid at t=0, the option seller has NO credit exposure to the buyer (the buyer can only decline to exercise — they can't default on a future obligation). However, the option buyer faces credit risk of the option seller — at maturity, if the option is in-the-money, the seller must pay. If the seller defaults, the buyer loses the payoff. This is the reverse of typical firm commitment bilateral credit risk.

Credit Default Swaps (CDS)

Credit Default Swap (CDS)
The most common credit derivative. A contract in which the credit protection buyer pays periodic fixed spread payments to the credit protection seller in exchange for the seller assuming the risk of loss from a credit event (default) by an underlying third-party issuer. The CDS notional is not exchanged — it serves as the basis for calculating payments.
Credit Protection BUYER
  • Pays a periodic fixed spread (CDS spread) to the seller throughout the contract
  • Receives a contingent payment from seller if a credit event occurs
  • Credit event = bankruptcy, failure to pay, involuntary debt restructuring
  • Settlement payment = LGD (loss given default) × CDS notional
  • Acts like an insurance buyer — pays regular premiums for protection
  • Benefits when issuer CDS spread WIDENS (credit deteriorates)
  • With existing bond exposure → CDS is a HEDGE
  • Without existing bond exposure → CDS is SHORT CREDIT (speculating on deterioration)
Credit Protection SELLER
  • Receives periodic fixed spread payments from buyer
  • Must make contingent payment to buyer if credit event occurs
  • Payment = LGD × Notional (timing and amount uncertain until event)
  • Acts like an insurance seller — receives premiums, assumes contingent risk
  • Benefits when issuer CDS spread TIGHTENS (credit improves)
  • Similar to a LONG credit position in the underlying issuer's bond
  • Loses when credit events occur — required to make large settlement payments
CDS — Key Relationships & Mechanics
CDS spread (bps) reflects: Probability of Default (POD) × Loss Given Default (LGD)
Higher CDS spread → worse credit quality → lower cash bond price
Lower CDS spread → better credit quality → higher cash bond price

CDS at inception priced at PAR SPREAD → NPV = 0 at initiation (like interest rate swap)
As CDS spread changes over time → MTM value deviates from zero

Credit event triggers contract termination:
→ Seller pays: LGD × Notional to buyer
→ Three defined credit events: Bankruptcy | Failure to Pay | Involuntary Debt Restructuring

CDS MTM during life: use effective duration to approximate MTM change
CDS spread widens → buyer GAINS (low fixed spread paid vs. higher market spread)
CDS hybrid nature: Despite the name "swap," CDS contracts are classified as contingent claims — because the contingent credit event payment occurs only IF a credit event happens. This is similar to an option payout that depends on a trigger event. However, CDS also shares features of firm commitments (regular fixed spread payments occur regardless — like a swap's periodic cash flows). CDS = hybrid of firm commitment (periodic spread) + contingent claim (credit event payment).

At par spread: Net present value of fixed leg = Net present value of contingent protection leg → NPV = 0. This is analogous to a swap priced at par.
💡 CDS as Insurance — The Structural Analogy

A CDS works very similarly to insurance:
Insurance buyer = CDS protection buyer: pays regular premiums, gets paid if the insured event occurs.
Insurance seller = CDS protection seller: receives regular premiums, pays out if the insured event occurs.
Key difference from traditional insurance: CDS buyers do NOT need to hold the underlying bond to buy protection. A CDS buyer without the bond is "naked" — purely speculating on credit deterioration (short credit). This "insurable interest" distinction is a key regulatory and conceptual point.

Long Call vs. Long Forward — When Does Each Outperform?

Long Call vs. Long Forward (X = F₀(T))
Long Forward Profit = S_T − F₀(T) [no premium, linear, symmetric]
Long Call Profit = max(0, S_T − F₀(T)) − c₀ [non-linear, limited downside]

Forward profit > Call profit when: S_T − F₀(T) > −c₀
→ i.e., when S_T > F₀(T) − c₀ (underlying moderately above or well above X)

Call profit > Forward profit when: S_T − F₀(T) < −c₀
→ i.e., when S_T < F₀(T) − c₀ (underlying well below X)
→ The call's downside protection (floor at −c₀) outperforms the forward's unlimited losses

Key insight:
Long call = long forward + downside protection — in exchange for paying a premium (c₀)
Long call vs. long forward analogy: The long call is like a long forward position with insurance against price declines below X. The premium (c₀) is the cost of that insurance.
Short put vs. long forward analogy: The short put is like a long forward with the upside gains above X forfeited in exchange for receiving a premium (p₀). The short put gains from rising prices (like a long forward) but profits are capped at p₀.
💡 Four Ways to Get Bullish on an Underlying

A market participant who is bullish (expects S_T to rise) can use any of these positions — each with different payoff profiles:
1. Long spot: Buy the underlying now at S₀ — direct exposure, requires full capital.
2. Long forward: Agree to buy at F₀(T) — no upfront payment, linear payoff, unlimited downside.
3. Long call: Pay premium c₀ for the right to buy at X — limited downside (just premium), unlimited upside. Premium = cost of the floor.
4. Short put: Receive premium p₀ for obligation to buy at X — capped upside (premium only), limited downside (X − p₀ if underlying falls to zero). Premium = compensation for taking on the downside.

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Forward contract = OTC firm commitment; buyer agrees to buy underlying from seller at F₀(T) at time T. No cash at inception. Payoff = Profit = S_T − F₀(T) for buyer; F₀(T) − S_T for seller. Single settlement at maturity.
  • ✅ Long forward benefits when S_T rises. Short forward benefits when S_T falls. Both sides have symmetric, unlimited risk — linear payoff profile (= linear derivative).
  • ✅ Futures = standardized exchange-traded forwards. Same final payoff as forwards but settled via daily MTM (mark-to-market) rather than a single settlement at maturity.
  • ✅ Daily MTM: Clearinghouse settles gains and losses daily. Futures buyer's margin account is credited (debited) when futures price rises (falls). Seller's account is the mirror image.
  • ✅ Initial margin = deposit required to open a futures position. Maintenance margin = minimum balance; set below initial margin. Margin call = triggered when balance falls below maintenance margin; must restore to INITIAL margin level (not just maintenance).
  • ✅ Variation margin = the amount deposited in response to a margin call. Price limits and circuit breakers are additional exchange safeguards.
  • ✅ Swap = series of future cash flow exchanges (firm commitment). One leg usually fixed (swap rate); other usually floating (MRR). Notional NOT exchanged — used only for payment calculation. Value = 0 at inception.
  • ✅ Interest rate swap (most common): Fixed-rate payer receives floating (MRR); floating-rate payer receives fixed. Swap rate set so PV(fixed leg) = PV(floating leg) at inception. Net payments exchanged each period.
  • ✅ Options = contingent claims. Buyer has RIGHT (not obligation); seller has OBLIGATION. Buyer pays upfront premium. Payoff to buyer is ALWAYS ≥ 0 (floored at zero via the max() function).
  • ✅ Call option: buyer's RIGHT TO BUY at X. Exercise when S_T > X. Payoff = max(0, S_T−X). Profit = max(0, S_T−X) − c₀. Breakeven = X + c₀.
  • ✅ Put option: buyer's RIGHT TO SELL at X. Exercise when S_T < X. Payoff = max(0, X−S_T). Profit = max(0, X−S_T) − p₀. Breakeven = X − p₀.
  • ✅ Long call: unlimited upside, max loss = c₀. Short call: max gain = c₀, unlimited downside. Long put: max gain = X−p₀, max loss = p₀. Short put: max gain = p₀, max loss = X−p₀.
  • ✅ European vs. American: European = exercisable only AT maturity (CFA exam default). American = exercisable at any time before or at maturity. Labels refer to exercise style, not geography.
  • ✅ Moneyness: Call ITM = S_t > X. Call OTM = S_t < X. Put ITM = S_t < X. Put OTM = S_t > X. ATM = S_t = X for both. ITM options have positive intrinsic value; OTM/ATM options have zero intrinsic value.
  • ✅ Option price = Intrinsic value + Time value. Intrinsic value = max(0, S_t−X) for call; max(0, X−S_t) for put. Time value always positive before maturity; decays to zero at maturity.
  • ✅ Non-linear (asymmetric) payoff is the defining feature of contingent claims. Firm commitments have linear (symmetric) payoff. This is the core contrast: non-linear = contingent claim; linear = firm commitment.
  • ✅ CDS (Credit Default Swap): credit protection buyer pays fixed periodic spread; seller assumes contingent credit event payment. Credit event = bankruptcy/failure to pay/involuntary restructuring. Settlement = LGD × Notional.
  • ✅ CDS buyer = short credit (benefits from spread widening). CDS seller = long credit (benefits from spread tightening). CDS priced at par spread has NPV = 0 at inception (like a swap). Hybrid: regular fixed payments (firm commitment feature) + contingent credit event payment (contingent claim feature).
  • ✅ Long call vs. Long forward (X = F₀(T)): Both benefit from rising prices. Long call protects downside (loss floor = −c₀ vs. unlimited for forward) but costs a premium. When S_T is well below X, long call outperforms forward (limited loss vs. large loss). When S_T is well above X, forward outperforms (no premium drag).
  • ✅ Short put vs. Long forward: Short put benefits from rising prices but caps upside at p₀ (premium received). Underperforms forward when S_T rises far above X (forward profit is unlimited; short put capped). Outperforms forward when prices fall somewhat (premium received provides a cushion).
