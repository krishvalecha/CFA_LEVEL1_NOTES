01

Floating-Rate Note (FRN) — Mechanics & Key Definitions

A floating-rate note (FRN) — also called a floater — is a debt instrument whose coupon payments are not fixed. Instead, the coupon is reset periodically based on a market reference rate (MRR) plus a stated spread. The coupon adjusts automatically with changing interest rates, which is the fundamental reason FRNs have much less price risk than fixed-rate bonds.

Most loans are floating-rate instruments. Banks prefer making floating-rate loans because their funding (deposits, short-term borrowings) is also floating — this maintains an asset-liability match on their balance sheet.

Antelas AG FRN — Term Sheet Summary (Key Terms)
  • Issuer: Antelas AG (German emerging technology company)
  • Maturity: 4 years | Principal: EUR 250 million
  • Coupon: MRR + 250 bps per annum
  • Reset & Payment frequency: Quarterly (MRR is reset quarterly; interest paid quarterly)
  • Payment structure: "In arrears" — MRR is determined at the START of each period; interest paid at the END
  • Day-count: Actual/360 or Actual/365 (most common for FRNs and loans)
  • Quoted margin: 250 bps — the fixed spread above MRR locked in at issuance

Key Terminology — All Definitions

Market Reference Rate (MRR)
The floating benchmark rate to which the FRN's coupon is linked. Usually a short-term money market rate (e.g., 3-month SOFR, EURIBOR, MRR). The MRR is reset on predetermined dates — typically at the beginning of each coupon period. Also sometimes generically called "Index" in pricing models.
Quoted Margin (QM)
The fixed spread above (or below) the MRR specified in the bond's indenture at the time of issuance. It compensates investors for the difference between the credit risk of the issuer and that implied by the MRR (which reflects the risk of high-quality banks). The QM is contractually fixed for the life of the bond — it does NOT change when the issuer's credit quality changes. Example: Antelas FRN QM = 250 bps.
Discount Margin (DM) / Required Margin
The spread over (or under) the MRR that the market currently requires to price the FRN at its fair value given current credit risk. The DM is the FRN equivalent of a yield spread for a fixed-rate bond. It is determined by the market at any point in time and changes when the issuer's credit risk changes. Also called the "required margin."
💡 Memory Hook — QM is Contractual, DM is Market

QM = contractually fixed (set at issuance, never changes) — like the coupon rate on a fixed-rate bond.
DM = market-determined (changes with credit risk) — like the YTM on a fixed-rate bond.
The gap between DM and QM is what drives the FRN's price away from par. When DM = QM, the FRN prices at par. When DM > QM, the FRN prices at a discount. When DM < QM, the FRN prices at a premium.

💡 "In Arrears" Payment Structure

For most FRNs and loans: the MRR is observed/set at the beginning of the coupon period, and interest is paid at the end of the period. This is called "in arrears." For example, a quarterly FRN determines its coupon on January 1 based on MRR observed that day, then pays that coupon on April 1. The coupon amount is known for the full period as soon as the MRR resets — this is a key difference from having unknown future coupon cash flows.

02

Quoted Margin vs. Discount Margin — The Price Driver

The relationship between QM and DM determines whether the FRN is priced at par, at a discount, or at a premium. This is the FRN equivalent of comparing coupon rate to YTM for a fixed-rate bond — the same logic applies, just with margins instead of rates.

ConditionFRN Price vs. ParReasonFixed-Rate Bond Analogy
DM = QM Price = Par (100) Required spread equals the quoted spread — investors receive exactly the compensation they require; no premium or discount needed Coupon rate = YTM → price at par
DM > QM Price < Par (Discount) Market requires more spread than the bond pays. The "deficient" interest payment per period = DM − QM. The discount = PV of this shortfall annuity for remaining life Coupon rate < YTM → price at discount
DM < QM Price > Par (Premium) Market requires less spread than the bond pays. The "excess" interest payment per period = QM − DM. The premium = PV of this excess annuity for remaining life Coupon rate > YTM → price at premium
★ The FRN Rule of Three — Commit This to Memory
DM = QM → FRN at PAR
DM > QM → FRN at DISCOUNT (market wants MORE than bond pays → penalizes price)
DM < QM → FRN at PREMIUM (market wants LESS than bond pays → rewards price)
What causes DM to change? Changes in the issuer's credit risk (most common), changes in liquidity, or changes in tax status. The MRR itself changing does NOT change the DM — the coupon adjusts automatically to absorb MRR moves, which is the whole point of a floater.

Example — Antelas credit downgrade:
Originally: DM = QM = 50 bps → FRN at par
After downgrade: DM rises to 75 bps, QM stays at 50 bps
Deficiency = 75 − 50 = 25 bps per period
FRN now trades at a discount = PV of 25 bps annuity for remaining periods

Example — Credit improvement:
If DM falls to 40 bps, QM still = 50 bps → excess = 10 bps per period → FRN at premium

Pull-to-par for FRNs: Between reset dates, if the MRR moves, the flat price temporarily deviates from par:

  • MRR rises between resets → current coupon (fixed until next reset) is now relatively low → flat price falls slightly below par
  • MRR falls between resets → current coupon is relatively high → flat price rises slightly above par
  • But at each reset date (assuming DM = QM), the flat price is pulled back to par — the same pull-to-par mechanism as for fixed-rate bonds, but operating on a much shorter cycle (quarterly or semiannual resets)
⚠️ Key Distinction — What Moves Price for Fixed vs. Floating Rate Bonds

Fixed-rate bond price changes: driven by changes in BOTH benchmark rates AND credit risk (spread). Both affect the YTM directly.
FRN price changes: driven almost entirely by changes in CREDIT RISK (changes in DM). Changes in the benchmark MRR affect the coupon — but the price barely moves because the cash flows adjust with the rate. This is the core advantage of floating-rate bonds: they hedge interest rate risk while retaining credit risk.

03

FRN Pricing Model — Equation 1

Unlike fixed-rate bonds, FRNs require a specialized pricing model because both the numerator (coupon) and denominator (discount rate) include the floating MRR. The simplified model below assumes: (1) pricing on a rate reset date (so flat price = full price, no accrued interest); (2) 30/360 day count so periodicity m is an integer; (3) the same MRR applies to all future periods (a key simplification — more complex models project future rates).

Equation 1 — FRN Pricing Model (Simplified)
PV = [(MRR + QM)/m × FV] / (1 + (MRR + DM)/m)¹
+ [(MRR + QM)/m × FV] / (1 + (MRR + DM)/m)²
+ ...
+ [(MRR + QM)/m × FV + FV] / (1 + (MRR + DM)/m)ᴺ
Variables:
PV = price of the FRN (flat price = full price on reset date)
MRR = market reference rate (annual percentage rate)
QM = quoted margin (annual percentage rate — fixed in the indenture)
DM = discount margin (annual percentage rate — market-determined required margin)
FV = face/par value of the bond
m = periodicity (number of payment periods per year; e.g., 2 = semiannual, 4 = quarterly)
N = number of periods to maturity

Coupon per period = (MRR + QM)/m × FV ← numerator uses QUOTED margin
Discount rate per period = (MRR + DM)/m ← denominator uses DISCOUNT margin

Key: divide all annual rates by m since they are stated as annual APRs.

Worked Example 1 — Pricing FRN (Given DM, Solve for Price)

Example: 2-year semiannual FRN, MRR + 0.50%, MRR = 1.25%, DM = 0.40%
Inputs: MRR = 0.0125, QM = 0.0050, FV = 100, m = 2, DM = 0.0040, N = 4

Coupon per period = (0.0125 + 0.0050)/2 × 100 = 0.875 per period
Discount rate per period = (0.0125 + 0.0040)/2 = 0.00825 per period

PV = 0.875/(1.00825)¹ + 0.875/(1.00825)² + 0.875/(1.00825)³ + (0.875 + 100)/(1.00825)⁴
PV = 100.196

Result: PV = 100.196 > 100 → PREMIUM because QM (50 bps) > DM (40 bps)

Worked Example 2 — Solving for Discount Margin (Given Price)

Example: 2-year semiannual FRN, MRR + 0.75%, MRR = 1.10%, Price = 95.50
Inputs: PV = 95.50, MRR = 0.0110, QM = 0.0075, FV = 100, m = 2, N = 4

Use Excel Solver: set PV = 95.50, solve for DM
→ DM = 3.12% = 312 bps

Interpretation: The FRN was issued at par (DM = QM = 75 bps).
After credit downgrade: DM rose to 312 bps, but QM is still fixed at 75 bps.
Deficiency = 312 − 75 = 237 bps per period → FRN trades at DISCOUNT (price = 95.50 < 100)
💡 Solving for DM — The Process

When given the market price of an FRN, solving for DM requires iteration (cannot be solved algebraically, like solving for YTM). Use:
Excel Solver: Set the cell containing PV formula equal to the observed market price. Set DM as the "changing variable." Solve.
This is the same approach as using Excel IRR or Goal Seek for YTM on fixed-rate bonds. The DM is an estimate — it is model-dependent on the simplifying assumption that the same MRR applies to all future periods.

⚠️ DM is an Estimate — Model Limitations

The DM from Equation 1 is a simplified estimate for three reasons: (1) the model prices as of a reset date — between reset dates, more complex adjustments are needed; (2) it uses a 30/360 day count, but most FRNs use actual/360; (3) it assumes the same MRR for ALL future periods — in reality, future MRR will change. More complex models project future MRR using forward rates and use spot rates for discounting. The exam tests the simplified model, but know why it's an approximation.

FRN Price vs. Par — Complete Rules & Pull-to-Par

Understanding when an FRN trades at a discount, par, or premium — and why — is critical. The key insight is that the MRR level itself does NOT drive the FRN away from par. The FRN adjusts its coupon automatically as MRR moves. Only the MARGIN gap (DM vs. QM) creates a persistent deviation from par.

At Par (DM = QM)
  • Investor receives exactly the required spread
  • No deficiency or excess payment
  • Priced at 100 on every reset date
  • Regardless of MRR level — because coupon adjusts with MRR
Discount (DM > QM)
  • Deficiency per period = DM − QM
  • Discount = PV of deficiency annuity
  • Caused by: credit downgrade, liquidity deterioration
  • Pulled back to par at each reset date (if DM stays > QM, pulled to new discount level)
Premium (DM < QM)
  • Excess per period = QM − DM
  • Premium = PV of excess annuity
  • Caused by: credit upgrade, improvement in issuer quality
  • Some issuers (very low credit risk) can issue at negative quoted margin (below MRR)
★ Price Deviation from Par — Conceptual Formula
Premium or Discount vs. Par = PV of (QM − DM) annuity for remaining periods

If QM > DM: Premium = PV of (QM − DM) per period for N remaining periods
If QM < DM: Discount = PV of (DM − QM) per period for N remaining periods (a negative deviation)
If QM = DM: No deviation → price = par
This annuity is discounted at the discount rate per period = (MRR + DM)/m.
The pull-to-par works because as N decreases, the PV of the annuity shrinks → the price deviation from par narrows as maturity approaches, just like for fixed-rate bonds.

💡 Why Banks Love Floating-Rate Loans

Bank liabilities (deposits, short-term borrowings) are predominantly floating-rate — they reset frequently with market rates. If banks made mostly fixed-rate loans, a rise in market rates would increase their funding costs while their loan income stayed fixed → a dangerous mismatch. By making floating-rate loans (tied to MRR), banks ensure that when their funding costs rise, their loan income rises too. This is the core of asset-liability management (ALM).

Money Market Instruments — Overview & Key Differences from Bonds

Money Market Instruments
Debt securities with original maturities of one year or less. They are a significant source of short-term financing for issuers and a short-duration, low-risk investment for investors. Short maturity means: investors can reinvest quickly, and issuers can refinance quickly — both reducing interest rate risk.

Types of Money Market Instruments

Discount Rate Basis Instruments
  • Treasury bills (T-bills) — short-term sovereign debt (1 to 52 weeks)
  • Commercial paper — short-term corporate unsecured debt
  • Bankers' acceptances — bank-guaranteed trade finance instruments
  • Quoted at face value; interest is the discount from face value
Add-On Rate Basis Instruments
  • Bank certificates of deposit (CDs) — time deposits at banks
  • Repurchase agreements (repos) — short-term collateralized borrowing
  • Market reference rate indices (SOFR, EURIBOR, etc.)
  • Quoted at issuance price; interest is added to principal at maturity

Money market mutual funds invest solely in eligible money market securities and are sometimes used as an alternative to bank deposits — they offer higher yields but are not insured like bank deposits.

Three Key Differences: Money Market vs. Bond Yield Measures

1
Simple interest, not compound interest. Bond YTMs are annualized and compounded (using TVM with (1+r)^N). Money market yields are annualized but NOT compounded — they use simple interest. This means money market rates understate the true compound return, especially for longer maturities within the sub-1-year space.
2
Different periodicities for different maturities. Bond YTMs use a consistent periodicity (e.g., all semiannual bonds use periodicity 2). Money market periodicity = Year/Days — a 90-day instrument has periodicity 365/90 ≈ 4.06; a 30-day instrument has periodicity 365/30 ≈ 12.2. Different maturities → different periodicities → rates are NOT directly comparable without conversion.
3
Non-standard interest rate conventions. Money market instruments use either discount rate (DR) basis or add-on rate (AOR) basis — not standard TVM formulas. These require different pricing equations (Eq. 2–4 below). Some use 360-day year; others use 365-day year. This inconsistency makes direct comparisons across instruments impossible without converting to a common basis.

Discount Rate Basis — Equations 2 & 3

What is a Money Market Discount Rate?
  • NOT the same as a "discount rate" in TVM (which is the rate used to calculate present value)
  • In the money market, the discount rate (DR) means: interest is embedded in (included in) the face value of the instrument
  • The investor pays LESS than face value; at maturity, receives face value — the difference IS the interest
  • The "quoted amount" for DR instruments = face value at maturity (FV)
  • Interest = FV − PV (the discount from face value)
  • T-bills, commercial paper, and bankers' acceptances are quoted on DR basis
Equation 2 — Price of a Discount Rate Money Market Instrument
PV = FV × (1 − Days/Year × DR)
PV = price (purchase price) of the instrument
FV = face value paid at maturity
Days = number of days from settlement to maturity
Year = number of days in the year (360 or 365 depending on convention)
DR = discount rate (annual percentage rate)

Worked Example — 91-day Indian T-bill, FV = INR 10,000,000, DR = 3.45%, Year = 360:
PV = 10,000,000 × (1 − 91/360 × 0.0345)
PV = 10,000,000 × (1 − 0.008725)
PV = 10,000,000 × 0.991275 = INR 9,912,750 ≈ 9,912,792
Equation 3 — Solving for the Discount Rate (Rearranging Equation 2)
DR = (Year/Days) × [(FV − PV)/FV]
First term (Year/Days) = the periodicity of the annual rate for this specific instrument
Second term [(FV − PV)/FV] = interest earned ÷ FACE VALUE (not purchase price)

Why this understates the true return:
In theory, an interest rate = earnings ÷ investment amount (PV). The DR uses FV in the denominator, not PV.
Since PV < FV (whenever DR > 0), dividing by FV gives a SMALLER number than dividing by PV.
Therefore: DR < actual rate of return on the investment (measured on the PV invested).
DR understates both: (1) the rate of return to the investor, and (2) the cost of borrowed funds to the issuer.
⚠️ Critical Exam Trap — DR Understates True Return

The money market discount rate is intentionally designed with FV in the denominator — which makes it an understated rate. The investor earned (FV − PV) on an investment of PV, not FV. The correct return measure should use PV in the denominator, which is what the add-on rate does. DR always understates the investor's true return when PV < FV (i.e., when DR > 0). This is why converting to AOR or BEY gives a higher number than the quoted DR.

08

Add-On Rate Basis — Equation 4

What is a Money Market Add-On Rate?
  • Interest is ADDED to the principal (investment amount) at maturity
  • The investor pays the principal (PV) upfront; at maturity receives PV + interest = FV
  • The "quoted amount" for AOR instruments = price at issuance (PV)
  • Interest = FV − PV, but now denominator is PV (the actual investment) — conceptually correct
  • Bank CDs, repos, and MRR indices (SOFR, EURIBOR) are quoted on AOR basis
  • AOR > DR for the same instrument — because AOR uses PV (smaller denominator) → higher rate
Equation 4 — Price of an Add-On Rate Money Market Instrument
PV = FV / (1 + Days/Year × AOR)
PV = price at issuance (principal invested)
FV = redemption amount at maturity (principal + interest)
Days = number of days from settlement to maturity
Year = number of days in the year (360 or 365)
AOR = add-on rate (annual percentage rate)

Solving for AOR from observed PV and FV:
AOR = (Year/Days) × [(FV − PV)/PV]

Note: denominator is PV (correct — interest earned ÷ amount invested). This is conceptually correct as a rate of return measure.

💡 DR vs. AOR — The Denominator Is Everything

Both rates have the same numerator: (FV − PV) = the interest earned. The ONLY difference is the denominator: DR uses FV, AOR uses PV. Since PV < FV, dividing by PV (AOR) gives a LARGER rate. So for the same instrument and same cash flows: AOR > DR. To convert: given a DR, the AOR will always be higher. This is the key relationship tested on the exam.

Bond Equivalent Yield (BEY) — Converting to a Common Basis

Bond Equivalent Yield (BEY)
A money market rate restated on a 365-day add-on rate basis. It allows money market instruments to be compared to each other and to bonds on a standardized basis. BEY is the standard "common basis" for converting money market instruments.

To compare money market instruments with different: (1) quote conventions (DR vs. AOR), (2) day-count assumptions (360 vs. 365), and (3) maturities — all instruments must be restated on a BEY basis.

Three-Step Process to Convert DR-Basis Instrument to BEY

1
Calculate the price (PV) using the discount rate.
PV = FV × (1 − Days/Year × DR)
Use Year = 360 if the instrument is quoted on a 360-day basis.
2
Calculate the Add-On Rate (AOR) using the price from Step 1.
AOR = (Year/Days) × [(FV − PV)/PV]
This restates the return using PV as the denominator (the correct investment amount).
3
Restate the AOR on a 365-day basis = Bond Equivalent Yield (BEY).
If Year used in Step 2 was 360: BEY = AOR × 365/360
The BEY is now the standard metric — comparable across all money market instruments and to bonds.
Worked Example — Converting 91-day T-bill (DR = 3.45%, 360-day) to BEY
Step 1: PV = 100 × (1 − 91/360 × 0.0345) = 100 × 0.99128 = 99.128

Step 2: AOR = (360/91) × [(100 − 99.128)/99.128]
= 3.956 × (0.872/99.128)
= 3.956 × 0.008797 = 0.03480 = 3.480%

Step 3: BEY = 3.480% × (365/360) = 3.528%

Result: A T-bill quoted at DR = 3.45% (360-day basis) has a BEY of 3.528%
Note: BEY (3.528%) > DR (3.45%) — confirming DR understates true return
The BEY (3.528%) is the rate that can now be directly compared to other money market instruments and to bond yields stated on a 365-day AOR basis.

Converting Money Market Rate to Semiannual Bond Basis

To compare a money market rate directly to a bond YTM (expressed on a semiannual bond basis, periodicity = 2), the BEY must be further converted using the periodicity conversion formula from Module 7.

MM Rate to Semiannual Bond Basis — Worked Example
Given: 91-day Indian T-bill, BEY = 3.50% (stated on 365/91 periodicity basis)
Target: Convert to semiannual bond basis (periodicity = 2)

Periodicity conversion formula: (1 + APRm/m)^m = (1 + APRn/n)^n

m = 365/91 (MM periodicity), n = 2 (semiannual bond basis)
(1 + 0.035/(365/91))^(365/91) = (1 + APR₂/2)²
(1 + 0.035 × 91/365)^(365/91) = (1 + APR₂/2)²
APR₂ = 3.515%

Difference: 3.515% − 3.500% = +1.5 bps
(The semiannual equivalent is slightly HIGHER since you're compounding less frequently)
General rule: The difference between a MM rate and its semiannual bond equivalent is small but depends on the rate level. At lower interest rates, the difference between any two periodicities shrinks. At higher rates, it grows. On the exam, this conversion is tested to confirm you know MM periodicity ≠ bond periodicity and that they cannot be compared directly.
💡 Money Market Periodicity = Year/Days

Unlike bonds (which always have a fixed periodicity of 1, 2, or 4), money market instruments have a periodicity that CHANGES with maturity: periodicity = Year/Days. A 90-day instrument in a 365-day year has periodicity = 365/90 ≈ 4.06. A 180-day instrument has periodicity = 365/180 ≈ 2.03. This is why a 90-day MM rate and a 180-day MM rate CANNOT be directly compared — even if they have the same AOR, they have different periodicities, so their effective annual returns differ.

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ FRN coupon = MRR + QM. MRR resets on predetermined dates (usually quarterly); QM is fixed for the bond's life. Payment structure: "in arrears" — MRR set at START of period, interest paid at END.
  • ✅ QM (quoted margin) is contractually fixed at issuance — never changes. DM (discount margin / required margin) is market-determined — changes with credit risk, liquidity, or tax status. DM = FRN equivalent of YTM spread for a fixed-rate bond.
  • ✅ FRN Rule of Three: DM = QM → price = par. DM > QM → price at DISCOUNT (deficient coupon). DM < QM → price at PREMIUM (excess coupon). This is the direct analog of coupon vs. YTM for fixed-rate bonds.
  • ✅ Deficiency/excess per period = |DM − QM|. The price deviation from par = PV of this deficiency or excess as an annuity over remaining periods, discounted at (MRR + DM)/m per period.
  • ✅ FRN pull-to-par: On each reset date (if DM = QM), the FRN reprices to par. Between reset dates, MRR changes cause small temporary deviations from par that reverse at the next reset.
  • ✅ FRN price risk is LOW: Cash flows adjust with MRR → MRR changes barely affect price. Only credit risk changes (DM changes) create meaningful price movements. This is why FRNs have very short effective duration.
  • ✅ Fixed-rate bond vs. FRN: For benchmark rate changes — fixed-rate bond price changes significantly; FRN price barely moves. For credit risk changes — both experience similar price impacts (discount/premium from spread gap).
  • ✅ FRN Pricing Formula (Eq. 1): Numerator uses (MRR + QM)/m × FV. Denominator uses (MRR + DM)/m as the discount rate per period. Priced as of reset date (no accrued interest). Simplified model — same MRR assumed for all periods.
  • ✅ Solving for DM from price: Given PV, use Excel Solver — set sum of discounted cash flows = observed price, with DM as the variable. DM is an estimate (model-dependent). DM > QM → discount; DM < QM → premium.
  • ✅ Banks prefer floating-rate loans to maintain asset-liability match — their funding is floating (deposits, short-term borrowings), so floating-rate assets prevent a damaging mismatch when interest rates rise.
  • ✅ Money market instruments: Original maturity ≤ 1 year. Types: T-bills, commercial paper, bankers' acceptances (DR basis); bank CDs, repos, MRR indices (AOR basis). MM mutual funds = alternative to bank deposits.
  • ✅ Three MM vs. bond differences: (1) Simple interest, not compound. (2) Variable periodicity (Year/Days) vs. fixed periodicity. (3) Non-standard conventions (DR vs. AOR, 360 vs. 365).
  • ✅ Discount Rate (DR) formula: PV = FV × (1 − Days/Year × DR). DR = (Year/Days) × (FV−PV)/FV. Quoted amount = FV. Instruments: T-bills, commercial paper, bankers' acceptances.
  • ✅ DR understates true return because denominator is FV (includes interest), not PV (the actual investment amount). DR always < AOR for the same cash flows.
  • ✅ Add-On Rate (AOR) formula: PV = FV / (1 + Days/Year × AOR). AOR = (Year/Days) × (FV−PV)/PV. Quoted amount = PV. Instruments: bank CDs, repos, MRR indices. AOR is conceptually correct (denominator = investment amount).
  • ✅ AOR > DR for the same instrument — same numerator (FV−PV), but AOR divides by smaller PV vs. DR divides by larger FV → AOR is always the higher number.
  • ✅ Bond Equivalent Yield (BEY) = MM rate restated on a 365-day AOR basis. Steps to convert DR → BEY: (1) PV = FV × (1−Days/360 × DR); (2) AOR = (360/Days) × (FV−PV)/PV; (3) BEY = AOR × 365/360.
  • ✅ BEY > DR always (corrects for DR understating return AND adjusts from 360-day to 365-day basis).
  • ✅ MM periodicity = Year/Days (varies by maturity). To convert to semiannual bond basis (periodicity 2): use the periodicity conversion formula (1 + APRm/m)^m = (1 + APRn/n)^n from Module 7.
  • ✅ Never directly compare: (a) MM instruments with different maturities — different periodicities; (b) DR-basis and AOR-basis instruments without conversion; (c) 360-day and 365-day instruments without adjustment. Always convert to BEY first.

═══ FRN KEY RELATIONSHIPS ═══
Coupon per period = (MRR + QM)/m × FV [QM = fixed; MRR = floating]
DM = required margin (market-determined, changes with credit risk)
QM = quoted margin (contractually fixed at issuance, never changes)
DM = QM → Price = Par | DM > QM → Discount | DM < QM → Premium
Deficiency = DM − QM per period → Discount = PV of deficiency annuity
Excess = QM − DM per period → Premium = PV of excess annuity

═══ FRN PRICING MODEL (Eq. 1) ═══
PV = Σ [(MRR+QM)/m × FV] / (1+(MRR+DM)/m)ᵗ + FV/(1+(MRR+DM)/m)ᴺ
Numerator: QM (what bond pays) | Denominator: DM (what market requires)
Simplified: on reset date, same MRR for all periods, 30/360 day count
Solve for DM given price → use Excel Solver (iterative, not algebraic)

═══ MONEY MARKET — DISCOUNT RATE ═══
PV = FV × (1 − Days/Year × DR)
DR = (Year/Days) × (FV−PV)/FV ← denominator FV → UNDERSTATES return
Instruments: T-bills, commercial paper, bankers' acceptances
Quoted amount = FV (face value at maturity)

═══ MONEY MARKET — ADD-ON RATE ═══
PV = FV / (1 + Days/Year × AOR)
AOR = (Year/Days) × (FV−PV)/PV ← denominator PV → correct measure
Instruments: bank CDs, repos, MRR indices
Quoted amount = PV (price at issuance)
AOR > DR (same instrument, same cash flows, PV < FV)

═══ CONVERTING TO BOND EQUIVALENT YIELD ═══
DR → BEY: Step1: PV=FV×(1−Days/360×DR) | Step2: AOR=(360/Days)×(FV−PV)/PV | Step3: BEY=AOR×365/360
AOR (360-day) → BEY: BEY = AOR × 365/360
BEY to semiannual bond basis: (1+BEY×Days/365)^(365/Days) = (1+APR₂/2)²
FRN vs. fixed-rate bond: Both use same Rule of Three (QM vs. DM ↔ coupon vs. YTM). FRNs insulated from MRR changes (coupon adjusts); sensitive only to credit risk changes (DM changes).
DR always understates return; AOR is conceptually correct. Converting DR → BEY always gives a higher number.
MM vs. bond yield measures: MM = simple interest, variable periodicity, DR or AOR basis. Bonds = compound interest, fixed periodicity, YTM basis. Never mix without conversion.
