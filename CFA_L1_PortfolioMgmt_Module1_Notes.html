Historical Return & Risk of Asset Classes

Historical Return vs. Expected Return
Historical return = what was actually earned in the past. Expected return = what an investor anticipates earning in the future. The two are conceptually different but often, historical mean is used as a proxy for expected return (an imperfect but common assumption).
Key Formula — Expected Return Decomposition
1 + E(R) = (1 + rrF) × [1 + E(π)] × [1 + E(RP)]
rrF = real risk-free interest rate (compensation for postponing consumption)
E(π) = expected inflation rate
E(RP) = expected risk premium for asset's risk
All three components are typically positive → expected nominal return is generally positive.

Historical Performance Rankings (1926–2017, US Data)

Asset Class Nominal Return Risk (σ) Key Observation
Small company stocks 12.1% 31.7% Highest return AND highest risk. Never had a negative decade
Large company stocks 10.2% 19.8% Negative in 1930s & 2000s; best in 1950s & 1990s
Long-term corporate bonds 6.1% 8.3% Never had a negative decade
Long-term government bonds 5.5% 9.9% ⚠️ Higher risk than corporates (unusual) — 1950s negative
Treasury bills 3.4% 3.1% Lowest risk; only negative in 1938 (deflation)

⚠️ Exam Trap — Government vs. Corporate Bond Risk

Long-term government bonds had HIGHER total risk (9.9%) than long-term corporate bonds (8.3%) historically — this does NOT mean they had greater default risk. Government bonds were just more variable in price (due to interest rate sensitivity of longer duration). Don't confuse total risk/volatility with default risk.

Risk–Return Trade-off

The positive relationship between expected risk and return — higher returns require accepting higher risk in efficient markets over long periods.

  • Historical ordering (high → low risk/return): small-cap stocks → large-cap stocks → corporate bonds → government bonds → T-bills
  • Nominal risk premium = Nominal risky return − Nominal risk-free rate
  • Real risk premium = Real risky return − Real risk-free rate
  • US equity real risk premium over bonds (1900–2017): 4.4% geometric, 6.5% arithmetic
  • The positive historical relationship confirms that representative investors are risk averse — this is the standard assumption in finance
💡 Geometric vs. Arithmetic Mean

Geometric mean ≤ Arithmetic mean (always). Equality only when all returns are identical. Geometric mean is more accurate for multi-period returns (it captures compounding); arithmetic mean is better for single-period expectations. The CFA uses geometric mean for long-term historical performance analysis.

Other Investment Characteristics

Mean-variance analysis assumes: (1) returns are normally distributed (fully defined by mean & variance), and (2) markets are informationally AND operationally efficient. When these assumptions break down, additional characteristics must be considered.

Distributional Characteristics — Deviations from Normality

Normal Distribution Properties
  • Mean = Median
  • Symmetric around mean
  • Defined by mean & variance only
  • 68% of obs within ±1σ
  • 95% of obs within ±2σ
  • 99% of obs within ±3σ
Real Return Distributions
  • NOT normally distributed
  • Exhibit skewness (asymmetry)
  • Exhibit excess kurtosis (fat tails)
  • Stock returns typically negatively skewed
  • Mean-variance framework may understate risk
Skewness
Asymmetry of the return distribution. Negative (left) skew: distribution concentrated to the right, with a long left tail → more extreme negative outcomes than normal would predict. Stock returns typically show negative skewness. Positive (right) skew: distribution concentrated to the left, long right tail.
Kurtosis (Fat Tails)
Higher-than-normal probability of extreme returns. Increases asset risk beyond what mean-variance captures. Addressed by techniques like Value at Risk (VaR) and conditional tail expectations. Was a major contributing factor to the 2008 financial crisis (extreme events underappreciated).

Market Characteristics — Liquidity & Trading Costs

Three components of trading cost:

  1. Brokerage commission — fixed fees paid to brokers
  2. Bid-ask spread — difference between buying and selling price (affected by liquidity)
  3. Price impact — how much the price moves in response to an order (affected by liquidity)
Bid-Ask Spread as % of Price
Bid-Ask Spread % = Spread ÷ Price
Example: $100 stock with $0.10 spread → 0.1% cost
$10 stock with $0.10 spread → 1.0% cost (10× more expensive to trade!)
Lower-priced and less-liquid stocks have higher percentage trading costs.
💡 Liquidity Matters Most When...

Liquidity concerns are more severe in: emerging markets (lower trading volumes), corporate bond markets (especially low credit quality — bonds may not trade for days/weeks), small-cap stocks, and for large institutional orders (greater price impact). Became especially apparent during 2008 global financial crisis.

Risk Aversion & Investor Types

Classic Test: Investor offered two alternatives with same expected value of £50:

  • Option A: Guaranteed £50 (certain outcome)
  • Option B: 50% chance of £100, 50% chance of £0 (gamble, E = £50)

The investor's choice reveals their risk preference type.

RISK AVERSE — Chooses the Guaranteed Outcome
  • Behavior: Prefers certain £50 over the gamble. May even accept £45 guaranteed over the £50 gamble
  • Key principle: Wants to minimize risk for given return; maximize return for given risk
  • Risk aversion coefficient (A): A > 0
  • Requires compensation (positive risk premium) to accept additional risk
  • This is the STANDARD ASSUMPTION for the representative investor in all CFA finance theory
  • Historical positive risk-return trade-off is evidence that markets are priced by risk-averse investors
RISK NEUTRAL — Indifferent Between Choices
  • Behavior: Only cares about return, NOT risk. Indifferent between the gamble and certain £50
  • Risk aversion coefficient (A): A = 0
  • Prefers higher return regardless of risk
  • Billionaires may exhibit risk-neutral behavior for trivial amounts (small fraction of wealth)
RISK SEEKING (RISK LOVING) — Chooses the Gamble
  • Behavior: Derives extra utility from uncertainty. May accept gamble with E = £45 over certain £50
  • Risk aversion coefficient (A): A < 0
  • Additional risk INCREASES utility
  • Examples: lottery tickets, casino gambling (expected value is negative but players still participate)
  • Note: isolated risky actions ≠ overall risk-seeking preference; only compulsive gamblers are truly risk-seeking
Risk Tolerance
The amount of risk an investor can tolerate to achieve an investment goal. Higher risk tolerance = greater willingness to take risk. Risk tolerance is inversely related to risk aversion (high tolerance ↔ low aversion).
💡 Mnemonic — The Three Investor Types

"ANS": Averse (A > 0, demands premium) · Neutral (A = 0, ignores risk) · Seeking (A < 0, pays for risk). Risk aversion coefficient A decreases as you become less averse and eventually goes negative for risk seekers.

Utility Theory & Indifference Curves

★ THE UTILITY FUNCTION ★ (Memorize!)
U = E(r) − ½ A σ²
U = utility derived from the investment
E(r) = expected return of the investment
A = risk aversion coefficient (higher A → more risk averse)
σ² = variance of the investment (NOT standard deviation)

Critical: variance (σ²) is used, not standard deviation. Risk aversion is applied to variance.

Properties of the Utility Function

  • Unbounded: can be highly positive or highly negative
  • Higher return → higher utility (positive first term)
  • Higher variance → lower utility for risk-averse investor (negative second term)
  • Effect amplified by A: the more risk-averse, the larger the reduction in utility from variance
  • Ordinal, not cardinal: utility measures RANKINGS, not absolute satisfaction. U = 4 is NOT "twice as good" as U = 2; we only know it's preferred
  • Not comparable across individuals: personal concept; cannot be summed or compared between people
  • Risk-free asset (σ² = 0): generates the same utility for ALL individuals regardless of their A
Investor Type Risk Aversion (A) Effect of Risk on Utility Indifference Curve
Risk AverseA > 0Risk reduces utilityUpward-sloping & convex
Highly Risk AverseA very largeLarge reduction in utility from riskSteep upward-sloping convex
Less Risk AverseA small but positiveSmall reduction from riskFlat upward-sloping convex
Risk NeutralA = 0No effectHorizontal (utility independent of risk)
Risk SeekingA < 0Risk increases utilityDownward-sloping (negative slope)
Indifference Curve
A set of risk-return combinations that provide the same utility to an investor. The investor is "indifferent" between any two points on the same curve. Plotted in (σ, E(R)) space with σ on x-axis and E(R) on y-axis.

Properties of Indifference Curves (for Risk-Averse Investors)

  • Upward-sloping: higher risk requires higher return to maintain utility (risk-return trade-off)
  • Convex (curves upward): due to diminishing marginal utility of return/wealth — as risk increases, investor needs an INCREASING amount of return per additional unit of risk
  • Higher curves = higher utility: investors want to move NORTHWEST (more return, less risk)
  • Curves never intersect for same individual (by transitivity: if X preferred to Y and Y to Z, then X preferred to Z)
  • Slope ∝ risk aversion: steeper slope = more risk averse
💡 Mnemonic — "Move Northwest"

Risk-averse investors always want to go northwest on the risk-return graph: North (higher return) + West (lower risk) = higher utility. If an exam question asks "which point has highest utility," look for the point furthest northwest that is still achievable.

⚠️ Exam Trap — Variance vs. Standard Deviation in Utility

The utility formula uses variance (σ²), NOT standard deviation. If σ = 20% = 0.20, then σ² = 0.04. Common mistake: plugging in 0.20 instead of 0.04. Also: when return is in decimal form, utility comes out in decimal form.

Example Calculation
Portfolio: E(r) = 10% = 0.10, σ = 15% = 0.15, A = 3
σ² = 0.15² = 0.0225
U = 0.10 − ½ × 3 × 0.0225 = 0.10 − 0.03375
U = 0.06625 (or 6.625%)
For a risk-neutral investor (A=0): U = E(r) = 0.10 (highest utility goes to highest return regardless of risk).
For a more risk-averse investor (A=5): U = 0.10 − ½(5)(0.0225) = 0.04375 (lower utility).

Portfolio Return & Portfolio Risk

Portfolio Return — Always a Weighted Average

Portfolio Return (N assets)
RP = Σ wi Ri     where Σ wi = 1
wi = weight of asset i in portfolio
Ri = return of asset i
Weights must sum to 1 (including cash/risk-free).
Weights are constant over the single period (no cash flows).
Two-Asset Portfolio Return
RP = w1R1 + (1 − w1)R2
Example: 25% in Asset 1 (20% return) + 75% in Asset 2 (5% return) = 0.25(20) + 0.75(5) = 8.75%

Portfolio Variance — NOT a Simple Weighted Average

★ Portfolio Variance — Two Assets (using Covariance) ★
σ²P = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ Cov(R₁, R₂)
Contains two variance terms plus one COVARIANCE cross-term (multiplied by 2).
★ Portfolio Variance — Two Assets (using Correlation) ★
σ²P = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ ρ₁₂ σ₁ σ₂
Uses the identity: Cov(R₁,R₂) = ρ₁₂ × σ₁ × σ₂
This is the most commonly tested form of the formula.
★ Portfolio Standard Deviation (Two Assets) ★
σP = √(w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ρ₁₂σ₁σ₂)
Take square root of the variance. This is the KEY formula for exam problems.
Covariance ↔ Correlation Relationship
Cov(R₁, R₂) = ρ₁₂ × σ₁ × σ₂

Equivalently: ρ₁₂ = Cov(R₁, R₂) ÷ (σ₁ × σ₂)
Covariance is unbounded (can be any real number), making it hard to interpret.
Correlation is bounded between −1 and +1, making it more intuitive.
Both contain the same directional information about co-movement.
General Portfolio Variance (N Assets)
σ²P = Σ Σ wi wj Cov(Ri, Rj)

= Σ wi² Var(Ri) + Σi≠j wi wj Cov(Ri, Rj)
First term: N variance terms (own variance of each asset).
Second term: N(N−1) covariance terms (all pairs, i ≠ j).
Note: Cov(Ri, Ri) = Var(Ri) — an asset's covariance with itself equals its variance.

Portfolio Return vs. Portfolio Risk — The Critical Distinction

MeasureFormulaWeighted Average?
Portfolio Return RP = Σ wiRi YES — always
Portfolio Variance Contains cross-terms with covariance NO — except when ρ = +1
Portfolio Std. Dev. σP = √σ²P NO — except when ρ = +1
⚠️ Exam Trap — Portfolio Risk is NEVER Just the Weighted Average

The #1 mistake: calculating σP as (w1σ1 + w2σ2). This is ONLY correct when ρ₁₂ = +1. For any ρ < +1, portfolio std. dev. will be LESS than the weighted average — this is the diversification benefit. Always use the full formula with the variance cross-term.

Correlation & Diversification Effects

Correlation Coefficient (ρ)
Measures the consistency or tendency for two investments to act in a similar way. Bounded between −1 and +1. Determines how much diversification benefit you get from combining two assets.

Three Critical Correlation Cases

Perfect Positive Correlation
ρ = +1
Assets move together 100% of the time. NO diversification benefit. Portfolio std. dev. = weighted average of individual std. devs.
Uncorrelated
ρ = 0
Movement of one asset provides no prediction about the other. Some diversification benefit; cross-term in variance becomes 0.
Perfect Negative Correlation
ρ = −1
Assets move in opposite directions 100% of the time. Maximum diversification possible. Portfolio can be made riskless with correct weights.

The Mathematical Proof of Diversification

Special Case: ρ₁₂ = +1 (Perfect Positive Correlation)
σ²P = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂σ₁σ₂
= (w₁σ₁ + w₂σ₂)² ← perfect square!

σP = w₁σ₁ + w₂σ₂
When ρ = +1, portfolio std. dev. IS a weighted average → NO diversification.
Both risk AND return are weighted averages → combining offers no benefit.
General Case: ρ₁₂ < +1 (Diversification Occurs)
σP = √(w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ρ₁₂σ₁σ₂) < (w₁σ₁ + w₂σ₂)
Portfolio std. dev. is STRICTLY LESS than the weighted average → risk reduction via diversification.
Return is still a weighted average → we get lower risk for the same return.

💡 Key Observations from the Table

1. Portfolio return is the SAME for every correlation level (only depends on weights).
2. At extremes (w=0% and w=100%), portfolio risk equals one asset's risk regardless of ρ.
3. Lower correlation → lower risk at every intermediate weight.
4. At ρ = −1, with the right weight (~70% in Asset 1), portfolio can approach ZERO risk.

⚠️ Key Concept — Curvilinear Opportunity Set

When plotting E(R) vs. σ, the risk-return curve formed by varying portfolio weights is:

  • A straight line only when ρ = +1 or ρ = −1 (extremes)
  • Curvilinear (bulges to the left/NW) for all intermediate ρ values
  • The more the curve bulges left, the more diversification benefit
07

Portfolios of Many Risky Assets

Key Insight — As N Grows, Covariance Dominates

Portfolio Variance with Equal Weights (1/N each)
σ²P = σ̄² / N + [(N−1)/N] × Cov̄
σ̄² = average variance across all assets
Cov̄ = average covariance across all asset pairs

As N → ∞: first term → 0 (variance contribution vanishes)
As N → ∞: second term → Cov̄ (covariance dominates)

Conclusion: In large portfolios, covariance between assets accounts for almost ALL portfolio risk.
Portfolio Std. Dev. with Equal Variance & Correlation
σP = √[σ²/N + ((N−1)/N) × ρσ²]
Assumes all N assets have the same variance σ² and the same pairwise correlation ρ.
As N becomes large: first term under the root → 0, leaving:
σP ≈ √(ρσ²) = σ√ρ

If ρ = 0: portfolio risk approaches ZERO as N grows.
If ρ > 0: portfolio risk floor = σ√ρ (cannot diversify below this).
💡 The Profound Conclusion

In a well-diversified portfolio, individual asset variance becomes irrelevant — what matters is the average covariance between assets. This is why we care so much about correlation: it determines the minimum risk we can achieve through diversification.

Historical Risk vs. Historical Correlation

  • Risk (volatility): relatively stable across decades for a given asset class (though bonds have been riskier in recent decades). Historical risk is a reasonable proxy for future risk
  • Within-country correlations: quite stable among assets of the same country
  • Between-country correlations: have been RISING in recent decades due to globalization and economic liberalization → reduced international diversification benefit
  • Correlation > 0.90: considered HIGH — assets don't offer much diversification
  • Correlation < 0.50: DESIRABLE for portfolio diversification

Avenues for Diversification

✓ Diversify with Asset Classes

Correlations among major asset classes are typically LOW. Include: domestic large/small caps, growth/value, corporate/government bonds, T-bills, emerging/developed markets, real estate, commodities (gold).

✓ Diversify with Index Funds

Cost-effective way to get broad exposure. A single asset class needs ~30 stocks; 10 asset classes = 300 securities → expensive. ETFs and index mutual funds are preferable for small portfolios.

✓ Diversify Across Countries

Different industry focus, economic policies, political climates. Currency returns are uncorrelated with stock returns → extra risk reduction. Include foreign investments in diversified portfolios.

✗ Avoid Employer's Stock Overexposure

You already have massive human capital tied to your employer (salary, promotions, job security). Additional stock investment = concentration risk. Evaluate employer stock like any other investment.

✓ Buy Insurance for Risky Portfolios

Insurance has negative correlation with assets (pays when assets lose value). Over time has negative expected return BUT reduces tail risk. Classic risk-return trade-off example.

✓ Gold, Commodities & Put Options

Gold historically negatively correlated with stocks. Put options pay when underlying falls (negative correlation). Both have low/negative expected returns but provide portfolio protection against catastrophic losses.

Rule for Adding an Asset to a Portfolio

★ Condition for Adding a New Asset ★
Add asset "new" to portfolio "p" if:

[E(Rnew) − Rf] / σnew > [E(Rp) − Rf] / σp × ρnew,p

(Sharpe ratio of new asset > Sharpe ratio of portfolio × correlation)
Equivalently: add if the new asset's expected return exceeds:
E(Rnew) = Rf + (σnewρnew,p / σp) × [E(Rp) − Rf]

Key insight: a low correlation (ρnew,p) makes the RHS smaller → easier to justify adding the asset.
Low-correlation assets are valuable even with modest returns; high-correlation assets need strong returns to justify inclusion.
💡 Why Insurance Makes Sense Despite Negative Returns

Insurance has negative expected return, BUT its correlation with your portfolio is negative (or near zero). In the "add asset" formula, negative ρ means the RHS threshold becomes NEGATIVE — so even a small negative return can satisfy the condition. Never reject an asset just because its expected return is low; consider its correlation.

⚠️ Costs of Adding Assets

Every new asset comes with costs: trading costs, tracking costs, and potentially REDUNDANT exposure (if the "new" asset is in an asset class you already own). Diversification has diminishing returns — beyond a point, more assets just add complexity without meaningful risk reduction.

The Efficient Frontier of Risky Assets

Building Up: From Two Assets to the Frontier

  • Two assets, ρ = +1: risk-return opportunity set = straight line connecting them
  • Two assets, ρ < +1: opportunity set curves/bulges to the left (toward NW) — diversification benefit visible
  • Adding more assets: opportunity set expands; new combinations become possible
  • All investable assets: the totality forms the investment opportunity set — a region in (σ, E(R)) space containing all achievable portfolios
  • Adding asset classes (e.g., international assets) shifts the opportunity set further NORTHWEST — better risk-return trade-offs
Investment Opportunity Set
The set of all portfolios that can be constructed from available investable assets. Includes all possible combinations of weights across all available securities. Forms a region in risk-return space with a left-boundary curve (the minimum-variance frontier).

The Three Key Concepts on the Frontier

1. MINIMUM-VARIANCE FRONTIER
  • Definition: The set of portfolios offering the minimum risk for each given level of expected return
  • Forms the LEFT-BOUNDARY of the investment opportunity set
  • Includes BOTH the upper portion (efficient) AND the lower portion (inefficient)
  • No rational investor would hold a portfolio to the right of this frontier (same return, higher risk)
2. GLOBAL MINIMUM-VARIANCE PORTFOLIO (GMVP)
  • Definition: The SINGLE portfolio with the LOWEST risk among ALL portfolios of risky assets
  • The LEFT-MOST point on the minimum-variance frontier
  • No portfolio of risky assets can have lower risk than GMVP (without adding a risk-free asset)
  • Found by solving: minimize σP subject to Σwi = 1
3. MARKOWITZ EFFICIENT FRONTIER
  • Definition: The portion of the minimum-variance frontier ABOVE and to the RIGHT of the GMVP
  • Contains portfolios offering the highest return for each level of risk
  • Contains ALL rational portfolio choices for risk-averse investors
  • Below GMVP: portfolios are INEFFICIENT (another portfolio exists with same risk but higher return)
💡 Visualizing the Frontier — The "Nike Swoosh"

The minimum-variance frontier looks like a sideways "C" or "bullet" shape. The GMVP is the leftmost tip. The top half (above GMVP) is the efficient frontier. The bottom half (below GMVP) is inefficient — for any portfolio on the bottom, there's one directly above it with same risk but higher return.

Slope Behavior on the Efficient Frontier

  • As you move RIGHT (east) along the efficient frontier, risk AND return both increase
  • But the slope DECREASES (curve flattens) — each additional unit of risk gives SMALLER incremental return
  • This reflects diminishing marginal returns — at high risk levels, additional risk yields very little extra return
  • This is why risk-averse investors don't pile unlimited risk into their portfolios
⚠️ Exam Trap — Three Related but Distinct Concepts

Opportunity Set = ALL achievable portfolios (a region).
Min-Var Frontier = LEFT BOUNDARY of the opportunity set (a curve).
Efficient Frontier = TOP HALF of the min-var frontier (from GMVP upward).
GMVP = SINGLE point at the LEFT TIP of the curve.
Don't confuse these. Exam questions often test whether you can identify which is which on a diagram.

Capital Allocation Line & Optimal Portfolio

Combining a Risk-Free Asset with a Risky Portfolio

Two-Asset Portfolio: Risk-Free + Risky (weight w₁ in risk-free)
E(RP) = w₁Rf + (1 − w₁)E(Ri)
σ²P = (1 − w₁)² σ²i [σf = 0, covariance terms drop out]
σP = (1 − w₁) σi
Because the risk-free asset has zero variance AND zero covariance with anything, the variance formula collapses to just the risky asset's contribution. This is WHY the CAL is a straight line!
★ CAPITAL ALLOCATION LINE (CAL) ★ (Memorize!)
E(RP) = Rf + [(E(Ri) − Rf) / σi] × σP
Intercept: Rf (risk-free return, where σP = 0)
Slope: (E(Ri) − Rf) / σi = the "market price of risk" = Sharpe ratio of the risky portfolio

The slope is the additional expected return per unit of risk taken. Also called the reward-to-variability ratio.
Capital Allocation Line (CAL)
A straight line plotting all combinations of a risk-free asset and a risky portfolio. Represents the portfolios available to an investor who can lend or borrow at the risk-free rate. Starts at Rf on the y-axis and extends through the risky portfolio.

Understanding Positions on the CAL

Position on CALWeight in Risk-Free (w₁)Weight in RiskyDescription
At Rf (y-intercept)w₁ = 100%0%100% in risk-free — zero risk
Between Rf and risky asset0% < w₁ < 100%0% < (1−w₁) < 100%Lending portion of wealth at Rf
At risky assetw₁ = 0%100%Fully invested in risky portfolio
Beyond risky assetw₁ < 0% (negative)> 100%Borrowing at Rf to invest more in risky = leveraged/margin
Borrowing Example
If w₁ = −0.50 (borrow 50% at Rf):
Invest 150% in risky asset
Portfolio return = 1.50 × E(Ri) − 0.50 × Rf > E(Ri)
Because E(Ri) > Rf, leverage amplifies both returns and risk.

The OPTIMAL Risky Portfolio & Optimal CAL

When we have MANY risky assets (efficient frontier) plus a risk-free asset, we can draw MANY possible CALs — one from Rf to each point on the efficient frontier.

  • Best CAL = the one with the HIGHEST slope (steepest) = highest Sharpe ratio
  • The best CAL is tangent to the efficient frontier
  • The point of tangency = OPTIMAL RISKY PORTFOLIO (P)
  • CAL(P) dominates every other CAL and every point on the efficient frontier
  • Portfolios on CAL(P) offer higher return for the same risk than any risky-asset-only portfolio
💡 The Key Insight — Adding a Risk-Free Asset Changes Everything

Without the risk-free asset, rational investors pick a point on the Markowitz efficient frontier. WITH the risk-free asset, all rational investors hold some combination of just TWO assets: the risk-free asset AND the optimal risky portfolio P. This is the insight that leads to the two-fund separation theorem.

The Two-Fund Separation Theorem

Two-Fund Separation Theorem
All investors — regardless of taste, risk preferences, or initial wealth — will hold a combination of two funds: (1) the risk-free asset, and (2) the optimal risky portfolio (P). What differs between investors is the PROPORTION they hold in each fund.

The two distinct decisions (separation):

  1. Investment Decision (same for all investors): Identify the optimal risky portfolio P. Determined purely by asset characteristics (returns, risks, correlations). Does NOT depend on individual risk preferences.
  2. Financing Decision (unique to each investor): Choose the allocation between Rf and P based on personal risk aversion. More risk-averse = more in Rf (lending); less risk-averse = more in P (possibly borrowing for leverage).

Finding the OPTIMAL INVESTOR Portfolio

Step-by-step to find the optimal portfolio for a SPECIFIC investor:

  1. Identify CAL(P) — the optimal capital allocation line (tangent to efficient frontier at P)
  2. Overlay the investor's indifference curves
  3. Find the highest indifference curve that touches CAL(P) — this is the TANGENCY point
  4. The tangency point = OPTIMAL INVESTOR PORTFOLIO
  5. Higher indifference curves are unattainable (above CAL)
  6. Lower indifference curves are achievable but sub-optimal (lower utility)
💡 Where Different Investors End Up on CAL(P)

Highly risk-averse investor: optimal portfolio close to y-axis (high w in Rf, low w in P). May invest up to 100% in Rf.
Moderately risk-averse: optimal portfolio between Rf and P — LENDING at Rf.
Less risk-averse: optimal portfolio close to P or AT P (100% in optimal risky).
Least risk-averse (risk tolerant): optimal portfolio to the RIGHT of P — BORROWING at Rf to leverage P.

⚠️ Two Different "Optimals" — Don't Confuse Them

Optimal Risky Portfolio (P): SAME for all investors. Tangency of CAL to efficient frontier. Does NOT involve individual preferences.
Optimal Investor Portfolio: DIFFERS by investor. Tangency of INDIFFERENCE CURVE to CAL(P). Depends on individual risk aversion.
The two-fund separation theorem is literally about separating these two decisions.

Summary of the Portfolio Construction Process
Step 1: Compute efficient frontier from ALL risky assets.
Step 2: Add risk-free asset → find CAL with maximum slope.
Step 3: Tangency point = optimal risky portfolio P (SAME for all).
Step 4: Overlay investor's indifference curves on CAL(P).
Step 5: Tangency of indifference curve & CAL(P) = optimal investor portfolio.
This process combines mean-variance optimization (steps 1-3) with utility theory (steps 4-5).

  • ✅ Utility is ordinal: ranks investments, doesn't measure satisfaction. Can't compare across individuals.

  • ✅ Indifference curves for risk-averse: upward-sloping, convex, never intersect, move NORTHWEST for higher utility.
  • ✅ Steeper indifference curve = more risk averse. Horizontal = risk neutral. Downward-sloping = risk seeking.

  • ✅ 2-asset variance: σ²P = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ρ₁₂σ₁σ₂.
  • ✅ Cov(R₁,R₂) = ρ₁₂σ₁σ₂. Covariance unbounded; correlation bounded [−1, +1].
  • ✅ ρ = +1: no diversification; portfolio risk = weighted avg of asset risks.
  • ✅ ρ < +1: diversification benefit; portfolio risk < weighted avg of asset risks.
  • ✅ ρ = −1: MAXIMUM diversification; can theoretically construct a RISKLESS portfolio with right weights.
  • ✅ Ρ = 0: uncorrelated; cross-term in variance formula drops out.
  • ✅ Correlation > 0.90: HIGH (little diversification). < 0.50: DESIRABLE for diversification.
  • ✅ Risk-return curve: straight line only when ρ = ±1; curves (bulges NW) for all intermediate ρ.
  • ✅ Large N portfolios: σ²P = σ̄²/N + [(N−1)/N]Cov̄. As N→∞, variance vanishes and covariance dominates.
  • ✅ Equal-variance/correlation formula: σP = √[σ²/N + ((N−1)/N)ρσ²]. As N→∞, σP → σ√ρ (floor).
  • ✅ Historical risk stable; historical correlations RISING across countries (globalization).
  • ✅ Stock-bond correlations near zero or negative → excellent diversifier.
  • ✅ Add new asset if Sharpe(new) > Sharpe(p) × ρnew,p. Low correlation makes threshold easier to beat.
  • ✅ Insurance, gold, put options: may have NEGATIVE expected return but valuable due to NEGATIVE correlation — classic risk-return trade-off.
  • ✅ Employer stock: avoid concentration because human capital already tied to employer.
  • ✅ MINIMUM-VARIANCE FRONTIER: set of portfolios with min risk for each return level. Left boundary of opportunity set.
  • ✅ GLOBAL MINIMUM-VARIANCE PORTFOLIO (GMVP): single leftmost point on min-var frontier. Lowest risk achievable from risky assets.
  • ✅ MARKOWITZ EFFICIENT FRONTIER: upper portion of min-var frontier (above GMVP). Contains all rational portfolio choices.
  • ✅ Below GMVP: inefficient — another portfolio exists with same risk but higher return.
  • ✅ Efficient frontier slope decreases moving right → diminishing marginal return per unit of risk.
  • ✅ CAL formula: E(RP) = Rf + [(E(Ri) − Rf)/σi] × σP. Straight line.
  • ✅ CAL slope = Sharpe ratio = market price of risk = extra return per unit of risk.
  • ✅ CAL is a straight line because risk-free asset has zero variance and zero covariance with anything.
  • ✅ Left of risky asset on CAL = lending at Rf. Right of risky asset = borrowing at Rf (leverage).
  • ✅ OPTIMAL RISKY PORTFOLIO (P): tangency of CAL with efficient frontier. Highest Sharpe ratio. SAME for all investors.
  • ✅ CAL(P) DOMINATES every other CAL and every point on the Markowitz efficient frontier (except P itself).
  • ✅ TWO-FUND SEPARATION THEOREM: all investors hold a combination of (1) risk-free asset and (2) optimal risky portfolio P.
  • ✅ Separation into two decisions: (1) investment decision (find P — same for all), (2) financing decision (allocate between Rf and P — differs by investor).
  • ✅ OPTIMAL INVESTOR PORTFOLIO: tangency of indifference curve with CAL(P). DIFFERS across investors based on risk aversion.
  • ✅ More risk averse → optimal portfolio closer to Rf. Less risk averse → closer to P or beyond P (leveraged).
★ KEY FORMULAS — QUICK REFERENCE (MEMORIZE ALL) ★
─── RETURNS & RISK ───
1 + E(R) = (1 + rrF) × [1 + E(π)] × [1 + E(RP)]
Nominal risk premium = Nominal risky return − Nominal Rf
Real risk premium = Real risky return − Real Rf

─── UTILITY ───
U = E(r) − ½ A σ²
(A > 0: risk averse · A = 0: neutral · A < 0: seeking)

─── PORTFOLIO RETURN (always weighted avg) ───
RP = Σ wi Ri     Σ wi = 1

─── PORTFOLIO VARIANCE (2 assets) ───
σ²P = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ Cov(R₁,R₂)
σ²P = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ ρ₁₂ σ₁ σ₂
σP = √(σ²P)

─── COVARIANCE ↔ CORRELATION ───
Cov(R₁,R₂) = ρ₁₂ × σ₁ × σ₂
ρ₁₂ = Cov(R₁,R₂) / (σ₁ × σ₂)
−1 ≤ ρ ≤ +1

─── SPECIAL CASE ρ = +1 (NO diversification) ───
σP = w₁σ₁ + w₂σ₂ (weighted avg!)

─── LARGE-N PORTFOLIO (equal weights) ───
σ²P = σ̄²/N + [(N−1)/N] × Cov̄
σP = √[σ²/N + ((N−1)/N) × ρσ²]
As N → ∞: σP → σ√ρ (risk floor)

─── ADD NEW ASSET CONDITION ───
[E(Rnew) − Rf]/σnew > [E(Rp) − Rf]/σp × ρnew,p

─── CAPITAL ALLOCATION LINE (CAL) ───
E(RP) = Rf + [(E(Ri) − Rf)/σi] × σP
Slope = Sharpe ratio = (E(Ri) − Rf) / σi

─── RISK-FREE + RISKY PORTFOLIO ───
E(RP) = w₁Rf + (1−w₁)E(Ri)
σP = (1 − w₁)σi
Remember: Portfolio return = weighted avg (always). Portfolio std dev = weighted avg ONLY if ρ = +1.
Utility formula uses VARIANCE (σ²), not std dev (σ) — common mistake.
CAL is straight because risk-free asset has zero σ and zero Cov with everything.
Two-fund separation: everyone holds Rf + P; only proportion differs.
