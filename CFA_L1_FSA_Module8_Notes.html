What Is a Lease & How Is It Classified?

A lease is a contract that conveys the right to use a specific asset for a period of time in exchange for consideration. The lessee uses the asset and pays; the lessor owns the asset and receives payments. Leasing is effectively a form of financing — from the lessee's perspective, it resembles acquiring an asset with a note payable.

For a contract to be a lease (or contain a lease), it must: (1) identify a specific underlying asset, (2) give the customer the right to obtain substantially all economic benefits from the asset during the contract term, and (3) give the customer — not the supplier — the ability to direct how and for what purpose the asset is used.

Advantages of Leasing (vs. Purchasing)

Lower Upfront Cash

Leases typically require little to no down payment — preserves cash for other uses.

Lower Financing Cost

Lease = secured borrowing. If lessee defaults, lessor repossesses the asset → lower effective interest rate than unsecured debt.

Obsolescence Protection

Especially useful for technology and equipment — lessee doesn't bear the risk of the asset becoming outdated.

Finance Lease vs. Operating Lease — The 5 Criteria

A lease is classified as a finance lease if any one of the five criteria below is met. The criteria are identical under IFRS and US GAAP, and are used by both lessees and lessors.

# Finance Lease Criterion Economic Logic
1 Lease transfers ownership of the asset to the lessee Lessee ultimately owns the asset → effectively a purchase
2 Lessee has a purchase option and is reasonably certain to exercise it Likely end-ownership by lessee → resembles purchase
3 Lease term is for a major part of the asset's useful life Lessee consumes most of the asset's economic benefits
4 PV of lease payments equals or exceeds substantially all of the asset's fair value Lessee is effectively paying the full price of the asset
5 The underlying asset has no alternative use to the lessor Asset was custom-built for lessee → economic substance is a sale
💡 Mnemonic — "TOP-SA" for Finance Lease Criteria

Transfers ownership · Option to purchase (reasonably certain) · Period is major part of useful life · Substantially all FV covered by PV of payments · Asset has no alternative use. Any ONE → finance lease. None → operating lease.

Lessee exemptions (not available to lessors): A lessee may elect to simply expense lease payments on a straight-line basis if: (1) the lease term is 12 months or less (both IFRS and US GAAP), or (2) the asset has a low value — up to USD 5,000 sales price (IFRS only). These exemptions are optional — lessee can choose to elect or not.

Examiner Focus — Classification

The exam tests: (1) the 5 criteria — know all five, any one is sufficient; (2) criteria are the same for IFRS and US GAAP; (3) criteria are used by both lessees AND lessors; (4) the lessee exemptions (12-month rule and USD5,000 IFRS-only low-value rule); (5) a lease resembling a rental = operating; resembling a purchase = finance. Conceptual logic: who bears the risks and rewards of ownership? If lessee → finance lease.

02

Lessee Accounting — IFRS vs. US GAAP

Key structural difference: IFRS has a single accounting model for all lessees (all leases treated like finance leases on the balance sheet). US GAAP has two models — one for finance leases and one for operating leases — and they differ primarily in income statement and cash flow presentation.

IFRS Lessee — Single Model (All Leases)

IFRS Lessee — At Inception
  • Record a Right-of-Use (ROU) asset on the balance sheet = PV of future lease payments
  • Record a Lease Payable (liability) = PV of future lease payments (same starting value as ROU)
  • Discount rate: rate implicit in the lease, or lessee's estimated secured borrowing rate
  • Note: both ROU asset and lease liability start at the same value, but diverge afterwards
IFRS Lessee — Subsequent Periods
  • Lease liability: Reduced using effective interest method. Each payment = interest expense + principal repayment. Interest expense = lease liability × discount rate. Principal = payment − interest.
  • ROU asset: Amortized, typically on straight-line basis over the lease term.
  • Since liability reduces by principal (effective interest method) and ROU reduces by straight-line amortization, they diverge after inception.
  • Income statement: Interest expense (on liability) + amortization expense (on ROU) — shown SEPARATELY. Front-loaded total expense (higher early on as interest is higher when liability is larger).
  • Cash flow: Principal repayment → Financing Activities (CFF). Interest expense → Operating or Financing (lessee's policy choice under IFRS).

US GAAP Lessee — Two Models

US GAAP Finance Lease
  • Identical to the IFRS lessee model above
  • ROU asset + lease liability at inception (both = PV of payments)
  • Liability: effective interest method
  • ROU: amortized separately from interest
  • IS: Interest expense + amortization shown separately (front-loaded)
  • CFS: Principal → CFF. Interest → CFO (US GAAP default)
US GAAP Operating Lease
  • Also records ROU asset + lease liability at inception (same PV)
  • Liability: same effective interest method (principal + interest split)
  • ROU amortization: ROU amortization = Payment − Interest expense (not straight-line). This means ROU and liability always remain equal.
  • IS: Single "Lease Expense" line = the full lease payment. Interest + amortization NOT shown separately. Total expense is straight-line (even). Less front-loaded than finance lease.
  • CFS: Entire payment → CFO. Nothing in CFF. Interest and principal not separated.
The ROU Amortization Difference — Key Formula
IFRS / Finance Lease: ROU Amortization = Gross Cost ÷ Lease Term (straight-line)
US GAAP Operating: ROU Amortization = Lease Payment − Interest Expense

Result for US GAAP Operating:
Total IS Expense = Interest Expense + ROU Amortization = Lease Payment ✓ (always equal to payment)
Lease Liability = ROU Asset (they track each other perfectly)
Under the US GAAP operating model: the income statement shows a flat "lease expense" each period equal to the lease payment. This is simpler but conceals the interest component. Analysts often recast operating leases as finance leases to see the true interest burden.

Comprehensive IFRS vs. US GAAP Lessee Comparison

⚠️ Analyst Trap — CFO Comparison Between Lessees

A company using finance lease accounting (or IFRS) will show higher CFO than an equivalent company using US GAAP operating lease accounting, because the principal repayment flows through CFF instead of CFO. When comparing companies across standards or across lease types, analysts must adjust CFO accordingly to get a like-for-like comparison.

Examiner Focus — Lessee Accounting

The exam loves: (1) IFRS has ONE model — all leases on balance sheet. US GAAP has TWO — finance vs. operating models differ on IS and CFS only. (2) Both models put ROU asset and lease liability on the B/S. (3) Finance lease IS = front-loaded (amortization + interest separate). Operating lease IS = flat/straight-line. (4) Finance lease: principal in CFF. Operating lease: all in CFO. (5) ROU and lease liability only remain equal under the US GAAP operating model. (6) Short-term ≤12 months or low-value (IFRS) → straight-line expense, no B/S recognition.

03

Lessor Accounting — IFRS & US GAAP

Lessor accounting under IFRS and US GAAP is substantially identical. Both standards require lessors to classify leases as finance or operating, which drives the accounting treatment. (Under US GAAP, finance leases are called "sales-type" or "direct financing" — immaterial distinction for analysts.)

Lessor — Finance Lease
  • At inception: Derecognize the leased asset. Recognize a Lease Receivable = PV of future lease payments (at the rate implicit in the lease). Recognize any gain/loss on "sale" immediately.
  • Subsequent: Lease receivable reduced by each payment using effective interest method. Each receipt = interest income + principal proceeds.
  • IS: Interest income reported. If leasing is primary business → shown as revenue.
  • CFS: Entire cash receipt → CFO
  • Asset leaves the lessor's books. The lease receivable replaces it.
Lessor — Operating Lease
  • At inception: Nothing changes on the balance sheet. Lessor keeps the asset on its books (at cost less accumulated depreciation).
  • Subsequent: Continue depreciating the asset. Recognize lease revenue on a straight-line basis regardless of cash timing.
  • IS: Lease revenue + depreciation expense (separately reported).
  • CFS: Cash receipts → CFO (same as finance lease).
  • No interest income — transaction is a rental, not a financing.
Item Lessor — Finance Lease Lessor — Operating Lease
Balance Sheet Derecognize asset; add Lease Receivable Asset stays on books (cost − accum. dep.)
Income Statement Interest income + gain/loss at inception Lease revenue (straight-line) + dep. expense
Cash Flow (receipts) CFO CFO
Depreciation None (asset derecognized) Continues as normal
💡 Quick Memory — Lessor Finance Lease Logic

Think of a finance lease from the lessor's view as a sale on credit. The lessor "sells" the asset (removes it from B/S), receives a promise to pay (records receivable), and earns interest income over the lease term — just like a bank that finances a purchase.

Defined Contribution Pension Plans

In a defined contribution (DC) plan, the employer contributes a specified (defined) amount into the plan. The ultimate retirement benefit the employee receives depends entirely on how well the plan assets perform. The employer has no further obligation once the contribution is made.

Who Bears the Risk?
  • Employee bears investment risk (what if markets fall?)
  • Employee bears actuarial risk (what if they live longer than expected?)
  • Employer's only job: make the agreed contribution
Financial Reporting — Simple
  • Contribution = pension expense on the IS
  • Cash paid = CFO outflow
  • If some portion unpaid at year-end → accrued compensation liability (current)
  • No pension asset or liability on B/S beyond the accrual
💡 DC Plans — Accounting Is Dead Simple

The employer writes a check (or accrues it). That's the expense. There are no assumptions, no discount rates, no actuaries, no funded status calculations. The exam barely tests DC plans beyond this concept — the complexity is all in defined benefit plans.

Defined Benefit Pension Plans — Core Concepts

In a defined benefit (DB) plan, the employer promises a specific retirement benefit to employees — typically a formula tied to salary and years of service. The employer must fund enough plan assets to meet this future obligation. This creates enormous complexity because the obligation must be estimated using many uncertain assumptions.

The employer bears all risks — investment risk (if assets underperform) and actuarial risk (if employees live longer, retire earlier, etc.).

The Pension Obligation — Key Concepts

The pension obligation is the present value of all estimated future pension payments to current and past employees. The discount rate used is the yield on high-quality corporate bonds (under both IFRS and US GAAP). Actuarial assumptions required include:

  • Employee turnover rates and mortality rates
  • Expected retirement ages
  • Future salary levels (for final-salary plans)
  • Discount rate (corporate bond yield)

These assumptions are inherently subjective → management has significant discretion in setting them → creates risk of earnings management.

Funded Status — The Balance Sheet Number

Pension Funded Status
Funded Status = Fair Value of Plan Assets − Present Value of Pension Obligation

If FV of Assets > PV of Obligation → Surplus → Net Pension ASSET on B/S
If FV of Assets < PV of Obligation → Deficit → Net Pension LIABILITY on B/S (non-current)
Under both IFRS and US GAAP, the net pension asset or liability (funded status) is reported directly on the balance sheet. This is the single most important number from a DB plan for financial analysis — it represents the employer's net exposure.
⚠️ Management Judgment = Earnings Management Risk

The discount rate and other actuarial assumptions massively affect the reported pension obligation. A higher discount rate → smaller PV of obligation → smaller liability → lower expense. Companies facing large underfunded plans have incentives to use optimistic assumptions. Analysts should compare discount rates and other key assumptions across peers.

Three Components of Change in Net Pension Asset/Liability (IFRS)

IFRS — IAS 19 — Three Components
ComponentWhat It IsRecognized In
① Service Cost PV of benefit earned by employees in current year + past service costs from plan changes Profit & Loss (IS)
② Net Interest Expense/Income Net pension liability/asset × discount rate. Reflects time-value change in the net obligation Profit & Loss (IS)
③ Remeasurements Actuarial gains/losses + actual return on plan assets vs. expected. NOT amortized — stays in OCI permanently OCI (never recycled to P&L)
💡 IFRS Remeasurements — Permanent OCI

Under IFRS, actuarial gains/losses go to OCI and are never amortized back into profit or loss. This keeps earnings smoother but buries pension volatility in OCI. Under US GAAP, there's a mechanism to eventually bring these into P&L — the "corridor" or immediate recognition approach (see next section).

Defined Benefit — IFRS vs. US GAAP Differences

The major difference between IFRS and US GAAP for DB pension plans is how they handle the "volatile" elements — specifically past service costs and actuarial gains/losses. US GAAP has five components (vs. three for IFRS) and uses a "smoothing" mechanism.

US GAAP — Five Components (vs. IFRS Three)

US GAAP — Five Pension Components
ComponentDescriptionWhere Recognized
① Service Cost PV of benefit earned by employees in current year IS — current period
② Interest Expense PV of pension obligation × discount rate (on the gross obligation, not net) IS — current period
③ Expected Return on Plan Assets Reduces pension expense. Expected (not actual) return. Expected = plan assets × expected rate of return. IS — reduces expense (current period)
④ Past Service Costs Changes to plan benefits (retroactive). Recognized in OCI, then amortized into IS over future service period. OCI → amortized to IS over time
⑤ Actuarial Gains/Losses Deviation of assumptions from reality. Recognized in OCI; amortized into IS over time (or immediately if elected). OCI → amortized to IS over time
⚠️ Key Difference — Interest Treatment

IFRS uses net interest (net pension liability/asset × discount rate). US GAAP uses gross interest expense on the obligation minus expected return on plan assets as separate line items. Result: same math, different presentation. Also note: IFRS uses actual return as part of remeasurements; US GAAP uses expected return in IS — the difference between actual and expected flows to OCI.

IFRS vs. US GAAP — Side-by-Side Summary

Item IFRS (IAS 19) US GAAP (ASC 715)
Service cost IS — current period IS — current period
Interest on obligation Net interest (net obligation × discount rate) → IS Gross interest on obligation → IS separately
Return on plan assets Actual return: split between net interest (included) and remeasurement (OCI) Expected return reduces IS expense; difference → OCI
Past service costs IS (current period) — includes past service costs in service cost line OCI → amortized to IS over future service period
Actuarial gains/losses OCI — permanently. Never recycled to IS. OCI → amortized to IS over time (or immediate if elected)
B/S amount Funded status (net) directly on B/S Funded status (net) directly on B/S
Earnings smoothing Less smoothing (past service cost hits IS immediately) More smoothing (past service cost + actuarial gains/losses amortized)
Examiner Focus — Defined Benefit Plans

The big testable ideas: (1) DB plan: employer bears all risk. DC plan: employee bears all risk. (2) Funded status = FV of plan assets − PV of obligation. Net liability = underfunded; net asset = overfunded. (3) IFRS: 3 components. US GAAP: 5 components. (4) IFRS past service costs → IS immediately. US GAAP → OCI then amortized. (5) Actuarial gains/losses: IFRS → OCI (never to IS). US GAAP → OCI, then amortized to IS. (6) US GAAP uses EXPECTED return on assets in IS; IFRS uses actual return (difference stays in OCI). (7) Higher discount rate → lower PV of obligation → smaller net liability → lower pension expense. Analysts should scrutinize assumption changes.

Share-Based Compensation

Share-based compensation aligns employees' interests with shareholders by linking pay to the company's stock performance. Common forms: stock grants, restricted stock units (RSUs), stock options, stock appreciation rights (SARs). Under both IFRS and US GAAP: measure at fair value at the grant date, recognize as compensation expense ratably over the vesting period. Changes in stock price after grant date do NOT affect expense already measured.

Forms of Share-Based Compensation

Stock Grants (RSUs)
  • Outright grants, restricted stock, or performance shares
  • Fair value = market value at grant date
  • Expense allocated over service/vesting period
  • RSUs: most popular form today — valuable as long as stock price > 0
  • Performance shares: tied to accounting metrics (ROA, earnings) → incentive to manipulate
Stock Options
  • Employee right to buy stock at exercise price after vesting
  • FV estimated using option pricing model (e.g., Black-Scholes, Binomial)
  • Can expire worthless if stock price < exercise price
  • Creates potential for excessive risk-taking (options have asymmetric payoff)
  • Declining in use vs. RSUs over time
SARs & Phantom Stock
  • SARs: cash payment based on stock price increase. No shares issued — no dilution.
  • Phantom stock: based on hypothetical stock — used by private companies
  • Cash-settled → liability accrual (not equity)
  • Still measured at FV; expense over vesting period

Stock Options — Key Dates and Accounting Rules

Four critical dates for stock option accounting:

DateDefinitionAccounting Significance
Grant Date Day options are granted to employees Fair value is measured here. Expense begins here if immediate vesting, otherwise starts accruing.
Vesting Date Date employee can first exercise options End of service period over which expense is recognized. Immediate vesting → full expense on grant date.
Exercise Date Date employee converts options to stock No additional P&L impact. Company receives cash (exercise price), credits equity. No expense based on market price at exercise.
Expiration Date Options expire unexercised (typically 5–10 years after grant) No additional expense. Compensation expense already recognized over vesting period is NOT reversed.

Option Pricing — Key Inputs & Their Effects

Option Fair Value Inputs (Black-Scholes / Binomial)
Inputs that INCREASE option FV (→ higher compensation expense):
  ↑ Stock price volatility (more upside potential)
  ↑ Estimated life of option (more time to exercise)
  ↑ Risk-free interest rate (higher cost to wait)

Inputs that DECREASE option FV (→ lower compensation expense):
  ↑ Dividend yield (dividends reduce stock price appreciation potential)

Known at grant date: Exercise price
Highly subjective: Volatility, expected life, forfeiture rate → manipulation risk
Accounting standards do not prescribe a specific model — companies choose (Black-Scholes, binomial, etc.). The choice and assumptions must be disclosed. Analysts should scrutinize these disclosures and compare across peers.

Accounting Entries — Stock Options

Stock option accounting has no cash effect at grant. The journal entries flow through equity only:

EventDr (Debit)Cr (Credit)Net Equity Effect
Over vesting period Compensation Expense (IS) Additional Paid-in Capital (APIC) No net change (expense ↓ Retained Earnings; APIC ↑) — net equity unchanged
On exercise Cash (exercise price received) Common Stock (par) + APIC (FV at grant + cash received − par) Equity increases by FV of option + cash received
If options expire No entry needed — APIC remains. Expense recognized is NOT reversed.

Key point: During the vesting period, compensation expense reduces retained earnings while APIC increases by the same amount → no net impact on total equity. Diluted EPS is affected because the options are treated as potentially dilutive securities.

Advantages & Disadvantages of Share-Based Compensation

Advantages
  • Aligns employee interests with shareholders
  • No immediate cash outlay required
  • Effective retention tool (vesting schedule keeps employees)
  • SARs: no dilution, limited downside for employee
Disadvantages
  • Dilutes existing shareholders (more shares issued)
  • Employee may not control stock price → weak incentive link
  • Risk aversion: managers with large stock ownership may avoid risky-but-profitable projects
  • Options: opposite problem — excessive risk-taking (options = asymmetric payoff: upside unlimited, downside = zero)
  • Performance shares: may incentivize accounting manipulation
Examiner Focus — Share-Based Compensation

Key points: (1) Fair value measured at GRANT DATE — no change for subsequent stock price moves. (2) Expense recognized ratably over VESTING PERIOD. (3) Option pricing: higher volatility, longer life, higher risk-free rate → higher FV → higher expense. Higher dividend yield → lower FV. (4) Vesting period end ≠ exercise date. Expense is all recognized by vesting date. (5) On exercise: cash in, equity up, no additional IS impact. (6) Unexercised expired options: compensation expense already recorded is NEVER reversed. (7) Options = dilutive securities → reduce diluted EPS. (8) Net equity impact during vesting = zero (expense hits retained earnings; APIC increases by same amount). (9) SARs = cash-settled → liability, not equity. No dilution.

Presentation & Disclosure Requirements

Both IFRS and US GAAP require extensive disclosures for leases, pension plans, and share-based compensation. These appear primarily in the notes to financial statements. The overarching objective is to let financial statement users assess the amount, timing, and uncertainty of future cash flows related to these items.

Lease Disclosures

Lessee Must Disclose
  • Carrying amount of ROU assets by asset class at period end
  • Total cash outflows for leases
  • Interest expense on lease liabilities
  • Depreciation/amortization on ROU assets by class
  • Additions to ROU assets during period
  • Maturity analysis of lease liabilities (annual for each of next 5 years + thereafter)
  • Nature of leasing activities, future undiscounted exposures, restrictions/covenants, sale-leaseback transactions
Lessor Must Disclose
  • Finance lease: selling profit/loss; finance income on net investment; variable lease payment income
  • Operating lease: lease income (variable vs. index-based)
  • Maturity analysis of payments receivable (annual for next 5 years + thereafter)
  • Qualitative/quantitative info on risk management for retained asset rights
  • Disaggregated info by class of asset for operating leases

B/S presentation: ROU asset typically in non-current assets (or "Other non-current assets"). Lease liability in non-current liabilities. Some companies may bundle into "Other assets/liabilities" if immaterial. The maturity schedule breaking out undiscounted payments by year (with a reconciliation from undiscounted to carrying amount) is critically useful for analysts assessing future cash commitments.

Defined Benefit Pension Disclosures

DB pension disclosures are the most extensive of all. Key items required under IAS 19:

  • Nature of benefits, regulatory framework, governance structure, and risks to which the plan exposes the entity
  • Reconciliation of opening → closing net pension asset/liability (with separate reconciliations for plan assets and PV of obligation) — shows service costs, interest, remeasurements, contributions, benefits paid
  • Sensitivity analysis: How changes in key assumptions (especially discount rate) affect reported amounts — very useful for analysts testing management assumptions
  • Composition of plan assets by category (equity, fixed income, real estate, etc.)
  • Expected future cash flows — employer contributions expected next year
  • Funding status (PV of obligation vs. FV of plan assets)
💡 What Analysts Use Pension Disclosures For

The sensitivity analysis (e.g., "what if discount rate changes by 1%?") lets analysts stress-test the reported obligation. The composition of plan assets reveals investment risk. The reconciliation of plan asset/liability balances shows how much is from actual contributions vs. market movements vs. actuarial changes.

Share-Based Compensation Disclosures

Companies must disclose details that let users understand the nature and cost of share-based payment arrangements. Required under IFRS 2:

  • Description of each type of share-based arrangement (vesting requirements, maximum term, settlement method)
  • Number and weighted-average exercise price of options: outstanding at start, granted, forfeited, exercised, expired, outstanding at end, exercisable at end
  • For other equity instruments (e.g., RSUs): number and weighted-average FV at grant date, and how FV was measured
  • Valuation model used (Black-Scholes, binomial) and key assumptions: exercise price, volatility, expected life, forfeiture rate, dividend yield, risk-free rate
  • Total compensation expense recognized in the period and unrecognized expense remaining (with expected recognition timeline)
Examiner Focus — Disclosures

(1) Lease maturity schedule: undiscounted payments reconciled to carrying amount by subtracting imputed interest. Useful for understanding true future cash commitment. (2) Pension sensitivity analysis: key tool for analysts to evaluate management assumptions. Higher discount rate assumptions → smaller liability → smaller expense. (3) Share-based: always check the option pricing assumptions disclosed — volatility and expected life are most subjective and most impactful. (4) DC plans: minimal disclosure required (just the expense recognized). DB plans: extensive disclosures required. (5) Both B/S line items (ROU asset, lease liability, net pension asset/liability) AND notes disclosures are tested.

═══ PENSION — IFRS vs. US GAAP ═══
IFRS (3): Service cost → IS | Net interest → IS | Remeasurements → OCI (never recycled)
US GAAP(5): Service cost → IS | Gross interest on obligation → IS | Expected return → IS (reduces)
            Past service costs → OCI → amortized | Actuarial G/L → OCI → amortized

═══ SHARE-BASED COMPENSATION ═══
Fair value measured at GRANT DATE
Expense recognized ratably over VESTING PERIOD
Journal entry: Dr. Comp Expense | Cr. APIC → net equity impact = zero
On exercise: Dr. Cash | Cr. Common Stock + APIC → no IS impact
Expired options: expense already recognized NOT reversed
Option FV ↑ with: ↑volatility, ↑life, ↑risk-free rate
Option FV ↓ with: ↑dividend yield
SARs: cash-settled → liability, no dilution
Top exam traps for Module 8:
(1) IFRS lessee = one model for ALL leases (not just finance). US GAAP has two separate models.
(2) US GAAP operating lease: ROU asset DOES appear on B/S — it's only the IS and CFS that differ from finance leases.
(3) Finance lease → higher CFO vs. operating lease (principal in CFF vs. CFO). Must adjust for comparability.
(4) Lessor cash flows: both finance AND operating → all receipts in CFO (same treatment).
(5) IFRS remeasurements: go to OCI and STAY there. US GAAP past service costs and actuarial G/L: go to OCI but eventually amortized to IS.
(6) US GAAP: expected return on assets (not actual) reduces IS pension expense. Difference between actual and expected → OCI.
(7) Higher discount rate → smaller pension obligation → lower expense → watch for assumption-based earnings management.
(8) Option expense is measured at GRANT DATE. Exercise date market price is irrelevant to expense.
(9) Options expire worthless: compensation expense is NOT reversed — the cost of the service rendered was real.
(10) SARs = cash settled = liability = no dilution. Stock options and RSUs = equity settled = dilutive.
