01

Forward Contract Price vs. Value — The Critical Distinction

The single most important conceptual distinction in this module is the difference between the forward price and the forward value. These are related but fundamentally different concepts:

Forward Price — F₀(T)
  • The delivery price agreed at contract initiation (t=0)
  • Set using the no-arbitrage condition: F₀(T) = S₀(1+r)ᵀ (plus any carry adjustments)
  • Fixed for the entire life of the contract — it never changes once the contract is entered
  • Ensures the contract has zero value at initiation (no cash changes hands)
  • Determines the basis on which the underlying will be exchanged at maturity
  • Analogous to the "coupon rate" on a bond — set once, stays fixed
Forward Value — Vt(T)
  • The current mark-to-market value of the forward contract AT ANY TIME t
  • Zero at inception (t=0), then changes over the life of the contract
  • Changes as: (a) spot price S_t moves, (b) time passes (T−t decreases), (c) risk-free rate r changes
  • A positive value = gain for the long position; negative = loss for the long
  • MTM gain for buyer = MTM loss for seller (zero-sum)
  • Analogous to the market price of a bond — changes daily
★ The Core Distinction — Price vs. Value
FORWARD PRICE: F₀(T) — Set at t=0 using no-arbitrage. FIXED forever.
Formula: F₀(T) = S₀(1+r)ᵀ [or with cost of carry adjustments]

FORWARD VALUE: Vt(T) — Changes continuously over the contract's life.
At t=0: V₀(T) = 0 (zero by construction — no cash changes hands at initiation)
At t=T: VT(T) = S_T − F₀(T) [from long/buyer perspective]
At t (0<t<T): Vt(T) = S_t − F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾ [from long perspective]

KEY: The forward PRICE is the agreed delivery price (fixed).
The forward VALUE is the current worth of holding that agreement (variable).
For futures contracts: the daily MTM settles the forward value to zero each day (via margin accounts). For OTC forward contracts: the MTM value accumulates until settlement at maturity (or is collateralized via credit support agreements). This is the key difference between futures (daily settlement) and forwards (single settlement at maturity).
💡 Forward Price vs. Forward Value — Bond Analogy

Forward price is like a bond's coupon rate — fixed at issuance, never changes.
Forward value is like a bond's market price — changes every day based on market conditions (interest rates, credit risk).
A bond's coupon rate stays at 5% even when the bond trades at 95 in the market. Similarly, the forward price F₀(T) stays fixed even when the current value of the forward contract fluctuates between positive and negative as market conditions evolve.

02

Value at Initiation — V₀(T) = 0

At contract initiation (t = 0), the forward price is set at F₀(T) = S₀(1+r)ᵀ — the no-arbitrage price. By construction, this forward price makes the contract neither an asset nor a liability to either party. No cash changes hands at initiation.

Equation 1 — Forward Contract Value at Initiation
V₀(T) = 0
Why is it zero? The forward price F₀(T) is specifically chosen so that the expected gain of one party exactly equals the expected loss of the other party — in present value terms. There is no advantage to either side at initiation, so neither would pay anything to enter the contract.

AMY Investments example: S₀ = €29.70, r = 1%, T = 1 year → F₀(T) = €29.70 × (1.01)¹ = €30. This €30 forward price makes V₀(T) = 0 for both AMY (buyer) and the financial intermediary (seller).

Important: While V₀(T) = 0, the forward price F₀(T) is NOT zero — it is the future value of the spot price.
⚠️ Common Confusion — Zero Value ≠ Zero Price

V₀(T) = 0 means the value of the forward CONTRACT is zero at initiation — not the forward PRICE itself. The forward price F₀(T) = $1,792.13 in the gold example — that's definitely not zero. The contract is free to enter (no premium, no payment) but the agreed delivery price is above zero. Never confuse these two: the price (what you'll pay at maturity) and the value (what the contract is worth today).

03

Value at Maturity — VT(T)

At maturity (t = T), the forward contract is settled. The value equals the settlement amount — the difference between the spot price and the agreed forward price.

Equation 2 — Forward Contract Value at Maturity
Long (Buyer): VT(T) = S_T − F₀(T)
Short (Seller): VT(T) = F₀(T) − S_T
S_T = spot price at maturity | F₀(T) = agreed forward price (fixed at inception)

Long gains when S_T > F₀(T) — they buy at a below-market price
Short gains when S_T < F₀(T) — they sell at an above-market price
Zero-sum: Long gain = Short loss (and vice versa)
OutcomeLong (Buyer) VT(T)Short (Seller) VT(T)
S_T > F₀(T)S_T − F₀(T) > 0 (Gain)F₀(T) − S_T < 0 (Loss)
S_T < F₀(T)S_T − F₀(T) < 0 (Loss)F₀(T) − S_T > 0 (Gain)
S_T = F₀(T)ZeroZero

Worked Example — VFO Biomian Forward at Maturity

VFO sells Biomian forward at F₀(T) = INR300.84 per share (VFO = short position)
Case 1: S_T = INR287
Long (Financial Intermediary): VT(T) = 287 − 300.84 = −INR13.84 (loss)
Short (VFO): VT(T) = 300.84 − 287 = +INR13.84 (gain) ← VFO sells above market!

Case 2: S_T = INR312
Long (Financial Intermediary): VT(T) = 312 − 300.84 = +INR11.16 (gain)
Short (VFO): VT(T) = 300.84 − 312 = −INR11.16 (loss) ← VFO sells below market
04

MTM Value During the Life of the Contract — Equation 5

Between inception and maturity (0 < t < T), the forward contract's MTM value reflects the current spot price minus the present value of the forward price discounted for the remaining time to maturity. As time passes and S_t changes, the MTM value fluctuates continuously.

Equation 5 — Forward Contract MTM Value at Time t (No Additional Carry)
Long (Buyer): Vt(T) = S_t − F₀(T) × (1 + r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾

Short (Seller): Vt(T) = F₀(T) × (1 + r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾ − S_t
S_t = spot price of underlying at time t (current market price)
F₀(T) = forward price (FIXED at contract inception — does not change)
(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾ = discount factor for remaining time (T−t) until maturity
F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾ = present value of the forward price at time t

Intuition: The long position is "worth" the current spot price (S_t) minus the present value of what they must pay at maturity (PV of F₀(T)). The higher S_t relative to PV(F₀(T)), the more valuable the long position.

Note at t=0: PV of F₀(T) = F₀(T)(1+r)⁻ᵀ = S₀ (by no-arbitrage), so Vt(T) = S₀ − S₀ = 0 ✓
Note at t=T: (1+r)⁰ = 1, so Vt(T) = S_T − F₀(T) ✓ (reduces to Equation 2)
Condition at time tLong Position MTMShort Position MTM
S_t > F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾MTM GainMTM Loss
S_t < F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾MTM LossMTM Gain
S_t = F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾Zero MTMZero MTM

Worked Examples — VFO Biomian MTM

Setup: F₀(T) = INR300.84, r = 4%, T = 0.5 (6 months). VFO = short position.
Example 2: Instantaneous price jump at t=0 to S_t = INR325
[At t=0: (T−t) = T = 0.5, so (1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾ reduces to (1+r)⁻ᵀ and F₀(T)(1+r)⁻ᵀ = S₀]
VFO (Short): Vt(T) = S₀ − S_t = INR295 − INR325 = −INR30 MTM LOSS
(Price rose → short position loses)

Example 3a: 3 months later (t=0.25), S_t = INR285, r = 4%
VFO (Short): Vt(T) = F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾ − S_t
= 300.84 × (1.04)⁻⁰·²⁵ − 285
= 300.84 × 0.99020 − 285
= 297.89 − 285
= +INR12.90 MTM GAIN

Example 3b: Same scenario but r = 8%
VFO (Short): Vt(T) = 300.84 × (1.08)⁻⁰·²⁵ − 285
= 300.84 × 0.98148 − 285
= 295.27 − 285
= +INR10.11 MTM GAIN

Higher r reduces PV(F₀) → reduces short position's MTM gain by INR2.79
💡 Effect of Risk-Free Rate Change on Forward MTM

For the long position (buyer): Higher risk-free rate → lower PV of F₀(T) → smaller amount to pay in present value terms → MTM value INCREASES (buyer benefits from higher rates).
For the short position (seller): Higher risk-free rate → lower PV of F₀(T) → smaller amount to receive in present value terms → MTM value DECREASES (seller is hurt by higher rates).
Intuition: The short position is "delivering at F₀(T)" — the present value of F₀(T) represents the current worth of what they'll receive. When rates rise, that present value shrinks, reducing the short's gain (or increasing their loss).

05

MTM Value with Cost of Carry — Equation 7

When the underlying has additional costs or benefits of ownership (dividends, storage costs), the MTM formula must be adjusted to reflect the remaining cost of carry between time t and maturity T. The forward price already incorporates these at inception (Eq. 6), but as time passes, the remaining costs/benefits must be updated.

Equation 6 — Forward Price with Cost of Carry (Recap)
F₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(I) + PV₀(C)] × (1 + r)ᵀ
PV₀(I) = PV of income/benefits; PV₀(C) = PV of costs. Already established in Module 4.
Equation 7 — MTM Value with Remaining Cost of Carry (Long Position)
Vt(T) = [S_t − PVt(I) + PVt(C)] − F₀(T) × (1 + r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾
S_t = current spot price at time t
PVt(I) = PV at time t of remaining income/benefits from t to T (e.g., future dividends not yet paid)
PVt(C) = PV at time t of remaining costs from t to T (e.g., storage costs not yet incurred)
F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾ = PV of the fixed forward price for remaining time

Key insight: Once an income payment is received (dividend paid) or a cost is incurred, it drops out of PVt(I) or PVt(C). The remaining PV only includes future expected carry items from t to T.
Breakeven spot rate St*: Set Vt(T) = 0 and solve for St → the spot price at which the contract has zero MTM value. This is the "indifference price" for entering or exiting the position.

Worked Example — Hightest UL Equity Forward (4 months after inception)

Setup: F₀(T) = EUR50.6311/share, quarterly dividend EUR0.30, r=5%, T=0.5 years
At t=4 months (T−t = 2 months remaining):
First dividend (at t=3 months) already PAID → NOT in PVt(I) anymore
Second dividend (at t=6 months = maturity T) still to be paid → included in PVt(I)

PVt(I) = EUR0.30 × (1.05)⁻²/¹² = EUR0.30 × (1.05)⁻⁰·¹⁶⁷ = EUR0.2976

Find breakeven St where Vt(T) = 0:
0 = (St − 0.2976) − 50.6311 × (1.05)⁻⁰·¹⁶⁷
0 = (St − 0.2976) − 50.2202
St = EUR50.2202 + EUR0.2976
St = EUR50.5178 per share ← breakeven (contract has zero MTM at this spot price)
💡 How to Handle Dividends Already Paid vs. Still to Be Paid

When using Eq. 7 mid-contract: Only include dividends that have NOT yet been paid in PVt(I). Dividends already paid between t=0 and t (the current date) are history — they have already been received by the asset holder and are no longer relevant to the remaining contract value. The forward price F₀(T) was set at inception incorporating ALL dividends over the full life — but for the MTM at time t, only remaining dividends matter.

FX Forward MTM During the Life — Equation 9

Equation 8 — FX Forward Price (Recap from Module 4)
F₀,f/d(T) = S₀,f/d × e^((rf − rd) × T)
rf = foreign currency rate | rd = domestic currency rate
Equation 9 — FX Forward MTM Value at Time t (Long Position)
Vt(T) = S_t,f/d − F₀,f/d(T) × e^(−(rf − rd) × (T − t))
S_t,f/d = current spot FX rate at time t (units of foreign per unit of domestic)
F₀,f/d(T) = fixed FX forward price agreed at inception
e^(−(rf−rd)(T−t)) = discount factor for remaining period using the interest rate differential

Long FX forward (buyer of domestic, seller of foreign): Benefits when the domestic currency strengthens (fewer foreign units required per domestic unit → lower exchange rate f/d)

Interest rate differential change effect:
Increase in (rf−rd) → discounts F₀ more heavily → lower PV of forward price → MTM increases for long position (if S_t,f/d > F₀e^(−(rf−rd)(T−t)))

Worked Example — Rook Point FX Forward MTM

Rook Point: Long USD/EUR forward. EUR = base/domestic. USD = price/foreign.
At inception (t=0):
S₀,USD/EUR = 1.192 | F₀,USD/EUR = 1.201 | r_USD (rf) = 0.50% | r_EUR (rd) = −0.25%
V₀(T) = 1.192 − 1.201 × e^(−(0.005+0.0025)) = 1.192 − 1.201 × e^(−0.0075) = 0 ✓

After instantaneous USD rate rise to 0.75% (rf now = 0.75%):
New rf − rd = 0.75% − (−0.25%) = 1.00%
Vt(T) = 1.192 − 1.201 × e^(−0.01 × 1)
= 1.192 − 1.201 × 0.99005
= 1.192 − 1.18905
= 0.00295 USD/EUR MTM GAIN

In dollar terms: USD1,192,000 − USD1,201,000 × e^(−0.01)
= USD1,192,000 − USD1,189,049.85
≈ USD2,950 MTM gain
💡 Why Does USD Rate Rise Give Rook Point a Gain?

Rook Point has agreed to buy EUR for USD at F₀ (long EUR/short USD). When the USD interest rate rises, USD becomes more expensive to borrow — making USD relatively "stronger" in terms of interest rate advantage. But also: higher rf raises (rf−rd), which increases the discount on the forward price (lowers PV of F₀). Since S_t > PV(F₀), Rook Point has an MTM gain. Intuitively: the discount on the forward price means Rook Point is effectively "getting a better deal" in present value terms as the discount increases.

Spot Rates & Bootstrapping from Coupon Bonds

Interest rates have a term structure — different rates exist for different maturities. Unlike equities and commodities, you can't price an interest rate forward using a single constant risk-free rate. Instead, we need zero (spot) rates for each maturity. The process of deriving zero rates from coupon bond prices is called bootstrapping.

Bootstrapping
The process of deriving zero (spot) rates from coupon-bearing bond prices using sequential (forward) substitution. Starting with the shortest maturity bond (which may already be a single cash flow), each subsequent zero rate is extracted by plugging previously calculated zero rates into the longer-maturity bond's pricing equation and solving for the new unknown zero rate.

Full Bootstrapping Example

Three government bonds: 1-year (1.5% coupon, price 99.125), 2-year (2.5%, 98.275), 3-year (3.25%, 98.000)
Step 1 — One-year zero rate (z₁):
99.125 = 101.5/(1+z₁)¹ → z₁ = 2.3960%
[1-year bond has ONE cash flow → YTM = zero rate exactly]

Step 2 — Two-year zero rate (z₂): [use z₁ already found]
98.275 = 2.5/(1.02396)¹ + 102.5/(1+z₂)²
98.275 = 2.4414 + 102.5/(1+z₂)²
(1+z₂)² = 102.5/95.8336 = 1.06967
z₂ = (1.06967)^0.5 − 1 = 3.4197%

Step 3 — Three-year zero rate (z₃): [use z₁ and z₂]
98.00 = 3.25/(1.02396)¹ + 3.25/(1.034197)² + 103.25/(1+z₃)³
98.00 = 3.1732 + 3.0384 + 103.25/(1+z₃)³
(1+z₃)³ = 103.25/91.7884 = 1.12478
z₃ = (1.12478)^(1/3) − 1 = 4.0005%
MaturityCouponPriceYTMZero Rate (Bootstrapped)
1 year1.50%99.1252.3960%z₁ = 2.3960% (same as YTM)
2 years2.50%98.2753.4068%z₂ = 3.4197% (higher than YTM)
3 years3.25%98.0003.9703%z₃ = 4.0005% (higher than YTM)
💡 Zero Rate vs. YTM — Why They Differ

For a bond with a single cash flow (a zero-coupon bond OR a 1-year bond with one final payment), the YTM equals the zero rate — there's only one cash flow, so there's only one relevant rate. For multi-year coupon bonds, the YTM is a "weighted average" of the zero rates for each year — it blends z₁, z₂, z₃, etc. into a single number. Since the spot curve is typically upward-sloping, the long-term zero rate z₂ > YTM₂ and z₃ > YTM₃ (because the earlier cash flows are discounted at lower rates than the final cash flow, making the "blended" YTM lower than the final-year zero rate).

Discount Factors — The Price Equivalent of Zero Rates

Discount Factor (DF_i)
The present value of one unit of currency received at period i, calculated using the zero rate for that period. The discount factor is the "price equivalent" of a zero rate — it directly gives the current value of a future cash flow without requiring compounding calculations. Also interpretable as the price of a zero-coupon bond paying 1 unit of currency at maturity.
Discount Factor Formula
DF_i = 1 / (1 + z_i)^i
From the bootstrapping example:
DF₁ = 1/(1 + z₁)¹ = 1/(1.023960) = 0.976601
DF₂ = 1/(1 + z₂)² = 1/(1.034197)² = 0.934961
DF₃ = 1/(1 + z₃)³ = 1/(1.040005)³ = 0.888982

Interpretation: DF₂ = 0.934961 means that €1 to be received in 2 years is worth €0.9350 today.
Equivalently: a zero-coupon bond with face value of €1 maturing in 2 years is priced at €0.9350.

No-arbitrage check (GBP example):
Zero-coupon bond: FV=£100, z₂=3.42%, observed price=£92.45
No-arbitrage price = £100/(1.0342)² = £93.4955
Price too low (£92.45 < £93.4955) → Arbitrage: Borrow at 3.42%, buy bond, hold to maturity
At T: Receive £100, repay £98.88 (= £92.45 × (1.0342)²) → Riskless profit of £1.12
💡 Discount Factors Are More Elegant Than Zero Rates

When pricing complex instruments (swaps, bonds, structured products), discount factors are often easier to work with than zero rates — you simply multiply each future cash flow by its discount factor and sum. Swaps, FRAs, and other interest rate derivatives are commonly priced as: PV = Σ (Cash Flow_i × DF_i). The discount factor already incorporates compounding for the relevant period, so no additional calculations are needed.

Implied Forward Rates (IFR) — Interest Rate Forwards

An implied forward rate (IFR) is the breakeven reinvestment rate that links a shorter-term and longer-term zero-coupon bond. It is the interest rate for a future period implied by today's spot (zero) curve. The IFR is the no-arbitrage rate for a one-period interest rate forward contract.

Forward Rate Notation — AyBy (or F_A,B)
IFR_A,B-A = Forward rate on a bond starting at period A and ending at period B
Tenor = B − A periods

Examples:
IFR₁,₁ (or 1y1y): 1-year rate starting 1 year from now (years 1→2)
IFR₂,₁ (or 2y1y): 1-year rate starting 2 years from now (years 2→3)
F₂,₃ (or 2y3y): 3-year rate starting 2 years from now (years 2→5)
F₃ₘ,₆ₘ: 6-month MRR starting in 3 months (common for MRR derivatives)
The first number = start date (forward period), second = tenor of the rate.
Equation 11 — General Implied Forward Rate Formula
(1 + z_A)^A × (1 + IFR_A,B-A)^(B-A) = (1 + z_B)^B
z_A = zero rate for shorter period (A periods) | z_B = zero rate for longer period (B periods)
IFR_A,B-A = implied forward rate for the period from A to B (tenor = B−A periods)

Breakeven condition: Invest for A periods at z_A, then reinvest at IFR_A,B-A for (B−A) more periods
→ must give same total return as investing for B periods at z_B

Solving for IFR:
IFR_A,B-A = [(1+z_B)^B / (1+z_A)^A]^(1/(B-A)) − 1

Worked Examples

From bootstrapped rates: z₁=2.396%, z₂=3.4197%, z₃=4.0005%
IFR₁,₁ (1y1y): 1-year rate starting in 1 year
(1.02396) × (1 + IFR₁,₁) = (1.034197)²
(1.02396) × (1 + IFR₁,₁) = 1.069564
1 + IFR₁,₁ = 1.044536
IFR₁,₁ = 4.4536%

Verification: USD100 × (1.02396) × (1.044536) = USD106.96
USD100 × (1.034197)² = USD100 × 1.06956 = USD106.96 ✓

IFR₂,₁ (2y1y): 1-year rate starting in 2 years
(1.034197)² × (1 + IFR₂,₁) = (1.040005)³
1.069564 × (1 + IFR₂,₁) = 1.124783
IFR₂,₁ = 5.1719%

MRR Forward Rate Example — Periodicity Conversion Required

3-month MRR = 1.25% | 6-month MRR = 1.75% | Find: 3-month IFR in 3 months (IFR₃ₘ,₃ₘ)
STEP 1: Convert 6-month MRR to quarterly rate (must match periodicity with 3-month rate)
(1 + APR₄/4)⁴ = (1 + 0.0175/2)²
APR₄ = 1.74619%

STEP 2: Apply Equation 11 (using quarterly periodicity)
(1 + 0.0125/4) × (1 + IFR₃ₘ,₃ₘ/4) = (1 + 0.0174619/4)²
1.003125 × (1 + IFR₃ₘ,₃ₘ/4) = (1.00436548)²
IFR₃ₘ,₃ₘ = 2.24299%

Verification:
①: CNY100,000,000 × (1+0.0125/4) × (1+0.0224299/4) = CNY100,875,000
②: CNY100,000,000 × (1+0.0175/2) = CNY100,875,000 ✓
⚠️ Always Match Periodicities Before Applying Equation 11

When rates have different periods (e.g., 3-month and 6-month MRRs), you CANNOT directly apply Equation 11. First convert all rates to the same periodicity using the APR conversion formula from Module 7 of Fixed Income: (1 + APRm/m)^m = (1 + APRn/n)^n. Only after converting to a common periodicity can you solve for the IFR correctly.

Forward Rate Agreements (FRAs)

Forward Rate Agreement (FRA)
An OTC derivative in which counterparties agree to apply a specific fixed interest rate to a hypothetical notional deposit for a specific future period. The underlying is a future MRR (market reference rate). The FRA buyer (long) pays the fixed rate and receives the MRR; the FRA seller (short) receives the fixed and pays the MRR. Settlement is cash-based — notional is NOT exchanged.

Key features of FRAs:

  • FRA is a one-period version of an interest rate swap — a swap is essentially a series of FRAs with different start dates
  • The implied forward rate (IFR) IS the no-arbitrage fixed rate for the FRA — ensures V₀(T) = 0 at inception
  • Net payment = difference between the MRR and the agreed fixed rate × notional × period fraction
  • Settlement timing: FRAs settle at the BEGINNING of the interest period (not the end), so the settlement amount must be discounted back by the MRR for the period
  • Used almost exclusively by financial intermediaries to manage rate-sensitive balance sheet positions
FRA Settlement Formulas (Yangzi Bank CNY FRA — Buyer = Fixed-Rate Payer)
Net Payment (at END of interest period):
Net Payment = (MRR_B-A − IFR_A,B-A) × Notional × Period

Cash Settlement (discounted to BEGINNING of interest period):
Cash Settlement (PV) = Net Payment / (1 + MRR_B-A × Period/Year)

Worked Example: IFR₃ₘ,₃ₘ = 2.24299%, Notional = CNY100,000,000
MRR₃ₘ sets at 2.15% (below the fixed rate of 2.24299%)

Net Payment (end of period):
= (0.0215 − 0.0224299) × 100,000,000 × 90/360
= −0.000930 × 100,000,000 × 0.25
= −CNY23,247.50 ← Yangzi Bank pays (MRR came in below fixed rate)

Cash Settlement (PV at beginning of period):
= −23,247.50 / (1 + 0.0215/4)
= −23,247.50 / 1.005375
= −CNY23,123.21 ← Yangzi Bank pays at beginning of period
Negative result for Yangzi Bank (buyer/fixed-rate payer): MRR (2.15%) settled below the agreed fixed rate (2.24299%). Yangzi Bank pays more on the fixed leg than it receives on the floating leg → net payment out. BUT: since Yangzi Bank used the FRA to hedge a liability on which it PAYS MRR, the lower MRR also means it pays LESS on the underlying liability. The two offset each other.

Discount at MRR (not the fixed rate): When discounting the settlement amount back to the start of the period, use the actual MRR (not the FRA fixed rate) as the discount rate.
FRA Buyer (Long / Fixed-Rate Payer)
  • Pays the fixed IFR rate
  • Receives the actual MRR at settlement date
  • Benefits when actual MRR > fixed rate (floating exceeds fixed)
  • Used to hedge against RISING interest rates
  • Example: Yangzi Bank hedges a floating-rate liability
FRA Seller (Short / Fixed-Rate Receiver)
  • Receives the fixed IFR rate
  • Pays the actual MRR at settlement date
  • Benefits when actual MRR < fixed rate (floating below fixed)
  • Used to hedge against FALLING interest rates (or lock in investment rate)
  • Example: Financial intermediary taking the other side of Yangzi's FRA
💡 FRA Settlement Timing — Beginning vs. End of Period

MRR sets at the beginning of the forward period (e.g., at 3 months for a 3×6 FRA). Interest would normally accrue and be paid at the end of the interest period (e.g., at 6 months). FRAs settle at the BEGINNING when the MRR is known — the settlement amount is therefore the PV of the end-of-period net payment, discounted at the MRR for the period. This "early settlement" feature distinguishes FRA settlement from a regular forward contract that always settles at maturity.

Exam Checklist & Quick Reference Summary

  • ✅ Forward PRICE F₀(T) = set at initiation, FIXED for the life of the contract. Set using no-arbitrage: F₀(T) = S₀(1+r)ᵀ (plus carry). Analogous to a bond's coupon rate.
  • ✅ Forward VALUE Vt(T) = mark-to-market value at any time t. Changes as spot price, time, and interest rates change. Analogous to a bond's market price.
  • ✅ At initiation (t=0): V₀(T) = 0. The forward contract is neither an asset nor a liability at inception. No cash changes hands (except for futures margin).
  • ✅ At maturity (t=T): VT(T) = S_T − F₀(T) for long; F₀(T) − S_T for short. Value equals the settlement amount. Zero-sum between buyer and seller.
  • ✅ During the life (Eq. 5, no carry): Vt(T) = S_t − F₀(T) × (1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾ for long. The second term is the present value of the forward price for the remaining period. Short position: negate this formula.
  • ✅ Long position gains MTM when S_t > PV_t(F₀). Short gains when S_t < PV_t(F₀). PV_t(F₀) = F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾.
  • ✅ Rising risk-free rate → lower PV(F₀) → MTM increases for long position; MTM decreases for short position.
  • ✅ With cost of carry (Eq. 7): Vt(T) = [S_t − PVt(I) + PVt(C)] − F₀(T)(1+r)⁻⁽ᵀ⁻ᵗ⁾. Only include REMAINING costs/benefits from t to T (not those already paid/incurred).
  • ✅ FX forward MTM (Eq. 9): Vt(T) = S_t,f/d − F₀,f/d(T) × e^(−(rf−rd)(T−t)). The discount factor uses the interest rate differential (rf−rd), not a single rate.
  • ✅ Interest rates have a term structure (different rates for different maturities). Cannot use a single constant risk-free rate for interest rate derivatives — need zero (spot) rates for each maturity.
  • ✅ Bootstrapping: Process of deriving zero rates from coupon bond prices via forward substitution. Start with shortest maturity, solve for its zero rate, then substitute into the next bond's equation to solve for the next zero rate, and so on sequentially.
  • ✅ 1-year bond zero rate = YTM (one cash flow, so there's only one relevant rate). For multi-year coupon bonds: zero rate ≠ YTM (YTM is a weighted average of spot rates).
  • ✅ Discount factor DF_i = 1/(1+z_i)^i. Represents the PV of 1 unit of currency to be received at period i. Also: the price of a zero-coupon bond paying 1 unit at maturity. Used to price FRAs and swaps directly.
  • ✅ Implied Forward Rate (IFR) formula (Eq. 11): (1+z_A)^A × (1+IFR_A,B-A)^(B-A) = (1+z_B)^B. The IFR is the breakeven reinvestment rate — investing A periods at z_A then rolling over at IFR equals investing B periods at z_B directly.
  • ✅ Forward rate notation: IFR_A,B-A or "AyBy". A = start year/period, B-A = tenor. Example: IFR₁,₁ = 1y1y = 1-year rate starting in 1 year. F₃ₘ,₃ₘ = 3-month rate starting in 3 months.
  • ✅ When solving IFR for MRR forward rates: Must first convert all rates to the SAME periodicity using the APR conversion formula before applying Equation 11. Failure to match periodicities gives incorrect answers.
  • ✅ FRA (Forward Rate Agreement): OTC derivative where counterparties agree on a fixed rate for a future period's MRR. FRA buyer = pays fixed, receives MRR (hedges against rising rates). FRA seller = receives fixed, pays MRR.
  • ✅ FRA fixed rate = IFR (no-arbitrage rate) → V₀(T) = 0 at inception. Like all forward contracts.
  • ✅ FRA net payment (end of period) = (MRR_actual − FRA fixed rate) × Notional × Period. Positive = FRA buyer receives; Negative = FRA buyer pays.
  • ✅ FRA settles at the BEGINNING of the interest period (when MRR is set), not the end. Must discount the end-of-period net payment back to the start of the period using MRR as the discount rate: PV settlement = Net Payment / (1 + MRR × Period/Year).
  • ✅ FRA vs. swap: FRA is a one-period swap (single exchange). A swap is a series of FRAs with different start dates. FRAs build up to form interest rate swap building blocks.
