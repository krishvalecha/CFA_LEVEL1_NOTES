01

Balance Sheet — Overview & Measurement Bases

The balance sheet (statement of financial position) shows what an entity owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the owners' interest in the net assets (equity) — all at a specific point in time. Unlike the income statement (a flow statement), the balance sheet is a snapshot.

The fundamental accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Analysts use the balance sheet to assess liquidity (short-term ability to pay), solvency (long-term financial stability), and the overall financial position of the entity.

Measurement Bases — Not Everything Is at Cost

Historical / Amortized Cost
  • Most common measurement basis
  • PP&E, inventory (under certain methods), most debt instruments held to maturity
  • Long-term debt (bonds payable) — carried at amortized cost
  • Provides reliability, but may diverge from economic reality over time
  • Amortized cost = Initial cost − Principal repaid ± Amortization of discount/premium − Impairment
Fair Value
  • The price to sell an asset (or transfer a liability) in an orderly market transaction
  • Used for certain financial assets, trading securities, derivatives
  • Changes flow to income statement (trading) or OCI (available-for-sale/FVOCI)
  • More relevant economically but introduces volatility
  • IFRS permits fair value model for intangibles if an active market exists
💡 Key Analyst Takeaway — Measurement Heterogeneity

Balance sheets mix measurement bases — some items at cost, some at fair value. This limits comparability across companies and requires analysts to read footnotes carefully. Two companies with identical economic positions can show dramatically different balance sheet values depending on their accounting policies and available elections. Always check the notes for the measurement basis of each major category.

02

Intangible Assets

Intangible assets are identifiable non-monetary assets without physical substance. "Identifiable" means the asset can either be separated and sold on its own OR arises from contractual/legal rights. Common examples: patents, licenses, trademarks, copyrights, franchises, customer lists.

The most important intangible that is not separately identifiable is goodwill, which arises in business combinations and is covered separately in the next section.

Measurement Models — IFRS vs. US GAAP

IFRS
  • Cost model: Carry at cost less accumulated amortization and impairment — the default
  • Revaluation model: Allowed ONLY if an active market exists for that intangible (rare in practice)
  • Research costs: always expensed
  • Development costs: capitalized if specific criteria are met (technological feasibility, intent & ability to complete, future economic benefits probable)
US GAAP
  • Cost model only — revaluation model not permitted
  • Both research and development costs generally expensed as incurred
  • Exception: software development costs capitalized after technological feasibility is established (but companies like Microsoft set feasibility very late → effectively expense everything)
  • No internal development capitalization for brands, customer lists, etc.

Useful Life Assessment — Finite vs. Indefinite

CategoryAmortizationImpairment TestingExamples
Finite Useful LifeAmortized systematically over best estimate of useful life. Method and life reviewed at least annually.Subject to impairment testing (same as PP&E)Patents (20-yr legal life), licenses with fixed terms, customer lists, trademarks with renewal limits
Indefinite Useful LifeNOT amortizedTested for impairment at least annually. Reasonableness of indefinite classification also reviewed annually.Broadcast licenses (renewable indefinitely), some trademarks, franchise rights with perpetual renewal
⚠️ Analyst Trap — "Indefinite" ≠ "Infinite"

An indefinite life means the entity cannot foresee a limit to the period over which the asset will generate net cash inflows. It does NOT mean the asset has infinite value. The annual review of whether indefinite classification remains appropriate is critical — a change to finite life triggers amortization from that point forward, hitting future earnings. Watch for changes in useful life estimates.

Internally Generated vs. Purchased Intangibles

IFRS: Research Phase vs. Development Phase — The Critical Split
  • Research phase: Activities seeking new knowledge (basic research, searching for alternatives). All costs → immediately expensed. No exceptions.
  • Development phase: Occurs after research phase. Design, testing of prototypes and models. Costs can be capitalized as intangible assets IF all of the following criteria are met simultaneously:
    • Technical feasibility of completing the asset
    • Intention to complete and use or sell the asset
    • Ability to use or sell the asset
    • Probable future economic benefits will flow to the entity
    • Adequate technical, financial, and other resources to complete development
    • Ability to reliably measure the expenditure attributable to the asset
💡 Memory Trick — Research vs. Development

"Research = Risk" — always expensed because outcomes are too uncertain. "Development = Dependable enough to capitalize" — once feasibility is established, future benefits are probable enough to put on the balance sheet. Think of it as: Research asks "can we do it?"; Development answers "yes, and here's how we're doing it."

Always-Expensed Items (Both IFRS and US GAAP)

The following types of internal costs are always expensed and can never be recognized as intangible assets, regardless of how valuable they may be economically:

  • Internally generated brands, mastheads, publishing titles, customer lists
  • Start-up costs (pre-operating costs)
  • Training costs
  • Administrative and general overhead
  • Advertising and promotion costs
  • Relocation and reorganization expenses
  • Redundancy and other termination costs
  • Research costs (always, under both standards)
Examiner Focus — Intangible Assets

The exam will test whether you can distinguish: (1) IFRS vs. US GAAP treatment of development costs; (2) finite vs. indefinite useful life and the resulting accounting difference (amortize vs. impairment-test only); (3) which items are always expensed. A common trap: internally generated brands and customer lists are never capitalized under either standard — but if a company is acquired and the acquirer can separately identify those same brand/customer list values, they CAN be recognized (as part of the purchase price allocation). This asymmetry is important: the same economic asset is on the balance sheet of an acquirer but not of the original developer.

Analyst Considerations for Intangibles

Analysts traditionally view reported intangible values — especially goodwill — with caution. Common adjustments include:

  • Excluding intangibles to compute "tangible book value" (remove intangibles from assets and reduce equity by the same amount)
  • Adding back amortization expense and impairment losses to compute adjusted pretax income
  • However: arbitrarily assigning zero value to all intangibles is not advisable — each intangible should be assessed individually. A patent with remaining exclusivity period and proven cash flows has real value.

Companies may also have valuable assets that are never recorded on a balance sheet because they are non-identifiable and the company lacks sufficient control over their future economic benefits — e.g., management skills, market share, reputation, brand recognition. These are reflected in the company's market price (market cap exceeds book value), and may be recognized as goodwill by an acquirer.

03

Goodwill

Goodwill arises when one company acquires another and pays more than the fair value of the acquiree's identifiable net assets (assets − liabilities). It is the most common intangible that is NOT separately identifiable — it cannot be sold on its own; it only exists in the context of the business as a whole.

Why Would an Acquirer Overpay?

Unrecognized Assets

Acquiree has valuable assets not on its own balance sheet (e.g., reputation, distribution network, trained workforce, customer relationships). The acquirer is paying for these.

R&D Value

The acquiree's R&D expenditures may not have met criteria for capitalization, but the research may have created real (though unrecognized) value — e.g., a pipeline drug near approval.

Synergies

The acquirer expects value from improved strategic positioning vs. competitors or operational synergies (e.g., cost savings, cross-selling) that don't exist in the target's standalone balance sheet.

Goodwill Recognition — The 3-Step Process

STEP 1
Determine the Purchase Price
The total consideration paid by the acquirer to purchase the target company. This includes cash paid, shares issued (at fair value), deferred payments, contingent consideration, etc.
STEP 2
Measure Net Identifiable Assets at Fair Value
All of the acquiree's identifiable assets (tangible AND intangible) are measured at fair value as of the acquisition date. The acquiree's liabilities and contingent liabilities are also measured at fair value. Net identifiable assets = Fair value of identifiable assets − Fair value of liabilities.
STEP 3
Goodwill = Purchase Price − Net Identifiable Assets
The excess of the purchase price over the fair value of net identifiable assets is recognized as goodwill on the balance sheet. If the purchase price is LESS than the fair value of net identifiable assets, that is a "bargain purchase" — the gain is recognized immediately in profit or loss.
Goodwill Formula
Goodwill = Purchase Price − Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets Acquired
Net Identifiable Assets = FV of Identifiable Assets − FV of Liabilities & Contingent Liabilities

Bargain Purchase: Purchase Price < FV Net Identifiable Assets → Gain on IS (not goodwill)
Note: Goodwill is measured using fair values at the acquisition date, not the book values from the acquiree's financial statements. This step-up to fair value is critical — it means all assets and liabilities are remeasured, and previously unrecognized identifiable intangibles (like customer lists and trademarks) are now recognized for the first time on the acquirer's consolidated balance sheet.

Subsequent Accounting — No Amortization, Annual Impairment Test

Goodwill Post-Acquisition Treatment (Both IFRS & US GAAP)
  • NOT amortized — unlike PP&E or finite-life intangibles
  • Tested for impairment at least annually (and whenever indicators of impairment exist)
  • If goodwill is impaired: impairment loss is charged against income → reduces earnings
  • Impairment loss is non-cash — doesn't affect cash flows, but does reduce total assets and equity
  • After an impairment loss: total assets are lower → return on assets (ROA = Net Income ÷ Average Total Assets) may actually increase in subsequent periods (lower denominator)
  • Impairment losses cannot be reversed under US GAAP (IFRS also does not allow reversal for goodwill)
⚠️ Goodwill Impairment — The Earnings Quality Signal

A large goodwill impairment charge signals that management overpaid for an acquisition — the expected synergies or cash flows never materialized. It is a non-cash charge that reduces net income but has no cash flow impact. Analysts typically exclude goodwill impairment from operating trend analysis but use it to assess management's acquisition performance. A company that repeatedly impairs goodwill has a track record of poor capital allocation.

Accounting Goodwill vs. Economic Goodwill

Accounting Goodwill

Based on accounting standards. Only recognized in acquisitions (purchase price > FV of net identifiable assets). Historical artifact — reflects what was paid, not current value. Carries forward at cost less impairment.

Economic Goodwill

Based on the ongoing economic performance of the entity — the present value of excess returns expected. Reflected in the company's stock price (market cap > book equity = "market-to-book premium"). Not necessarily visible on the balance sheet at all.

Analyst Adjustments for Goodwill

Because goodwill recognition and impairment can significantly distort comparability between companies (one grew organically, another by acquisition), analysts often make these adjustments:

  • Exclude goodwill from balance sheet ratios — use tangible assets (total assets − goodwill) in ratios like total assets turnover and leverage ratios. This makes comparisons between acquirers and organic growers more meaningful.
  • Exclude goodwill impairment from income statement analysis — strip out impairment charges to assess underlying operating trends. However, don't ignore impairment entirely: it reveals acquisition quality.
  • Assess acquisition price relative to earnings — compare P/E of acquisition price to the target's earnings power. High goodwill from a high-price acquisition is a financial risk if earnings disappoint.
Examiner Focus — Goodwill

The exam will test: (1) the three-step goodwill calculation; (2) goodwill = not amortized, tested for impairment annually; (3) impairment is non-cash and irreversible; (4) a bargain purchase creates a gain on the income statement, not negative goodwill on the balance sheet; (5) the accounting vs. economic goodwill distinction; (6) why goodwill impairment may increase future ROA (smaller asset base). Also: management judgment in valuing identifiable intangible assets affects how much gets allocated to goodwill vs. separately identifiable intangibles — and separately identified intangibles with finite lives are amortized, so this allocation matters for future earnings.

04

Financial Instruments

A financial instrument is a contract that gives rise to a financial asset of one entity and a financial liability or equity instrument of another entity. Examples: bonds (asset to holder, liability to issuer), loans, equity investments, derivatives. Derivatives are financial instruments whose value is derived from an underlying factor (interest rates, exchange rates, commodity prices, security prices, credit ratings) — and require little or no initial investment.

The key question: how are financial instruments measured after initial recognition? The answer depends on their classification, which drives significantly different income statement and balance sheet impacts.

Three Measurement Categories — The Master Framework

CategoryMeasurement BasisWhere Unrealized Gains/Losses GoIFRS NameUS GAAP Equivalent
Category 1 Amortized Cost No unrealized G/L recognized — only interest income on IS Amortized Cost Held-to-Maturity (HTM) — debt only
Category 2 Fair Value Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) — bypasses income statement FVOCI (Fair Value through OCI) Available-for-Sale (AFS) — debt only under US GAAP; equity permitted under IFRS
Category 3 Fair Value Profit or Loss (income statement) — hits earnings directly FVTPL (Fair Value through Profit or Loss) Trading Securities (debt & equity)

When Is Each Category Used?

Category 1: Amortized Cost (HTM under US GAAP)
  • IFRS: Used when (a) cash flows occur on specified dates AND consist solely of principal and interest (the "SPPI" test), AND (b) the business model is to hold the asset to collect contractual cash flows (hold-to-maturity intent).
  • US GAAP: "Held-to-maturity" — same intent, applies only to debt securities. The entity must have both the positive intent AND the ability to hold to maturity.
  • Examples: long-term bonds bought with intent to hold to maturity, loans made to other companies
  • Fair value changes? Ignored on the balance sheet — asset stays at amortized cost. Only interest income hits the P&L.
Category 2: FVOCI / Available-for-Sale
  • IFRS Debt: Used when business model involves both collecting cash flows AND selling the financial assets. Meets SPPI test.
  • IFRS Equity: Company may make an irrevocable election at acquisition to measure equity investments at FVOCI. This election is permanent — cannot be changed later.
  • US GAAP: "Available-for-Sale" — applies only to debt securities. US GAAP does NOT allow equity investments in FVOCI/AFS category.
  • Balance sheet: asset at fair value. Unrealized gains/losses → OCI (equity section, accumulated other comprehensive income — AOCI). When the asset is sold, the cumulative OCI gain/loss is "recycled" to the income statement.
Category 3: FVTPL / Trading Securities
  • IFRS: Default category for financial assets not assigned to Category 1 or 2. Also used if the company makes an irrevocable FVTPL election at acquisition.
  • US GAAP: All equity investments (except those giving significant influence) are measured at FVTPL. "Trading" debt securities — acquired with intent to sell before maturity.
  • Balance sheet: asset at fair value. Unrealized gains/losses → directly to income statement (profit or loss). This creates earnings volatility.
  • Highest earnings volatility — every market fluctuation hits net income.

Critical IFRS vs. US GAAP Differences on Financial Instruments

Equity Investments — IFRS
  • Default: FVTPL (unrealized G/L → income statement)
  • Option: irrevocable FVOCI election at acquisition (unrealized G/L → OCI, never recycled to P&L even on sale)
  • No HTM/AFS categories for equity
Equity Investments — US GAAP
  • ALL equity investments (except those conferring significant influence) → FVTPL mandatory
  • No AFS option for equity — this was eliminated under ASU 2016-01
  • Unrealized G/L always hits income statement — no OCI option

Worked Example — Impact of Classification on Financials

Example: €100m Bond Investment, 5% coupon (semi-annual), value increases €2m after 6 months
Item Amortized Cost (HTM) FVOCI (AFS) FVTPL (Trading)

HTM: balance sheet stays at cost (€100m), no gain recognized. FVOCI: balance sheet shows FV (€102m) but gain bypasses income statement → sits in AOCI. FVTPL: balance sheet shows FV (€102m) AND gain hits income → highest reported earnings volatility.

Examiner Focus — Financial Instruments

The exam loves to test: (1) which category → where do unrealized gains/losses go (IS vs. OCI); (2) the US GAAP difference — ALL equity (except significant-influence investments) is mandatorily FVTPL; AFS applies only to debt; (3) IFRS allows FVOCI for equity via irrevocable election; (4) realized gains/losses (from selling) always go through the income statement regardless of classification; (5) amortized cost = initial recognition − principal repaid ± amortization of discount/premium − impairment. Know the Exhibit 5 table cold — the examiner often presents scenarios and asks where the gain/loss appears.

05

Non-Current Liabilities

All liabilities that are not classified as current are non-current (long-term) liabilities. Typical non-current liabilities include: long-term financial liabilities (bonds payable, bank loans), lease liabilities, deferred tax liabilities, post-employment benefit obligations (pension liabilities), and deferred revenue (non-current portion).

Most liabilities are reported at amortized cost on the balance sheet, though certain cases (financial liabilities held for trading, derivatives, some hedged instruments) are carried at fair value.

Long-Term Financial Liabilities — Bonds and Loans

Bonds Payable — Amortized Cost Treatment
  • Issued at par: Carrying amount = face value throughout life. No amortization of discount/premium needed. Simple case: $10m bonds at par → $10m liability always.
  • Issued at discount (below par): e.g., $10m face value bonds issued at 97.5% → proceeds = $9.75m. The $250,000 discount is amortized over the bond's life, increasing the carrying amount to $10m at maturity. Each period: interest expense > coupon paid (the amortized discount is added).
  • Issued at premium (above par): e.g., issued at 102% → proceeds = $10.2m. The $200,000 premium is amortized over the bond's life, decreasing the carrying amount to $10m at maturity. Each period: interest expense < coupon paid (the amortized premium is deducted).
  • At maturity: carrying amount (amortized cost) = face value for all bonds, regardless of original issue price.
Amortized Cost of Bond — Key Relationship
Carrying Amount = Face Value − Unamortized Discount (discount bond)
Carrying Amount = Face Value + Unamortized Premium (premium bond)

Interest Expense (IS) = Carrying Amount × Effective Interest Rate
Coupon Payment (Cash) = Face Value × Coupon Rate

Discount amortization: Interest Expense > Coupon → carrying amount increases toward face value
Premium amortization: Interest Expense < Coupon → carrying amount decreases toward face value
This is the effective interest method — the standard method under both IFRS and US GAAP. The straight-line method (equal amortization each period) is a simplification sometimes allowed under US GAAP if results are not materially different.

Deferred Tax Liabilities

Deferred tax liabilities (DTLs) arise from temporary timing differences between: taxable income (what you report to the tax authority) and financial statement income (what you report to investors). A DTL means you're paying less tax NOW than your income statement expense suggests — the extra tax is deferred to future periods.

The most common cause: using accelerated depreciation for tax purposes but straight-line depreciation for financial reporting. Tax depreciation is front-loaded → lower taxable income in early years → lower taxes payable now → DTL. In later years, tax depreciation is lower → higher taxable income → pay off the DTL.

DTLs also arise when: subsidiary profits not yet distributed (not yet subject to tax), or income recognized later for tax than for financial reporting.

Deferred Tax Liability (DTL)

Tax payable in future periods. Taxable income < Book income currently → paying less tax now. The difference accumulates as a DTL (future tax obligation).

Common cause: Accelerated depreciation for tax. Book income > taxable income early in asset life → DTL builds up early, reverses later.

Deferred Tax Asset (DTA)

Prepaid tax — you pay tax NOW but will get the benefit later. Common cause: Unearned revenue taxed immediately when received (e.g., in the US, cash received is often taxable immediately), but recognized as revenue over time for financial reporting.

Also arises from: tax loss carryforwards, warranty provisions (deductible only when paid, not when expensed).

💡 DTL Memory Trick

"DTL = Deferred Tax = Delaying the Tax Bill." You owe the tax eventually, but you're paying it later than your income statement suggests. The government is essentially giving you a temporary interest-free loan. Think of Apple's $31.5 billion DTL — mostly accumulated offshore profits not yet repatriated and taxed — a massive deferred obligation lurking in "other non-current liabilities."

Common-Size Balance Sheet Analysis

Vertical common-size analysis of the balance sheet: express every line item as a percentage of total assets. This removes the effect of size differences, enabling meaningful comparison across companies of different sizes and across time periods.

Unlike the income statement common-size (where everything is % of revenue), the balance sheet uses total assets as the base for all items — both asset items AND liability/equity items.

What Common-Size Analysis Reveals

Liquidity Profile
  • High % cash + marketable securities → strong liquidity
  • High % inventory + receivables with low cash → liquidity risk
  • Compare current assets % vs. current liabilities %
  • Current ratio can be inferred from common-size data
Capital Structure & Strategy
  • High % goodwill → growth via acquisitions
  • Zero goodwill → organic growth strategy
  • High % PP&E → capital-intensive, manufactures in-house
  • High % intangibles → tech, pharma, media companies
Solvency & Leverage
  • High % total liabilities relative to total assets → higher leverage = higher financial risk
  • High % long-term debt → depends on cash flow stability
  • Compare equity % over time — declining equity share = increasing leverage

Sector Patterns — Key Benchmarks to Know

S&P 500 Sector Balance Sheet Norms (Median, 2017 Data)
  • Energy & Utilities: Highest PP&E (73% and 72% of assets). Capital-intensive physical infrastructure.
  • Financials: Highest total liabilities (87% of assets). Highly leveraged by nature — deposits and debt fund assets. Financial leverage ratio is very high.
  • Information Technology: Lowest leverage — highest cash & investments (22.7% of assets median). Lowest long-term debt (22.9%) and highest equity (39.3%).
  • Consumer Staples & Health Care: Highest intangibles/goodwill — reflects brand value and pharmaceutical acquisitions.
  • Telecom Services: Highest long-term debt (46.8%) — capital-intensive infrastructure with relatively stable cash flows supporting high leverage.
  • Utilities & Real Estate: Very low receivables and inventories. Lowest cash relative to assets.
Examiner Focus — Common-Size Balance Sheet Interpretation

Exam questions often give you a common-size balance sheet for two companies and ask you to draw conclusions about liquidity, solvency, and strategy. Key patterns: Company with high goodwill → made acquisitions; Company with zero goodwill → organic grower; Company with very high A/P but low cash → potential liquidity risk (like a retailer living on trade credit). A company with 95%+ liabilities (like Company C in the LES example) may be technically liquid today but is highly vulnerable to cash flow shocks. Remember: common-size analysis gives RATIOS not absolute amounts — you still need the raw numbers to assess the actual size of obligations.

07

Liquidity & Solvency Ratios

Balance sheet ratios split into two categories: liquidity ratios (short-term ability to meet obligations) and solvency ratios (long-term financial stability). All ratios involve only balance sheet items — no income statement needed (unlike interest coverage ratios, which are discussed in later modules).

Liquidity Ratios

RatioFormulaWhat It MeasuresKey Point
Current Ratio Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities Broadest measure of short-term liquidity. Can the company cover all current liabilities with all current assets? >1 = current assets exceed current liabilities. Higher is generally better, but very high may indicate inefficient use of cash.
Quick Ratio (Acid Test) (Cash + Mktable Securities + Receivables) ÷ Current Liabilities More conservative — excludes inventory (least liquid current asset) and other prepayments. Better for companies where inventory is slow to convert to cash (e.g., real estate, manufacturing).
Cash Ratio (Cash + Marketable Securities) ÷ Current Liabilities Most conservative. Only uses the most liquid assets. Can the company pay ALL current liabilities immediately? Very low for most companies — holding that much cash would be inefficient. Useful for distressed analysis.
💡 Liquidity Ratio Hierarchy

Think of the three ratios as a funnel: Current ratio includes everything (inventory, prepayments, receivables, cash). Quick ratio removes inventory and prepayments. Cash ratio only keeps cash and near-cash. Each ratio is more conservative than the last. A company that looks liquid on the current ratio but much worse on the quick ratio likely has a lot of slow-moving inventory — a red flag for liquidity risk in a downturn.

Solvency Ratios

RatioFormulaWhat It MeasuresHigher Ratio Means
Long-Term Debt-to-Equity Total Long-Term Debt ÷ Total Equity Long-term financial leverage. How much LT debt per unit of equity? Higher financial risk and leverage
Debt-to-Equity (D/E) Total Debt ÷ Total Equity Total financial leverage (both current and long-term debt). Higher financial risk and leverage
Total Debt Ratio Total Debt ÷ Total Assets Proportion of assets financed by debt. Also = 1 − Equity ratio. Higher leverage; creditors finance more of assets
Financial Leverage (Equity Multiplier) Total Assets ÷ Total Equity Multiplier effect of debt. Used in DuPont analysis (ROE = ROA × Financial Leverage). Higher leverage; amplifies ROE but also risk
Key Solvency Formulas
Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
Quick Ratio = (Cash + Mktable Sec + Receivables) ÷ Current Liabilities
Cash Ratio = (Cash + Mktable Sec) ÷ Current Liabilities

LT Debt-to-Equity = Total Long-Term Debt ÷ Total Equity
Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt ÷ Total Equity
Total Debt Ratio = Total Debt ÷ Total Assets
Financial Leverage = Total Assets ÷ Total Equity

Tangible Book Value = Total Equity − Goodwill − Intangible Assets
Tangible Equity Ratio = Tangible Book Value ÷ Total Assets
Note: "Total debt" can mean different things depending on context. Sometimes it refers to interest-bearing debt only (notes payable + bonds payable + bank loans + capital leases) and excludes trade payables and other non-financial liabilities. Other times it means all liabilities. Always clarify which definition is being used in a given question or analysis.

Limitations of Balance Sheet Ratio Analysis

⚠️ Ratio Analysis Limitations — Don't Rely Blindly

Point-in-time snapshot: Balance sheet ratios reflect the financial position on one specific date. Companies can "window dress" — e.g., paying down short-term debt just before the reporting date to boost the current ratio, then re-borrowing after. The ratio looks better but nothing fundamental changed.

Components matter more than the ratio: The current ratio treats all current assets equally, but inventory is far less liquid than cash. A current ratio of 2.0 backed mostly by slow-moving inventory is worse than a current ratio of 1.5 backed by cash and receivables.

Industry context is essential: A D/E ratio of 5:1 is alarmingly high for a manufacturer but completely normal for a bank. Financial companies have structurally high leverage by design. Never interpret a ratio without a sector benchmark.

Accounting differences distort comparisons: Different depreciation methods, inventory costing, and intangible asset treatments create ratios that are not apples-to-apples across companies. Analytical adjustments are required.

Exam Checklist — Module 3: Analyzing Balance Sheets

  • ✅ Intangible assets = identifiable, non-monetary, no physical substance. Identifiable = separable OR arises from contractual/legal rights. Goodwill = NOT separately identifiable.
  • ✅ IFRS measurement models for intangibles: Cost model (default) OR revaluation model (only if active market exists). US GAAP: cost model ONLY.
  • ✅ Finite useful life: Amortized systematically over useful life. Method and life reviewed annually. Subject to impairment. Indefinite useful life: NOT amortized. Tested for impairment annually. Classification reviewed annually.
  • ✅ Internally generated intangibles — IFRS: Research phase = always expensed. Development phase = capitalize if 6 criteria met (feasibility + intent + ability + future benefits + resources + reliable measurement).
  • ✅ US GAAP: Both R&D costs generally expensed (except post-feasibility software development). No development capitalization for most internally generated intangibles.
  • ✅ Always expensed (both standards): Internally generated brands, customer lists, start-up costs, training, admin overhead, advertising, restructuring costs.
  • ✅ Purchased intangibles ARE capitalized if they arise from contractual rights, other legal rights, or can be separated and sold. Same economic asset — different treatment depending on whether internally developed or acquired.
  • ✅ Goodwill = Purchase price − FV of net identifiable assets acquired. Recognized only in acquisitions. NOT amortized. Tested for impairment at least annually.
  • ✅ Bargain purchase: Purchase price < FV net identifiable assets → GAIN on income statement (not negative goodwill on balance sheet).
  • ✅ Goodwill impairment: Non-cash charge. Reduces earnings and total assets. Irreversible. May increase future ROA (lower denominator). Signals management overpaid for acquisition.
  • ✅ Accounting goodwill vs. economic goodwill: Accounting = acquisition-based, on balance sheet. Economic = ongoing performance, reflected in stock price. Economic goodwill can exist with zero accounting goodwill (organic growth).
  • ✅ Three financial instrument measurement categories: (1) Amortized Cost = hold-to-collect, SPPI test; (2) FVOCI = hold-to-collect AND sell (debt), or irrevocable equity election (IFRS); (3) FVTPL = default / trading intent.
  • ✅ Where unrealized G/L goes: Amortized Cost → nowhere (no recognition). FVOCI → OCI (bypasses IS). FVTPL → Income Statement (hits earnings directly).
  • ✅ US GAAP equity investments: ALL equity (except significant influence) → FVTPL mandatory. No AFS/FVOCI option for equity. Unrealized G/L always hits income statement.
  • ✅ IFRS equity investments: Default = FVTPL. Irrevocable FVOCI election available at acquisition. If FVOCI elected for equity, gains/losses never recycled to P&L even on sale.
  • ✅ Bonds payable — amortized cost: Discount bond: carrying value increases to face value over life (interest expense > coupon). Premium bond: carrying value decreases to face value (interest expense < coupon). At maturity: all bonds carry at face value.
  • ✅ Deferred tax liability: Taxable income < Book income → pay less tax now → DTL (future obligation). Most common cause: accelerated depreciation for tax, straight-line for books.
  • ✅ Common-size balance sheet: Each item as % of total assets (NOT revenue). Enables comparison across companies and over time regardless of size. Reveals capital structure, liquidity profile, and strategic priorities.
  • ✅ Liquidity ratios (ascending conservatism): Current ratio (all current assets ÷ CL) → Quick ratio (exclude inventory and prepayments) → Cash ratio (cash + mktable sec only). All higher = more liquid.
  • ✅ Solvency ratios: LT D/E, D/E, Total Debt Ratio, Financial Leverage (= Total Assets ÷ Equity). Higher = more leveraged = more financial risk. Interpret always relative to sector norms.
  • ✅ Ratio limitations: Point-in-time only. Inventory ≠ cash in liquidity analysis. Industry context essential. Accounting policy differences distort cross-company comparisons. Current ratio is sensitive to year-end window dressing.

═══ INTANGIBLE ASSETS ═══
Finite life → Amortize over useful life + Impairment testing
Indefinite life → NO amortization + Annual impairment test
IFRS: Research → Expense | Development → Capitalize (if 6 criteria met)
US GAAP: R&D → Expense (exception: post-feasibility software costs)
Internally generated brands/customer lists/start-up: ALWAYS expensed (both standards)

═══ GOODWILL ═══
Goodwill = Purchase Price − FV of Net Identifiable Assets
Net Identifiable Assets = FV Assets − FV Liabilities
Bargain purchase: Gain on IS (not negative goodwill)
Post-acquisition: NOT amortized. Annual impairment test. Non-cash if impaired.
Impairment → lowers assets → may increase future ROA

═══ FINANCIAL INSTRUMENTS — 3 CATEGORIES ═══
Amortized Cost (HTM) → Interest income on IS only. No FV changes recognized.
FVOCI (AFS) → FV changes → OCI (equity). Interest income → IS.
FVTPL (Trading) → FV changes → IS (income statement). Highest volatility.
Realized gains (sale) → ALWAYS to income statement, all categories.
US GAAP equity: ALL equity → FVTPL mandatory (no AFS for equity)
IFRS equity: Default FVTPL. Irrevocable FVOCI election available.

═══ BONDS PAYABLE — AMORTIZED COST ═══
Discount bond: Interest exp > Coupon paid → Carrying value ↑ toward face
Premium bond: Interest exp < Coupon paid → Carrying value ↓ toward face
At maturity: Carrying amount = Face value always

═══ DEFERRED TAX LIABILITY ═══
DTL: Tax paid < Book tax expense now → defer to future
Common cause: Accelerated depreciation (tax) vs. Straight-line (books)
DTA: Tax paid > Book tax expense now → prepaid tax (e.g., unearned revenue taxed on receipt)

═══ LIQUIDITY RATIOS ═══
Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
Quick Ratio = (Cash + Mktable Sec + Receivables) ÷ Current Liabilities
Cash Ratio = (Cash + Mktable Sec) ÷ Current Liabilities

═══ SOLVENCY RATIOS ═══
LT Debt-to-Equity = LT Debt ÷ Equity
Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt ÷ Equity
Total Debt Ratio = Total Debt ÷ Total Assets
Financial Leverage = Total Assets ÷ Equity (= 1 ÷ Equity Ratio)
Tangible Book Val = Equity − Goodwill − Intangibles
Top exam traps for Module 3:
(1) Goodwill is NOT amortized under either IFRS or US GAAP — only impairment tested annually.
(2) Bargain purchase = GAIN on income statement, NOT negative goodwill on balance sheet.
(3) Goodwill impairment is non-cash — reduces earnings and assets but NOT cash flow from operations.
(4) IFRS allows development cost capitalization; US GAAP generally does NOT.
(5) Internally generated brands, customer lists = NEVER capitalized (but acquired ones ARE).
(6) US GAAP equity investments → FVTPL mandatory. No AFS/FVOCI for equity.
(7) IFRS FVOCI election for equity is irrevocable AND gains never recycled to P&L on sale.
(8) Unrealized gains on FVOCI → OCI (bypasses IS). Unrealized gains on FVTPL → directly hits IS.
(9) Discount bond: interest expense > coupon (amortization increases carrying value). Premium: opposite.
(10) DTL: accelerated tax depreciation → lower taxable income now → less tax paid now → DTL (future obligation).
(11) Common-size balance sheet base = TOTAL ASSETS (not revenue). Everything expressed as % of total assets.
(12) Current ratio is the broadest liquidity measure; Quick ratio excludes inventory; Cash ratio is the most conservative.
(13) Post-goodwill-impairment: total assets fall → future ROA may INCREASE (smaller denominator). A counter-intuitive exam favorite.
