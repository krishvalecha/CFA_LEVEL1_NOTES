01

Essential Definitions

Population
All members of a specified group. The complete set we are trying to learn about.
Parameter
A quantity computed from or used to describe a population. E.g., population mean µ, population variance σ². Fixed (unknown) values.
Sample
A subset of the population used to estimate population parameters. Must be representative for valid inference.
Statistic
A quantity computed from or used to describe a sample. E.g., sample mean X̄, sample variance s². A random variable — it varies from sample to sample.
Sampling Error
The difference between an observed sample statistic and the population parameter it estimates. Arises because we observe only a subset. Sampling error ≠ mistake — it is an inherent property of sampling.
Sampling Distribution
The distribution of all distinct possible values a statistic can assume when computed from samples of the same size drawn from the same population. A statistic is itself a random variable.
Sampling Plan
The set of rules used to select a sample from the population. Defines the method and size of sampling.
Resampling
Technique that repeatedly draws samples from the original observed data to perform statistical inference without closed-form formulas.
💡 Parameter vs. Statistic — The Core Distinction

Parameter describes the POPULATION (Greek letters: µ, σ², σ) — it is fixed but unknown. Statistic describes the SAMPLE (Latin letters: X̄, s², s) — it is known but varies. The whole game of inference is using statistics to estimate parameters.

02

Sampling Methods

Two top-level categories: Probability Sampling — every member has an equal chance of selection, produces representative samples, more accurate and reliable. Non-Probability Sampling — selection depends on researcher judgment or convenience, significant risk of non-representative samples.

Probability Sampling Methods

Method How It Works Best For Key Advantage Key Limitation
Simple Random Sampling PROB Each element has equal probability of selection. Every possible sample of size n is equally likely. Homogeneous populations where all elements are broadly similar. Unbiased; straightforward; sample statistics are valid estimates of population parameters. May not represent important subgroups; requires ability to identify all population members.
Systematic Sampling PROB Select every kth member from the population until desired sample size is reached. When population cannot be fully coded or listed. Practical when population list is unavailable. Result is approximately random. Risk of periodicity bias if the population has a repeating pattern at interval k.
Stratified Random Sampling PROB Divide population into strata (subgroups); draw simple random samples from each stratum proportional to its size; pool samples. Heterogeneous populations with identifiable subgroups (e.g., bond indexing by duration, sector, credit quality). Guarantees representation of all subgroups; produces estimates with smaller variance than simple random sampling. Requires knowledge of population strata and their relative sizes.
Cluster Sampling PROB Divide population into clusters (mini-representations of whole). Randomly select clusters.
One-stage: take all members of selected clusters. Two-stage: randomly sample within selected clusters.		 Very large, geographically dispersed populations (e.g., national investor surveys). Most time-efficient and cost-efficient probability method for large populations. Lower accuracy than other probability methods at equal sample size; cluster sample may be less representative of entire population.

Non-Probability Sampling Methods

Convenience Sampling NON-PROB
  • Elements selected based on ease of access or availability to the researcher.
  • Samples are NOT necessarily representative of the population.
  • Advantage: Very quick and low cost.
  • Use case: Preliminary/pilot stage of research; cost-constrained situations; small-scale studies before large-scale sampling.
  • Risk: High potential for non-representative results.
Judgmental Sampling NON-PROB
  • Elements handpicked based on researcher's knowledge and professional judgment.
  • Selection can be affected by researcher bias → skewed results.
  • Advantage: Researcher expertise can create a more targeted, relevant sample quickly. Expert judgment can be more efficient than mechanical selection.
  • Use case: Auditing — experienced auditors select accounts/transactions using sound judgment to ensure sufficient audit coverage.
  • Risk: Researcher bias; does not represent whole population.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Stratified vs. Cluster Sampling

These look similar but work oppositely. In stratified sampling: ALL strata are included, and you randomly select elements WITHIN each stratum. In cluster sampling: you randomly select WHICH clusters to include, then include ALL (or a random subset of) members of chosen clusters. Cluster = select whole groups; Stratified = select from all groups.

🧠 Mnemonic: Sampling Method Accuracy Ranking

"Stratified > Simple Random > Cluster" for accuracy at equal sample size. Remember: Stratified = Smaller variance (best). Cluster = Cheapest but least accurate. Non-probability methods (Convenience, Judgmental) are not on the accuracy scale — they may not be representative at all.

Sampling from Multiple Distributions

A critical practical issue: if a sample is drawn from a population that is actually a mixture of different distributions (e.g., returns in normal markets vs. crisis periods), the analyst must be careful. Pooling data from different distributions without recognizing their difference can produce misleading statistics — the sample may appear to come from a single distribution when it does not. Always assess whether the population is homogeneous before choosing a sampling method.

03

Sampling Error & Data Biases

Sampling Error (Formal Definition)
The difference between the observed value of a statistic and the quantity it is intended to estimate, as a result of using subsets of the population. It arises from sampling variation — we observe only a subset of the full population.

A random sample reflects the properties of the population in an unbiased way. Sample statistics computed from random samples are valid (unbiased) estimates of population parameters. However, any individual sample will differ from the population — this is expected sampling variation, not an error in the pejorative sense.

The trade-off of sampling is: time and money saved in exchange for introducing sampling error. Larger samples reduce (but never eliminate) sampling error.

Common Data & Sampling Biases to Know

Bias TypeDescriptionInvestment Example
Data-Mining Bias Repeatedly searching for patterns in the same dataset until a significant result is found by chance. The "discovered" pattern is a statistical artifact, not a real relationship. Backtesting hundreds of trading strategies on the same historical data and reporting only the one that worked best.
Sample Selection Bias Systematically excluding certain data from the sample. Creates a non-representative sample even if the sampling process within the included data is random. Survivorship bias: studying only currently existing funds excludes funds that failed/closed, overstating average performance.
Look-Ahead Bias Using information in analysis that was not actually available at the time of the decision being modeled. Using annual earnings data (published in March) to make investment decisions modeled as of January 1 of the same year.
Time-Period Bias Using a time period for a study that is either too short (capturing a specific regime) or too long (spanning multiple structural regimes). Results may not generalize. Basing equity risk premium estimates only on the 1990s bull market, or using data spanning pre/post gold standard periods.
💡 Survivorship Bias — Most Common Exam Application

When a database only includes funds/companies that are still alive today, historical average performance is upward biased because all the failures are excluded from the sample. This is the most tested form of sample selection bias.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

★ Central Limit Theorem — Formal Statement
Given a population with mean µ and finite variance σ²,
the sampling distribution of the sample mean X̄
from random samples of size n will be:

X̄ ~ approximately Normal( µ, σ²/n )

…when sample size n is LARGE (n ≥ 30 as a rule of thumb).
This holds regardless of the shape of the underlying population distribution — as long as the population has finite variance.
The mean of the sampling distribution of X̄ = µ (population mean).
The variance of the sampling distribution of X̄ = σ²/n (population variance divided by n).

Three Key Properties of the Sampling Distribution of X̄ (Large n)

1
Approximately Normal — regardless of the shape of the original population distribution. Even if returns are skewed or fat-tailed, X̄ will be approximately normally distributed for large n.
2
Mean of X̄ = µ — the mean of the sampling distribution of the sample mean equals the true population mean. X̄ is an unbiased estimator of µ.
3
Variance of X̄ = σ²/n — as sample size n increases, the variance of the sample mean DECREASES. Larger samples → sample mean is clustered more tightly around µ → less sampling error.
Why CLT Is So Powerful
  • We rarely know the population distribution in practice — it could be skewed, fat-tailed, bimodal, etc.
  • CLT lets us use the normal distribution for inference about population means regardless of the underlying distribution (provided n is large and variance is finite).
  • It is the foundation for constructing confidence intervals and hypothesis testing for means.
  • Works for ANY distribution with finite variance — uniform, exponential, binomial, etc.
The n ≥ 30 Rule of Thumb
  • When n ≥ 30, X̄ is approximately normally distributed for most practical purposes.
  • If the underlying population is itself close to normal, even smaller samples (n < 30) may be sufficient.
  • If the underlying population is very non-normal (highly skewed, heavy-tailed), n may need to be well in excess of 30 for the CLT to apply well.
  • There is no universal bright line — n ≥ 30 is a guideline, not a guarantee.
⚠️ CLT Does NOT Say the Population Becomes Normal

A very common misconception: the CLT says the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal — NOT that the population becomes normally distributed. The original population keeps whatever shape it has. Only the distribution of X̄ (across many repeated samples) approaches normality.

🧠 Memory Trick: What the CLT Gives Us

Remember "3 Ns": the CLT tells us that X̄ is Normal, with mean = µ (No bias), and variance = σ²/n (shrinks with n). That's Normal, No-bias, Narrowing — the three gifts of the CLT.

05

Standard Error of the Sample Mean

The standard deviation of a sample statistic is called its standard error. The standard error of the sample mean measures how much the sample mean is expected to deviate from the true population mean — i.e., the precision of the estimate. It is the most important application of the CLT in practice.

Standard Error of the Sample Mean — Two Cases
CASE 1: Population σ is KNOWN
σ_X̄ = σ / √n

CASE 2: Population σ is UNKNOWN (use sample std dev s)
s_X̄ = s / √n

where s = √[ Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)² / (n − 1) ]

← In practice, σ is almost ALWAYS unknown → use Case 2.
σ_X̄ or s_X̄ = standard error of the sample mean.
n = sample size. Note the √n in the denominator — standard error shrinks as sample size grows (∝ 1/√n, not 1/n).
s² = sample variance — uses (n−1) in denominator (degrees of freedom correction) to make it an unbiased estimator of σ².
Sample Variance Formula (Equation 3)
s² = Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (Xᵢ − X̄)² / (n − 1)
Divide by (n−1), not n. This is the "degrees of freedom" correction — using X̄ (estimated from the data) in place of µ costs one degree of freedom, so we divide by (n−1) to get an unbiased estimator of σ².
s (sample standard deviation) = √s² = the estimate of σ used in Case 2 above.

Standard Deviation vs. Standard Error — Critical Distinction

Standard Deviation (σ or s)
  • Measures dispersion of individual data points around the mean.
  • Describes how spread out the data is.
  • A property of the data / distribution.
  • Use when: "How variable are individual observations?"
  • Does NOT shrink to zero as n → ∞ (data always has dispersion).
Standard Error (σ_X̄ or s_X̄)
  • Measures precision of a sample statistic (e.g., sample mean) as an estimate of the population parameter.
  • Describes how much the sample mean varies across samples.
  • A property of the estimator / sampling process.
  • Use when: "How accurate is my estimate of µ?"
  • Shrinks as n increases — larger sample → more precise estimate.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Standard Deviation ≠ Standard Error

The terms are NOT interchangeable. Standard deviation is about data spread; standard error is about estimation precision. Confusing them in a question is a classic trap. If a question asks about the accuracy of the sample mean as an estimator of the population mean → it's asking about standard error.

📐 Worked Illustration: Effect of Sample Size on Standard Error

Suppose population σ = 20%. Compare standard errors for different sample sizes:

n = 25: s_X̄ = 20% / √25 = 20% / 5 = 4.00%
n = 100: s_X̄ = 20% / √100 = 20% / 10 = 2.00%
n = 400: s_X̄ = 20% / √400 = 20% / 20 = 1.00%
Quadrupling sample size HALVES the standard error (∝ 1/√n relationship).

To cut standard error in half, you must quadruple the sample size — diminishing returns to larger samples.

💡 1/√n Relationship — Key Insight

Standard error = σ/√n. This means to halve the standard error, you need to quadruple (4×) the sample size. To reduce it to 1/3, you need 9× the sample size. The precision gains from larger samples follow diminishing returns — going from n=25 to n=100 halves the SE, but going from n=100 to n=400 only halves it again despite tripling the additional observations.

Bootstrap Resampling

Bootstrap
A resampling method that uses the original observed sample as a proxy for the population, then repeatedly draws samples of the same size as the original — with replacement — to build an empirical sampling distribution of a statistic.

How Bootstrap Works — Step by Step

1
Start with original sample of size n (e.g., 12 monthly returns). This sample serves as the "estimated population."
2
Draw a resample of size n WITH replacement from the original sample. Each observation has equal probability of being drawn in each draw. Some observations may appear more than once; others may not appear at all.
3
Compute the statistic of interest (e.g., mean, median, standard deviation) for each resample. Record it.
4
Repeat steps 2–3 many times (e.g., B = 1,000 times). Each repetition gives one value of the statistic.
5
Build the bootstrap sampling distribution from all B resample statistics. This approximates the true sampling distribution of the statistic.
6
Estimate standard error using Equation 4, or construct confidence intervals directly from the bootstrap distribution's percentiles.
Bootstrap Standard Error of the Sample Mean (Equation 4)
s_X̄ = √[ 1/(B−1) × Σᵦ₌₁ᴮ (θ̂ᵦ − θ̄)² ]
s_X̄ = estimated standard error of the sample mean from bootstrap.
B = number of resamples drawn from the original sample.
θ̂ᵦ = the mean of resample b (one of the B resamples).
θ̄ = the mean across ALL B resample means (the grand mean of resamples).

Note: this formula looks just like sample variance — it is the sample standard deviation of the B resample means.
📐 Worked Example (from Module): Bootstrap SE Calculation

Original sample: n = 12 monthly returns. Bootstrap: B = 1,000 resamples drawn with replacement.

Results: Mean across all 1,000 resample means (θ̄) = −0.01367. Sum of squared deviations of resample means from θ̄ = 1.94143.

s_X̄ = √[ 1/(1000−1) × 1.94143 ]
s_X̄ = √[ 1/999 × 1.94143 ]
s_X̄ = √[ 0.001944 ]
s_X̄ = 0.04408 (i.e., ~4.41%)

This is the bootstrap estimate of how precisely the sample mean estimates the population mean.

Key Characteristics of Bootstrap

Advantages
  • Does not require a closed-form analytical formula or distributional assumptions — "model-free" / "non-parametric."
  • Works for ANY estimator — mean, median, standard deviation, correlations, complex risk metrics — even when no analytical formula exists.
  • Potential advantages in accuracy over conventional parametric methods.
  • Can construct confidence intervals for complex statistics without formulas.
  • Widely applicable in finance: historical simulations in asset allocation, backtesting investment strategies, gauging strategy performance vs. a benchmark.
Limitations / Characteristics to Know
  • Computationally intensive — requires many repetitions (often 1,000+).
  • Bootstrap usually gives different results each run because resamples are randomly drawn.
  • Number of repetitions B is left to the analyst to determine — no fixed rule.
  • Quality depends on the original sample being representative of the population — if the original sample is biased, bootstrap amplifies that bias.
  • Also called model-free resampling or non-parametric resampling.
💡 "With Replacement" — Why It Matters

Bootstrap draws WITH replacement — after each draw, the observation is put back before the next draw. This means: (a) the same observation can appear multiple times in one resample, (b) some observations may not appear at all, and (c) each resample has the same size n as the original. This is what allows the bootstrap to simulate the variability of the statistic across hypothetical repeated samples from the population.

Jackknife Resampling

Jackknife
A resampling technique where subsamples are created by taking the original sample and leaving out one observation at a time — without replacement. For a sample of size n, jackknife produces exactly n subsamples, each of size (n−1).

Primary use case: Reducing the bias of an estimator. Secondary uses: estimating the standard error and constructing confidence intervals of an estimator.

How it works: From a sample of n observations, create n subsamples by dropping observation 1 (subsample 1), then dropping observation 2 (subsample 2), etc. Each subsample has n−1 observations. Compute the statistic of interest on each subsample. Use the variability across these n estimates to assess sampling error.

Bootstrap vs. Jackknife — Head-to-Head

Feature Bootstrap Jackknife
Sampling method Draw samples WITH replacement from original Leave out one observation at a time (no replacement)
Resample size Same size as original sample (n) One less than original sample (n − 1)
Number of repetitions Analyst-determined (typically 1,000+); no fixed rule Always exactly n repetitions (one per observation)
Reproducibility Different results each run (random draws) Same results every run (deterministic process)
Primary application Estimating standard error and CIs for any statistic; most powerful and widely used Reducing estimator bias; also finds SE and CIs
Computation Computer simulation required; more intensive for large B Systematic and structured; always n computations
Finance applications Asset allocation simulations, strategy backtesting vs. benchmark Bias correction in complex estimators
🧠 Memory Trick: Bootstrap vs. Jackknife

Bootstrap = Boot up the computer and run randomly (WITH replacement, random, variable results, YOU decide how many). Jackknife = Jack out one at a time, very systematically (WITHOUT replacement, systematic, same results every time, ALWAYS n repetitions). Boot = random + computer; Jack = systematic + deterministic.

⚠️ Exam Trap: Jackknife Does NOT Use Replacement

Bootstrap uses WITH replacement; Jackknife does NOT. The jackknife simply omits one observation at a time — it never replaces observations or draws randomly. This is why jackknife always produces the same result (deterministic), while bootstrap produces different results each run.

CLT & Standard Error — Quick Reference

Central Limit Theorem Summary
Population: any distribution, mean = µ, variance = σ² (finite)
Sample size: n (large, typically n ≥ 30)

Sampling distribution of X̄:
→ Shape: approximately Normal
→ Mean: E(X̄) = µ ← unbiased
→ Variance: Var(X̄) = σ²/n ← shrinks with n
→ Std Dev: σ_X̄ = σ/√n ← standard error
As n increases: (1) sampling distribution of X̄ becomes more normal, (2) variance σ²/n decreases → X̄ becomes more precise estimate of µ. This is the entire mathematical justification for "bigger samples are better."

Exam Checklist — Must-Know Points

  • ✅ Parameter vs. Statistic: Parameter = describes population (Greek: µ, σ²) — fixed, unknown. Statistic = describes sample (X̄, s²) — known, random variable that varies across samples.
  • ✅ Sampling error = observed statistic minus population parameter. It is NOT a mistake — it's the inherent cost of using a subset. Larger samples reduce but never eliminate it.
  • ✅ Probability sampling (SRS, Stratified, Cluster, Systematic) gives every member equal selection chance → representative samples → valid statistical inference. Non-probability sampling (Convenience, Judgmental) does NOT → risk of biased, non-representative samples.
  • ✅ Stratified random sampling guarantees all subgroups (strata) are represented AND produces smaller variance estimates than SRS. Used heavily in bond indexing.
  • ✅ Cluster sampling: randomly select WHOLE clusters; most cost/time efficient probability method; LOWEST accuracy at equal sample size.
  • ✅ Stratified vs. Cluster distinction: Stratified = all strata included, sample within each. Cluster = randomly pick which clusters, take all (or subsample) within chosen clusters only.
  • ✅ Central Limit Theorem: For large n (≥ 30), X̄ ~ Normal(µ, σ²/n) regardless of underlying population distribution (as long as σ² is finite). CLT normalizes the distribution of X̄, NOT the population.
  • ✅ Three CLT properties of X̄ for large n: (1) Approximately normal, (2) E(X̄) = µ (unbiased), (3) Var(X̄) = σ²/n (decreasing in n).
  • ✅ Standard error of sample mean: σ_X̄ = σ/√n (known σ) OR s_X̄ = s/√n (estimated σ). In practice always use s/√n.
  • ✅ Standard deviation ≠ standard error. SD = spread of individual data; SE = precision of the sample mean as estimator of µ. SE shrinks with larger n; SD does not.
  • ✅ 1/√n relationship: To halve the SE, you must quadruple (4×) the sample size. Diminishing returns to larger samples.
  • ✅ Sample variance formula: s² = Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)² / (n−1). Uses (n−1) — degrees of freedom correction — to make s² an unbiased estimator of σ².
  • ✅ Bootstrap: Resamples of size n drawn WITH replacement from original sample. Results differ each run. Analyst decides number of repetitions B. Model-free/non-parametric. Works for ANY estimator including median. Used for SE estimation, CI construction, backtesting, asset allocation simulations.
  • ✅ Jackknife: Drops one observation at a time WITHOUT replacement. Always exactly n subsamples of size (n−1). Same results every run (deterministic). Primary use: bias reduction in estimators. Also provides SE and CIs.
  • ✅ Bootstrap vs. Jackknife key contrast: Bootstrap = random, with replacement, variable results, analyst-chosen B. Jackknife = systematic, without replacement, reproducible, always n repetitions.
  • ✅ Survivorship bias (sample selection bias): studying only surviving funds/companies overstates historical performance because failures are excluded.
  • ✅ Look-ahead bias: using data not available at the time of the modeled decision. Data-mining bias: finding patterns in data by chance through repeated testing on the same dataset.
