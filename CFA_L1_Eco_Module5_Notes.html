01

Core Concepts & Actors

Key Definitions

Geopolitics: The study of how geography affects politics and international relations. Analysts study actors and how they interact politically, economically, and financially.

Geopolitical Risk: Risk associated with tensions or actions between actors (state and non-state) that affect the normal and peaceful course of international relations. Tends to rise when the geographic and political factors underpinning country relations shift.

Two Types of Actors

State Actors
  • National governments, political organizations, country leaders
  • Exert authority over national security and resources
  • Examples: President of South Africa, Sultan of Brunei, UK Prime Minister, Malaysia's Parliament
  • Drive political cooperation/non-cooperation decisions
Non-State Actors
  • Participate in global affairs but do NOT control national security or resources
  • Examples: NGOs, multinational companies, charities, influential individuals (business leaders, cultural icons)
  • Primary drivers of globalization
  • Subject to but do not make political cooperation rules

National Interest — Hierarchy of Priorities

Every country has a hierarchy of national interests — survival/essential needs at the top, "nice to have" at the bottom. Governments cooperate where it benefits the nation-state, but higher priorities override lower ones when they conflict.

Key influences on the hierarchy: resource endowment, leadership style, political cycle length, cultural factors. Short political cycles (few years) make it hard to prioritize long-term risks (e.g., climate change, income inequality).

Cooperation Spectrum
Non-Cooperation ← ────────────────── → Cooperation
Non-Cooperative: Arbitrary rules · restricted movement · capital controls · retaliation · no technology exchange
Cooperative: Rules standardization · tariff harmonization · free movement of goods/services/capital/people · reciprocation · technology exchange

Why Countries Cooperate

  1. National security / military interest — protect citizens, borders, and institutions from external threats. Geographic factors matter: landlocked countries (e.g., Switzerland) depend heavily on neighbors.
  2. Economic interest — secure essential resources through trade; level the global playing field through standardization.
  3. Resource endowment — countries with abundant resources have leverage; resource-scarce countries must cooperate to survive.
  4. Standardization — creating common protocols for trade, capital flows, banking (e.g., Basel III via BCBS, SWIFT for financial messaging, containerization for shipping).
  5. Soft power — influencing other countries without force: cultural programs, university exchanges, advertising (e.g., EU Erasmus+, South Korea global tourism campaigns).
  6. Strong institutions — rule of law, property rights, government accountability → more stable, more durable cooperative relationships.
💡 Soft Power vs. Hard Power

Soft power = influencing others through cultural appeal, ideas, and values — without force or coercion. Hard power = military force or economic coercion. Most countries use a blend of both depending on their national interest hierarchy and resource endowment.

02

Globalization vs. Nationalism

The Core Distinction

Globalization
  • Active trade of goods and services across borders
  • Free capital flows and currency exchange
  • Cultural and information exchange
  • Primarily driven by non-state actors (companies, individuals)
  • World Bank Openness Index: 27% (1970) → 60%+ (2019)
  • Post-2008: headwinds from GFC + nationalism
Nationalism / Anti-Globalization
  • Promotion of country's own economic interests to the detriment of others
  • Limited cross-border trade, investment, and capital flows
  • Restricted currency exchange
  • Focus on national production and sales
  • Example: "America First" policies (2018+), tariffs on imports, NAFTA renegotiation

Motivations for Globalization (Non-State Actors)

MotivationDescriptionExamples
Increasing Profits Either: ↑ sales (new markets, new customers) OR ↓ costs (lower taxes, cheaper labor, economies of scale) Auto industry global consolidation; offshore manufacturing
Access to Resources & Markets Secure sustainable access to raw materials, talent, or investment opportunities not available domestically. Includes portfolio flows (short-term) and FDI (long-term). Mining companies, low-domestic-return investors seeking foreign returns
Intrinsic Gain Benefits beyond profit — personal growth, education, productivity improvements from learning. A stabilizing force that increases empathy and reduces likelihood of geopolitical threats. University exchange programs; cultural exposure; accelerated productivity

Benefits of Globalization

  • Increased profits through new markets and cost reduction
  • Greater product variety and consumer choice
  • Higher quality goods through global competition
  • Increased efficiency and labor mobility
  • Economies of scale in production (e.g., global auto industry)
  • Investment opportunities in foreign markets (portfolio flows and FDI)

Costs & Risks of Globalization

  1. Unequal economic gains — aggregate improvement ≠ improvement for everyone. Factory moves create jobs abroad but destroy them domestically; income inequality rises within countries.
  2. Lower ESG standards — companies operating in lower-standard countries may adopt local (lower) standards for environmental protection, labor, or corporate governance.
  3. Political backlash — income inequality and job displacement drive nationalism and political non-cooperation; a force for rollback of globalization.
  4. Supply chain interdependence and vulnerability — dependence on single-country production for key inputs (e.g., rare earth metals from China, copper from Chile, semiconductors). COVID-19 semiconductor shortage → auto industry crisis.

Deglobalization Response Strategies

  1. Reshoring the essentials: Rebuild domestic supply chains for critical goods (PPE, pharmaceuticals). Reduces manufacturing and procurement risk.
  2. Reglobalizing production: Duplicate or diversify supply chains across multiple countries to reduce single-point-of-failure risk.
  3. Doubling down on key markets: For major markets (China, Canada, Mexico), production "In country, for the country" alongside external supply chains. Productivity gains may still outweigh political risk.
⚠ Exam Point — Portfolio vs. FDI Flows

Portfolio investment flows = short-term investments in foreign assets (stocks, bonds). Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) = long-term investment in the productive capacity of a foreign country (factories, infrastructure). Both are important dimensions of globalization that can be affected by geopolitical risk.

03

International Trade Organizations

Bretton Woods Background

Following the Great Depression's disastrous policy mistakes (competitive devaluations, trade barriers, protectionism → global trade collapse + unemployment), 45 governments met in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire in July 1944 to build a framework for international economic cooperation. Result: creation of the IMF (formally Dec 1945) and World Bank.

The planned third institution — the International Trade Organization (ITO) — was never ratified. Instead, the GATT (General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade), signed October 1947 by 23 nations, governed international trade until the WTO replaced it on 1 January 1995.

IMF
Founded: Dec 1945 · Bretton Woods
  • Mandate: Stability of the international monetary system (exchange rates + international payments)
  • Lends foreign exchange to members with balance of payments problems
  • Only under strict conditions; monitors borrowers' macroeconomic policies
  • Provides forum for cooperation on monetary problems
  • Promotes exchange rate stability and open payments system
  • Surveillance of global/regional/country economies
  • Post-2008: enhanced lending, improved monitoring, resolving global imbalances
  • Examples: Greek sovereign debt crisis (€110bn bailout 2010); East Asian Financial Crisis (Thailand 1997 currency peg collapse)
World Bank
Founded: 1944 (Bretton Woods) · Operates via IBRD + IDA
  • Mandate: Help developing countries fight poverty and enhance environmentally sound economic growth
  • IBRD: AAA-rated bonds; market-based lending to developing countries at low interest
  • IDA: World's largest source of interest-free loans and grants to the poorest countries. Replenished every 3 years by 40 donor countries.
  • Neither IBRD nor IDA operates for profit
  • Also provides analysis, advice, and capacity building
  • Investment perspective: creates basic economic infrastructure for domestic financial markets in developing countries
WTO
Founded: 1 Jan 1995 · Replaced GATT (1947)
  • Mandate: Legal and institutional foundation of the multinational trading system
  • Only international organization regulating cross-border trade globally
  • Covers ~60 agreements, annexes, decisions (GATT for goods + GATS for services + TRIPS for IP)
  • Implements, administers, and operates individual trade agreements
  • Platform for negotiations and dispute settlement
  • Reviews members' trade policies (surveillance)
  • Technical cooperation for developing countries
  • China joined WTO: Dec 2001 — landmark event
  • Doha Round (2001): ambitious but no final agreement reached → led to more bilateral deals
OrganizationPrimary FocusKey ToolWho Benefits Most
IMF Monetary system stability; exchange rates; BoP crises Conditional emergency lending; economic surveillance Countries with balance of payments crises
World Bank Poverty reduction; sustainable development Low/no-interest loans (IBRD/IDA); grants; technical assistance Developing and poorest countries
WTO Rules-based multilateral trade Trade agreements; tariff harmonization; dispute settlement All trading countries; especially multinational corporations
🧠 Mnemonic — "IMF = Monetary stability; World Bank = Development loans; WTO = Trade rules"

Quick memory check: IMF → International Monetary stability · World Bank → Welfare/development · WTO → World trade rules and disputes. All three work together but with distinct, non-overlapping mandates.

04

Archetypes of Country Behavior

Two-Axis Framework

Countries are plotted on two axes: Cooperation ↔ Non-Cooperation (political) and Globalization ↔ Nationalism (economic). This creates four archetypes. Countries rarely sit at pure extremes — they exist on spectrums and can move over time.

Key insight for risk analysis: It matters not only which quadrant a country is in today but also its direction of movement and stability within that quadrant. A hegemon moving toward cooperation is less of a risk than a multilateral actor breaking away.

Hegemony
Non-Cooperative + Globalized
  • Regional/global leaders using political/economic influence to control resources
  • State-owned enterprises control key export markets
  • Benefits: political dominance + stabilizing force on others who align
  • Risk: as hegemons gain/lose influence → may become more competitive
  • Examples: Russia (natural gas leverage over Europe), China (technology transfer requirements)
Multilateralism
Cooperative + Globalized
  • Mutually beneficial trade relationships; extensive rules harmonization
  • Private firms fully integrated into global supply chains with multiple partners
  • Benefits: higher growth rates from global cooperation
  • Risk: vulnerable to geopolitical risks of partner countries (interdependence)
  • Examples: Germany, Singapore, European Union, Japan (now)
Autarky
Non-Cooperative + Nationalist
  • Political self-sufficiency; little or no external trade or finance
  • State-owned enterprises control strategic domestic industries
  • Benefits: complete control over technology, goods, services, media
  • Risk: economic and political isolation; poverty; lack of innovation
  • Examples: North Korea (ongoing), Venezuela (sanctions-driven), China (much of 20th century)
Bilateralism
Cooperative + Nationalist
  • One-at-a-time agreements with individual countries; no multilateral treaties
  • May cooperate with many countries but separately
  • Regionalism = mid-point: group of countries cooperating, possibly excluding others
  • Moving toward cooperation organically leads to globalization
  • Example: Japan (historically); today considered multilateral
💡 Exam Tip — Interdependence Paradox

Countries that are more cooperative and globalized (multilateral) are: (1) less likely to be targeted by geopolitical attacks because the cost to partners is high, but (2) more vulnerable when risks do occur because they have more touchpoints across borders. Greater interconnection = greater risk exposure.

Tools of Geopolitics

Overview: Three Tool Categories

Geopolitical tools may be cooperative (increase flows: goods, services, capital, labor) or non-cooperative/conflictual (reduce these flows). Tracking which tools actors use signals their movement within the cooperation-globalization framework.

Tool TypeCooperative ExamplesNon-Cooperative Examples
National Security Military alliances (NATO), collective security agreements, peacekeeping missions Armed conflict (most extreme), espionage, terrorism, military coercion
Economic Multilateral trade agreements (WTO, MERCOSUR), common market (EU), common currency (euro), tariff harmonization Nationalization of industries, tariffs & quotas, voluntary export restraints (VER), domestic content requirements, export subsidies
Financial Free currency exchange across borders, allowing foreign investment, economic and monetary unions (EMU) Limiting access to local currency markets, restricting foreign investment, economic & financial sanctions, capital controls

National Security Tools — Key Points

Armed conflict: Most extreme non-cooperative tool. Two impacts: (1) Destruction of physical infrastructure → long-term damage to capital stock (e.g., Syria: economy halved by 2016 from 2010 levels). (2) Migration/refugee flows → reshapes labor supply, capital flows, neighboring countries (e.g., Syria → Germany: 1 million refugees; long-term demographic and growth benefits for Germany).

Espionage: Indirect national security tool — obtaining political/military information covertly.

NATO: Cooperative security alliance (EU, US, UK, Canada). Originally formed against Soviet Union; now focuses on nuclear non-proliferation and collective security.

Economic Tools — Key Points

Nationalization: Transferring industry from private to state control. Most common in sectors vital to economic security (energy). Example: Argentina renationalized YPF (2012) to control fossil fuel revenue and foreign exchange.

Voluntary Export Restraints (VER): Country voluntarily limits its own exports — a form of non-cooperation.

Domestic Content Requirements: Requires a certain proportion of domestic input in exported goods — protectionist.

Export subsidies: Government supports domestic companies competing internationally (e.g., Boeing vs. Airbus dispute).

Financial Tools — Key Points

Sanctions: Targeted restrictions on financial transactions, asset freezes, oil embargoes. Used as non-cooperative leverage (e.g., US/EU sanctions on Venezuela, Russia).

US dollar dominance: International interbank market heavily USD-denominated. Cooperative benefit: liquidity and facilitation of global trade. Risk: countries without USD reserves become vulnerable to US monetary policy tightening → liquidity shortages.

🧠 Mnemonic — "NEF" = National Security, Economic, Financial (3 Tool Types)

For each type, ask: Does this tool increase or decrease flows? Cooperative = increases flows (trade, capital, people, info). Non-cooperative = decreases flows. This is the key organizing principle for the exam.

06

Types of Geopolitical Risk

Event Risk
  • Revolves around set dates known in advance
  • Elections, legislation, referenda, political anniversaries
  • Risk: outcome may diverge from consensus expectation
  • Predictability gives investors time to prepare — does NOT reduce likelihood, velocity, or impact
  • Example: Brexit referendum (23 June 2016) — planned; "Leave" result surprised markets → immediate GBP decline, equity volatility, long-term structural disruption
Exogenous Risk
  • Sudden, unanticipated risk — cannot be planned for
  • Affects cooperative stance, ability to globalize, or both
  • Includes "black swan" events — rare, difficult to predict, high impact
  • Usually high-velocity (immediate market impact)
  • Examples: Japan earthquake/tsunami (March 2011) → equities fell 20.4%; COVID-19 pandemic → S&P 500 fell ~34% (Feb–Mar 2020); sudden uprisings, invasions, natural disasters
Thematic Risk
  • Known risks that evolve and expand over time
  • Low-velocity, long-term impact
  • Difficult to act on because gradual development makes prioritization hard
  • Examples: Climate change, pattern migration, rise of populism, terrorism, cyber threats (Equifax breach 2017: −34.9% in 1 week; broader industry cybersecurity cost increases)

Assessing Geopolitical Risk — Three Dimensions

DimensionDefinitionKey Considerations
Likelihood Probability the risk will occur More art than science. Collaborative/globalized countries: lower likelihood but more vulnerable when risk occurs. Internal stability, economic need, and leadership motivations increase likelihood.
Velocity Speed at which risk affects investor portfolios High velocity (short-term): Black swan/exogenous events → market volatility, flight to quality. Medium-term: Company costs/valuations impaired. Low velocity (long-term): ESG impacts, structural changes to asset allocation. Note: some risks have BOTH speeds (Brexit: immediate GBP fall + long-term structural damage).
Size & Nature of Impact Magnitude and breadth of the risk's effect on portfolios Discrete impact: One company/sector affected. Broad impact: Entire sector, country, or global economy. Impact is amplified during economic downturns. High-impact risks merit extensive analysis; low-impact risks may not.
💡 The Three Dimensions Together

Never assess likelihood, velocity, or impact in isolation — they must be considered jointly. A high-likelihood risk with low impact may not merit action. A low-likelihood risk with catastrophic impact warrants scenario planning even without regular monitoring. The combination determines portfolio action priority.

07

Geopolitical Risk & the Investment Process

How Geopolitical Risk Manifests in Portfolios

VelocityMarket ImpactAsset Classes AffectedInvestor Action
High (Short-term) Immediate asset price volatility; "flight to quality" (buy safe assets, sell risky assets) Commodities, FX, equities, government bonds (yields fall) Tactical portfolio changes; hedging; potential buying opportunity
Medium-term Impaired company processes, costs, and investment opportunities → lower valuations. Sector-specific effects. Equities of specific sectors; corporate bonds Sector rotation; revaluation of affected companies
Low (Long-term) Structural impacts on economic growth, revenue, supply chains; ESG concerns; higher required discount rates Asset allocation across asset classes and geographies; investment styles Strategic asset allocation shift; reduce geographic exposure; long-horizon positioning

Key Investment Consequences

Higher discount rate / risk premium: For countries/sectors perceived to be at consistent geopolitical risk, investors require higher compensation → higher discount rate → lower asset valuations. This is why EM/frontier market assets trade at a discount to developed markets.

Flight to quality: During high-velocity events, investors sell risky assets (equities, EM currencies, commodities) and buy safe assets (US Treasuries, gold, CHF, JPY). US 10-yr yield fell ~68% (1.57% → 0.54%) during COVID March 2020 selloff.

Comparative advantage disruption: Geopolitical tools can tilt comparative advantage — labor and capital flow away from high-risk regions → lower growth, higher required returns.

Scenario Analysis & Signposting

Scenario Analysis
  • Evaluate portfolio outcomes across potential states of the world
  • Start with base case → upside and downside scenarios
  • Can be qualitative or quantitative (stylized factor sensitivity)
  • Risk: groupthink — teams must build creative, diverse processes
  • Goal: strengthen conviction and enable opportune investment action
Signposting (Traffic Light System)
  • Green = Low likelihood/velocity/impact → no action needed
  • Amber = Medium → higher caution and preparedness
  • Red = High → action plan necessary
  • Key rule: distinguish politics (signposts) from policy (actual portfolio drivers). Policy changes create durable impacts; political noise often does not.
  • Example: Brexit signposts went green → amber (referendum announced 2015) → red (polls showed Leave majority, May 2016)

Acting on Geopolitical Risk

Top-down (asset allocation): Adjust geographic exposures based on likelihood, velocity, and impact. Countries with consistent multilateral history → more reliable investments, higher capital flows. Countries with consistent military threat → lower growth potential, reduced allocation.

Bottom-up (portfolio management): Use geopolitical risk as a factor in multifactor models. Highly diversified production → higher likelihood exposure but lower impact (multiple production alternatives). Use as binary yes/no factor or adjust confidence intervals in other factors.

Investor-specific application: Low risk tolerance → reduce geopolitical exposure through low-volatility choices or hedging. Long time horizon → exogenous shocks may be buying opportunities. Short time horizon / near retirement → same shock could critically impair terminal portfolio value.

⚠ Key Exam Point — Politics vs. Policy

Big political differences between leaders don't automatically create large investment impacts. What matters is the policies enacted, not the political rhetoric. Analysts who focus on policy changes (trade rules, capital controls, sanctions, regulations) make better investment decisions than those distracted by political theater.
