Standard I: Professionalism — Applied Vignettes

Ten vignettes cover I(A) through I(E). The exam consistently tests edge cases: when dissociation is required vs. escalation, what counts as a misrepresentation by omission, where personal conduct crosses into professional misconduct, and the nuances of the new I(E) Competence standard.

Mandracken
I(A) Knowledge of the LawInadequate Remedy — Must Dissociate
SBS Bank charges custody clients a SWIFT messaging fee higher than actual cost — adding a hidden margin and misrepresenting it as a pass-through. Mandracken (CFA) discovers this, reports to his supervisor. The supervisor's fix: lower rates for new clients only; revisit existing clients at renewal only. No remediation for past overcharges.
What must Mandracken do?
✅
CORRECT: Refuse to participate in any client interactions using the current fee schedule until the bank revises SWIFT fees to reflect actual costs for all clients — past, present, and new. The supervisor's fix is inadequate on three counts: (1) new clients only, (2) existing clients only at renewal, (3) no remedy for past clients.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Implement the supervisor's fix": Complying with an inadequate partial remedy while continuing to interact with clients under a fraudulent fee schedule = continuing participation in the violation. Under I(A), inaction and continued association = participation. Trap B — "Report to the board of directors": This may eventually be necessary but is premature. First: dissociate. Then: escalate through compliance. Only if those fail does escalating to the board or regulators become appropriate. Cannot skip straight to the nuclear option.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Once you identify a violation and escalate it, if the proposed remedy is inadequate, you must dissociate from the activity — not go along with a partial fix. Adequacy of the remedy matters. A partial correction that does not address all affected parties = still a violation you must dissociate from.
Pellie
I(A) Knowledge of the LawAML / Wilful Blindness
Pellie (CFA, CEO) approves suspicious AML reports without inquiry because the client has a longstanding relationship. The account shows hundreds of transactions ($90M in / $84M out), very few actual investments, transfers to money-laundering-risk jurisdictions, and vague descriptions ("consulting fees"). AML review is required by law.
Are Pellie's actions a violation?
✅
YES — violation of I(A). I(A) requires compliance with ALL applicable laws, including AML regulations. Rubber-stamping required AML reports without review — justified by personal relationships — violates I(A). The transaction pattern should have triggered deep investigation, not blanket approval.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Protecting client confidentiality": III(E) confidentiality does not protect illegal client activity. That exception is explicit in the standard. Confidentiality cannot be weaponised to shield clients who may be breaking the law. Trap C — "Clearing firm will handle it": KIG's own AML obligation cannot be delegated to a third party. Legal obligations cannot be outsourced.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Wilful blindness = violation. Approving compliance reports without conducting any review is not compliance — it is theatre. Personal relationships cannot override legal duties. And III(E) confidentiality never shields illegal client conduct.
Mwangi
I(A) Knowledge of the LawForgery — Even With Client Approval
Clients need to sign new documents to switch insurance products. Documents aren't ready; later Mwangi can't reach clients. Manager threatens cancellation causing overdraft. To prevent harm, Mwangi signs documents on clients' behalf.
Are Mwangi's actions a violation?
✅
YES — violation of I(A). Signing documents on clients' behalf = forgery, which is illegal regardless of intent, motive, or client consent. The law requires genuine client signatures — no exception for good intentions or practical difficulty.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Clients gave permission for the exchange": General approval of the transaction ≠ meeting the legal requirement for signatures. Two separate things. Trap C — "Clients explicitly gave permission to sign on their behalf": Even explicit client permission cannot override a statutory signature requirement. The law protects clients whether or not they want that protection — clients cannot waive statutory requirements by consenting to the adviser.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Good intentions and client welfare do not override legal requirements. Forging signatures — even with full client knowledge and approval — is still forgery and still a violation. The "I was helping them" defence fails every time under I(A).
Myers
I(B) Independence & ObjectivityPolitical Donation / Pay-to-Play / Mixed Motives
Myers (CFA, hedge fund partner) wants to donate $10,000 to candidate DeFrietas — genuinely for environmental reasons, but also because DeFrietas could influence state pension fund allocations to Corboba.
What should Myers do?
✅
CORRECT: Refrain from donating. Myers has mixed motivations — one legitimate, one that violates I(B). I(B) prohibits offering gifts/benefits that could reasonably compromise another's independence and objectivity. A donation intended partly to influence a hiring decision = prohibited pay-to-play regardless of the co-existing legitimate reason.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Personal funds and insignificant amount": Source of funds is irrelevant if the intent is to influence. And $10,000 may be significant in the context of the campaign. The test is whether the donation could reasonably compromise independence — Myers himself acknowledged the business angle. Trap C — "Legitimate environmental reasons justify it": Having a legitimate reason does not neutralise a prohibited reason. Both motivations exist simultaneously. The improper motive taints the action even when a proper motive also exists.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Mixed motives: when a member has both legitimate and improper motivations, the improper motivation prevails for I(B) purposes. You cannot net off good intent against bad intent. Political donations intended to gain business = explicitly prohibited pay-to-play under I(B).
Lee
I(C) MisrepresentationGuaranteed Returns on Volatile Investments
Lee (CFA) advises a couple to move $125,000 retirement funds into AKC large-cap equity fund, incurring a $30,000 penalty. She tells them they will "make up the loss with better investment returns." AKC compensates planners based on products sold.
Are Lee's actions a violation?
✅
YES — violation of I(C). Promising specific returns on volatile equity investments = misrepresentation. Future equity returns are inherently uncertain. Note also: I(B) concerns (compensation scheme influencing recommendation) and III(C) suitability concerns (is a $30,000 penalty hit in the couple's best interest?)
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Acceptable if product is suitable": Suitability is separately required but insufficient. A suitable product can still be recommended through misrepresentation. Trap C — "Full disclosure of penalty makes it acceptable": Disclosing one negative (the penalty) does not neutralise a separate misrepresentation (the guaranteed return claim). Two separate issues — one does not cure the other.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Guaranteeing returns = misrepresentation, always. Equity returns cannot be guaranteed; any statement that they "will" achieve a specific outcome violates I(C). The presence of legitimate disclosures elsewhere doesn't cure a discrete misrepresentation.
Andersen
I(C) MisrepresentationOmission — Key Personnel Departure
Andersen (CFA, CEO) submits a pension fund proposal including a key personnel list. Before the decision, one of those listed key personnel — who participated in the in-person presentation — leaves the firm.
What should Andersen do?
✅
CORRECT: Immediately inform the pension plan. The pension plan explicitly requested key personnel details — that information is material to its hiring decision. Withholding the departure = misrepresentation by omission. Must disclose now, not after finding a replacement.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Hiring the firm, not the individual": The plan's own behaviour (requesting a personnel list) demonstrates individuals matter. Andersen cannot override the client's stated priorities. Trap C — "Hire replacement first, then inform": The plan needs this information during its decision process — not after Andersen resolves it internally. Timing is the issue: immediate disclosure required.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Misrepresentation by omission is as prohibited as active misstatement. When material previously-disclosed information changes, and the counterparty clearly considers it material, you must immediately update them. "I'll fix it internally first" is not acceptable.
Brodeur
I(C) MisrepresentationSocial Media / "Secure Funding" Was a Joke
Brodeur (CFA, CEO of LeTour) posts on social media: "Considering taking LeTour private at $420/share. Funding is secure." Reality: no deal agreed, $420 was "a joke," no advisers retained, only a vague general conversation with a sovereign wealth fund. Stock jumps 6%.
Are Brodeur's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of I(C). The post contradicted known facts: no SWF agreement, no price discussed, price was a joke, no advisers retained. Brodeur knew or should have known his statements were false and misleading.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Social media disadvantages some investors": Social media CAN be a valid disclosure channel — it doesn't need to reach every investor. The problem here is the content being false, not the channel. Trap C — "Only 'considering' — speculative/nonmaterial": Source (CEO) + subject matter (major transaction) + investor relevance = material. "Secure funding" when no deal exists is not speculative — the CEO knows the facts and directly contradicted them.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Social media is a valid disclosure platform but honesty requirements are identical to any other channel. Pre-disclosed CEO social media accounts are formal communication channels — false statements violate I(C) regardless of the informal medium.
Hanse
I(D) Misconduct — NOT a ViolationCivil Disobedience / Personal Beliefs
Hanse (CFA, ESG portfolio manager) is arrested multiple times at Extinction Rebellion protests in the financial district, convicted of minor offenses (unlawful assembly, disorderly conduct). Employment contract allows termination for criminal conviction.
Do Hanse's actions violate the Code?
✅
NO — does NOT violate the Code and Standards. I(D) prohibits acts involving dishonesty, fraud, or deceit, OR acts that reflect adversely on professional reputation. Civil disobedience for personal beliefs generally involves neither dishonesty/fraud nor professional reflection. Location in the financial district alone is insufficient to link personal conduct to professional activity.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Violated employment contract = violated Code": Employment contract violations and Code violations are separate. The Code is not violated every time an employer's contract is breached — only if the conduct relates to the specific behaviours the Code addresses. Trap C — "Arrested in financial district = professional context": Geographic proximity to financial institutions doesn't make personal conduct professional. No dishonesty, fraud, or deceit was involved.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
I(D) is not a blanket "any conviction = violation" rule. The conduct must involve dishonesty, fraud, or deceit, OR must reflect adversely on professional competence/integrity. Civil disobedience for personal beliefs = neither. This is the clearest illustration of the personal conduct line.
Mang
I(D) MisconductFraudulent "Error Correction" / Deceiving Clients About Performance
Mang (CFA) uses the firm's 30-day error correction policy fraudulently: identifies already-risen securities, falsely claims he forgot to buy them at the lower price, uses the error account to cover the difference, nets a guaranteed profit for clients, calls it a "gift" or "no-risk trade." No actual error was made.
Are Mang's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of I(D) Misconduct. Mang is engaging in professional conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, and deceit — misusing a legitimate policy to create fictitious trades and deceiving clients about the nature of his investment performance.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Acting in clients' benefit": Client benefit does not justify fraudulent means. Lying about trades and deceiving clients about your actual performance = I(D) violation regardless of favourable client outcomes. Trap C — "Bank not harmed": Financial harm to the employer is not a prerequisite for an I(D) violation. The conduct is independently dishonest and deceptive — harm to any specific party is irrelevant.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Beneficial outcomes do not cleanse fraudulent processes. Deceiving clients about the nature of your performance (hiding that wins are engineered, not earned) is itself a violation. No-harm-no-foul does not apply — the conduct is the violation, not its consequences.
Mahajan
I(E) CompetenceNew Role — Covered Sectors But Not Supervisory Responsibility
Mahajan (CFA, research analyst) moves to a firm covering new sectors (transportation, construction) AND takes on first-time supervisory responsibility. He studies extensively on new sectors but takes no steps to develop supervisory skills.
Does Mahajan comply with I(E)?
✅
NO — fails I(E) on the supervisory dimension. Mahajan addresses sector competence but ignores supervisory competence. I(E) requires competence for ALL responsibilities of the new role. He needed to develop supervisory skills (management training, compliance credentials, internal training) before assuming that responsibility.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Sufficient because he took courses on new sectors": Sector competence ≠ total role competence. He missed an entire dimension. Trap B — "Fails because no formal CE programme": I(E) does NOT require a specific formal CE programme. The failure is the absence of any effort to develop supervisory competence — not the absence of a formal programme specifically.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Competence must be role-specific and comprehensive. When a role expands, ALL new responsibilities require competence development — not just the comfortable ones. I(E) does not specify how competence is demonstrated; it requires that it IS demonstrated for everything the role demands.

Standard II: Integrity of Capital Markets — Applied Vignettes

Khatri
II(A) Material Nonpublic InfoOverheard MNPI / Public Setting ≠ Public Info
Khatri (CFA candidate) overhears his brother-in-law Patel (attorney on a ZP acquisition) on the phone at a cricket tournament, hears "GIT" repeatedly, observes Patel and the ZP owner in private huddles. Deduces an acquisition is imminent. Buys 5,000 more ZP shares. ZP is acquired at a 30% premium one week later.
Are Khatri's actions a violation?
✅
YES — violation of II(A). Khatri came into possession of MNPI by overhearing confidential conversations and observing private interactions. Patel's carelessness does not give Khatri permission to use what he overheard. The information was confidential, nonpublic, and material — he knew or should have known this.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Own speculation": The required deduction was minimal given his inside knowledge of the situation. Mosaic theory applies to public or non-material nonpublic info combined with public research — not to deductions from clearly confidential communications he overheard. Trap C — "Information in a public environment": Physical location does not determine whether information is public. Information is nonpublic until disseminated to the marketplace — being overheard at a cricket match does not make it public.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Public location ≠ public information. MNPI can be received in a public setting — it's the nature of the information, not the location of receipt, that matters. Mosaic theory fails when any piece of the deduction chain is itself material and nonpublic.
Kwame
II(A) Material Nonpublic InfoSelective Disclosure to Analysts / Meeting Tenor Is Material
Kwame (CFA, CFO of PH3D biotech) emails select sell-side analysts that the regulatory meeting was "very positive and productive" and the company was "pleasantly surprised" by the reaction. Does not share the actual preliminary test data. Stock rises 19%.
Are Kwame's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of II(A). Kwame selectively disclosed material nonpublic information to select analysts. The meeting's positive outcome is material (it moved the stock 19%) and nonpublic (shared only with analysts, not the general market). Correct action: public announcement first, then discuss with analysts.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Didn't share the test data": Sharing the positive tenor of the meeting IS material information — investors would want to know the meeting outcome even without the specific data. Materiality doesn't require sharing the most sensitive detail. Trap C — "Only responding to questions": The obligation not to selectively disclose applies regardless of whether communication was initiated by Kwame or the analysts. Being "just responsive" is not a defence.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Selective disclosure to a small group = MNPI violation even if the discloser doesn't initiate it and even if the most sensitive data wasn't shared. The meeting's outcome is itself material. Fix: public announcement first, then discuss with analysts. The channel must reach the general market.
Abbha
II(B) Market ManipulationFake Shareholders / Subsequent Good Performance Irrelevant
Abbha (CFA) helps list Superior Energy by filing 31 fake shareholders with false names and addresses to meet the exchange's 300-shareholder minimum. Company later performs well and early investors make money.
Do Abbha's actions violate the Code?
✅
YES — violation of II(B). Abbha engaged in information-based manipulation — inflating apparent shareholder count to create a false impression of investor interest and liquidity. This misled the exchange and prospective investors about the true level of market interest, enabling a listing that otherwise would not have qualified.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Company proved to be strong": Outcome does not retroactively legitimise a manipulation scheme. The manipulation occurred at the point of the false filing — subsequent performance is irrelevant. Trap C — "No investors were harmed": Harm to investors is not a required element of II(B). The prohibition is on the manipulative practice itself — harm to any party is irrelevant to whether the violation occurred.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Manipulation via false information (not just false trades) = II(B) violation. The outcome-doesn't-matter rule applies: no harm, no financial loss, and subsequent good performance are all irrelevant. The act of creating false impressions is the violation.

Standard III: Duties to Clients — Applied Vignettes

Maste
III(A) Loyalty, Prudence, CareDisclosure Cannot Waive Fiduciary Duty
Maste (CFA) creates a "Client Protection Policy" that: (1) excuses Dov from liability for failures to act in clients' best interest, (2) removes the obligation to conduct suitability analyses, (3) misleads clients into thinking they cannot make legal claims against the firm.
Are Maste's actions a violation?
✅
YES — multiple violations. The policy attempts to use disclosure to strip clients of fundamental protections. Disclosure cannot waive core fiduciary obligations. Violations: III(A) (waiving loyalty duty), V(A) (waiving suitability), III(B) (removing fair dealing), I(C) (misrepresenting clients' legal rights).
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Fully disclosed terms = acceptable": Disclosure is powerful but cannot be weaponised to strip clients of fundamental protections. There are rights clients cannot be made to waive regardless of disclosure. Trap C — "Free to negotiate advisory agreement terms": Contract freedom has limits. You cannot negotiate away fundamental ethical obligations — loyalty, prudence, and care — through disclosure in the client agreement. The Code sets a floor below which terms cannot go.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Disclosure is not a magic wand. Core fiduciary duties cannot be contracted away through any mechanism — disclosure, consent, or waiver. Clients must be protected even from their own agreements when those agreements waive non-waivable obligations.
Gaini
III(A) — NOT a ViolationExecution-Only Account / Contractual Liquidation
Laube has a self-directed FX account — Gaini executes but does NOT advise. Client agreement permits Gaini to liquidate all positions without notice if margin is deficient. While Laube is on vacation, pending orders execute, margin requirements exceed account balance. Gaini liquidates all positions per the contract — realising a $37,000 loss.
Does Gaini violate the Code?
✅
NO — Gaini's actions are appropriate. The relationship is execution-only (no advisory role). The liquidation policy was clearly disclosed in the client agreement. Gaini acted per the contract to protect market integrity. The duty of loyalty is defined by the nature of the relationship — not by a general obligation to maximise client welfare in all circumstances.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Duty to protect Laube's financial position": In an execution-only account, Gaini is NOT an investment adviser and is NOT obligated to actively monitor or protect her investment interests. Different relationship = different duty. Trap C — "Should have liquidated only one position": The contract explicitly authorised full liquidation at Gaini's discretion. A less drastic alternative is not required if the contract doesn't mandate it.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
The scope of III(A) duty depends entirely on the nature of the relationship. Execution-only has different obligations than advisory. A clearly disclosed, negotiated contractual liquidation provision is valid — it is not a waiver of fiduciary duty; it is the definition of a legitimately negotiated relationship.
Braung
III(A) Loyalty — Expense BillingWeekend Travel May Be OK / Entertainment Never OK
Braung (CFA) advises a municipality on bond issuance. He books weekend travel to save on fares, brings his wife. They attend sporting events and theater. He also makes travel changes due to other client meetings. Which expenses can be billed to the municipality?
Which expenses are billable?
✅
CORRECT: Only weekend accommodation and meals — IF the total weekend travel cost is less than equivalent weekday travel. Weekend travel saves on flights — the net cost to the client may be lower even with extra hotel/meal nights. This serves the client's interest by minimising total costs.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Entertainment tickets at reasonable amounts": Theater and sporting events for Braung and his wife = personal entertainment, not business expenses. Charging these to the client = deceptive and unfair under III(A) — no connection to a business meeting with the client. Trap C — "Travel change fees from other clients": Costs from changes made to accommodate other clients' schedules = Braung's own overhead. The municipality did not cause those changes and should not bear the cost.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
III(A) loyalty in expense billing: only charge clients for costs genuinely incurred for their benefit. Weekend stay can be justified if it produces a lower total cost to the client. Personal expenses — spouse travel, entertainment, other client's change fees — cannot be passed to this client.
Scherzer
III(B) Fair Dealing — NOT a ViolationPremium Tier Service / Fair ≠ Equal
Scherzer (CFA) sends monthly recommendation reports to all clients simultaneously. She also offers a weekly report (with contextual info that may implicitly signal changes) for a $1,000 annual subscription — available to any client. Monthly report emailed to all clients on Friday; posted publicly on Monday.
What is Scherzer required to do?
✅
CORRECT: Nothing — Scherzer's actions comply. III(B) requires simultaneous dissemination of recommendation changes — she does this. Offering a premium weekly subscription is permissible as long as: all clients are eligible to subscribe, it is disclosed, and no actual recommendation changes occur in the weekly report that disadvantage non-subscribers.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Must post monthly report publicly at the same time as emailing clients": The Code does not require simultaneous public website posting — clients receive it simultaneously, which is what matters. Trap C — "Must send weekly reports to all clients free": Premium service tiers are explicitly permitted. The key constraint: no actual recommendation changes in the premium report before they appear in the free report. Contextual analysis that implicitly signals future changes = acceptable.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Fair ≠ equal. Premium tiers are allowed. The hard line: premium subscribers cannot get a trading advantage from actual recommendation changes before other clients. Simultaneous dissemination of actual changes is the rule — additional contextual analysis in premium tiers is fine.
Marte
III(C) SuitabilityTax Benefits Don't Override Suitability + Disclosure Doesn't Cure It
Marte (CFA) offers clients in Puerto Rico shares in a leveraged (up to 50% of assets) closed-end fund with 67%+ local securities. Clients have modest net worth and conservative/moderate objectives. Marte puts 85%+ of their assets in this fund.
Are Marte's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of III(C). High concentration (85%+) + internal leverage in a single illiquid fund = unsuitable for conservative/moderate investors. Leverage amplifies downside; concentration eliminates diversification; one market event could devastate accounts. Tax benefits are one factor — they do not override a fundamentally unsuitable risk structure.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Tax benefits justify it": Tax efficiency is one factor, not a trump card. Some allocation to a tax-advantaged fund might be suitable; 85%+ with leverage and concentration is not. Trap C — "Full disclosure of risks = acceptable": Disclosure does not cure unsuitability. If the investment is objectively unsuitable, no amount of disclosure makes III(C) compliant. Disclosure and suitability are separate, independent requirements.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Suitability is assessed in total portfolio context: concentration + leverage + illiquidity + client risk profile considered together. One attractive feature (tax benefit) cannot overcome fundamentally unsuitable risk characteristics. Disclosure never cures unsuitability.
Duri
III(C) Suitability + III(A) LoyaltyClient Direction Does Not Waive Suitability + Self-Referral Conflict
Duri (CFA) helps clients move retirement funds into SMSFs investing in direct property — without conducting suitability analysis, just accepting clients' stated reasons. She reclassifies them as "growth" investors and recommends her own accounting firm Tabak prepare their annual accounts.
Are Duri's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — multiple violations. III(C): Duri must conduct proper suitability analysis even when clients request a change — client direction does not waive the obligation. Reclassifying clients as "growth" to superficially justify the investment is manipulating the suitability criteria rather than honestly assessing them. III(A): recommending her own firm Tabak creates an undisclosed conflict of interest.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Following client directives": Clients can request changes but advisers must still assess suitability. Accepting requests without analysis = failing III(C). Reclassifying the client's objectives to match the investment (rather than vice versa) is a backwards approach explicitly prohibited by the standard. Trap C — "Acceptable if Tabak's services are competitive": Cost and quality don't eliminate the conflict. The self-referral conflict must be disclosed regardless of service quality.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Client requests do not waive the suitability obligation. When a client wants to change strategy, the adviser must conduct full independent analysis. Reverse-engineering suitability classifications to fit a chosen investment = exactly what the standard prohibits. Self-referrals without disclosure = III(A) conflict of interest violation.
Jergenn
III(D) Performance PresentationAccurate But Incomplete = Still a Violation
Jergenn (CFA) presents 2014-2020 performance history in 2022 marketing materials — accurate and correctly calculated. But the history is from a prior composite of separate accounts; the CIS fund itself only started in 2021. No disclosure of this distinction is made.
Are Jergenn's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of III(D). Performance must be fair, accurate, AND complete. The numbers are accurate but presenting 2014-2020 history without disclosing it's from a different entity gives the false impression the CIS fund has a long track record. The omission makes an accurate presentation misleading.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Performance is accurate": Accuracy is necessary but not sufficient. III(D) requires fair AND complete — not merely technically correct. Accurate data in a misleading context = violation. Trap C — "Acceptable if net of fees": Fee presentation is a separate issue — it has nothing to do with whether the historical data's source entity is properly disclosed.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Technically accurate ≠ compliant with III(D). All three conditions required simultaneously: fair + accurate + complete. Predecessor performance histories must be clearly labelled — presenting them without disclosure implies a track record the current entity doesn't actually have.
Giddings & Marsh
III(E) ConfidentialityBoth Violated — Policy Existence ≠ Adequate Compliance
Marsh (CFA) downloads client data to his personal home server without permission — server is hacked, client data posted for sale online. Giddings (CFA, compliance officer) had policies in place but had not audited or tested their effectiveness. Marsh was able to download company files despite the policies.
Who violated the Code?
✅
BOTH Marsh AND Giddings violated the Code. Marsh: misappropriated client data to personal server = III(E) violation. His misconduct facilitated the breach even though he didn't cause the hack. Giddings: compliance policies were insufficient — Marsh was able to download files. Giddings had a duty to implement AND audit/test safeguard effectiveness. Having policies on paper that don't work = inadequate compliance.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Only Marsh violated": Giddings also violated as compliance officer by failing to audit whether policies actually worked. Trap B — "Only Giddings violated": Marsh is primarily at fault — he downloaded data to his personal server. Both violated.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
III(E) responsibility runs in both directions. Employees must follow confidentiality policies; compliance officers must ensure policies actually work (not just exist). Policy existence ≠ adequate implementation. Regular auditing and testing of safeguard effectiveness is part of the compliance officer's obligation.

Standard IV: Duties to Employers — Applied Vignettes

Nickoli
IV(A) Loyalty to EmployerDisparaging Employer / Pre-Resignation Client Warming
Nickoli (CFA) decides to leave HHI weeks before resigning. Before submitting her resignation, she tells clients she is leaving, shares negative views about HHI (leadership, direction, employees leaving), promotes Vesuvius, identifies clients interested in switching. She shares those clients' names with Vesuvius the day after resigning, then solicits them.
Is Nickoli's conduct appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of IV(A). Two violations: (1) Disparaging HHI to clients while still employed = harming employer. (2) Using final weeks to identify and warm up clients for Vesuvius = leveraging employment relationship for a competitor while still being paid by HHI — breach of loyalty even though actual solicitation started post-resignation.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Didn't actually solicit until after leaving": The violation isn't just in the solicitation — it's in the preparatory activities while still employed. Using employment time to warm up client defections = breach. Trap C — "Protecting clients' best interests": Even if Vesuvius is genuinely better for clients, a member cannot harm their current employer to serve those perceived client interests. Clients' relationship is with HHI, not with Nickoli personally.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Loyalty to employer continues until resignation is effective. Disparaging the employer to clients and warming up client defections while still employed both violate IV(A). You may inform clients you're leaving — you may NOT use that opportunity to undermine the employer or identify targets for your new firm.
Kuznetsov
IV(A) — Whistleblowing Protects Clients = NOT a ViolationClient Protection Overrides Employer Loyalty
Kuznetsov (CFA) stops selling his firm's underperforming, expensive products when he realises they harm clients. Supervisor pressures him to resume. He secretly records conversations, copies client records as evidence, files a regulator complaint, and is fired.
Are Kuznetsov's actions appropriate?
✅
YES — appropriate. When the employer conducts business contrary to clients' interests, IV(A) loyalty to employer is overridden by III(A) duty to clients. Reporting to regulators to protect clients = justifiable whistleblowing. Copying client records to give to regulators as evidence is also justified in this context. Note: recording conversations may violate local laws in some jurisdictions.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Copying client records violates III(E)": In this context, disclosing records to the regulator to expose harm to those clients is a justified exception — legally authorised disclosure to protect clients. Trap C — "Reporting to regulator violates IV(A) loyalty": Whistleblowing to protect clients from employer's unethical conduct is explicitly NOT a violation of IV(A). The hierarchy is real: Client > Employer.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Client interests (III) trump employer loyalty (IV) when the employer is actively harming clients. Whistleblowing to regulators to protect clients is justified and does not violate IV(A). When priorities conflict, the higher priority wins.
Clemence
IV(A) Loyalty — Taking Client Data on DepartureGood Intentions Don't Override Employer's Property Rights
Clemence (CFA) leaves DeLaurier on good terms, provides full handover. On her last day, she downloads a spreadsheet of all DeLaurier clients, prospects, and former clients (names, AUM, contact info) to her personal email. Intent: send a "thank you" note — not to compete with DeLaurier.
Is Clemence's conduct appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of IV(A). The client list is DeLaurier's property. Taking it — regardless of intent — violates the duty not to divulge confidential information or cause harm. She also took far more than her own clients' info — she took all of DeLaurier's clients, prospects, and former clients. Better path: ask DeLaurier's permission to send a farewell note before leaving.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Protecting clients' interests": Benign intent does not authorise taking proprietary data. Trap C — "Can contact personal friends through personal channels": Yes — she can contact people she knows through personal means (social media, memory). But she cannot use DeLaurier's property to facilitate even a benign farewell.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Client lists and databases are employer property. Taking them on departure = IV(A) violation regardless of intent. The correct approach: ask permission first. If granted, use only what's needed. If not granted, use personal channels for personal contacts only.
Estevez
IV(B) Additional CompensationIssuer-Paid Research / Written Employer Consent Required
Estevez (CFA) helps select which companies get BIR research coverage (issuer-paid model). Some companies offer her a personal bonus if they are selected for coverage. BIR does not know about these bonuses.
Are Estevez's actions acceptable?
✅
CORRECT: Acceptable only if BIR gives written approval. IV(B) requires written consent from the employer before accepting compensation that creates a conflict of interest. Estevez must disclose the bonus to BIR, BIR must consent in writing, and BIR must then disclose to report users both that the company paid Estevez and that it pays for research coverage.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Independence automatically compromised": Issuer-paid research is NOT automatically unethical. It is a legitimate model with proper safeguards — disclosure, separation of recommendations from payment, strict procedures. Trap B — "Acceptable if no MNPI used": Using MNPI is a separate issue (II(A)). Not using MNPI has nothing to do with whether IV(B) written consent is needed. Two separate obligations.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Issuer-paid research is not automatically a violation. Key safeguards: written employer consent, full disclosure of all compensation to report users. IV(B) written consent must come before accepting the bonus — verbal or implicit consent is insufficient.
Duhih
IV(C) Responsibilities of SupervisorsWork-From-Home / Occasional Check-Ins = Inadequate
Duhih (CFA, branch manager) allows Lewes (CFA) to work fully from home with no office access and only occasional check-ins. Unknown to Duhih: Lewes works for a competitor and secretly splits commissions from client accounts he helps open.
Has Duhih violated the Code?
✅
YES — violation of IV(C). Duhih's supervision was minimal and noticeably ineffective. Allowing an employee to work fully remotely with no office access and only occasional check-ins falls below "reasonable efforts to ensure compliance." Supervisors must detect AND prevent violations through effective compliance systems.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Acceptable if HQ conducts audits": HQ audits supplement but do not substitute for onsite supervisory oversight. The branch manager cannot outsource supervisory responsibility to HQ. Trap C — "Acceptable if RCG lacked adequate policies": If firm policies were inadequate, Duhih's obligation was to: tell management, help develop procedures, or decline supervisory responsibility. A lack of adequate tools is not an excuse for failing to supervise.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Minimal supervision = inadequate supervision. IV(C) requires reasonable efforts to detect AND prevent violations. "Occasional check-ins" for a remote employee with no office access doesn't meet that standard. HQ audits supplement — they do not replace — active supervisory oversight.
Sasha Denikin
IV(C) Supervisors — Cannot Accept Role Without AuthorityCCO With No Real Power Must Decline the Role
Sasha (CFA) is made CCO of Galak but has no real authority — his father Franz controls everything, blocks client contact, ignores compliance issues Sasha raises. Sasha has no experience. He raises issues repeatedly but cannot enforce anything. Eventually resigns.
Are Sasha's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of IV(C). Sasha accepted CCO responsibility knowing he had no authority, no experience, and no ability to enforce compliance. IV(C) requires that if you cannot exercise supervisory responsibility, you must decline in writing to accept the role until the firm enables proper supervision. Accepting the title without the authority = violating the standard.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Franz holds real power — can't be supervised by subordinate": Franz's status as founder does not exempt him from CCO oversight. A CCO has compliance authority over all employees including founders. Trap C — "Resigning when frustrated = acceptable": Resignation came too late. Sasha should have declined the CCO role initially — or demanded proper authority before accepting — rather than continuing in a role he was failing to discharge for months.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
If you cannot exercise supervisory responsibility due to inadequate systems, lack of authority, or lack of competence — decline in writing until the firm fixes it. Accepting a CCO title without genuine authority = violation of IV(C). Resignation is not a retroactive cure for months of failing to supervise.

Standard V: Investment Analysis — Applied Vignettes

Harrel & Chong
V(A) Diligence & Reasonable BasisBoth Violated — Must Independently Evaluate Third-Party Research
Harrel (CFA candidate) writes issuer-paid research on Corix, accepting all management claims without verification — everything was fabricated. Chong (CFA charterholder) incorporates Harrel's report without independent verification, issues a "buy" recommendation. Corix is exposed as fraudulent — clients lose money.
Who violated the Code?
✅
BOTH Harrel AND Chong violated V(A). Harrel: relied exclusively on management claims without independent investigation. Chong: relied on Harrel's report without independently verifying its conclusions — especially problematic given (1) issuer-paid research warrants heightened scrutiny and (2) Harrel had no firm management structure to enforce due diligence standards.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Only Harrel violated": Chong independently violated V(A) by failing to critically assess third-party research. Passing through a report without evaluation = V(A) failure. Trap B — "Only Chong violated": Harrel's failure to conduct any independent investigation is a clear V(A) violation regardless of the ultimate fraud — diligence is required before publishing.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Using third-party research requires independent evaluation — you cannot pass it through. Issuer-paid research warrants heightened scrutiny. Both the original researcher and the relay analyst bear V(A) responsibility for the quality of the research and analysis they produce and distribute.
Dukis
V(B) Communication with ClientsMaterial Methodology Change Must Be Disclosed Even If It's an Improvement
Dukis (CFA, credit ratings MD) changes the DSCR calculation methodology for CMBS ratings — more accurately reflecting risk. Publishes new ratings without disclosing the methodology change. New method results in higher ratings than the old method would have given.
Are Dukis's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of V(B). V(B) requires disclosure of material changes to the investment/analytical process. The DSCR change was clearly material — it changed ratings outcomes. Investors rely on methodology consistency to interpret ratings over time. An undisclosed change undermines comparability and transparency even if the new method is better.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "New methodology more accurately reflects risk": Improvement in accuracy does not eliminate the disclosure obligation. Better methodology + no disclosure = violation. Better methodology + disclosed change = compliant. Trap C — "DSCR is only one element": The DSCR was a key quantitative metric — the methodology change materially affected ratings outcomes, proving its materiality. Material components must be disclosed regardless of how many other factors also contribute.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Material changes to investment/analytical processes must be disclosed regardless of whether the change is an improvement. Transparency and comparability are the goals — "we made it better" is not a substitute for "we changed it and here's how."
Duermott
V(C) Record RetentionPersonal Knowledge of Clients ≠ Adequate Records
Duermott (CFA, president) has close personal relationships with all clients, knows their situations intimately, and adjusts portfolios appropriately. But he updates client risk profiles and records only "when he finds the time" — not systematically or promptly.
Are Duermott's business practices appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of V(C). V(C) requires maintaining records to support investment actions and communications. Updating records "when he finds the time" = inadequate record maintenance. Personal knowledge of clients does not substitute for documented records — without records, there is no audit trail, no ability to demonstrate suitability, and no regulatory protection.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Communicates regularly with clients": Communication and record-keeping are separate obligations. Regular client contact is good — but undocumented = V(C) violation. Trap C — "Adjusts portfolios appropriately": Making appropriate investment changes without records documenting why = both a V(C) and potential III(C) documentation problem. Cannot demonstrate suitability was properly assessed.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Personal knowledge and client relationships are valuable but irrelevant to the record-keeping requirement. V(C) is about documentation — if client circumstances change and the record isn't updated promptly, there is no audit trail to demonstrate the account was managed in compliance with the client's current situation.

Standard VI: Conflicts of Interest — Applied Vignettes

Reebh
VI(A) Avoid or Disclose ConflictsPartial Disclosure — Using Subadvisers vs. Being Paid BY Subadvisers
Reebh (CFA) allocates client assets to third-party subadvisers who pay Lux fees based on assets placed. Clients know subadvisers are used — but don't know Lux receives payments from those subadvisers.
Are Reebh's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — not unless the financial arrangement is disclosed. Clients knowing subadvisers are used ≠ clients knowing Reebh is financially incentivised to select subadvisers who pay him. That financial conflict must be disclosed or the arrangement avoided altogether.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Disclosed use of subadvisers = sufficient": Disclosing the structural use of subadvisers ≠ disclosing the financial arrangement between Lux and those subadvisers. Two different disclosures — the financial conflict was missing. Trap B — "Payments are an improper referral fee": Reebh is not referring clients to subadvisers — he is hiring them on clients' behalf. This is a VI(A) conflict issue, not a VI(C) referral fee issue.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Partial disclosure does not satisfy VI(A). The specific conflict — that Lux receives payments from the subadvisers it hires — must be disclosed. Disclosing that subadvisers are used while concealing the financial incentive structure leaves clients unable to properly evaluate whether Reebh's selection is in their best interest.
Yang
VI(B) Priority of TransactionsFront-Running — Personal Before Client / Direction Irrelevant, Timing Is Everything
Yang (CFA) buys 50,000 Zhongpin shares and call options for his personal account. Shortly after, he directs $29.8M of Prestige client funds to buy 3M+ Zhongpin shares. His personal position benefits from the price impact of the large client purchase.
Are Yang's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — front-running = violation of VI(B). Yang traded personally in the same securities he then directed his clients to buy in much larger quantities. Client transactions must have priority. Yang reversed the order for personal gain.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Personal investments align with clients — no conflict": Trading in the same direction as clients is not the issue — TIMING is. Personal trade before client trade = front-running regardless of directional alignment. Trap C — "Prestige clients not negatively affected": Whether clients were harmed is irrelevant to VI(B). The priority principle requires client-first regardless of actual harm.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Front-running: personal trade before client trade in the same security = VI(B) violation. Direction doesn't matter — timing does. Harm to clients is not a required element. The priority rule is absolute: clients first.
Kapadia
VI(B) Priority — Facilitating Front-Running for OthersNo Direct Financial Benefit Still = Violation
Kapadia (CFA, fund trader) tips friends and relatives about upcoming fund trades before executing them. They trade ahead of the fund. Kapadia doesn't personally trade or directly benefit financially — only benefits through relationships.
Are Kapadia's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of VI(B). Kapadia facilitates front-running by others using confidential fund trading information. Personal benefit includes non-financial benefit (relationship benefit). Enabling others to front-run is as prohibited as doing it yourself.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Only inappropriate if clients are harmed": No-harm-no-foul does not apply. VI(B) is categorical regardless of whether actual trades harmed client returns. Trap C — "Didn't share individual client info": Sharing information about the fund itself is sharing confidential information. Fund trading plans are confidential regardless of whether individual client details are disclosed.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Personal benefit includes non-financial benefit (relationship benefit). Enabling others to front-run = prohibited. Confidential fund trading information must not be shared with anyone outside the proper information chain.
Perrkins
VI(B) Priority + III(B) Fair DealingCherry-Picking via Omnibus Account + "I'll Fix It Later" Fails
Perrkins (CFA, CIO) uses omnibus block trades, allocates after market close — assigning 75% of profitable trades to his own/wife's accounts and 82% of losing trades to three largest clients. Wife is the compliance officer.
Are Perrkins's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — multiple violations. VI(B): delaying allocation until intraday performance is observed, then directing winners to personal accounts = front-running via allocation cherry-picking. III(B): systematically allocating losing trades to largest clients = unfair dealing. Disclosure of a fraudulent scheme does not make it acceptable.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap B — "Disclosure makes it acceptable": Disclosing a fraudulent trade allocation scheme does not cure it. Some conduct is so fundamentally wrong that disclosure cannot sanitise it. Trap C — "Will reverse it to favour clients later": Ethics is not a ledger exercise. You cannot temporarily steal from clients intending to give it back later. Compliance is required at all times — not on a net basis over time.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Trade allocation must not be used to benefit personal accounts at clients' expense. Omnibus accounts with post-close allocation create a major conflict risk — pre-established objective allocation methodologies must be set BEFORE trades execute. "I'll make it up later" is never an ethical defence.
Kiang
VI(C) Referral FeesParties / Gift Cards / Fee Discounts = Referral Fees Requiring Disclosure
Kiang (CFA) hosts elaborate parties and distributes gift cards to clients who referred others. Offers fee discounts to clients who sent particularly lucrative referrals. No disclosure to prospective clients that referral incentives exist.
Are Kiang's actions appropriate?
✅
NO — violation of VI(C). Parties, gift cards, and fee discounts given in exchange for referrals constitute referral fees that must be disclosed to prospective clients before engagement. A prospective client needs to know whether the recommendation they received was motivated by genuine satisfaction or by financial incentive.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Reward for client loyalty": A party or gift for ALL clients regardless of referrals = loyalty reward (acceptable). A party/gift/discount ONLY for clients who provided referrals = referral fee requiring disclosure. The referral condition is what makes it a fee. Trap B — "Treats all clients fairly": All clients can access the benefits by making referrals — but fair access to the benefit doesn't replace disclosure of its existence to prospective clients.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Any benefit given in exchange for referrals — cash, gifts, discounts, parties — = referral fee under VI(C) requiring disclosure to prospective clients. Benefits given universally regardless of referral activity do not require disclosure as referral fees.

Standard VII: CFA Membership — Applied Vignettes

Taveras
VII(A) — General Difficulty OK / Specific Questions NOT OKPost-Exam Discussion Rules
Taveras (CFA) leads an exam prep course. After the exam, at a celebration, students share their experience — difficulty levels, surprise at untested areas. Taveras asks for opinions on the hardest questions.
What is Taveras permitted to do?
✅
CORRECT: Sharing general difficulty opinions with future candidates is acceptable. Saying "the exam was harder than expected" = general experience-sharing that doesn't compromise exam security. However: soliciting or passing on specific question content violates VII(A). Asking for "opinions on the most difficult questions" risks eliciting specific content — caution required.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Cannot discuss exam at all post-exam": A total ban on post-exam discussion is too broad. General experience-sharing is permitted — the restriction is on specific content, not all discussion. Trap B — "Can pass specific information to future candidates": Specific exam content = confidential. Sharing with future candidates = compromises exam integrity = VII(A) violation.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
Post-exam: general experience and difficulty opinions = permitted. Specific questions, tested content areas, question formats = prohibited. General impressions don't compromise security; specific details do.
Ahmed & Bennett
VII(B) + I(A) — Both ViolatedLapsed Membership / Silent Witnessing of Misrepresentation
Bennett (CFA) hasn't paid dues for 4 years — membership lapsed. Continues using CFA designation, claims it's permanent like a university degree. In a sales meeting with a prospect who specifically wants CFA charterholders, Bennett falsely says all investment staff are charterholders. Ahmed (CFA, newly hired) knows Bennett's membership has lapsed but says nothing.
Who violated the Code?
✅
BOTH Bennett AND Ahmed violated the Code. Bennett: VII(B) — using a lapsed CFA designation and misrepresenting it as permanent. Dues are required to maintain the right to use the designation. Ahmed: I(A) — knowingly remaining silent while a violation occurs in his presence = participating in and failing to dissociate from the violation.
⚠️ Wrong Answer Traps
Trap A — "Only Ahmed violated": Ahmed violated — but so did Bennett (the primary violator). Trap B — "Only Bennett violated": Ahmed's silence in a meeting where he knew false information was being given to a prospect = participation in the misrepresentation under I(A). Knowing silence = complicity.
🔑 Core Rule Tested
CFA designation requires annual dues payment — it is NOT permanent like a university degree. Lapsed membership = lapsed right to use the designation. And silence in the face of a known ongoing violation = participation. Ahmed needed to speak up, correct the record, or dissociate from the meeting.

Cross-Vignette Pattern Master List — The 20 Recurring Exam Logic Patterns

Ethics Application questions follow recognisable patterns. Once you can identify the pattern, you can solve the question even with unfamiliar facts. Memorise the rule, not just the scenario it came from.

★ MODULE 5 — 20 RECURRING APPLICATION PATTERNS ★
═══ GROUP 1: DISSOCIATION, ADEQUACY, AND INTENT ═══
P01: Inadequate remedy → must dissociate, not comply with partial fix [Mandracken]
P02: Good intentions + illegal means = violation [Mwangi — forgery even with client approval]
P03: Outcome never retroactively justifies a bad process [Abbha — fake shareholders; Mang — fraudulent trades]
P04: Harm to any party is NOT required for a violation [Yang, Kapadia, Perrkins — no harm ≠ no violation]

═══ GROUP 2: DISCLOSURE LIMITS ═══
P05: Disclosure cannot waive core fiduciary duties — loyalty, prudence, care, suitability [Maste]
P06: Partial disclosure ≠ full disclosure — must disclose THE specific conflict [Reebh]
P07: Disclosing a fraudulent scheme does not make the scheme acceptable [Perrkins]
P08: "I'll make it up later" is never a valid ethical defence [Perrkins]

═══ GROUP 3: MNPI AND MANIPULATION ═══
P09: Public location ≠ public information [Khatri — overheard at cricket match]
P10: Responding to analyst questions ≠ public disclosure — must use proper public channel [Kwame]
P11: Mosaic theory fails if ANY element is itself material and nonpublic
P12: Good outcome does not cure manipulation [Abbha — well-performing company]

═══ GROUP 4: CLIENT DUTIES ═══
P13: Nature of the relationship defines the duty — execution-only ≠ advisory ≠ discretionary [Gaini]
P14: Client direction does not waive suitability obligation — adviser must independently assess [Duri]
P15: Accurate but incomplete performance = violation — all three conditions required [Jergenn]
P16: Policy existence ≠ adequate compliance — must audit and test effectiveness [Giddings]

═══ GROUP 5: EMPLOYER DUTIES AND SUPERVISION ═══
P17: Loyalty continues until resignation is effective — disparaging or warming up clients before = violation [Nickoli]
P18: Whistleblowing to protect clients from employer's unethical conduct = NOT a violation [Kuznetsov]
P19: Cannot accept supervisory title without authority — must decline in writing if systems inadequate [Sasha Denikin]
P20: Silent witnessing of a known violation = participation under I(A) — must speak, correct, or dissociate [Ahmed]
The three questions to ask for any ethics application scenario:
(1) What STANDARD is being tested? Identify the standard and sub-section from the facts.
(2) What is the SPECIFIC RULE being applied? (e.g., "adequacy of the remedy" under I(A); "simultaneous dissemination" under III(B))
(3) What are the WRONG ANSWER TRAPS? Identify the trap type before selecting your answer.

The five most common wrong answer trap types across all Ethics Application questions:
TRAP TYPE A — "No harm was done / clients weren't harmed" → irrelevant; conduct is the violation regardless of outcome
TRAP TYPE B — "The outcome was good / company performed well" → retroactive justification never works
TRAP TYPE C — "Full disclosure was made" → disclosure cannot cure fiduciary waivers or fundamentally unethical conduct
TRAP TYPE D — "The client approved / directed it" → client approval cannot override legal requirements or suitability obligations
TRAP TYPE E — "Good intentions / acting in clients' benefit" → beneficial motive does not neutralise a fraudulent or prohibited process
